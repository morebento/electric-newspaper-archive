Wednesday June 3, 1998

Two-faced: making a mountain out of the Adelaide Hills
by Hendrik Gout

State Cabinet is facing a show-down over a peculiar proposal to do something unpleasant with the Adelaide Hills.

The proposal is peculiar because it has already been knocked back once, by the Mitcham City Council.

The unpleasantness has to do with a building huge place on the escarpment of the famous Adelaide Hills.

The escarpment, called the Hills Face, is the backdrop to the city and suburbs.

It was to keep the Hills Face as we know them now that the Mitcham Council rejected the plan two years ago next month.

It was an easy decision. It didn't meet the zoning laws, any more than a rat poison factory isn't allowed over your back fence. Zones are to separate industry from residential, offices from dumps, and hotels, restaurants, and accommodation centres from the Hills Face.

The developer, Andrew Garrett, knew it didn't meet zoning laws when he bought the property.

He was nevertheless so seemingly surprised when the council said "No!" that he took the case to an appeal to the relevant court.

A virtual second before the council was going to defend itself in the court, Mr Garrett withdrew the appeal.

A virtual second before that event, a Government block came up for sale in the same area; it changed hands for almost half a million dollars.

The Government has a legislative path by which it can over-ride the wishes of the city and the decision of the council - it can deem the new plan for a boozery, eatery, and accommodation palace a ‘major project' and therefore exempt from zoning restrictions.

The Department which looks after the natural environment is aghast. Minister Dorothy Kotz's officers in the Department of Environment and Heritage are busily gathering files and preparing to brief their Minister.

She'll be advised to fight the proposal. If she accepts their advice she will have to argue her case in Cabinet.

The Department of Urban Planning is also involved. Its Minister, Diana Laidlaw, does not have a record of winning from behind in Cabinet. Her departmental experts will be the ones to nominally advise her to approve or reject. They are the supposed experts in urban planning. A planning stuff-up is not what you'd want to tell your child about when he asks, "What did you do at work today, Daddy?"

The Department of Urban Planning is already in discussion with staff in other departments, but the traffic is expected to be mainly one-way. The Premier is well aware of the proposal. So is his department. The Premier does not want another fight on his hands, but he would like to show that at least somebody wants to build something in his State, even if it's outside certain current laws.

What will be decided between these three conflicting imputs?

Democrat Leader Mike Elliott had no doubt what should happen. The State Government must stick to the State Development Plan, and reject the resort and grape plantation.

"The zoning is quite clear," Mr Elliott told Parliament yesterday.

"The developer would have known that under the current zoning he would not be allowed to undertake suck a project.

"This sort of development gives South Australia a bad name.

"When these projects finally fail we then hear the cry of anti-development, yet it's not a matter of being anti-development. It's of ensuring the correct location of projects in the first place."

Which probably means that a rat poison factory ought to be kept in a rat poison factory area, specially if it's a big one - a major project.
