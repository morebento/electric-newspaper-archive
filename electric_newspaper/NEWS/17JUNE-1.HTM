Wednesday June 17, 1998


The current Premier, John Olsen, at Estimates yesterday
Auditor corporeal

In what the ALP claims is an unprecedented move, the State Opposition was last night gagged from quizzing Auditor-General Ken McPherson about electricity and water privatisation.

Shadow Treasurer Kevin Foley said this morning the chairman of Parliament's Estimates Committee, Liberal David Wotton, shut down the Estimates Committee early, thereby preventing sensitive questioning by Labor on the Olsen Government's privatisation agenda.

"It was clear from the outset of questioning that the Olsen Government appeared to have information it wished to keep hidden from public scrutiny," Mr Foley said.

"We asked Mr McPherson again about the State 3 Separation Steering Committee report which he had previously stated should be available to the Parliament and which shows that the need for a multi-million dollar write-down on the value of ETSA was known as early as December 1996.

"John Olsen used this massive write-down as an excuse to sell ETSA.

"We also asked Mr McPherson about Hindmarsh Soccer Stadium, the EDS building on North Terrace and the water privatisation contract.

"This is one of the few opportunities available to the Opposition to question the Premier and the Auditor-General in the Parliament about significant risks facing the taxpayer in this State," Mr Foley said.

"For the Government to take this unprecedented move shows the Olsen Government has learned very little from the results of the last State election in which the Liberals lost 13 seats."

Meanwhile, the Premier was a little reluctant himself to answer the odd question - or the even one.

Last week, Treasurer Rob Lucas told a business lunch that the States ought to charge their own income tax.

Despite being asked repeatedly by Opposition Leader Mike Rann at Estimates Committee whether Mr Olsen agreed with the Treasurer, Mr Olsen kept dodging the question.

Before the election, Mr Olsen dismissed a leaked paper which suggested the Government supported a State-based income tax.

The paper also canvassed a goods and services tax, and on this issue the Premier was also silent under questioning, refusing to say whether he supported a GST.
