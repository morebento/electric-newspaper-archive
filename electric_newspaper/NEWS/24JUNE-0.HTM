Wednesday June 24, 1998

Olsen Government has many volts
by Hendrik Gout

The State Government's planned sale of SA power utilities has stalled - the victim of half-truths, smear, and over-enthusiasm by the would-be sellers.

The privatisation PR team, the sale brokers, and the Premier's office are now in damage-control mode, with every likelihood that Parliament will vote against the Government's enabling legislation.

The sale process appeared on track until the agenda was taken over by confidants of the Premier's office.

On Monday, Treasurer Rob Lucas - who nominally has carriage of the sale process - was forced to deny a report in The Australian newspaper that the Government was still secretly planning to go ahead with a high-voltage transmission line called Riverlink, which would have brought cheap New South Wales electricity to South Australia.

Mr Lucas announced on June 15 that NEMMCO, the national electricity regulator, had cancelled the project. (You can read the full NEMMCO report at http://www.nemmco.com.au/riverdeter.htm although you'll need a late version of Netscape. NEMMCO's website is at http://www.nemmco.com.au/nemmco.htm )

Monday's Australian quoted unnamed industry sources who believed the decision to scrap Riverlink was taken by the Olsen Government to soften the Australian Democrats' opposition to a power sale (see separate story: E-day for Democrats).

The story said State Treasury officers met with their NSW counterparts on June 17 - two days after the Lucas announcement of Riverlink's cancellation - to discuss ways of pursuing the deal.

Mr Lucas said the story was wrong.

He said there had been no meeting since his June 15 statement.

Opposition Leader Mike Rann yesterday called on Mr Olsen to explain. Mr Rann said he had "confirmed with the New South Wales Government that a meeting between Treasury officers, at which Riverlink was discussed, did take place".

Mr Lucas says the scrapping was welcomed by the Government. "It is important to stress that NEMMCO's decision was an independent decision," Mr Lucas said. "SA was advised after it had been made."

This would mean a body on which the State had no representation made a $110 million decision without telling the State.

It also means that SA was denied access to cheap NSW power by a body not resident in South Australia.

In his Monday statement, Mr Lucas said any suggestion that SA Treasury officials had been involved in discussions to reactivate Riverlink at a later date were "both dishonest and deliberately mischievous".

The Electric Newspaper understands that this statement was not drafted in the Treasurer's office, but was put together with the collusion of two public relations practitioners working on the ETSA sale.

The PR team comprises Olsen confidant and friend, journalist Alex Kennedy, and a former staffer of Labor Premier John Bannon, Geoff Anderson.

At Parliamentary Estimates, Mr Lucas revealed these two have already been paid $25,000 for six week's work. Their brief is to counter voter resistance to electricity privatisation.

So seriously does the team take its work that it created an incident last week, when Australian Democrat energy spokesperson Sandra Kanck was inspecting the two Torrens Island power stations.

These stations are now owned and operated by Optima Energy. They are part of the proposed sale.

Ms Kanck had organised a tour through the stations, and a briefing by Optima executives.

But when she arrived, the briefing did not commence. There was a wait until Ms Kennedy and a representative of Morgan Stanley arrived. Only then did the tour and briefing begin.

"It was as if the Optima people couldn't be trusted to say anything. They had to have a nanny," Ms Kanck said later.

Before Ms Kanck had completed her scheduled briefing, it was cut short because "Alex Kennedy had to go back to the office", Ms Kanck said.

The Democrats have already exposed what they say is "another false statement" in the State Government's advertising privatisation campaign.

The taxpayer-funded brochure titled ‘Electricity Reform: Your Questions Answered' issued shortly after the Premier announced his intention to sell ETSA and Optima states "The Auditor-General's latest annual report states that ETSA has the highest costs for delivering electricity of all Australian authorities".

When one of Sandra Kanck's staffers combed through the report, he found no mention of that supposed finding.

Ms Kanck rang the Auditor-General. He confirmed that there was no mention of these comparisons.

Ms Kanck then contacted the Electricity Supply Association of Australia.

South Australia is far from the most expensive. It comes behind Victoria, which has already privatised, and the Northern Territory, which has a sparse population scattered over a huge area.

"It's disgraceful that taxpayer's money has been squandered to mislead them about the efficiency of South Australia's electricity utilities," Ms Kanck said.

"If the Government had made a similar statement about a private corporation it would be liable for damages for breach of the Trade Practices Act."

Hundreds of thousands of the brochures have already been distributed. The Treasurer admitted the error of fact when it was uncovered by Ms Kanck, saying the reference had been ‘incorrectly assigned'. The brochure is still being distributed unchanged.

So seriously does the Government suspect it may lose the vote on privatisation that Mr Lucas last week called on MP's not to lock themselves into opposing the sale, as National Party MHA Karlene Maywald has already done.

Ms Maywald said her constituents in rural South Australia were totally against privatisation.

The Premier is scheduled to make a major statement on privatisation in Parliament next Tuesday. Liberal sources said yesterday that with leadership tensions running high, Mr Olsen will need to make it a commanding performance to ease backbencher angst about the Government's direction and tactics.
