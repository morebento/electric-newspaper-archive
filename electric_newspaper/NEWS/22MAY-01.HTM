Friday May 22, 1998

Here's how it happened

In September 1965, an unknown Major-General Suharto took command of the military after an abortive communist coup in which six senior army generals were killed.

With the ruthlessness of Stalin and Mao, Major-General Suharto crushed the attempted coup. As many as 500,000 people were killed, primarily in communal violence, between October 1965 and early 1966.

In March, executive power transferred from Sukarno to Suharto. The next day Suharto banned the Communist Party of Indonesia. Two weeks later, Suharto was formally named acting president by appointed People's Consultative Assembly, and formally elected president a year later.

In 1973, Suharto was appointed unopposed for second five-year term by MPR, and re-appointed in 1978, 1983, 1988, 1993 and 1998 by the MPR. The ruling Golkar, an association of "functional groups" organised by the army in 1964, comfortably wins on each occasion.

Suharto’s troubles began in July last year, when the rupiah started to crumble. Indonesia asked the IMF for financial help - the IMF-orchestrated a bailout of more than $40 billion in aid. In February this year, Suharto announced new steps on the rupiah's exchange rate as riots over rising food prices worsened. At least five people died in January-February rioting.

None of this stopped Suharto being re-elected unopposed to a seventh five-year term in March.

Student protests broke out across the nation. Suharto announced new Cabinet, which included his eldest daughter and several political and business associates of the First Family.

Thousands of students held campus protests, calling Suharto the "cause of all disasters." Indonesia raised fuel prices by up to 71 percent, three days of riots broke out in the North Sumatran capital of Medan which cost six lives, and on May 12 six students were shot and killed during clashes with police in Jakarta.

By May 14, chaos reigned. Mobs attacked and damaged buildings linked to firms controlled by Suharto's family and friends. The rupiah was worth 11,500 to the dollar. And then it was all too much.

Suharto had to go. Yesterday, he went.
