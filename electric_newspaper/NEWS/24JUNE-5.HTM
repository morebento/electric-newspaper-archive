Wednesday June 24, 1998

Pie in the sky looks less half-baked

The U.S. aerospace company Kistler yesterday successfully tested part of its re-useable launch vehicle- and moved a step closer to being able to use South Australia as a launch site.

Kistler completed a key phase of its parachute drop tests, a six-parachute cluster, believed to be one of the largest canopy deployments in the world.

The testing simulated the safe return of the first stage K-1 vehicle following separation and return to the launch site.

Kistler has two sites where it can launch the K-1 if the vehicle proves technically and commercially successful.

One site is in Nevada, and the other is in the far north of South Australia at Woomera.

Aborigines from the Woomera region yesterday signed an agreement allowing Kistler to have access to the area, and in return expect to get jobs, financial consideration, training and business opportunities, and the promise of on-going consultation.

Building work for the rocket launching site may begin in July if Kistler holds to its timetable.

The Kistler K-1 is a two-stage reusable launch vehicle that separates prior to orbit, requiring landing mechanisms for both stages. The six-parachute cluster is designed to support the landing of the first stage, the Launch Assisted Platform (LAP). The six parachutes total almost a hectare of canopy material that are attached to extension lines about 35 kilometres long.

An instrumented payload weighing 40,000 pounds, near the estimated weight of Kistler's first stage, was dropped from an aircraft flying at an altitude of 10,000 feet above the Yuma Proving Grounds in Yuma, Arizona. Twelve seconds into flight, the cluster of parachutes were deployed, allowing the object to touch down safely.

"We're very pleased that Kistler Aerospace now holds the record in deploying the world's largest parachute system ever," said Robert Wang, chairman of Kistler Aerospace. "This achievement moves Kistler one-step closer to launch."
