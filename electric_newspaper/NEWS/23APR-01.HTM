Thursday April 23, 1998

Ingo's enemies sharpening Armitage's scalpel
By Hendrik Gout

Poor Graham Ingerson. Promoted well above his abilities, desperate to succeed, and headed for the chop. And if he trips up, it will be because he's the Premier's fall guy.

Mr Ingerson is Deputy Premier of South Australia. In State Parliament, Mr Ingerson said that he was first made aware of a $96.8 million devaluation of the Electricity Trust of SA in December last year, when he read it in ETSA's annual report.

But a write-down of such significance would normally be conveyed by any statutory authority to its Minister.

Which is why Under-Treasurer Gerard Bradley was in the dock yesterday. He was giving evidence in the trial of his ex-boss. When did Mr Bradley tell the Deputy Premier of the write-down, Parliament's Economic and Finance Committee wanted to know. Before or after December?

Mr Bradley is no fool. No promotion above abilities here. Mr Bradley is a thorough professional. And when he got that question, he knew its importance.

He took it on notice. The answer will be delivered to the committee this afternoon. If he tells the committee he did not inform Mr Ingerson, then the Deputy Premier will be as safe. If he answers that he did tell Mr Ingerson before December, then Mr Ingerson is for the long jump.

All of which is not happy news for Mr Ingerson. It wasn't his idea to sell the State's electricity generating and distribution network. That spark of inspiration came from Mr Ingerson's boss, Premier John Olsen, and it came a long time ago - before the last State election.

Had Mr Olsen gone to the polls with the ETSA sale as part of his policy, the Liberals would now be sitting on the Opposition benches. Mr Ingerson's problem is that the Government has always maintained that it decided after the election to sell ETSA. That's the trouble with re-writing history. You have to keep editing.

Not everyone is terribly distressed by Mr Ingerson's discomfort. Many Liberals think he should go back to selling proprietary medicines. The Government Enterprises Minister, Michael Armitage, may well agree. Mr Armitage has many times Mr Ingerson's talent, and if he hasn't heard he's a likely candidate for the job of Deputy Premier, then he's not listening.

Mr Olsen, too, won't mind if Mr Ingerson is pushed on his own sword. The bumbling Ingo has been something less than an asset to the Premier.

On the other hand, if Mr Ingerson was told of the ETSA write-down before the election, then it raises serious questions about Mr Olsen's own version of history - that is, the Government had no intention of selling ETSA until December.

Today's committee hearing is open to the public. It should be more entertaining than watching the soaps on television - even if the plot is less believable.
