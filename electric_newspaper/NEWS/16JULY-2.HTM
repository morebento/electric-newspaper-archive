Thursday July 16, 1998

Hard Labor for Mike Rann
by Hendrik Gout

Opposition Leader Mike Rann has had fun poking fun at the divided Liberal leadership, but things in his own Party are not exactly a bed of daffodils. There is much happening within the newly-swelled ranks of the Parliamentary ALP, and Mr Rann is having to pay it some heed.

The most obvious is the influence of Terry Cameron, a canny and once-powerful figure in the ALP who headed the Centre Left faction when it was at a force to be reckoned with in Labor politics.

This faction imploded three years ago, but not before Mr Cameron had had himself re-endorsed for a safe seat in the Legislative Council's red leather lounge.

Mr Cameron hardly set the world on fire, but he relished his shadow Transport portfolio and the status and profile it gave him.

After the last State election, Mr Cameron lost his front-bench position and Labor's boss in the Legislative Council Carolyn Pickles picked up the shadow portfolio.

Mr Cameron was mightily miffed. Since then he has had many a good word to say about Mike Rann, but none of them spoken very convincingly.

Mr Cameron is openly courting the Government's desperate hope that he will vote for the ETSA privatisation legislation.

Official Labor policy is for ETSA and Optima to stay in public ownership. Any Labor Member who voted with the Government against Party policy would face the certain prospect of expulsion from the Party.

Meanwhile, the Labor factions have decided to install the newly-elected Member for Lee, Michael Wright, as Ralph Clarke's replacement to the shadow Ministry. Mr Clarke has stood aside from his shadow portfolios following assault charges laid against him over a domestic incident. He has pleaded not guilty to the charges.

Mr Clarke will not return to the frontbench whatever the outcome of that case, and is likely to face a challenge to his endorsement in the safe Labor seat of Ross-Smith. A fornt-runner could be Leesa Chesser, who ironically lost a pre-selection battle against Michael Wright in Lee. Another name put forward is prominent Adelaide barrister John Rau, who some in the Party are keen to see boost Labor's strength in Parliament.

Labor factional leaders are already discussing who should be awarded this plum prize. Not all those discussions have been harmonious.

Mr Rann is not facing any challenge to his own leadership, but scrutiny of Labor's internal divisions have so far escaped the scrutiny placed on dissension in the Government.

And all in the Labor Party agree on one thing - they'd like to keep it that way.
