Tuesday June 16, 1998

The stripper
by Chris Warren

It's called 'award stripping', and it could be coming to a workplace near you.

The loss of hard-won award conditions as a trade-off for enterprise bargaining agreements will accelerate next month, when the Federal Government's controversial Workplace Relations Act really starts to bite.

Building and construction unions yesterday staged one-day stoppages around the country to tell their members what you could lose with 'award stripping'.

Some of the 20 award conditions which could disappear from the National Building and Construction Industry Award include

  • Contract of employment. Employers want to remove restrictions on hiring casuals, and introduce regular part-time work as part of the process to replace full-time employees with casuals.
  • Penalty rates. Building workers will be paid ordinary hours on weekends, and give up time-and-a- half on Saturdays and double time on Sundays.
  • Payment of wages. Workers to be paid weekly, fortnightly, or monthly, which adds to the uncertainty of being not being paid by fly-by-night builders.
  • Inclement weather. Employers want to change this clause so that building workers can be made to toil in the rain.

Similar moves are underway in the liquor trades and mining industries.

Yesterday in the city of Adelaide, a rally by 500 building workers condemned the Howard Government's Workplace Relations Act, and in particular the insidious practice of award stripping.

"Every single provision in your award came because other people fought long and hard to get it," the Construction Union's Allen Harris told the rally outside the Federal Government's Employment Advocate's office in Currie Street.

"They want to take it off for one reason only - to make bosses richer," Mr Harris said.

At the other end of town, the Master Builders' Association was defending 'award stripping' with the argument that building workers had to be more flexible and more productive.

"What we have to do is structure working hours so that it makes it easier for people to work, and hopefully that will being in more employment," said MBA chief executive Rob Stewart.

Mr Stewart said there were plenty of people waiting to work in the building industry who would be happy to forego award conditions for an enterprise agreement.

That argument doesn't cut much ice at Roxby Downs.

One thousand Roxby workers downed tools in defiance of an Industrial Commission order which outlawed yesterday's 24-hour strike.
