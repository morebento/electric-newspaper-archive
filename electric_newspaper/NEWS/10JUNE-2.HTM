Wednesday June 10, 1998

One billion reasons not to sell ETSA

The Australian Democrats expect to get financial data on the effect of an ETSA sale this week - three months after asking the Government for the figures.

Legislation to sell ETSA cannot pass the Legislative Council without Democrat support. The Democrats say they will not vote for the sale unless the Government provides hard facts on the financial impact of keeping or selling the power utilities.

One of the considerations will be the amount of penalties involved in the early repayment of existing ETSA loans.

As with most loans, banks will charge a premium if loans are repaid early. This happens because - as with a home loan - people want to repay loans negotiated at times when interest rates were high, and take out new loans when interest rates drop.

The Electric Newspaper understands that some ETSA loans are being repaid at more than 15 per cent interest.

A normally well-placed source told The Electric Newspaper today that the penalties for early repayment of existing ETSA loans could total as much as $1 billion.

If this is the case, then the Government will have to do some fancy footwork to make a convincing case for the sale. Democrat energy spokesperson said this morning she was already suspicious of the soon-to-be-released figures because of the long delay in having them provided.

Treasurer Rob Lucas dismissed suggestions that the figures had been artificially weighted in favour of the sale. The reason for the delay, he said, was so that the figures could be put into a form legible to the layperson.

Meanwhile, the State ombudsman’s office is still ploughing through 12,00 documents which the Government has refused to release to the State Opposition under Freedom of Information.

These include the Schroders Report and the State 3 Separation Committee Report - both documents vital to the case -0 which the Government has with-held.

The State Opposition appealed to the ombudsman, whose office is studying the material - understood to total over 100,000 pages of vital information - to see if it should be released under Freedom of Information legislation.
