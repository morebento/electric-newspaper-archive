Thursday July 9, 1998

Back to the benches

The future of SA ex-deputy Premier Graham Ingerson is likely to be clearer today, with a special committee investigating whether he misled Parliament expected to make a report before its three-week deadline expires.

Normally, such a committee in other Parliaments (this is the first one in South Australia) would hear from a range of witnesses, including the accused, before delivering a judgement.

But some Government strategists are of the opinion that the case against Mr Ingerson is almost certain to be proven, and wish to minimise the damage by convincing Mr Ingerson to accept a change of portfolio.

If the committee does find Mr Ingerson misled the House, convention dictates that he would be sacked as Minister and return to the backbenches.

If Mr Olsen does not withdraw Mr Ingerson’s Ministerial commission, the Opposition is considering moving a motion of no-confidence against him, which would need the support of the three cross-bench MP’s to succeed.

The Government is now in damage-control mode. Senior Liberals believe the election of Rob Kerin to the deputy Premiership on Tuesday signalled the start of the Government’s reconstruction, and wish to avoid another damaging round of instability which a petulant and possibly vengeful sacked Minister could cause.

They believe their long-term interests are best served by a quick decision and a speedy resolution of the Ingerson affair.

Government sources were delighted yesterday that Opposition Leader Mike Rann had been embarrassed into attending a briefing on the proposed sale of ETSA and Optima Energy. Mr Rann had initially refused to attend the briefing unless he was granted certain conditions, but enough Labor MP’s accepted the invitation to the briefing to make it difficult for the Labor Leader to refuse.

They told The Electric Newspaper they had finally succeeded in turning the heat up on the Opposition over the ETSA sale, and didn’t want the Ingerson affair to be a distraction now that there were perceived cracks in Labor’s anti-privatisation stance.

They were also delighted by the equivocation of Labor MLC Terry Cameron, who has refused to categorically rule out voting against the sale. The Government Bill will pass the Upper House if the sole Independent in the Legislative Council, and at least one Labor defector, votes with the Liberal Party.
