Friday May 29, 1998

We took out our highlighters. The Advertiser's Miranda Murphy

Gerard Bradley gives the Budget overview

The Advertiser's Greg Kelton seeks an explanation

Treasury's Bob Schwarz: almost infinite patience

The Budget: the key to the lock-up

At 11.30, we were handed the Budget papers at the posh Stamford Hotel, over the road from Parliament House. We surrendered our mobile phones at the front door, where sat our friendly guard who made sure we didn't leave, we took out our highlighters and yellow stickies, and we tried to make sense over a thousand pages of Statements, budget lines, economic indicators, and tables.

Under-Treasurer Gerard Bradley gave us a quick overview, with overheads and crisp explanations.

And then we dived into the stack of documents.

About an hour later, most of us had a cup of coffee. Then we called over the nearest Treasury officer, and invited him to have a coffee with us. And we sought answers to the incomprehensible.

Treasury officers in South Australia are as good as any in Australia. Their patience is almost infinite. If they didn't know the answer to a technical question, they called over a colleague who did. If we wanted help to find a table, or a comparison, we were guided through the documents until the answer was found.

There are the men and women who carry out Government fiscal policy. They are among the most powerful people in South Australia.

At one o'clock, Treasurer Rob Lucas faced a media conference inside the lock-up. He made a reasonable fist of it. His options were limited, he told us. Debt reduction, asset sales, underlying deficit... the words rolled off the roof of the mouth. But we didn't hear words like victory, like recovery, like low unemployment.

"Treasurer, last year, the then Treasurer Stephen Baker stood just where you're standing, and told us we were in the home straight. Who shot the horse?" asked The Australian's Matthew Abraham.

To which the Treasurer replied that we're now at the start of a four-year financial plan. In which case, asked another reporter, aren't we back where we started? Not so, said the Treasurer. Debt is down, he said.

So we asked about debt reduction. What will happen when all the assets are sold? What direction will the Treasurer take then? The same as now, more or less, was the implied answer.

We asked about unemployment. "Aren't you telling unemployed young people to hang on - just hang on?" asked a journalist. No, said the Treasurer.

Well, if our economy is still virtually stagnant, after four years of Liberalism, would he concede that previous Budgets hadn't worked? "With the benefit of hindsight..." the Treasurer began.

And we gathered our laptops, collected our highlighters, bundled our Budget papers, and let out the door. We strolled across to Parliament House and watched Mr Lucas deliver his Budget speech.

We could have predicted this Budget's direction. We could have, with the benefit of foresight.
