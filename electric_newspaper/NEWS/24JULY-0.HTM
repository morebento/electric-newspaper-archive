Friday July 24, 1998

John Olsen to Ian Mclachlan "When Ingerson decided to stay on, I clapped like this!"

Liberal Party execution fails when hangmen go home

The governing Liberal Party in South Australia last night showed it could muster malice and vengeance, but not tactics. A move to expel a rogue Liberal MP from the Party failed when not enough people turned up to stab him in the front.

They'd gone home.

On Wednesday, the Liberal Member for Hammond, Peter Lewis, voted with the Opposition and National Party MP Karlene Maywald in a motion of no confidence against Industry and Trade Minister Graham Ingerson.

Mr Ingerson had been found by a specially-established privileges committee to have "deliberately misled" Parliament.

The entire Government, including Mr Ingerson and Premier John Olsen, voted to adopt the committee's report.

But the Government would not support a censure motion against the Minister, and also voted against the no confidence motion. Mr Lewis was the only Liberal MP to cross the floor.

"We've got the right policies to govern this State," Mr Lewis said last night. "All we need to do now to ensure that the public's confidence is restored in us as a Party and in us as a Government in the Parliament, is to also show that we've got some principles that they can rely on in the way that we conduct affairs in the Parliament and provide information."

The Premier's supporters showed their principles soon after. They tried to muster sufficient votes in a hastily-called party-room meeting to expel Mr Lewis from the Liberal Party.

Had this succeeded, Mr Lewis would have been punished for upholding established Westminster traditions, while Mr Ingerson keeps his Ministerial portfolios and frontbench positions for defying them.

But the Liberal's move against Mr Lewis failed when it was realised the House had risen for the night and too many of their numbers had scampered out the doors and into the night.

Mr Lewis said later that it would split the Liberal Party wide open if he was expelled from the Party while Mr Ingerson remained on the frontbench.

"It's not Peter Lewis that they're cutting out. What they are saying is that principles don't matter," Mr Lewis said.

Mr Lewis is now openly critical of the Premier, saying Mr Olsen is "not the best person to lead the Party".

The Premier is understood to disagree.

This morning, deputy Premier Rob Kerin denied that a party-room meeting had been scheduled, saying Mr Olsen had simply given Mr Lewis an ultimatum to stop criticising his leadership.

"Peter's statements were felt to be causing a lot of disunity which is damaging to both the Government and therefore the State," Mr Kerin said, echoing the views of those who think that the Government is the State.

"The Premier has given Peter an ultimatum to either stop those statements or resign, and a very frank discussion followed from that, and Peter and I are meeting today to further discuss it but there was not a move for a party-room meeting last night."

Mr Olsen deposed the then Premier, Dean Brown, after a long campaign of destabilisation which did not see Mr Olsen give himself an ultimatum or threaten himself with expulsion.

A Channel Seven poll of 3,500 respondents conducted on Wednesday night showed a massive majority - 89 per cent - think Mr Ingerson should resign or be sacked.

"But what about the other million South Australians?" Mr Olsen smirked when asked to respond to the survey by Channel Seven's political reporter, Mike Smithson.

The normally loquacious Mr Smithson had no reply except an incredulous facial expression.
