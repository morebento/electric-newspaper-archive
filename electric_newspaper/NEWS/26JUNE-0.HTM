Friday June 26, 1998

Fuse blows on ETSA privatisation
by Hendrik Gout

The Australian Democrats yesterday announced their intention to vote against the proposed sale of ETSA, citing Government "deception, exaggeration, and rubbery figures" as the reason for rejecting the key Government policy.

The move intensifies pressure on embattled Premier John Olsen, whose political life is largely dependent on showing his Party he has the authority to implement the sale.

Mr Olsen was in Darwin when he heard the news of the Democrats' decision. In Adelaide, Treasurer Rob Lucas vowed to fight on and introduce the sale legislation in both Houses of Parliament, but ruling out a lease option which does not need Parliamentary approval.

The Democrats' decision was made after 1000 hours of research and consultation by the Party's energy spokesperson, Sandra Kanck.

"The harder I looked at the evidence, the less convincing the Government's arguments were," Ms Kanck said.

"The alleged threat of losses of ‘State bank proportions' should ETSA and Optima not be sold was no more than a cynical attempt to frighten the public into supporting the sale.

"My investigation exposed the claimed budgetary benefits as hollow rhetoric that would lead to a net loss in the medium and long term.

"All the evidence indicates keeping ETSA and Optima will protect South Australian electricity users from predatory pricing.

"It's apparent the decision to sell had more to do with politics than economics," Ms Kanck said.

The Government was not surprised by the decision, although it had retained a candle of hope that the Democrats would come up with some sort of compromise sale, perhaps allowing the ‘poles and wires' to be sold while keeping the electricity generating component in public hands.

The Government is now hoping a Labor member in the Upper House will defect and vote with the Government. Labor MLC's contacted last night said there was a chance this might happen, but that the odds on this were longer than raising the Titanic.

Opposition Leader Mike Rann rejected hints that a Labor defector might cross the floor.

"The decision against privatisation was passed unanimously by Caucus," he said.

Mr Lucas revealed that the Government had held talks with a number of Upper House Labor representatives over the past month, but declined with a smile to predict what the scenario would be when the legislation hits the Legislative Council.

Liberal parliamentarians contacted last night were gloomy. One backbencher who voted for Mr Olsen during the leadership spill said the Premier was "not quite in deep shit, but not smelling too flash".

Another senior Liberal said no amount of posturing would make up for failure to deliver. "I think Joe Public is starting to think we can't run the State, and that's never a good thing for a Government," he said.

Ms Kanck was unmoved by the Government's threat of introducing a mini-budget to further cut State spending and increase taxes, saying nothing in the Government's figures indicated a major change to the State's finances since previous Budgets had said debt reduction was on target.

And Mr Rann also dismissed a suggestion that together with the Democrats, Labor would wear the blame for a mini-budget.

"I think if you look back, the people who get the blame for taxes are always the Government," he said.
