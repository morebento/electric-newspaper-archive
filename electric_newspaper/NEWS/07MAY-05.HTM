A tale of two lunches
By Hendrik Gout

On the one hand, there was Peter Reith, the Federal Workplace Relations, ducking protesters to have a feed and a spruik. On the other, there was Clive Armour, managing director of the South Australian Electricity Trust. He had to have take-away.

Peter Reith is not finding life easy. His attempt to smash the Maritime Union has ended up in decisive losses before the Federal Court and the High Court. His critics have called on him to resign. The federal Opposition wants a judicial inquiry into aspects of the anti-union campaign. But Mr Reith not only had the guts to come into town and speak before a lunch organised by a chamber of commerce, he actually seemed to enjoy it. And his media secretary did his best to make the Minister available for interviews after coffee and dessert.

Clive Armour had an order, but it wasn't a luncheon order. It was a command from the State Government to shut his trap.

Such is the difference between the State and Federal Governments.

Mr Armour, as ETSA chief, was to speak to an Australian Institute of Company Directors lunch. Because the State Government is in the midst of the controversial process of privatising ETSA, Mr Armour was ordered, presumably by Minister Michael Armitage, to stay mum. He could eat, but he couldn't talk with his mouth full.

The enforced gag shows the level of paranoia in senior State Government circles. Senior business and commercial leaders in SA were appalled by the action. The Olsen Government is rapidly losing the confidence of people who sit around boardrooms in Adelaide. Even in the secretive world of high-level company administration, no chief executive has been gagged like Mr Armour was.

This State Government has become an object of derision, and a subject of dismay, among many company directors.

Compare this with Mr Reith. He joked, be explained, he cajoled. He argued for waterfront reform. He garnered support.

Outside the Reith luncheon, and later at a northern Adelaide suburban hotel where Mr Reith attended a Liberal Party fundraiser, a few dozen vocal opponents chanted slogans and blew whistles to heckle the Minister. He managed to cope.

A pity, then, that the State Government is in hibernation, growling at anyone who disturbs it. The Premier, Mr Olsen, has taken to phoning reporters who've filed stories unfavourable to the Government and berating them. This is hardly professional. It also shows the shallowness of the media advice Mr Olsen is receiving from his media secretaries.

The State Government is now throwing more taxpayers' money at this problem. It has appointed a new spin doctor, Stephen Duffield, whose new job it is to come up with a new-style propaganda campaign selling Government initiatives.

He will probably be issued with a State Government credit card. It may possibly be good for a serious business lunch. After all, if he talks about privatising electricity, he can charge it.
