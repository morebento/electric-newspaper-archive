Monday July 20, 1998

What goes up, ought to come down
by Hendrik Gout

Officials of the U.S. Kistler aerospace company, Government representatives and industry figures will ascend on the northern South Australian township of Woomera on Thursday, for a ceremony to mark the start of construction of Kistler's new rocket launching facility.

The announcement was made yesterday not by the company or the Government, but by Opposition Leader Mike Rann.

"Construction will begin shortly on Australia's first commercial spaceport, 15 kilometres from the Woomera airport and close to the Woomera rocket range made so famous in the 1950's and 1960's," Mr Rann said.

Mr Rann will meet Kistler's chairman Robert Wang on Wednesday.

Kistler signed an agreement with the Australian Government in April to allow the company to use the disused Woomera rocket range to launch the world's first re-usable launch vehicle. Using the cost savings achievable through reusability, Kistler hopes to transform the commercial launch industry by offering low cost, reliable access to space.

The company wants to provide launch services to the roughly 1,700 new commercial telecommunications satellites forecasted over the next decade.

The vehicle is still not fully tested. Kistler has been unable to obtain US licences to operate the vehicle at its testing facility in Nevada.

"I will be asking Mr Wang to help SA refute absurd claims in US newspapers that Kistler will remain in Australia only as long as it takes to get the US Federal Government to grant it a licence to launch its rockets from Nevada," Mr Rann said.

"We need to squash that kind of speculation and Thursday's ceremony will give Mr Wang an opportunity to announce a long-term commitment to Woomera's future," he said.

Writing in the Nevada Review-Journal, reporter John G. Edwards claimed Kistler Aerospace Corporation was going to Woomera only to demonstrate the safety and reliability of its satellite technology.

Mr Edwards said Kistler has been unable to gain Federal Aviation Administration approval for its satellite blast-offs from its Nevada grounds, so it decided to demonstrate its satellite launching system in southern Australia first.

The Review-Journal story quoted Tim Carlson, chief of the NTS Development Corporation, which promotes development of the Nevada test site, as saying the Australian tests will boost efforts to obtain FAA approval for Nevada launches.

Mr Carlson described FAA officials as cautious about safety considerations involved with rocketry for commercial satellites.

"It's absolutely good news" for Nevada, agreed Mr Wang. "It will expedite the process down in Nevada once we launch in Australia."

Mr Wang told the Review-Journal he hoped the FAA would approve a launch license for Kistler at the test site based on the Australian launch results.

"We are something new that the FAA has never dealt with before," Wang explained. "There are no regulations on the books right now" for commercial satellite launches.

"We are working very diligently to prove that we are worthy of a launch license," Mr Wang said. "We are very pleased with the progress we are making with the FAA."

Three months ago, Kistler representatives told The Electric Newspaper that the company wanted to develop two launch sites - one at Nevada and another at Woomera.

The company denied aerospace industry speculation that Kistler would wind up its Woomera operation immediately it gained FAA clearance to operate out of Nevada.

Industry insiders in the United States told The Electric Newspaper last week they could see no advantage in any company maintaining two operational launch sites in different hemispheres.

They said they found it strange that the Kistler corporation would not have mentioned its Nevada problems when they were in South Australia in April. The State Government has so far declined to comment on speculation that the company is using the Woomera tests as proving flights for its technology, or that the company's commercial launches will take off from Nevada once FAA approval has been granted.

SA Premier John Olsen, Federal Defence Minister Ian Mclachlan and Federal Industry Minister John Moore will attend the Woomera ceremony.
