Wednesday July 29, 1998

entrance to the Coorong

Coorongs don't make a right

The Coorong - a huge inland sea once fed by Australia's longest river and with access to the Southern Ocean - is getting a dose of fresh water.

The Mighty Murray starts in the Victorian Alps, and is joined by tributaries from as far as the south-east Queensland border ranges.

The river eventually flows into Lake Alexandrina and from there to the sea. The Coorong is a natural lagoon which has suffered from human intervention in the river's flow.

A series of weirs now blocks the river's flow into the Coorong, causing it to become a salt-water lake and completely changing its ecology.

Over the next two weeks, one of the weirs near the Murray Mouth will be opened to discharge fresh water into the Coorong, while at the same time lowering the water level in Lake Alexandrina.

The Murray is now managed along its entire length. Billions of litres of water are pumped out of the river for irrigation - so much so that the flow has now been reduced to a trickle and the river's mouth is almost blocked by sand.

The Coorong's fragile ecology depends on getting a mix of fresh water from the Murray and sea water from the ocean. Because the Murray tap has effectively been turned off, it has become progressively more saline.

About 1,000 megalitres a day will be released into the Coorong until mid-August.
