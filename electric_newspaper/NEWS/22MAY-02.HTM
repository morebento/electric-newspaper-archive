Friday May 22, 1998

ETSA: putting the art before the Bourse

It's now clearly apparent that the State Government is in complete bewilderment about how to sell ETSA and Optima Energy.

Premier John Olsen announced his Government's intention to sell on February 17. He gave three ways to sell - by trade sale, public float, or long-term lease.

The long-term lease, he told Parliament, was the least attractive option because it would reduce the price. But, he said, it had the advantage of not needing Parliamentary approval.

In other words, the Government was looking at the lease option if legislation to sell ETSA and Optima Energy was blocked by the Democrats and Opposition in the Upper House.

Yesterday, depending on how one reads the cards, the Government either handed itself four aces and a joker, or is now holding a multi-billion dollar bluff.

The day began with a media conference at Parliament by the Democrats' energy spokesperson, Sandra Kanck. For the past months, Ms Kanck has been travelling Australia, gathering information here and interstate, trying to formulate the Democrats' response to the sale.

So far, the Democrats have yet to be convinced that the sale would be in South Australia's best interests.

So all the reporters gathered by the side door of Parliament, in a leafy courtyard, and Ms Kanck began.

"It's my understanding that without Parliamentary approval, the return gained from any lease would be drastically reduced," she said.

"The Government could also be forced to accept an annual payment rather than a lump sum up front.

"Under these circumstances, a lease would lead to a substantial net budgetary loss, defeating the purpose of selling ETSA and Optima," Ms Kanck said.

What's this? A lease is not an option?

Well, Ms Kanck explained, on Wednesday she had seen the Treasurer, Mr Lucas. That's what he'd told her.

It took a minute for this to sink in. What it means, if Mr Lucas's new figures are accurate, is that without Parliamentary approval, ETSA and Optima is not worth selling.

But getting accurate figures is not easy. The Government doesn't have them, or if it does is not releasing them. Treasurer Lucas an hour later released some documents sought by the Labor Party under Freedom of Information legislation, but kept the vast majority - about 1,200 - to itself.

"The list of documents we have not been given runs to 73 pages," Shadow Treasurer Kevin Foley told reporters.

"Missing from the documents are the Schroders report, the separation steering committee report, detailed information on the lease deal with Edison capital, and information about the Government's pre-election planning for electricity privatisation.

"This makes a joke of the Liberals' claim that they want a full debate," Mr Foley said.

In fact, we know a lot less about the implications of an ETSA sale than we know.

For example, we don't know - because the Government won't say - how much we'll pay in penalties if current loans are repaid early. We don't know how many loans are outstanding, and at what interest rate. A fortnight ago on commercial radio, Premier John Olsen said he'd only just become aware of the highest interest rate loan, at 17 per cent. Yet the Treasurer had told Ms Kanck this information two months ago. The Schroders report, which recommends selling ETSA, remains secret. A conflicting report, by respected Queensland economist Prof John Quiggin and SA University's John Spoehr, is in the public domain, but has been dismissed by the Government without analysis.

Ms Kanck also said that both the ACCC nor the NCC had told her ETSA did not need to be privatised, that competition payments were not necessarily at risk without a sale, and that there was still no reliable evidence of the social impact of a sale.

"If selling ETSA is such a good deal, Mr Olsen, what are you scared of? Why won't you release this information," Mr Foley asked.

Good question, Kevin. Over to you, John.
