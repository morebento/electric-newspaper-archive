Tuesday July 28, 1998

Ringing up the bill

South Australians are to pay up to $200 million for a new emergency services radio network, in a deal which may be difficult to unscramble.

The bulk of the money could come from a special levy on householders. The network aims to better connect police, fire, ambulance, the Country Fire Service, and the State Emergency Service with themselves, each other, and the public.

According to Premier John Olsen, the Olympic Games are partially responsible for the huge bill.

"We have been advised by the South Australian police that as a result of the 2000 Olympics they are required to have in place a working and effective communications system up to nine months prior to the games themselves," Mr Olsen said.

"The new service will allow our emergency services to have access to improved technology, functionality, and best practice in a wide area mobile radio and paging.

"As well, a number of Government agencies have discovered that their existing networks are at risk of the Year 2000 problem. So for many reasons it is critical to get this network upgraded," Mr Olsen said.

The State Opposition has asked why the Motorola company, which will provide hardware for the network, did not have to go through a tendering process.

Shadow Emergency Services Minister Patrick Conlon said Mr Olsen would have a difficult task explaining why Motorola was awarded a contract valued between $60 and $134 million without going through the tender process, in contravention of the State Supply Act.

Motorola was awarded the contract when it signed an incentive package with Mr Olsen, during the time he was Infrastructure Minister in 1994.

"In September 1994, Mr Olsen denied in Parliament there had been any deal struck for Motorola to receive any incentives over and above its $16 million incentive package," Mr Conlon said.

"Back then he said: ‘No side deals in relation to the development of the main package - the main package stands and falls alone. I repeat, there has been no formal or informal discussion with Motorola about other components of business'," Mr Conlon said.

"The then chief executive officer of Information Technology, Ray Dundon, however confirmed that Motorola's contract for the radio network was tied to its incentive package."

At $200 million, the system is possibly the most expensive per capita in the world.

The populous US State of Florida, where crime is almost a leisure activity as well as a profession, recently revamped its emergency services network at a cost well below the $200 proposed for South Australia.

No provision is made in the State Budget for the revamp. However, the Government has determined to cut its expenditure on police by about $4 million, and to reduce the hours police spend on emergency call-outs.
