Friday May 29, 1998

The Budget: what others are saying

Labor has not cheered in the Budget, teachers are chalking it up as an F, real estate floggers reckon it's a good deal, and the UTLC says we're being held to ransom.

Calling it a high tax, low job Budget which hits families, Opposition Leader Mike Rann said job prospects for the State were so depressing that the Government had abandoned the use of Australian Bureau of Statistics in order to come up with its one per cent growth estimate.

"This Budget has a jobs ‘black hole', Mr Rann said. "After announcing extra traineeships two days ago, the Premier's Budget says he will cut another 550 public sector jobs, with 100 teachers going."

The United Trades and Labor Council also attacked the jobs outlook. "With such poor predicted jobs growth we need a more interventionist role for Government in expanding, not cutting, the public sector, and concentrating on industry development," UTLC assistant secretary Michelle Hogan said.

The Real Estate Institute of South Australia welcomed the Budget as a responsible document and said it was essential the debt reduction strategy was implemented as quickly as possible.

"South Australia must become internationally competitive or we will continue to struggle to grow our economy," REISA president Trevor Dunsford said, tenses mixed.

The Convention and Tourism Authority, which benefits form the decision to commit $55 million to upgrade the Adelaide convention centre, was delighted.

"It means we can continue to increase our share of this high yield sector of the tourism market," the ACTA's executive director, Belinda Dewhirst, said.

The Farmers Federation took the debt reduction line, saying we should now sell ETSA. Federation president Wayne Cornish said, "We seem to be on track to improving our credit rating and this must be viewed as an important step forward."

The nurses union was less pleased.

ANF secretary Gail Gago said the Budget failed to address the funding crisis which it said had overtaken SA hospitals and health services.

"South Australia's health needs continue to be caught up in the feuding between Federal and State Governments over funding," she said.

"It is time that both Governments put the public first, sit down, and sort out the mess."

The Australian Democrats supported the Government's decision to raise taxes and charges, but said it was difficult to know whether the increases would be fairly spread across the community.

Democrat leader Mike Elliott called for an assurance that people on low fixed incomes (which, incidentally, describes most wage-earners in South Australia) would not be unfairly burdened.
