Wednesday May 27, 1998

Jobs: the Government has another go
by Hendrik Gout

The State Government last night announced a $100 million strategy which it says will create a thousand new jobs a year for the next two years.

Premier John Olsen, delivering the package in a speech at Parliament House last night, immodestly described it as "the most significant employment package in this State's history".

The package continues a scheme to train people in their late teens and early 20's to give them a better chance at a job - and make a pool of skilled, young South Australians available to business.

This scheme was to have run out of funds in two months.

More will be spent to fund an extra 1,500 trainees in small business .

At least eight new Community at Work projects will start by the year 2001.

And annually, for the next three years, 200 university graduates will be hired in the public service.

"In looking for solutions within our policy parameters we have taken advice from a broad cross-section of the community," Mr Olsen said.

The package was developed with the support of an Olsen initiative called the Jobs Partnership, which is a relatively formal round table, chaired by Mr Olsen, at which sit people from large and small business, unions, and community groups.

Those involved in the Jobs Partnership, contacted by The Electric Newspaper last night, said they were unaware of the package until it was announced last night. One industry representative who supported the forum said she would have liked to have seen it before its announcement.

The Leader of the Opposition, Mike Rann, issued a media release shortly after the Premier's address, but would not make himself available for interview.

Mr Rann's statement said he hoped that unlike the Brown/Olsen Government's four previous so-called unemployment initiatives, this new package woulld actually deliver real and permanent jobs.

"However, I am concerned that this may be just like all other employment initiatives which to date have done nothing to make real inroads into unemployment in this State," Mr Rann said.

"The commitment to employ 600 graduates in the public sector over the next three years, while welcome, barely makes a dent on the more than 14,000 public servants sacked by the Liberals since they came to office in December 1993."

Mr Olsen, who has previously said SA was experiencing a 'jobless recovery', acknowledged the Government had to act.

"Unemployment remains a blight on this community," Mr Olsen said.

"To say otherwise is to ignore the facts.

"And what is fact is that most people want to work.

"It has always been the Government's strong view that those who want to work deserve the opportunity... without having to uproot their lives and families to move interstate.

"It has therefore been an enormous disappointment to us that unemployment levels in this State remain unacceptably high despite promising economic indicators. That they do so only makes us all the more determined to correct the problem," Mr Olsen told the House.

Some Labor MP's last night were saying in the corridors that it was old news, recycled. Others said it was going in the right direction, and one seasoned hand said, "If it makes the jobs, it's worth the money."
