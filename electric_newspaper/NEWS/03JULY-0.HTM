Friday July 3, 1998

Dead but not buried
by Hendrik Gout

SA deputy Premier Graham Ingerson may to be forced to resign as chaos within the Liberal Government worsens. Government strategists were last night openly wondering if Mr Ingerson could be found an honourable way to leave. Their answer was no.

Mr Ingerson is also unlikely to fall on his own sword. The deputy Premier does not even like a shaving nick.

Mr Ingerson is facing the first privileges committee in State Parliament's century and a half-long history. The committee is investigating whether Mr Ingerson misled the House when he told an estimates committee that he had not made a particular phone call about the appointment of a chief executive officer to a horseracing authority.

A statutory declaration signed by a prominent Liberal and given to the State Opposition contradicts the Minister's claim.

On Wednesday, Opposition Leader Mike Rann asked the Speaker of the House, John Oswald, to rule whether a prima facie case existed that Mr Ingerson had misled Parliament.

Mr Oswald had little alternative.

Mr Ingerson's fate now rests with an independent and two Liberals on that five-member privileges committee. The Liberals are Graham Gunn, a former Speaker himself, and Mark Brindall, a junior Minister whose own career may depend on his conduct as chairman of the committee.

The other members of the committee are two Labor appointees, frontbenchers Kevin Foley and Pat Conlon, and the independent conservative Rory McEwen.

A senior Minister, who spoke to The Electric Newspaper on the condition that he was not named, said it would be in the Liberal's interests for Mr Ingerson to leave quietly, irrespective of the outcome of the committee. He said there were a growing number of Liberals who thought the same way.

Mr Ingerson has been in luke-warm water several times as Minister. The temperature is rising, and the water is getting tinged with yellow. These are troubles of Mr Ingerson's own making.

But the issue is far wider than the fate of one lacklustre MP in a safe Liberal seat. It goes to the heart of John Olsen's Premiership, and to the spleen and kidney as well.

It was Mr Ingerson's defection from the Dean Brown camp which delivered Mr Olsen the numbers to oust the then Premier and take the leadership in a bloody coup two years ago. The Minister said the fall-out from Mr Ingerson's blundering could sweep away the Premier himself.

Already, four Ministers are positioning themselves to be available to take over the deputy Premiership. The most likely candidate is former Health Minister and present Government Enterprises Minister Michael Armitage.

It is still possible that the Labor Party may have played its cards a fraction early. One Labor figure known for his generally astute analysis said last night that it may have been in the Opposition's long-term interests to have had Mr Ingerson hanging in the breeze for as long as possible.

The Opposition is concerned that Mr Olsen's increasing erratic behaviour (see separate story) will be steadied by a better deputy, therefore increasing support for the unpopular Government.

The privileges committee has the same powers to call witnesses as a royal commission. Certain to be called is the man who was sacked after - though not necessarily because of - Mr Ingerson's alleged intervention, the former chief executive officer of the SA Thoroughbred Racing Authority, Merv Hill, and the man who blew the whistle on Mr Ingerson, authority chairman Rob Hodge.

Mr Hodge's statutory declaration, which dumped Mr Ingerson into the mire, shows the depth of Liberal in-fighting. Mr Hodge was once a member of the Liberal Party's federal executive, and a vice-president of the State branch of the Party. He is generally seen as a conservative, deeply loyal to Liberal principles, and very worried about the Government's perceived direction.

That direction came under further scrutiny yesterday (see separate story) with the release a non-scientific study from one of Mr Olsen's economic advisers, but the Premier's main worry now is now his own survival rather than that of his deputy.

The Electric Newspaper understands from Liberal sources that if Mr Armitage does a reasonable job as deputy, the Party could turn to him to revitalise the Government's flagging stocks, rather than return to Dean Brown as Premier. Some ALP figures believe Mr Armitage will be a much tougher opponent than either Mr Olsen or Mr Brown, but the outcome of the privileges committee is not yet certain. Graham Ingerson may yet live on to bungle another day.
