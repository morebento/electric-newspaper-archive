Wednesday May 6, 1998

Ingerson's folly: he's coming back
by Hendrik Gout

South Australian deputy Premier is due to return to Adelaide this weekend. It should be quite a homecoming party.

Mr Ingerson is on a 12-day 'investment mission' to Europe. His wife, also flying, wining, and dining at taxpayer expense, is possibly vital to the success of his 'mission'. She wouldn't have gone otherwise.

Yesterday, Mr Ingerson issued two media releases. One concerned the SA engineering company Brister & Co. Apparently this company will make stainless steel tanks using a process owned by an English-based engineering group, Galglass. Not one person here or in London could entertain the thought for a fleeting second that the deal would have stalled without the deputy Premier flying to Europe.

The second media release claimed attendance figures at the Morphettville Racecourse had gone by 50 per cent.

Morphettville Racecourse is in suburban Adelaide. This is more than a trot away from London, where Mr Ingerson broke this news.

The State Opposition has this week, and last week, called on Mr Ingerson to return home immediately. This is not a good idea. He should stay longer.

Mr Ingerson's performance is so dismal that a bitter public fight has already broken out within the Liberal Party over his replacement. Mr Ingerson supposedly represents the electors in the safe Liberal Seat of Bragg. If Mr Ingerson is pushed under a double-decker London bus, there will be no shortage of Liberals keen to replace him in Bragg.

Yesterday, Opposition Leader Mike Rann released almost a dozen pages of documentation which appear to show that the Government knew about the supposed risks of not selling the State's electricity generating and distribution network a very long time before the last election.

According to both the Premier, John Olsen, and Mr Ingerson, the first they knew about the full extent of this exposure was in December last year - after the election.

Mr Rann said a report on those risks had gone to Cabinet a full year before the Government claims to have made the surprise discovery which forced it to break its election policy.

Mr Rann called for that report, and a separate analysis by investment house Schroders, to be publicly released.

The Government said 'no'.

"Evidence before the Economic and Finance Committee from the Auditor-General reveals that detail on these risks was contained in the Stage 3 report from the Separation Steering Committee," Mr Rann said.

"That Stage 3 report referring to risk went to the Olsen Cabinet in December, 1996.

"The Olsen Government had already decided to privatise ETSA long before the last State election, and he was already moving to do so.

"If selling ETSA is such a good idea for our State, why doesn't he release all this material?" Mr Rann said.

Which is a bit like asking if Santa is such a nice guy, why does he hide for 364 days a year.
