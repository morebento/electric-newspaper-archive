Wednesday July 29, 1998

You are what you bleat

Over 250 people in Adelaide last night showed their concern over something most people haven't even heard about yet - the possible risks of genetically modified food. If what you eat becomes you, then many Australians may soon choose to look at the fine print on the labels.

The Adelaide Forum on Genetically Manipulated Food was told at Burnside last night that whether those labels mention the product contains DNA of other animals or plants depends on the decision of Health Ministers meeting in Sydney over the next two days.

Last night's attendees were warned DNA modified-crops poised a threat to world horticultural biodiversity.

"We live at the crest of a major genetic experiment," said Bill Hankin, Director of Australian Heritage Seeds.

The research scientists are creating what amounts to new organisms which will replicate and cross pollinate with normal plant species, altering them irreversibly, Mr Hankin said.

Mr Hankin told the meeting that just like rabbits and blackberries, just one "significantly adverse species running wild" could have a big impact.

The six multinational corporations, Monsanto, Novartis, DuPont, AgroEvo, Zeneca and Dow - responsible for ploughing resources and money into genetically engineered food - were accused of threatening the genetic blueprints in nature for the sake of profit.

The creation of a "terminator gene" in seeds given to Third World farmers produces higher yields but then self-aborts sometime after the first crop to ensure continual seed sales, he said.

"I think that we really need to ask ourselves why we should be prepared to accept, as a community, the risks of genetic engineering when the benefits do not flow to the community but to the few corporations controlling the technology," said plant geneticist Dr Phil Davies.

Dr Davies urged caution with a technology that is "imprecise and unpredictable" and clearly in it's infancy.

Dr Kate Clinch-Jones agreed that if modern science could explain merely 3% of DNA, then the risks were unknown.

"We do know that the effect of a gene and even the gene itself can be modified by other genes present and also by environmental factors," Dr Clinch-Jones said.

"With genetically engineered food, all sorts of genetic material from bacteria, viruses and non-food plants are being incorporated into the food chain. The risk of these being allergenic is unknown and the foods will not be labelled unless our Health Ministers make a decision on this point so that you will not known that you're eating something that's been genetically engineered and you won't know what its been engineered from," Dr Clinch-Jones warned.

Genetically modified foods that we may be eating include all processed foods using imported soybeans or soybean derivatives such as lecithin, and are present in a huge range of foods including baby foods, bread, breakfast cereals, soup mixtures, ice cream, pasta, biscuits, and chocolate.

"We would like to see full disclosure labelling of all foods, not just genetically engineered foods, just as a matter of principle, and a moratorium on the introduction of genetically engineered foods because they haven't been proved to be safe," said a Natural Law Party spokesperson from New Zealand, Guy Hatchard.

Bob Phelps, Director of the Australian Gene Ethics Network, said that genetically engineered food are part of the industrialisation of food supplies, labelling it an 'industry' rather than a science.

"If gene-tech foods come into the food supply and we say that they should not come into the food supply, then we must be empowered to look out for own interests and our own safety and decide how we want to spent our food dollars on the basis of the best knowledge available," Mr Phelps said.

Although the conveners of the meeting - The Gene Ethics network and the Natural Law Party - said they had formally contacted many organisations to represent the industry viewpoint and none accepted, one wildcard did appear.

He was the Director of the Co-operative Research Centre of Weed Management Systems, Associate Professor Rick Roush, who disagreed.

"I don't think there's any health risks with Bt cotton or Roundup Ready Soybeans... and I've studied them at length," Prof Roush said.

He said labelling of foods may inhibit growers to adopt the technology, and labelling risked public misunderstanding of the subtle differences between minor and major alterations to food.

"The public has a right of choice," organiser Vladimir Lorenzon replied.
