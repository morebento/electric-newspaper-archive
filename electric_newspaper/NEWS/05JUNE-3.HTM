Friday June 5, 1998

Olsen's nose grows longer
by Hendrik Gout

Yesterday, the Opposition released documents which conclusively prove that Premier John Olsen was up to his transistors in ETSA privatisation as long as two and a half years ago.

This was embarrassing for the Premier, whose office had already been spent into a spin by the Opposition's release of a document, dated February 16, from ETSA's managing director.

It says there was a Cabinet sub-committee which was looking at ETSA privatisation as early as 1996 - contrary to every claim made by the Premier.

The submission was developed, wrote ETSA's managing director in a memo to the ETSA Board Chairman, according to a concept "outlined by Minister Olsen in December (1995) involving outsourcing ETSA Transmission and selling off 50 per cent of the transmission assets as part of the process".

The minute also confirms that the Electricity Sector Reform Unit, the EDA, and ETSA officers were all involved in developing the Minister's concept.

Yet on February 18, when Opposition Leader Mike Rann asked in Parliament whether the Premier, as Minister for Infrastructure, did raise, discuss, or seek support with Ministerial colleagues for a proposal to sell all or part of ETSA, he received the following answer from Mr Olsen: "The simple fact is that the Government did not consider the sale of ETSA or any part of it."

Yesterday, the Opposition attempted to establish a Parliamentary privileges committee, claiming Mr Olsen had misled the House. If such a finding was made, Mr Olsen would have had to resign as Premier. Such an outcome was averted when the Speaker, John Oswald, found the Premier had no case to answer.

"I am constrained by the quotations in Hansard attributed to the Premier and delivered to me by the Leader of the Opposition. There is nothing in these quotations which conclude the notion that the Premier deliberately misled the House as required to set up a privileges committee," Mr Olsen ruled last night.

The Opposition Leader, Mike Rann, rose on a point of order when the Speaker handed down his ruling.

"If this were the House of Representatives he would be gone," Mr Rann said.

The Premier argued that he didn't bring the proposal to the Cabinet sub-committee. It was brought forward by a public servant. It was, according to the ETSA memo, "favourably received".

"Naturally the matter is confidential at this point," the memo continues.

On 26 February this year, Mr Rann asked the Premier whether as Minister for Infrastructure in the Brown Government, Mr Olsen had raised, discussed, or sought support with Ministerial colleagues for a proposal to sell all of part of ETSA.

Mr Olsen replied, "The simple fact is that the Government did not consider the sale of ETSA or any part of it."

In a media conference on February 18, Mr Olsen was asked by Matthew Abraham, a journalist from The Australian, "Can we clear up whether, three years or roughly two years ago, just before becoming Premier, as Infrastructure Minister, you put a proposal on ETSA and Optima."

John Olsen replied, "To a Cabinet sub-committee?"

"To a Cabinet sub-committee," Mr Abraham asked again.

"No," said Mr Olsen, "I did not."

It is not possible, under existing Parliamentary or political precedent, to be forced to resign for misleading the public.
