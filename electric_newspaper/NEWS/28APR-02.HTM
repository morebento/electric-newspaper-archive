Tuesday April 28, 1998

No need to Bragg about Ingerson
by Hendrik Gout

While South Australia's Deputy Premier, Graham Ingerson, is in France trying to drum up overseas students for SA universities, the Liberal Party is trying to drum up a candidate to take over Mr Ingerson's seat of Bragg.

"Mr Ingerson's performance is not on the nose," a senior Liberal acknowledged yesterday. "But we have to keep in mind that no-one is around forever, and we wouldn't like him to fall under the proverbial bus and not have thought about a successor (in the seat)."

There is now a growing number of Liberals who would not cry into their Kirin if the bus came along sooner rather than later. Mr Ingerson's performance has swung between dreadful and merely mediocre since before the election.

Improbable rumours circulated in some Liberal circles yesterday that the Premier's former chief of staff, the ex-Victorian Liberal MP Craig Bildstien, would be nominated for the seat, leading one Bragg electoral member to suggest this would be like replacing Mr Ingerson with Mr Ingerson.

"We could certainly do without that sort of controversy," she said.

State Treasurer Rob Lucas has ruled himself out of moving from the Legislative Council to the House of Assembly, although senior Federal Liberals are known to view the Treasurer as a possible future deputy and possible Premier.

But other Liberals contacted yesterday and this morning suggested the Premier, Mr Olsen, would be more comfortable without a high-flier and possible challenger as Deputy.

Meanwhile, Parliament's Economic and Finance Committee has sent the bloodhounds out to try to locate Mr Ingerson's former chief of staff, Jane Cooper.

Ms Cooper was to testify tomorrow in relation to the Deputy Premier's claimed lack of knowledge about a $96 million write-down in the value of the State's Electricity Trust.

She has not been formally contacted, apparently because she has gone missing.

In a separate development, Democrat MLC Sandra Kanck has announced she will hold talks today with the Australian Competition and Consumer Commission's Alan Asher about the ETSA sale.

The Democrats have not yet decided whether or not to support legislation allowing the sale of ETSA. Instead, Ms Kanck has been holding a series of meetings as part of her investigations.

Later this week, Ms Kanck will gather evidence from trade unions and from John Spoehr and John Quiggan, of Adelaide University's Department of Social Enquiry.
