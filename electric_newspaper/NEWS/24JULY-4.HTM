Friday July 24, 1998

Kistler Chairman, Robert Wang: "It's flat" he said.

A sodding affair in the desert
by Hendrik Gout in Woomera

Take 116 people from two continents, fly them to a cold desert in South Australia's far north, and scrape some soil - and you've got the potential for quite a little ceremony.

The Chinese American and the Aboriginal Australian shake hands across the cultures.

A sodding ceremony in the middle of somewhere (from left, John Olsen, Federal industry Minister John Moore, Kistler Chairman Robert Wang, and Defence Minister Ian McLachlan.

The stake which marks the site of the rocket hanger.

John Olsen claims South Australians will design satellites.

The food was adequate.

Yesterday, the Kistler Aerospace corporation did just that.

From Canberra came two Federal Ministers, Defence Minister Ian McLachlan and Industry Minister John Moore, with media accompaniment. From Sydney came more reporters, including a television team from Business Sunday, and industry observers. From Melbourne came another handful. And from Adelaide came the Premier and Opposition Leader, and still more media.

And from the United States came, among others, the chairman of Kistler Aerospace, Robert Wang.

"This is a very special day," said Mr Wang, before reaching for a chrome-plated shovel to mark the beginning of the project.

Kistler proposed to build a US$ 45 million launch facility at the disused Woomera rocket range, where it hopes to send commercial communications satellites into low earth orbit using a two-stage, re-useable rocket system.

The system is still being tested. Kistler has also signed a similar launch facility agreement in Nevada, but the company has not been given US regulatory clearance to perform those tests at its home ground. US authorities will assess the Woomera tests before allowing the company to launch in Nevada.

Mr Wang was adamant that Kistler would not desert Australia even if it got US approval to use the US site.

"It doesn't make sense to invest $45 million here and then not use it. My shareholders would never allow it."

Mr Wang said time would prove Kistler was committed to the Woomera site. It plans to conduct its first test early next year.

Yesterday's ground-breaking ceremony showed how much faith the company, and Federal and State Governments have in the project. Kistler believes that its re-useable K-1 rocket will provide a cheaper method of launching satellites, and that the market for satellite launchers is virtually unlimited. It says almost 2,000 communication satellite launches are planned or proposed over the next 10 years.

The company has assembled an experienced and well-respected team to develop its K-1, and has contracted companies such as Northrop Grumman and Lockheed Martin to develop various parts of its rockets and landing systems.

The company's stated commitment to SA received something of a setback when Premier John Olsen boasted the State would, as a consequence, develop expertise in satellite design - something which one company representative said was not impossible but "highly speculative".

Exaggerated claims have long bedevilled Australia's entry into the aerospace industry. Mr McLachlan and Mr Moore were at pains yesterday to make more realistic - although still optimistic - assessments of the benefits of the Kistler space program.

Mr Wang said he was supremely confident that the K-1 would be successful, and that Woomera would see as many as 200 satellites launched a year. He said industry demand and growth were such that there was ample reason to develop two launch sites, one in South Australia and another at Nevada - and perhaps more in the future.

The spaceport is due for completion late this year.
