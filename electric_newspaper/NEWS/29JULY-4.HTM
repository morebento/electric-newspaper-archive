Wednesday July 29, 1998

Locals oppose Hills development
by Krista Eleftheriou

People living in the Adelaide foothills have entered the second round in their fight to stop a huge motel, restaurant, and winery complex being built on the sensitive Hills Face.

More than 300 people met in the nearby suburb Netherby last night, calling on the State Government to keep its election promise to protect the Hills Face Zone, and therefore block the development by prominent Adelaide winemaker Andrew Garrett.

Mr Garrett is seeking advice from State Planning Minister Diana Laidlaw on the likelihood of receiving major development status for the planned winery, vineyard and restaurant, thus allowing him to circumvent local protection provisions.

Development of a vineyard on his Brown Hill property was refused by the Mitcham Council in 1996, because it did not comply with the Hills Face legislation which has been in place for more than 30 years.

Mr Garrett told The Electric Newspaper last month the vineyard would be visible from the city and suburbs.

Environment defendants’ office lawyer Mark Pannel, who wrote a thesis on ‘fast tracking the major development act’, opposed the development.

"If you want to fast track a development you either change the decision-maker or change the rules - in SA we change both," he said.

Mr Pannel said if the project received major development status, all legal rights of the residents would be abandoned.

He said residents would have no legal right to appeal the decision even if there was a serious aspect overlooked by the major development council.

Mr Pannel said other States had a list of developments that are considered major, but in SA the guidelines were ambiguous.

"In South Australia it is entirely up to the Minister’s opinion," he said.

Foothills action group chairman and local Anglican Reverend Andrew Cheesman said the decision would set a precedent.

He said it could spark a flood of developers buying up the cheap land - cheap because of the Hills Face Zone restrictions - and subsequently developing the hills area.

Hills Face Zone network chairman Bill Mallyon said Adelaide was recognised internationally as a beautiful city.

"Most people on the Adelaide plains realise what an asset they have when they look up at the hills," Mr Mallyon said.
