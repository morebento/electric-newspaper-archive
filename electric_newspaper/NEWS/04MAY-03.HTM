Monday May 4, 1998

Biggest May Day crowd 'in years'

Australia's declining level of trade unionism was about to turn around, according to organisers of Adelaide's May Day parade on Saturday. They said the sacking of Patricks' wharfies had galvanised unionists around the country.

In Brisbane, Sydney, Melbourne, and in regional cities around the country, crowds were larger than they have been since the early 1980's.

In South Australia about 3,000 marched from Victoria Square in Adelaide City to Rymill Park, carrying banners about past and present struggles for improved working conditions.

Social issues, including child care, cuts to State and Commonwealth public services and privatisation were all targeted.

There were young and old men and women, children, students, and babies in prams, as the march stretched four city blocks.

"The actions of the Federal Government in its attack on waterside workers have put a shiver of fear up all Australian unionists," said the United Trades and Labor Council secretary Chris White.

Mr White called Australian Prime Minister John Howard 'Hitler-like', and said the Government was dividing the Australian people in a cynical and wrongful attempt to reduce wages and conditions nationally.

The stars of this year's May Day parade were definitely the members of the Maritime Union. "MUA - here to stay" came the chant, to regular applause.

But while the number of marchers exceeded organisers' expectations, the crowd watching the parade were more bemused than outwardly supportive.

City shoppers' reactions ranged from mild annoyance about the streets being closed to traffic for 30 minutes, to complete ignorance about what the parade was about.

"May Day?" said one teenager. "Isn't that about a maypole and ribbons?"

'No," said his companion. "It's about the eight-hour day."

"March with us!" exclaimed a woman with a placard about the privatisation of the Commonwealth Employment Service. "It's your future." The teenagers shook their heads, uncomprehending.

But at Rymill Park, the faithful were in no doubt about why they'd come.

"There's always a struggle between labour and capital," said a now-retired member of the Nurses' Federation. "Unless we keep fighting, we'll be swept under."

"This is about human dignity, that's all," said a Commonwealth public servant. ":It's got to the stage where some people are afraid to speak out about the way employers think they can treat their staff." She declined to give her name.

"When you see a crowd this big, it means the bosses aren't winning," an Adelaide University student smiled. "This is a celebration. It's proof that people can stick together, that we do care about what happens to everyone around us, about uni cuts, and things we take for granted. Because unless we demonstrate and show the world that we won't be treated like sheep, we'll just get pushed around again and again."

There were zealots in the parade, of course, and the same slogans which are perennial favourites. But as the speeches eventually wound to a close and people started to take part in the post-parade 'family picnic', it was clear that May Day is also about reassurance and catching up with colleagues and friends.

"It's great, this," said a metalworker, muching a sausage in a roll with his mates. "When you see these sorts of numbers, and get together like this, you know there's ordinary people still have a chance."
