Thursday June 4, 1998

Free education: go straight to court
by Hendrik Gout

A snafu over school fees has revealed a shortfall of up to several million dollars in the schools budget - and a possible legal challenge.

Some years ago the then Labor Government allowed school councils to charge a fee for free education. There was general compliance until the fees reached several hundreds of dollars per child and more than $1,000 dollars a family, by which time there was widespread resentment.

Parents of children who didn't pay the new school fees on principle generally got away with it, until the present Government passed a regulation which allowed school councils to sue parents for the monies.

Some parents challenged this in court, and won. The Education Minister, Malcolm Buckby, closed this technical loophole last month.

The Government now has a newly discovered worry. Regulations don't have to be passed by Parliament, but they can be disallowed by Parliament if it acts quickly.

This is what happened to the regulation allowing school councils to sue parents for fees.

The Government is now relying on untested Crown law advice which goes as follows: the regulations were in effect for a few weeks between their proclamation and their disallowance. This means they were legal for a few weeks in that year. The imposition of yearly school fees was therefore legal for some period during that year - so school councils can sue for the yearly fee.

Based on this very tenuous legal advice, the Government is now planning to re-regulate this year to allow school councils to sue. Parliament will once again disallow the regulation, but because the regulations apply for some time during 1998, the councils will be able to sue again this year.

The architect of this cunning scheme remains anonymous.

But if anyone tests this rationale in court, he'll probably be in the public gallery. It will be an interesting case to watch.
