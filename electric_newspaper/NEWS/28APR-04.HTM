Tuesday April 28, 1998

The case of the baffling barnacle

It's an evolutionary puzzle with lurid tabloid overtones.

Flinders University marine biologist Dr Jon Havenhand and his colleague Associate Professor Ib Svane of Sweden's Kristineberg Marine Research Centre have been looking into the remarkable and complicated sexual tendencies of the barnacle.

Their research is focusing on Ibla barnacles which habitually lives among colonies of Galeolaria tubeworms. Galeolaria are the creators of the calciferous lumps we see at the high tide marks on jetties or on the bottom of ships.

Like many marine organisms, the Ibla barnacle is a hermaphrodite; it is both male and female at the same time.

In their adult form, barnacles do not move - they remain fixed to the surface on which they settle.

This lack of mobility goes some way to explaining the barnacle's improbable male sexual organ.

"Body-size specific, they have the longest penis of any organism on the planet," Dr Havenhand said.

Although this remarkable appendage makes ordinary fertilisation possible, the barnacle is not content with one mode of sexual reproduction.

"Some of the Ibla larvae - which swim around in the ocean-borne plankton for three or four weeks - will settle down in among the Galeolaria tubeworms and turn into a miniature version of a hermaphrodite," Dr Havenhand said.

These subsequently grow up into a normal adult barnacle, reaching a size of about three centimetres long. But other larvae behave in an entirely different fashion.

"Some of the larvae will swim around or crawl around until they find an adult; it seems they then crawl inside the adult, glue themselves down and turn themselves into a miniature male."

The dwarf males then fertilise the eggs of the hermaphrodite adults, which effectively become female.

Dr Havenhand said the co-existence of these two modes of sexual reproduction within the same species is extremely unusual.

"There are quite a lot of species which have so-called obligatory dwarf males, and there are quite a lot of species which are just hermaphrodites," Dr Havenhand said.

"Having both is extremely rare - all the available theories say it shouldn't happen.

"Effectively the barnacle is having a bob each way.

"While a bob each way is a safe option at the race track, in an evolutionary context you'd lose, because there are so many others betting it all on the nose and winning, by reproducing in large numbers."

So, in essence, the Ibla barnacle is breaking evolutionary rules.

"Why this is so, nobody knows," Dr Havenhand said.

Dr Havenhand and Associate Professor Svane have spent two months at the Port Lincoln Marine Science Centre in beginning to unravel the mystery by studying the behaviour of the Ibla larvae in effort to find out if their behaviour is predetermined, or if their sexual fate is a response to their environment.

By injecting individual larvae into thousands of 'untenanted' adult hermaphrodite barnacles, the marine biologists have established that roughly half of the larvae develop into dwarf males.

"So it looks like the larvae have predetermined sex, which is interesting because that then means these larvae have to find an adult. The question then becomes how do they do that?

"In a broad biological sense, it's important to determine whether the cues that cause one of these little larvae to settle after a month at sea are physical or biological."

And finding out what induces them to settle may have future practical applications such as keeping boat keels free of encrustations, Dr Havenhand said.

"Some of the best anti-fouling substances now being developed biologically are products which are known to deter larval settlement," he said.
