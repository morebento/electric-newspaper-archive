Thursday July 16, 1998

Ingerson tries to tough it out
by Hendrik Gout

Embattled SA Minister Graham Ingerson is believed to have decided to face a no-confidence motion in Parliament rather than hand in his resignation to the Premier, Mr Olsen.

And sources close to Mr Olsen indicated yesterday the Premier was unlikely to either demand Mr Ingerson's resignation or to sack his accident-prone former deputy, despite wearing Mr Ingerson around his neck like a dead albatross.

Mr Ingerson holds the key to several important Government decisions. He knows, for example, about when the Government decided to sell the State's power utilities. The Government went into the election claiming it had no plans to sell the SA electricity trust or Optima Energy. Within weeks after being returned by the narrowest margin, Mr Olsen said he had just decided ETSA and Optima had to be sold.

A privileges committee investigating Mr Ingerson's behaviour about another issue - the sacking of a racing official - has already compiled its report. The Electric Newspaper understands that the committee's majority finding is that Mr Ingerson deliberately misled the House.

That report will be presented to Parliament on Tuesday.

Sources close to the Premier said yesterday that the Government was "bracing itself" for a no-confidence motion in Mr Ingerson, which it now expected to lose, but that would not necessarily man Mr Ingerson would automatically step down.

"There's nothing in the rulebook that says he must resign after a no-confidence motion," the source said. Asked whether convention constituted a rule, he replied that there was "a world of difference between the two".

Labor tacticians believe Mr Ingerson will be a soft and easy target if he stays on, harming the Government and damaging it by his presence.

But Liberal powerborkers are dismayed by what they see as the lack of Mr Ingerson's consideration for the long-term interests of the Liberal party, and by Mr Olsen's "cowardice" in refusing to sack his one-time deputy.

There are those in the Party who want Mr Ingerson to leave not just the Ministry, but the Parliament as well.

This would see a by-election in the safe Liberal seat of Bragg, in Adelaide's wealthy eastern suburbs.

Influential Liberals with control of branches in the area would like to see Vicki Chapman, a politically-savvy Liberal who would certainly aspire to the Ministry and is considered by some to have the potential to be a future Leader, gain Liberal endorsement for the seat.

Mr Olsen's faction is resisting the move, believing that Ms Chapman is unlikely to support Mr Olsen in any leadership struggle. The Premier's supporters want to install someone like Treasurer Rob Lucas in Bragg.

Mr Lucas is in the Legislative Council, and has said publicly he does not want to move to the House of Assembly. Privately, there is considerable opposition within the Party to such a change.

Nevertheless, all these machinations may be academic. Mr Ingerson has dug himself a deep hole, but his head's still sticking out of the ground. Faced with the possible consequences of having an angry and possibly vengeful ex-Minister on his backbench, John Olsen is not inclined to fill in the grave.
