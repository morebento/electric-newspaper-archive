Tuesday July 21, 1998

Buyers shell out for SA oysters
by Krista Eleftheriou

South Australia's oyster industry can not keep up with demand for its quality produce, according to OYSA manager Bob Simmons.

The State's oyster farmers generated more than $6 million last financial year, most of which flowed into regional SA.

The industry has been growing at more than 20 per cent since OYSA, the marketing branch of the State's oyster industry, was established in 1994.

OYSA is responsible for marketing and selling South Australian oysters to the domestic, national and international market.

It represents more than 80 per cent of the State's oyster farmers.

"The past three months produced record sales, during what is traditionally the slowest time of year," Mr Simmons said.

Ceduna oyster farmer and chairman of directors of the OYSA Board Leon Stott said declining regional populations and high unemployment has been turned around in some rural centres with the development of the oyster industry.

"Employment opportunities have been enormous. Between 40 to 50 per cent of Smoky Bay residence are directly involved in the oyster industry. Prior to that there was massive unemployment in the town," Mr Stott said.

Mr Stott estimates more than $2 million was spent maintaining the local oyster farms in the west coast regional centre of Ceduna during last year alone.

Bob Simmons said oyster farmers had to overcome a number of external problems during recent years to maintain their industry.

"The NSW's Wallis Lakes scare resulted in slower growth in 1996, because people did not differentiate between NSW's Sydney Rock Oyster and South Australia's Pacific," Mr Simmons said.

After 12 months of negotiations by OYSA, Mr Stott flew to Japan with the Premier last month to sign the an export contract.

"The Japanese wanted huge numbers of oysters, but we could only supply 1000 dozen a week - a fraction of what they asked for," Mr Stott said.

Mr Stott said OYSA did not want to over-commit for fear of neglecting the domestic market.

"The Japanese agreement is very important to the industry. If we have another Wallis Lakes our domestic market will plummet, but we can increase our supply to Japan to maintain the industry," he said

OYSA also exports to Europe and the United States.

While the State's oyster industry has been exceeding all budget predictions, NSW's industry has been steadily declining during the past 20 years.

The Sydney Rock Oyster is farmed in rivers and estuaries. As a result urbanisation, sewage and stormwater run off has been identified by the NSW's oyster farmers as the key factors destroying their industry.

Mr Simmons said OYSA had introduced a quality assurance program into SA to ensure only the best oysters are commercially sold.

He said Tasmania and NSW were now following SA's lead to provide a benchmark for quality in the industry and to prevent the health scares experienced in NSW.

South Australian oysters are grown in the open sea, in seven isolated regions of State, unaffected by pollutants.

Mr Simmons said this 'clean, green' image of SA oysters provide endless market opportunities.

"The only thing limiting the growth of the oyster industry is a lack of oysters. Customers are calling up wanting 1000 dozen oysters and we can only supply 500," Mr Simmons said.
