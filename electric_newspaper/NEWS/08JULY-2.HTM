Wednesday July 8, 1998

Cocked Gunn misfires

There were strange and strained words yesterday as the historic committee investigating claims against ex-deputy Graham Ingerson met for the its first day of hearings at Parliament House.

The committee had been due to meet at 1 o'clock in a first floor committee room. The Opposition wants the hearings open to the public, the Liberals and Independent member want it held in secrecy.

As the committee began, Labor frontbencher Kevin Foley asked chairman Mark Brindall if it would at least be possible for the media to take some film and photos of the committee, a request to which Mr Brindal good-naturedly acceded.

Television cameras began rolling and still photographers began snapping, when ten minutes late in walked Graham Gunn.

He took one look at the media and ordered them out. Mr Brindall told him they were taking a few photos, Mr Gunn said nobody had asked him, Mr Foley said Mr Gunn hadn't been there because of his lateness, and Mr Gunn took his seat, furious and berating the chairman and the Opposition.

At the back of the room, journalists were giggling at Mr Gunn's fit of pique, and as Mr Gunn's attack on Mr Foley intensified the astonished media were left wondering how the fate of Graham Ingerson could rest, at least in part, in the hands of a man who could barely contain his fury over a completely trivial incident.

The committee could call at least three witnesses: Graham Ingerson himself, former Thoroughbred Racing Authority chairman Rob Hodge, and the man relieved of his position as the authority's chief executive officer, Merv Hill.

But the committee may not hear from any witnesses if Liberal strategists decide Mr Ingerson's case is so weak that it is indefensible.

Such a decision would mean that the Liberals would hang Mr Ingerson out to dry; they would concede his misleading of Parliament, and Mr Ingerson would be forced to resign as Minister as well as already having resigned as deputy Premier.

Such a move would at least avoid the embarrassment of having allegations against Mr Ingerson formalised at the committee.

But other Liberals believe Mr Ingerson has a chance, although a small one, of toughing it out through the committee hearings.

The committee meets again this morning, once again in camera.

It is entirely possible that the committee will not need its scheduled three weeks to bring back a report.

While Mr Olsen's tactic of forcing Mr Ingerson's resignation as deputy saved the Premier from a spill of all leadership positions yesterday, Mr Ingerson's departure from the Minister can also be cleanly dealt with. Mr Olsen can simply appoint another Minister to replace Mr Ingerson without needing a partyroom vote.
