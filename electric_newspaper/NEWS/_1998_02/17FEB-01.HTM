Tuesday February 17, 1998

Australian troops leave for Gulf today

Australian troops will board their specially-prepared Hercules aircraft bound for the Persian Gulf today, following Cabinet's approval to deploy a Special Air Services regiment in the looming American-led war against Iraq.

Diplomatic efforts have so far failed to satisify United Nations inspectors, who have been refused access to certain Iranian sites where 'weapons of mass destruction' are suspected of being stored.

Unlike the situation in the 1991 Gulf War, when most Arab States backed the U.N. effort to oust Iraq from Kuwait, most countries in the Middle East are not joining the United States its military solution to the impasse.

Australian Democrats Leader Senator Meg Lees said today she deeply regretted the Prime Minister's decision to send the troops.

"It is a great pity that the only discussion about the possible impact our action will have on the authority of the U.N. or on Australia's interests occured behind closed doors with caucus at Thredbo rather than in the national Parliament," Senator Lees said.

"Australia's international standing, particularly in the politically and economically important Gulf States opposed to intervention in Iraq, is likely to decline rather than be enhanced as the result of our blindly following the U.S. into this conflict."

The Prime Minister, Mr Howard, says he believes "most Australians support the use of force".

The United Nations has not authorised military action to enforce sanctions against Iraq, and does not believe force should yet be used.

Meanwhile, Britain's Channel Four television news has reported that the United States helped Iraq develop its chemical and biological weapons programs in the 1980's, while Britain sold Baghdad the antidote to nerve gas as late as March 1992.

Channel Four said it had found U.S. intelligence documents which showed 14 consignments of biological materials were exported from the U.S. to Iraq between 1985 and 1989.

These included 19 batches of anthrax bacteria and 15 batches of botulinum, the organism that causes botulism.

The exports, backed by the State Department, were licensed by the Department of Commerce.

No less than 29 batches of material were sent after Iraq had used gas in an attack on the Kurdish town of Halabja in 1988, killing 5,000 people, it said.
