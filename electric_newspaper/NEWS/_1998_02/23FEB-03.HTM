Monday February 23, 1998

Nuclear industry radiates in SA
By Chris Warren

South Australia is rushing headlong into the nuclear industry with barely a whimper of protest.

Two new uranium mines, a tripling of yellowcake production at Roxby Downs, and an outback dump for radioactive waste have all been announced in the past few weeks.

To top it all off, the owner of the Honeymoon uranium mine, Martin Ackland of Southern Cross resources, is predicting Australia will switch from coal-fired to nuclear power stations within the next 20 years.

So what's the rush?

Australian Democrat Sandra Kanck believes the uranium industry is in overdrive to open up as many mines as possible before the advent of the next Federal Labor Government.

After ditching its nonsensical 'three mines' policy of the 1980's, the ALP says there will be no new uranium mines approved after it wins office in Canberra.

State Labor MPs are conspicuously quiet about the two new mines at Beverley and Honeymoon.

Both will trial the in-situ leaching process which involves pumping a weak acid solution deep into the ground, where it dissolves traces of uranium ore.

Yellowcake is recovered from the solution, and will be stored in drums until the mines are goiven approval for commercial production.

The liquid waste goes back into the ground, and that's why conservationists are worried about the contamination of the aquifers.

David Noonan, from the Australian Conservation Foundation, says other heavy metals will find their way into the waste solution, and he claims nobody knows where it will end up once it seeps outside the perimiters of the mining leases.

"Acid solution has verer been used outside Eastern Europe, and there's a risk of it finding its way into the Great Artesian Basin," Mr Noonan said.

"In-situ leaching is the safest form of mining," according to Charles Foldenaeur, of Heathgate Resources, which has just started recovering yellowcake from Beverley.

"Ninety per cent of uranium is extracted by this process in the United States. It's technically sound, and there are no problems at all with the process."

The dismantling of nuclear warheads is currently providing fuel for the nuclear power industry, but once that source has been exhausted, uranium producers are confident the demand for yellowcake will improve, and they say Australia is in the box seat with almost 30 per cent of the world's uranium reserves.

Pushing the case for nuclear power stations in this country, Honeymoon's Martin Ackland says the output of mhis mine alone equals eight million tonnes of coal, which converted to electricity would pump 19 million tonnes of carbon dioxide into the atmosphere.

But the safe disposal of nuclear waste is still unsolved.

Low level radioactive waste will be trucked to and stored in a 'near surface repository' on a two kilometre-square site at Billa Kilina, an outback wasteland which includes the towns of Woomera, Rocky Downs, and Andamooka.

So far the Federal Government won't say to the spent nuclear fuel rods from the Lucas Heights reactor, which are being re-processed in Scotland and which will be returned to Australia for permanent disposal within 20 years.
