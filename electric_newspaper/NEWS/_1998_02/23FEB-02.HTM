Monday February 23, 1998

Joy for John: Olsen wins important ally in ETSA sale
By Simon Royal

Joy and John are not terms which have been used much in the same paragraph, let alone the same sentence, in recent days.

Yet joy and glee, as well as a fair degree of relief, is what the Premier must be feeling after a two-day visit to Adelaide by Port Augusta mayor, Joy Baluch.

The State Government has won an important, if unexpected, ally for its proposed sale of ETSA and Optima in Ms Baluch. True to her usual feisty form, the mayor's initial reaction to the Government's policy backflip was one of outright hostility.

"I felt shock and I felt horror and I felt they were a pack of bloody liars." That was on Tuesday. Today, the mayor says she is feeling better.

"Having calmed down, what alternative have we got? I mean, I have to listen to rational discussion," she said.

It is worth remembering that Joy Baluch has only just finished nursing her city through the protracted and painful business of the Australian National sale. While the subject of memory is at hand, no-one in Port Augusta has forgotten last December's announcement by Optima of the loss of 20 jobs at the power station.

Who, facing such odds, helped change the mayor's mind? The credit belongs to Optima executives, the Minister for Human Resources, Dean Brown, and the local Member for Stuart, Graham Gunn.

There is a lot at stake for Port Augusta in any sale or lease of ETSA and Optima.

The northern power station provides about 180 jobs.

As in most regional cities and towns, there is not exactly a surfeit of jobs in Port Augusta.

Some people are arguing that the northern power station is the most vulnerable of any of the State's generating facilities to a 'run-down' scenario.

In other words, a private investor finding Port Augusta old, uncompetitive, and too expensive to bring up to standard, simply allows it to run down.

For the sake of argument, that investor may choose to import power from one of its interstate facilities, buy power from another private power player, or put the money into upgrading other South Australian stations.

Mayor Baluch claims on figures shown to her by Optima, Torrens Island is the facility most vulnerable to vagaries of the national electricity grid.

To an extent, ETSA sources confirm her view, but also say part of Port Augusta is not long for this world.

How can this apparent contradiction hold true?

Compared with Torrens A power station, Port Augusta is a model of modernity - that is, with the exception of the old Playford B station, which despite having been patched up is not expected to soldier on for more than a few years.

The thing to remember is that there are two stations in the northern facility: Augusta and Playford B. Augusta is the efficient station, but even it may have a long-term blot on its horizon. Efficient as it is, Augusta burns second grade fuel.

This brings up the question of Leigh Creek and the coal it supplies to Port Augusta.

Leigh Creek coal is brown coal. It is relatively dirty and relatively energy inefficient. However, it fires the northern power station well enough, because ETSA installed expensive purpose-built German boilers for the task.

But what is a private operator some years down the track, and faced with repairing those hideously expensive German boilers, decides it is not worth the expense? That's not likely to be done at a place using expensive equipment to burn poor fuel.

In fact, apart from distilling the oil from coal, there's not much else one would use Leigh Creek coal for, apart from fuelling a power station. No Port Augusta, no Leigh Creek?

The Government should not yet too happy about Mayor Baluch's conversion on the road to Gepps Cross. Saying, as she does, there is "no alternative but to support the Government" is hardly a ringing endorsement. Her support is conditional.

Joy Baluch also believes Professor Fred Hilmer, author of the National Competition report and spiritual father of the integrated electricity grid, should "line up next to Pontius Pilot".

Number one on the mayor's list of conditions is the retention of jobs at the power station. There is a 'no forced redundancy' clause in ETSA work agreements, but that carries through only to December. Premier Olsen has consistently maintained there will be no forced redundancies through the sale of ETSA and Optima.

The unions remain unconvinced, and as they told ABC television news on Wednesday, unless the redundancy clauses are improved, they will pull the plug on power.

Joy Baluch's other big condition revolves around a proposed magnesium plant, for which Port Augusta is apparently the preferred site. That plant is subject of a feasibility study, expected to finish in March.

Should the plant go ahead it will be a $250 million investment , creating 480 jobs. What effect, if any, the possible sale of the northern power station would have on the viability of a magnesium plant remains unclear. The lack of clarity is Mayor Baluch's worry.

The mayor's reluctant support for selling ETSA and Optima doesn't mean all is well for the Government. There's the voting public of Stuart to be reckoned with. Joy Baluch claims a couple of electricity workers have called her to say, "Whatever you do, we are behind you". These may be the only two votes, in Port Augusta at least, on which sitting Member Graham Gunn can count.

Until recently, Mr Gunn was saying he's fought the good fight for the last time; it was his turn to drift off to the land of directorships. Mr Gunn changed his tune in recent days and is now looking at staying.

Selling ETSA, contrary to Government policy at the last election, will make the next election perhaps the most difficult of Graham Gunn's career.

According to Mayor Baluch, Mr Gunn was going to start the process of his re-election by making sure that everyone in Port Augusta gets a copy of the Premier's speech to Parliament announcing the sale.

"Bugger that," said the mayor, "I told him (Graham Gunn) I just want a pamphlet with dot points."

Labor came close to winning Stuart at the State election last October. Mr Gunn holds the seat by 1.6 per cent and it is now the most marginal country electorate. It can't be too difficult a task for Labor Leader Mike Rann to convince the people of Stuart that selling the northern power station is contrary to their best interests. How much privatisation can one town bare?

In fact, the only person who is not likely to suffer any great pain out of all this is Mr Rann. Consider just a few of the possible scenarios. If the Democrats cave in over a total sale, then that's good for Mr Rann - from Labor's viewpoint, the democrats got far too many primary votes in the last election. If John Olsen fails to get a sale and goes down the path of a lease, a course for which the Government says it doesn't need Parliamentary approval, then that is good for Mr Rann too. Mr Rann can then say that not only has John Olsen broken his promise, but has ignored the express will of Parliament.

Both Mike Rann and Mike Elliott can argue they have a mandate to block the privatisation of ETSA and Optima, having run anti-privatisation/outsourcing lines during the election.

Unfortunately John Olsen can't do the same because(he says) he didn't realise he was going to sell the State's power facilities until after the election. Oopsie, but look on the bright side - perhaps we won't have to listen to the dreaded M word much during this business.

And even if the worst comes to pass and we say Aloha to ETSA, will Labor be very upset? Not according to senior ETSA sources, who say that some ALP MPs are in favour of electricity privatisation.

Whatever the long-term merits or perils of selling our power facilities, the Government seems right about the positive impact of a sale on the State's Budget (in the short to medium term, anyway).

And who will be paying the pain for all this?

John Wayne Olsen, of course.

Mike Rann should remove all the pins from his Ollie voodoo doll immediately.

After all it has been through, and all it's about to do, it deserves a really big kiss.
