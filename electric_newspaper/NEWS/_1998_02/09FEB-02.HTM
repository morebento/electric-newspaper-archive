Tuesday February 10, 1998

Fudging the figures in Waymouth Street

The latest audit figures on the circulation of "The Advertiser" show that it's all in the interpretation.

During the six months to the end of December, the average number of 'Tisers sold Mon-Fri lifted by 3,057 copies a day. Those who hatched the tabloid format had confidently forecast a sales increase of 20,000.

Hardly something to rejoice over.

Yet the Advertiser tried to convince its readers that "Sales Rise with Our New Shape."

Considering that the new shape was introduced for only the last two months of the latest figures, its impact is hard to judge.

But the Advertiser isn't restraining its judgement. "The Advertiser's new shape has proved a big hit with South Australia" shouts an article on Page two of last Friday's edition. This was despite the fact that Mon-Fri sales are up by a measly 0.06%!

And, horror of horrors, sales of the Saturday edition actually fell. If the 'Tiser's logic is to be followed, 1504 people decided to give the thumbs down to the Tabloid. The percentage drop in sales was 0.6%..

Not that you will read anything about that in "The Advertiser." It's happy to compare its Monday to Friday figures with the preceding six months, but when looking at the Saturday figures it conveniently compares them with 12 months ago to give the impression of a continued increase.

What's gone wrong ?

The expected big increase in sales in the northern and southern suburbs hasn't eventuated, although numbers are up in both areas and over the counter sales in these areas have shown a worthwhile increase.

But few inroads have been made into the static (or declining) home delivery sector.

In fact, the Tiser didn't foresee the big drop in sales in the inner eastern and western suburbs.

Home deliveries in these areas have declined and over the counter sales are down substantially.

At one eastern suburbs outlet, sales of the 'Tiser are down 20 percent.

Clearly, the figures are far from healthy, given the huge publicity campaign that launched the new paper.

Interestingly the broadsheet stable mate, The Australian has benefited. Its sales in some parts of the eastern suburbs are up by as much as 10 percent, no doubt contributing to the paper's overall national sales increase of one percent for the six months.
