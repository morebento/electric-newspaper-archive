Monday February 23, 1998

No women bishops, but reconciliation:
Anglican General Synod
Adelaide, 14-20th Feb.1998
By Elaine Moore

After a long week of deliberation on issues both within and outside the Anglican Church, the General Synod in Adelaide wound up with a 'liturgical' act of reconciliation - the traditional, symbolic gesture of foot washing.

The token of humility and reconciliation was enacted by the spiritual head of the Anglican Church of Australia, Archbishop Keith Rayner, washing the feet of two indigenous Bishops at an early morning Adelaide church service.

The service involved one of the indigenous bishops acknowledging the Church's desire to reconcile, inviting the Archbishop to join him at the altar. Prayers and a formal apology were extended on behalf of the Anglican church, followed by an expression of absolution by Aboriginal bishop Arthur Malcolm and Torres Strait Islander Bishop Ted Mosby.

A flame was taken from a candle representing the Holy Spirit, and used to light some leaves - smoke being the Anglican symbol of rising prayer.

"While the smoke is rising, the three of us will wash each other's feet, which will be followed by a Eucharist service," Archbishop Rayner said.

Archbishop Rayner had spoken to the General Synod earlier in the week about the importance of symbolism in society, socially and politically. He criticised Prime Minister John Howard for recognising symbolism as fair reason for Australia's change to a Republic, and yet not fair reason for a reconciliated Australia.

Meanwhile, Australia’s Anglicans have called on the nation to avoid racial violence by seeking to resolve Native Title arguments through reconciliation and to avoid a federal election on race.

"We must not have an election based on race," Archbishop Keith Rayner told reporters in Adelaide.

"The consequences for Australian life if there was a race-based election will be very serious," he said.

Archbishop Rayner expressed his support for the use of such alarming headlines as "blood on the streets" in relation to the Bishop of Bathurst , Bruce Wilson’s address to the Synod.

"I want to say to the Government there is still action which needs to be taken to get the parties together in a spirit that really recognises what their concerns are," he said.

"I entirely agree with Bishop Wilson that we must not have an election based on race," he said.

Describing areas of rural NSW as 'under siege', Bishop Wilson told delegates the number of race riots and violence in the area already indicated there was a very high level of tension.

"If there is a federal election based on a racial issue like the Wik ten-point plan there is no doubt there will be blood on the streets of NSW," Bishop Wilson said.

"“Take a drive through much of outback rural NSW and have a look at the ways shops have to protect themselves ... that is indicative of a community where there is a great deal of tension and the potential for violence."

Archbishop Rayner said the Anglican church had apologised verbally, and in action by creating a special place in it’s equivalent of the federal parliament for Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander delegates - including two indigenous Bishops.

"We have show the spirit of reconciliation required by the gospel moves through this church in word and deed, and I call on all Australians to adopt Christian spirit of reconciliation towards indigenous Australians," Archbishop Rayner said.

The comments came as the Synod passed a lengthy series of sometimes complex motions which included a call on Prime Minister John Howard to reconsider the issues raised by the Senate amendments to the Government’s Native Title Legislation.

In an afternoon dedicated to indigenous affairs, the Synod:

  • Created a special class of indigenous delegates to Synod,
  • Created a special representative council for indigenous Anglicans - called NATSIAC (the national Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander Anglican Council),
  • Called on the nation to seek an honorable, decent and worthy compromise in the issue of native title.
  • Stated, amongst other things, support for sensitive consultation in the area of Native Title, such consultation dealing "carefully with the expectations, aspirations, fears and insecurities of all parties",
  • Urged Australians "to seek an honorable, decent and worthy compromise in the issue of native title",
  • Told Anglican organisations and agencies to foster reconciliation in their respective areas of operation,
  • Requested Prime Minister John Howard to "reconsider the issues raised by the Senate amendments to the Government’s Native Title Legislation" and "to revisit these issues and hold urgent discussions with all stakeholders".

The General Synod also passed a motion affirming that Christians may conscientiously believe that the ordination of women to the priesthood and to the episcopate is either inappropriate or appropriate was passed by The motion was in accordance with the prevailing mood of Synod this week - one that encouraged unity whilst recognising that on some issues there was a diversity of views.

The motion was moved by the Rev Dr Robert Doyle of Sydney and was seconded by Dr Muriel Porter of Melbourne.

Referring to the ordination of women as priests and perhaps sometime in the future as bishops, Dr Doyle said "A problem for many of us is that of conscientious objection to these developments."

However, he said, "In our safeguarding of conscientious objection we ought to avoid placing restrictions on the kinds of judgements made by synods, bishops, parishes and rectors."

Dr Porter, who earlier in Synod moved the motion dealing with the question of the ordination of women as bishops, said she had "great pleasure in seconding this motion".

"We need to celebrate and honour our diversity and avoid excluding people," she said.

The full text of the motion is as follows: "This synod affirms that a Christian conscience ought to be bound by Holy Scripture, as being the ultimate rule and standard of faith, and the creeds and articles of the scriptural faith of the Church."

"It also affirms that Anglicans who conscientiously believe that the ordination of women to the presbyterate and to the episcopate is inconsistent with the teaching of the New Testament along with those who conscientiously believe it is consistent stand within the Fundamental Declarations of the Constitution of The Anglican Church of Australia and doctrine of the Book of Common Prayer and the Thirty-nine Articles."

The political operatus of the Anglican General Synod with its social and political debate registers the Anglican Church's determination to impress on the wider society the issues with which its internal community is coming to terms.

Like the Constitutional Convention, it is possible that it is very relevant as a reflection of a microcosm of views in society.
