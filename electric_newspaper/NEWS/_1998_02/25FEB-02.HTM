Wednesday February 25, 1998

New Zealand's privatised power blows fuse
By Phil Osargo in Wellington

New Zealand's Deputy Prime Minister, Winston Peters, has claimed in Parliament that that one of the country's private electricity companies, Mercury Energy, is directly to blame for the week-long blackout which has virtually closed down Auckland's central business district.

Most department stores and offices in Auckland, and hundreds of large and small business, have had to close. The blackout is estimated to cost at least $10 million in lost production.

Mr Peters said Mercury Energy had so run down its services that it hired another company, Power New Zealand, which supplies electricity to parts of the North Shore and Coromandel, to service its Auckland customers.

He said Mercury Energy had ordered Power New Zealand staff to paint out their own company's name from trucks and uniforms.

"They actually got Power New Zealand to paint out Power New Zealand from the trucks and the uniforms that they're wearing so they would not be found out to be so bereft of staff and expertise and technical assistance," Mr Peters said.

"And we want to know why they would go to that sort of waste and even now, in the middle of this crisis, try to deepen this deception by actually paying a company they're hiring to paint out the name of the hired company.

"That's only one example of what Mercury Energy is guilty of and it's a disgrace."

Auckland city MP Judith Tizard said voters were having to bear the costs of the catastrophe.

"It should not be up to the individual retailers, residents, and commercial operators in Auckland's C.B.D. take Mercury Energy to court to recover the costs of this.

"This episode is going to be a catastrophe, and is already in a commercial sense.

"It is going to cause families, family businesses, and individuals to be bankrupt."

Mr Peters wants an official inquiry to discover more about the 'curious' deal which sees Mercury Power being run by an Auckland legal company called Russell McVeagh.

The deputy Prime Minister said the directors had repeatedly gone to court on legal issues surrounding Mercury Power's contract, and had lost every time.

"I want to know what Russell McVeagh is doing in this arrangement and how culpable and guilty are they for controlling the directorships over which they have so much authority," Mr Peters said.

Meanwhile, one of the Government's political opponents has this time sided with the Government over the issue. ACT leader Richard Prebble said it was extraordinary that Russell McVeagh was producing most of Mercury Power's directors.

"Even more extraordinary is that they are receiving in legal fees $1 million a year, and from this outage I imagine the legal costs of Mercury will probably go to $3 million a year, " Mr Prebble said.

When New Zealand power was privatised and outsourced, the Government claimed it would free up billions of dollars to spend on schools, hospitals, and community services. It also claimed privatisation would lead to cheaper power.
