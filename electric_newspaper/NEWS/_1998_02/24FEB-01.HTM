Tuesday February 24, 1998

Dubai revisited; the maritime plot thickens
By Neil Wiese

New evidence has emerged which may link Patrick stevedoring with December's abortive bid to train Australian military personnel in Dubai as non-union wharfies.

Patrick chairman Chris Corrigan recently admitted that he had publicly lied in December when he denied prior knowledge of the Dubai training scheme undertaken by Victorian shelf company Fynwest Ltd.

Corrigan continued to deny direct involvement in the bid to train a stevedoring workforce to break the Maritime Union of Australia's waterfront labour monopoly.

But The Electric Newspaper has learnt that a previous business colleague of Corrigan, based in Hong Kong, was instrumental in organising the training of Australian military which crumbled under pressure on the Dubai government from international unions.

Investment banker Marc Faber said he was approached last September by a "consortium" to arrange the leasing of a Melbourne dock and equipment. He said he held negotiations with Corrigan.

The Hong Kong banker would not name the consortium's investors, nor could he recall who specifically conceived the plan to train former and active military as a private stevedoring force.

Fellow Hong Kong-based businessman James Fulton was instrumental in registering Container Terminal Management Services (CTMS), the shelf company which Fynwest's directors Michael Wells and Peter Kilfoyle used to channel payments to the Dubai port authorities responsible for training the Australian military.

Fulton, an executive with PCS Asia, said CTMS was set up "on orders from Melbourne".

The new revelations coincide with publicly stated plans by the National Farmers Federation-operated PCS stevedoring group to expand its non-union stevedoring workforce, currently being trained at Port Melbourne's Webb dock, to Brisbane and Darwin.

Maritime reform Minister Peter Reith yesterday warned the union movement his Government would act swiftly if any union tried to thwart the PCS operation with "threats".

Mr Reith said, "I was very interested to read....quotes from the National Union of Workers' Greg Sword who said the NUW had written to 2,000 companies in Victoria, basically warning them off of the (PCS) operations. I have advised my department to commence investigations into the threats coming from the NUW."

The Minister said the NUW's reported actions may have breached the Trade Practices Act.

Meanwhile Patrick, which sub-leased Webb dock to PCS, is bleeding financially in the face of relentless MUA industrial action.

Patrick's Port Melbourne operations were hit by two 48 hour strikes last week, holding up more than $20m. worth of cargo. The double-whammy virtually overlapped a previous 19 day strike at Webb dock by MUA members which ended only after an order from the Industrial Relations Commission.

Even as Victoria's Supreme Court yesterday granted Patrick an injunction preventing further MUA industrial action at the company's Melbourne facilities, the union gave notice of possible industrial action at Patrick's non-Melbourne operations over new workplace enterprise agreements.

Mr Reith labelled the new threat "a contrivance to try to obtain immunity from civil action".
