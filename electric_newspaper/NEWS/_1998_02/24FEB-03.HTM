Tuesday February 24, 1998

A matter of importance

What is more important, a $1 billion risk or a perceived conflict of interest?

This question was being asked by members of Parliament's Economic and Finance Committee last night, as it was revealed that the Auditor-General had directly approached at least one Minister and one Parliamentary Secretary over aspects of his report.

The Auditor-General, Ken MacPherson, says he spoke with then Minister John Olsen about the Minister's chairmanship of a public works committee.

Mr MacPherson says he raised this because he saw the Minister may have had a conflict of interest as chair of a committee which dealt with issues for which he had Ministerial responsibility.

Mr MacPherson also raised with Liberal MP and Parliamentary secretary Joan Hall her membership of a committee which awarded contracts to the Hindmarsh soccer stadium redevelopment.

As Parliamentary secretary, Ms Hall was part of the Government which should have independently checked the tendering committee's process and procedures.

Mr MacPherson, in answer to a question from Labor's Trish White, told the Economic and Finance committee that he had approached the Premier regarding these matters in August.

"I specifically asked to go to see him," Mr MacPherson said, "because I felt that if they (Oswald and Hall) kept to that path, there would be a problem.

"People of the Legislature could not be appointed to offices of the Crown and still be independents as Members of Parliament and discharge their duties."

In these circumstances, Opposition front-benchers believe that if Mr MacPherson uncovered a brand-new risk regarding exposure on ETSA which needed an urgent fix, he would have brought the matter personally to the Premier's attention.

Labor members of the committee conclude that there were two scenarios: either the Auditor-General sent dire warnings to Government on ETSA risk which weren't passed on to the Premier (in which case, they say, senior departmental heads should roll), or the risk issue is not so urgent that the Premier had to be told at the time, nor does it have to be addressed by a fire-sale of ETSA.
