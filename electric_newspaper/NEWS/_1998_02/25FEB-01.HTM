Wednesday February 25, 1998

Five missing months mocked
By Hendrik Gout

The State Government's billion-dollar credibility gap stretched to breaking point yesterday, as the Premier maintained as many as seven top advisors withheld the draft Auditor-General's report from him for five months.

The Premier, Mr Olsen, needs the public to believe that he did not see the report before the State election. During the campaign he consistently vowed to keep ETSA and Optima in public ownership.

Mr Olsen now says that the report is justification for him reneging on that promise, but that he was unaware of the report until December.

This is despite his chief executive officer, former State Bank group executive Ian Kowalick, having been given a copy of the draft report in July last year.

In Parliament yesterday, Mr Olsen repeatedly refused to answer questions from Opposition Leader Mike Rann on the issue.

"Given the Premier's claim that the Auditor-General's annual report contained a warning of possible taxpayer risk of between $1 and $2 billion, and that Mr Ian Kowalick failed to tell the Premier about it for five months, will the Premier now sack Mr Kowalick?" Mr Rann asked the Premier.

Three times the Speaker, Mr Oswald, had to instruct the Premier to make his replies relevant to the questions.

Mr Rann also released a leaked 1996 document, prepared and authorised by Mr Olsen, which contained future policy initiatives including the privatisation of ETSA.

Senior public servants with a combined 55 years experience in Government who were contacted by The Electric Newspaper late yesterday said they had never heard of any so-called convention which the Premier says prevented Mr Kowalick or the head of Treasury, Gerard Bradley, from passing the Auditor-General's draft report to their Ministers.

Outside the House, Mr Rann said that when he was a Minister he was regularly briefed on draft Auditor-General's reports.

Referring to the claimed five-month delay, Opposition frontbencher Patrick Conlon said, "It's not true - it's not real."

In a grievance debate, Mr Conlon said the Premier was "absailing without a rope."

"Any reasonable person would say he (the Premier) probably knew about (the report) since July," Mr Conlon said.

Government Ministers yesterday prompted a series of Dorothy Dixers from their own backbench so they could say they could use the money from an ETSA sale in their portfolios.

The Government is likely to step up its taxpayer-funded propoganda now that the Premier is on the back foot over the issue.

The Premier has already used public funds on commercial television appearances to laud the planned sale.
