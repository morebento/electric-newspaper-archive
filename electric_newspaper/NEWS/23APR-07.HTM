Thursday April 23, 1998

Shadow Treasurer Kevin Foley remains poker faced as he hears of the Deputy Premier's Pontious Pilot defence.

Mr Foley examines the press release announcing Graham Ingerson's reasons for not knowing about the $96 million write-down.

Elvis lives, so does Ingo
by Hendrik Gout

Graham Ingerson lives to flounder another day. A meeting of all Liberal Party MP's at Parliament House broke up yesterday afternoon without mentioning the $96 million writedown in the value of the Electricity Trust of South Australia. Nor did the Liberal meeting discuss the Deputy Premier's apparent negligence in not reading his briefing papers about the issue.

"It wasn't even mentioned, as far as I recollect," said Police Minister Iain Evans as he left the meeting.

"It was a normal Party meeting. Why should it be on the agenda? You're after blood for some reason," he told reporters.

Not after it, Mr Evans. We're stepping through it. It's seeping from the wounded career of a Deputy Premier.

On Wednesday, Under-Treasurer Gerard Bradley was asked at by the Economic and Finance Committee when he had told Mr Ingerson about a $96 million devaluation in the value of the Electricity Trust of S.A.

According to Mr Ingerson, the first he knew about it was when he read it in the ETSA annual report tabled in Parliament in December.

According to the Under-Treasurer, the information was sent much earlier - in June and July - to the Minister.

So during the Party meeting, Mr Ingerson left the room and walked along the corridor to his Parliament House office. There, he prepared a media statement. It was the Pontious Pilot defence.

"I acknowledge the fact that Treasury Observer reports for ETSA Board meetings for June and July 1997 addressed to the Minister for Infrastructure included references to possible losses," that statement said.

"However, I did not see those reports.

"Standard procedure in my office at that time was that my senior advisers read all ETSA Board papers and minutes as well as Treasury Observer reports.

"My advisers then raised issues of concern with me. However, no advice was provided to me on the issue of possible losses," the written statement said.

We are now expected to believe that Mr Ingerson's advisers read the briefing about a $96 million write-down, and did not tell their boss.

Sure.

We'll find out more about that when the Economic and Finance Committee calls Jane Cooper, the Minister's former chief of staff, to give evidence next Wednesday.

But even so, Shadow Treasurer Kevin Foley is not mollified.

"He doesn't read briefings involving a $100 million worth of taxpayer write-down, and I will be producing further evidence to clearly demonstrate that he can't blame his staff," Mr Foley told The Electric Newspaper.

"Graham Ingerson's political career is over. He may not yet want to admit that, but it is over. There is no greater political crime than to blame your staff for your mistakes. Graham Ingerson was responsible for his office, he was responsible for his staff.

"John Olsen has to sack potentially the State's most incompetent Minister. How can you have the State's second most senior Cabinet Minister who now advises us it was his staff's fault."

"How long do you think Mr Ingerson will be Deputy," reporters asked junior Minister Mark Brindall as he left the Liberal Party meeting.

"The matters of the leadership are matters for the Party," Mr Brindall hinted darkly.

Well, Mr Ingerson won't even be in South Australia when Ms Cooper appears before the committee. The Deputy Premier is going to Europe for 12 days, all expenses paid. By you.

And you can bet he's read the details of his flight times. Even if they haven't been pointed out to him by his staff.
