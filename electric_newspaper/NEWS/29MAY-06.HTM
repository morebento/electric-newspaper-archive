Friday May 29, 1998

The Budget: ETSA betsa

SA Treasurer Rob Lucas says that without the sale of ETSA, he will introduce a mini-Budget in October to provide up to $150 million of further tax increases or expenditure reductions, which would take effect in the later years of the four-year financial plan.

"This is not a threat," he threatened.

The Government's argument sheds a new light on electricity privatisation. In February, Premier Olsen announced his Government's intention to sell ETSA and Optima Energy.

He said this certainly wasn't his intention a few months earlier, before or during the election campaign, when he promised to keep ETSA in public ownership.

But he said new information regarding national competition policy was available to him only in December.

Yesterday, we were told that ETSA had to be sold - but not for those reasons.

Now, we're told we have to do it to avoid an extra $150 million in taxes.

"Selling our power utilities is not a decision that has come easily," Mr Lucas told Parliament, as he read his Budget speech from a specially installed lectern in the House of Assembly.

"But the benefits in terms of slashing debt and therefore interest costs, freeing up resources that can be spent on health and education, and minimising the risk to taxpayers of major losses in the new electricity market are undeniable."

The sham of this argument is that it has existed, if it does at all, for a long time - certainly before February, certainly before December, and certainly before the election.

Opposition Leader Mike Rann was caustic.

"In last year's Budget, John Olsen told us he would balance the next four budgets without these tax increases and without the sale of ETSA," Mr Rann said.

The Australian Democrats said the Government's threat of a mini-budget was bully-boy tactics without justification.

Democrat leader Mike Elliott said the Government was resorting to playing politics on the issue instead of providing evidence for the sale of ETSA and the need for an extra $150 million if there was no sale.

"More than three months after announcing its plan to sell ETSA, the democrats are still waiting for the proof that South Australians will receive real savings from the sale," Mr Elliott said.

The United Trades and Labor Council is planning a rally on June 3 in protest against the proposed sale.

The State Government is publicly declining to say how much it expects to get for ETSA, saying it will compromise the sale process.

Anyone who has bought, or advertised for sale, a house or car may be tempted to be inclined to be ever so slightly sceptical about this line of reasoning.

The fact is, the Government doesn't advertise the sale price because it simply does not know what it will be.

But yesterday, Mr Lucas said that other Government assets, including the Lotteries Commission, the TAB, and Homestart would not be sold unless there was a demonstrable benefit to the State.

This would depend on the sale price, Mr Lucas told the media conference, saying the monies had not been factored into this Budget because no decision had yet been made.

Mr Lucas pointed to the proposed sale of Adelaide casino as an example where an asset had been withdrawn from sale because the best price offered was not high enough.

The same reasoning does not apparently apply to the ETSA sale. The Government is locking itself into an ETSA sale, going so far as "not threatening" to wack an extra $150 million in taxes or expenditure cuts unless the sale goes through - even though it does not know how much a buyer will offer.

The real question now is the attitude of the Australian Democrats. With Labor standing fast against the sale, the Democrats hold the key to the passage of the ETSA sale bill. The Democrats are already convinced that a long-term lease of ETSA is not a financial option, which means an enormous pressure on the three Upper House Democrats to vote for the Bill or face Government claims that the Party will be responsible for an extra $150 million mini-budget.

But of course, we knew that. In February.
