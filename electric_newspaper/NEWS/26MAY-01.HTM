Tuesday May 26, 1998

Parliament resumes today: Ingerson expectant

State Parliament resumes at two o'clock this afternoon, amid mounting speculation that the deputy Premier, Graham Ingerson, is to face an official inquiry into his role in the ETSA affair.

Mr Ingerson has told Parliament he was unaware of details of the Electricity Trust's $96 million write-down when a handful of briefing papers, board information, and the draft annual report had already been sent to his office.

The State Opposition has called for Mr Ingerson to face a privileges committee over the issue, alleging that he may have misled the House.

The decision is in the hands of the Speaker, John Oswald, who has so far declined to indicate his proposed course of action. Even so, it will need to be carried by a vote in the House of Assembly, and that means Independents will need to vote with the Labor Party to set up an inquiry.

One Minister contacted late yesterday said he expected the Independents to not automatically block the inquiry, but said 'there's still a lot of talking to be done tomorrow'.

He said it would be wrong to assume that Mr Ingerson had no friends in Cabinet, and this wasn't a case of handing Mr Ingerson's future over on a platter.

The State Government claims that it decided only in December that it wanted to sell ETSA. It says it relied on the Auditor-General's report, which was finalised before the State election but not tabled in Parliament, on a Schroder's report which the Government has refused to reveal, and on a report from the Institute for Public Affairs.

But while Mr Ingerson is waiting for the Speaker's verdict, the Australian Democrats still have to decide the more important question - will they support the ETSA sale?

The Government last week turned down the cold on the Democrats, saying that new figures suggested a long-term lease would not be an option because it would reduce the price to such a degree that the State would lose money.

The Premier has previously said that if Parliament did not support legislation for a public float or trade sale of ETSA and Optima Energy, he would by-pass Parliament and sell the electricity infrastructure through a long-term lease which does not require Parliamentary approval.

But the Government now claims a lease is impractical.

This means the Democrats hold the balance of power in a literal as well as a figurative sense.

The Treasurer, Mr Lucas, has promised the Democrats he will this week release his assessment of a high-level report by Queensland economist and academic John Quiggin and South Australian John Spoehr, which said SA would actually lose money in the long-term if ETSA and Optima were sold.

The Democrats are also waiting for questions asked of Mr Lucas in February, relating to the cost of the Government's advertising campaign on ETSA privatisation.

Meanwhile, the ALP is sceptical about the Democrats' ability to hold out against the proposed privatisation.

Opposition Treasury spokesman Kevin Foley says he expects the Democrats to ultimately support the sale, while the Government is far from confident.

The Democrats are publicly saying they have not yet decided, and are still gathering evidence about the financial and social effects.
