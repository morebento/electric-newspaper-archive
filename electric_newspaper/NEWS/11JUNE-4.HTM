Thursday June 11, 1998

Pre-schoolers celebrate; but boogie man coming

As South Australian kindergartens celebrate early childhood learning as part of Public Education week, pre-schools and child care centres across the country are fighting to maintain quality education despite decreasing budgets.

The director of Campbelltown Pre-school, Mary Christie, said quality early childhood teaching laid the foundation for good literacy skills.

"Both past and present Governments have been decreasing funding to public pre-schools for a number of years," Ms Christie said.

She said these cuts undermined the quality of programs and teachers involved in helping children learn reading, writing and numeracy.

She said while public education was being slashed, the Government was increasing its funding to private education.

She said the media was quick to report problems in the public education system, which often left the community with a one-sided view.

"Private schools do not provide the best education. The public system has been excellent and in many cases people are simply being misled," she said.

Democrats leader Meg Lees yesterday visited Adelaide's Lady Emma Child Care Centre to highlight the crisis facing child care institutions.

She said many centres which had waiting lists last year were now operating at well below capacity and faced closure.

"As a result of the changes introduced by the Howard Government in 1996, many centres have had to close rooms, lay off staff, stop buying toys and stop supplying meals and nappy services," she said.

Senator Lees said many parents could no longer afford child care and were forced to place their children in "backyard" child care or simply leave them at home alone.

"We are watching the emergence of a generation of kids who are not getting the benefits of professional education and socially developmental child care," Senator Lees said.
