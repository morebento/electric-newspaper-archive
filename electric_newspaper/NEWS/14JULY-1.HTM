Tuesday July 14, 1998

Labor's Ralph Clarke - fought against the groyne


Jim Douglas

Campaign off, party on

Several hundred West Beach campaigners had their final fling on Saturday night - a huge party to mark the end of their two-year campaign to save Adelaide's northern beaches from erosion.

And while their fight is over - and lost - residents will continue to monitor the project, which involves the building of a large groyne and a boat launching facility.

The residents wanted to stop the boat harbour because of fears that the Government will be unable to maintain in perpetuity a very expensive sand management program, without which the northern beaches will suffer serious erosion.


The West Beach campaign - dead but not forgotten
The anti-harbour movement was comprised of more than families who live in the affected area. They were supported by the Australian Democrats and much of the Labor Opposition, environmentalists, and those with concerns about its financial implications. The sand replenishment program will cost as much as $1 million.

The local Liberal MP, Steve Condous, promised before the election that he would lie down in front of the bulldozers to stop the project. He then voted in its favour once the election was over.

Faced with no other apparent option, the anti-harbour protesters tried direct action, regularly forming blockades at the construction site and trying to delay work on the project. They collected at least $8,000 in public donations, with which they bought a full-page advertisement listing their concerns in the Advertiser, a newspaper which supports the building of the groyne and harbour.


Democrat MLC Sandra kanck helped raise the issue in Parliament
But now that the groyne is close to built, and the inevitability of its completion is inevitable, the residents' fight is over.

They have been defeated.

So last weekend, at the Henley Sailing Club, they hired a band, blew up balloons, and had a party.

As amateur campaigners, they were deeply committed and totally sincere. They were also facing the might of the State Government's powerful propaganda machine. They had little chance of success.

But despite their failure to stop the project, the mood at the party was buoyant. The band was good, there were amusing anecdotes told of early morning blockades and sleepless nights, and they spoke of a camaraderie forged between people who had not known each other before this campaign.

"We've lost," said a young mother, "but at least we can say we tried."
