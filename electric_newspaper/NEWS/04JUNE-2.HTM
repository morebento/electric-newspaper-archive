Thursday June 4, 1998

Pack up your troubles

ETSA may still go ahead and close 12 regional offices around South Australia, despite Ministerial panic.

ETSA announced last week that it would close its offices in Victor Harbour, Mt Gambier, Port Augusta, Murray Bridge, Mount Barker, Bamera, Clare, Kadina, Port Pirie, Whyalla, Port Lincoln, and Gawler.

The announcement was not popularly received in Victor Harbour, Mt Gambier, Port Augusta, Murray Bridge, Mount Barker, Bamera, Clare, Kadina, Port Pirie, Whyalla, Port Lincoln, or Gawler.

From then on, people would have to deal with ETSA through a call centre which could be based anywhere, but probably still in South Australia.

"Country people still value being able to speak face to face with someone from ETSA when they have a problem associated with their power supplies," Democrat Sandra Kanck told State Treasurer Rob yesterday.

"The closures of the offices would reduce employment opportunities in the 12 regional centres and inflict further damage on the already fragile economies of regional South Australia," she told him.

The Australian Services Union says ETSA informed them "such closures were consistent with the operation of a private sector owner."

The State Government sent its most senior ministers into the provinces after the sale decision to reassure country people they had nothing to fear from electricity privatisation. In fact, Mr Olsen and Mr Lucas emphasised at meetings in the Riverland and the Iron Triangle privatisation would be good for them.

All this landed in the lap of the new Minister with responsibilities for ETSA, Treasurer Rob Lucas.

Everyone in Parliament knows ETSA is already being prepared for sale, but controversial office closures were not going to inspire confidence in that sale process.

On Monday, Mr Lucas ordered that ETSA wasn't allowed to decide to close regional offices - for the time being. He told ETSA to put such decisions on hold "pending a further review of the rationale for closures and the impact on service standards in country areas".

The review, such as it is, will actually consist of an exercise to see whether it would be better to close the offices now, or after Parliament has passed the privatisation Bill. Its cold logic will suggest the latter option.

Mr Lucas said on Monday that since his recent appointment he has not yet - Michael Armitage used to look after ETSA until Mr Olsen took it away from him last week - been fully briefed on the details. Mr Lucas told Parliament today the decision was still on hold, or "Hold On!", as one MP said.

Two metropolitan offices were also announced for closure, at Holden Hill and St Mary's. Staff at one of those centres said yesterday they expected the "Hold" decision applied only to regional offices, and they were expecting the order to pack their bags.
