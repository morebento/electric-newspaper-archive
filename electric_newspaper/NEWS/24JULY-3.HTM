Friday July 24, 1998

Home to the homeless - but no more

Out in the cold
by Chris Warren

Two of Adelaide's oldest charities are closing their night shelters for homeless men in Whitmore Square.

The William Booth Hostel, run by the Salvation Army since 1899, will no longer offer emergency accommodation after the end of this month, and there are also separate plans to close St. Vincent de Paul's night shelter and re-locate that in new premises away from the Square.

Despite the consequences of such a major upheaval, both services claim the homeless will not be disadvantaged. In fact, they've given assurances the new shelters will offer more privacy and dignity for their clients.

The Salvos are putting the finishing touches to a $2.4 million low income accommodation complex in Dawkins Place, behind the Red Cross Blood Bank.

Ten modern, self-contained units will be offered to single men who want to move out of hostel accommodation and learn independent living skills for periods of up to nine weeks, and paying $65 weekly rent.

"Our idea is to allow the funding for crisis accommodation to go one agency (St. Vincent de Paul), and allow us to work with those who want to move forward and give themselves more independence," said Captain Barry Casey, the Salvation Army's officer in charge of the project.

Every night, just before dusk, men of all ages start lining up outside Vinnies night shelter where for $5, or less if they're broke, they can get a hot pie, a shower, and a clean bed.

But administrator Mike Abbott admits conditions inside the hostel are Dickensian with open dormitories, no privacy, and shared toilet facilities.

Vinnies is negotiating with the State Government for funding to build a new shelter with private accommodation facilities close to Whitmore Square, which is a favoured haunt of Adelaide's homeless.

"We've made it quite clear that we need to operate the existing shelter with all its faults until such time we can build a new one," Cpt. Abbott said.

All charities are alarmed by the growing number of homeless young men and women, some of whom are trying to support children under the age of three.

St. John's Youth Services is the only agency funded to provide emergency accommodation to the under 25's, but this service has no resources for young mothers with babies.
