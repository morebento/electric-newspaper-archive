Beer pops at wharves
By Hendrik Gout and Andrea Stylianou

Almost a thousand wharfies and their supporters rushed to Saint Vincent St. at Port Adelaide after the news of an emphatic victory in the Federal Court swept the nation.

At 2pm EST the Federal Court ordered Patrick Stevedores to reinstate the 1400 men sacked two weeks ago for being members of the Maritime Union of Australia.

Patricks chairman Chris Corrigan reacted less than happily to the news.

Mr Corrigan said the company would abide by the court order, and now has to discuss with its labor supply companies.

Patrick Stevedoring, which once employed its own wharfies, hived its labor force to set-up companies, which did the actual sackings. These other companies have not a lot of assets, unlike the parent company.

The labor supply companies sacked their entire workforce, claiming insolvency.

Patricks will appeal the decision before the Full Bench of the Federal Court.

MUA secretary John Coombes reacted more than happily to the news.

"There still is apparently justice in this country and with respect to workers who have been illegally dismissed," Mr Coombs said.

"It's a landmark decision.

"This is about the question of whether or not you're entitled to believe that you've got some security and that employers are not stripping companies of assets and forming new companies and changing your employment arrangements so they can simply throw you on the scrapheap."

In the Federal Court judgement, Justice Tony North's said there was an element of conspiracy in the sacking of Patrick's 1,400 unionised workforce.

ACTU President Jenny George was at Port Adelaide's Patricks office in support of the Federal Courts decision.

She said that "it is a good day today" and that it "re-established faith in the law and the democratic nature of society."

"We have fought a fight for every worker in this country", she said.

State Opposition Leader Mike Rann was also the rally to celebrate the decision, saying that the ruling is about the rights and livelihood of every Australian.

31 affiliated unions in South Australia with about 120,000 union members supported the reinstatements.

The National Farmers Federation, which is bankrolling the moves to smash union control of the dockside labor, was mightily distressed.

NFF president Donald McGauchie said the ruling was outrageous.

He said the NFF's waterfront front, PNC, would still pay the wages of its recently-hired, non-union men even though the Federal Court ordered them out of the docks.

The Workplace Relations Minister, Peter Reith was a practiced impassive.

It would have no impact on the Government's resolve to "fix up" the waterfront, he said.
