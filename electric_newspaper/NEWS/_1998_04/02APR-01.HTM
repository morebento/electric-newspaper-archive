Thursday April 2, 1998

Lighting the Wik
by Garry Quill in Canberra and Hendrik Gout in Adelaide

A High Court ruling in Canberra yesterday encouraged the Federal Government to proceed with its controversial Wik legislation, and simultaneously left the State Government in South Australia with authority to build an unwanted bridge to an unimportant island.

The case had its beginnings with a privately-funded marina on Hindmarsh Island, a low, scrubby lump of land in Lake Alexandria near the mouth of the River Murray.

Access to the island is by ferry only, and the second stage of the marina was dependent on the marina owners building a bridge between the island and the mainland, a distance of a few hundred metres.

Originally, this bridge was to have been privately built and funded. Then came a State Government commitment to put the money up front and have it paid back over time. Eventually, it agreed to pay for the bridge with taxpayers' money.

A group of aboriginal women objected, claiming it infringed on their sacred beliefs. A subsequent Royal Commission found these claims to be fabricated although the Federal Government had by then intervened, stopping the bridge under the Heritage Protection Act.

A Federal election and a change of Government later, special legislation was passed to authorise the bridge. It was the constitutionality of this legislation which was tested by the High Court here yesterday.

Five of the six judges ruled that the legislation was constitutional, and two further ruled that the Federal Government had the power to make laws which were detrimental to aborigines.

For the federal Government, this is an important judgement. It wants to introduce other legislation which will affect not just Hindmarsh Island, but the nation as a whole. This is the Wik land rights legislation, which may also be detrimental to aborigines.

The Federal Government will now proceed with this legislation.

But in South Australia yesterday, the focus was not on the national implications of the High Court decision, but on local issues.

Tom and Wendy Chapman, the marina developers, and their Westpac bankers have lodged a $70 million compensation claim against the State Government for its tardiness in connecting Hindmarsh Island to the mainland.

"We would like to resolve legal claims by the Chapmans and Westpac against the State before construction on the bridge commences," SA Attorney-General Trevor Griffin said.

"We would hope to have work started within the next few months, but that is largely dependent on the readiness of the Chapmans and Westpac to negotiate to reach a settlement with us," Mr Griffin said.

The Chapmans, meanwhile, were beaming. "We have finally emerged from one of the country's longest, silliest, and most expensive political and legal sagas," said Wendy Chapman.

The aboriginal women who brought the action were distraught. "Once again, the legal and political structures of non-indigenous Australian have been unable to recognise and address indigenous rights and cultural aspirations," the women said.

Meanwhile, at Goolwa near Hindmarsh Island yesterday, the reaction was decidedly mixed.

"Who'd want to go there, anyway?" said a stubble-faced fisherman who declined to give his name, pointing to Hindmarsh Island.

"It's clapped-out, that (the island) is. Ferry or bridge, I'm staying on this side."

"We need the bridge," said a shopowner. "This place is dead now and getting deader. The bridge will help." "You obviously haven't waited for hours trying to get across on the ferry," a woman said as she was driving to pick up her children from school. "In the holidays it's hell."

It now seems certain that the bridge will indeed go ahead. Unanswered questions still remain. What will be done to manage the increased number of people likely to visit Hindmarsh Island and the fragile wetlands of the Coorong and Lake Alexandria? What will be the taxpayer's total financial exposure to build a bridge for what is a private marina development?

And importantly, has all the hurt, the distrust, and the division between white and black communities, and internally between members of the aboriginal community over the long and complex legal and social wrangling, really been worthwhile?
