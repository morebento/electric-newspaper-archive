Wednesday April 22, 1998

Don's vision Lights a spark
by Jack Hoogland

The level of public interest in the first of this year's Whitlam Lecture Series was evident from the start; the original Norwood Town Hall venue was quickly sold out and it was soon evident that even the Adelaide Town Hall was too small. In the end, about 4000 people came to hear Don Dunstan‚s lecture, "We Intervene or We Sink" at the Entertainment Centre.

Mr Dunstan was introduced by Philip Adams, and the lecture attended by, among others, Gough Whitlam and Jennie George. The central theme of economic intervention versus economic rationalism was presented in philosophical and historical terms which served as a basis for a critique of practices indulged in by governments around Australia.

According to Mr Dunstan, the conditions that existed in the last half of the 19th century which gave rise to communism were the result of unregulated lassè faire capitalism, and the current trends towards minimal, non-interventionist states constituted a return to those conditions.

While not indulging in 'the politics of nostalgia', Mr Dunstan said history had shown proven ways of dealing with the economy to provide citizens with security of food, shelter, work and services. He lamented that modern Economics courses had little or no historical component and that history as a subject was not considered economically useful.

"If we don't know where we‚re from, then we can‚t make intelligent judgements about where we're going", he said.

Drawing on his own experiences, and from this State‚s own history of public enterprise, Mr Dunstan gave examples of Government-established industries that have been beneficial to South Australia, from the Radiata Pine plantations in the State‚s south-east to the acquisition of the Adelaide Electric Supply to form ETSA.

He said that the proposed sale of ETSA was an example of 'pea and thimble' trickery, where for the sake of a temporary balancing of the Treasury books, the Government was likely to saddle the consumers of the State with high on-going costs, while forgoing hundreds of millions of dollars worth of revenue.

Dunstan was unapologetic in his criticism of economic rationalism and the extent to which it undermined the public good, referring to it as 'oxymoronic'. He highlighted the deception involved in the proposed ETSA sale and the failure to materialise of the claimed benefits to the state arising from the SA Water deal.

But while the lecture dealt significantly with matters economic, he stressed that intervention was also required on other levels, citing Wik, industrial relations and civil liberties as examples. Participation in determining society's direction was important, and he singled out the OECD's Multilateral Agreement on Investment as being particularly undemocratic.

In ending his lecture, Dunstan likened society to lemmings rushing unchecked into the ocean, and that for society to survive, intervention and regulation were essential.
