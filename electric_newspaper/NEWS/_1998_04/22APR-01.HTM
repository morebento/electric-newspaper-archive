Wednesday April 22, 1998

Unions win, men still out
by Hendrik Gout

Waterside workers are likely to turn up for work today - and still find themselves locked out.

Yesterday afternoon, the Federal Court had found that Patrick’s had engaged in "unlawful conspiracy" in firing its entire waterfront workforce.

Justice Tony North ordered that the men be reinstated immediately.

But a few hours later, Patrick Stevedores successfully applied for a stay of execution against the Federal Court ruling that the company had to re-instate the 1400 men illegally sacked two weeks ago.

Justice North's ruling in part hung on Patrick's corporate organisation, by which it managed to dismiss the men.

Instead of employing its people directly, Patrick Stevedores late last year set up other companies, then two weeks ago declared these insolvent, and dismissed the men without redundancy pay... in fact, in some instances without back pay for work the men had already done.

"There is an argument... that the Patrick owners and the Patrick employers have engaged in an unlawful conspiracy," Justice North read out to the jam-packed courtroom.

"There is an argument that this was done because the men were members of the union," he said.

There was jubilation on the docks around Australia, and dark despair in the Prime Minister's suite and the office of the Federal Industrial Relations Minister, Peter Reith.

The gloom there was matched only by the darkness at Patrick's Sydney boardroom. Patrick share prices have gone down like the Titanic in an icecube tray since the fight's dragged on.

Just yesterday, Woolworths managing director Reg Clairs warned Australian boardrooms that this war on the waterfront, proclaimed by Patrick's and declared by Mr Howard and Mr Reith, was going to cost business plenty.

Mr Clairs spoke for all of corporate Australia when he said profits were at risk. In some cases, they're already missing in action. Toyota is facing standowns and is an innocent victim of the Government's anti-union agenda. Other large corporate players are quietly trying to tell the Government to stick its head in or win the war quickly. This is not largesse on the part of business; it's self-preservation. It's all very well to go busting a union, but let's not muck up the bottom line.

The stock exchange has already suspended trading in Patrick's' parent company. This is serious news indeed for the stevedoring company. Its customers already have cargo plying the oceans and no certainty of having the ships unloaded. The Exchange takes a dim view of companies not disclosing information vital to its share price. The Australian Securities Commission does too.

So in the Patrick's media conference, it was not a smiling chairman, Patrick Corrigan, who looked around the room and said what everyone expected him to say: "Appeal".

He's going to drag out the battle.

So yesterday afternoon, five Federal Court judges, including one in Adelaide, linked up by video-conference and did two things. They put a stay of execution on - they said that Patrick's did not have to re-instate the men until 5 pm this afternoon. Secondly, they gave the company right to appeal before the Full Bench of the Federal Court.

Because if the men do go back, Mr Corrigan is in deep shit.

"The orders that have been handed down in the Federal Court in Melbourne we find to be totally unworkable," Mr Corrigan told a news conference almost as crowded as the courtroom had been an hour before.

"We're in the position now where we're paying two workforces, whereas we can only employ one."

But that's a risk he shouldn't have gambled on... at least, not according to Justice North, who said the company had taken a risk - and lost. He said Patrick's could have foreseen its sackings would be challenged in the court.

So could the Federal Government. To do battle to crush a powerful union one must have two weapons - the support of the uninformed, and the support of the courts.

The Prime Minister spent time on 2UE radio in Sydney, uninforming. "We certainly didn't conspire to break the law. We certainly didn't conspire in the sense that it is understood in the law," he said to the incomprehensible audience.

The Federal Opposition Leader was equally incomprehensible. He called on the Prime Minister to call the warring parties together and solve the dispute around the negotiating table. It was almost as if the Opposition Leader thought the Prime Minister was a neutral third party.

Justice North was swayed in his decision by the Maritime Union's promise not to claim immediate wages from the artificially insolvent labor force companies.

Meanwhile, the National Farmers Federation is also looking a shotgun in the eye. The NFF has more than its share of union bashers, but there are also member cockies and pastoral companies which just want to go farming, not union-busting. A lot of their affiliation fees (the NFF charges its own type of union fees) has gone into setting up a non-union, lower-paid waterfront labor force.
