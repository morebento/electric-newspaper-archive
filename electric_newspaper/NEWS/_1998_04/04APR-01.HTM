Saturday April 4, 1998

Adelaide loses part of its history
By Andrea Stylianou

A heritage building in the heart of Adelaide's main shopping centre has been taken from its provisional heritage list, and will eventually be knocked down.

It's the David Jones building in Rundle Mall.

The building was provisionally placed on the list after nomination by experts, but after sitting on this list for just four days, the Miniister responsible for heritage buildings took it off.

Under new powers provided by the Heritage Act, Minister Dorothy Kotz can remove the heritage listed building if she reckons wider issues of public interest outweigh the decision for the listing.

Ms Kotz announced yesterday that she had "formed the view that it would not be in the public interest to confirm the entry of the David Jones building on the Heritage Register."

She gave as part of her reason what she considered "the importance of maintaining and improving the vitality of Rundle Mall as an important part of the City of Adelaide."

If the building had been left on the list potential purchasers and developers would have encountered difficulties in selling the building and this would not support development in South Australia.

"South Australia wants development," she said. She did not cite any polling.

"The public interest is better served by allowing the redevelopment of two important sites in our city centre to go ahead," she said.

David Jones Limited is "delighted".

Managing director Peter Wilkinson said it will now sell the unwanted building. David Jones recently closed a chain of retail outlets called John Martins, a century-old South Australian retailer which David Jones had taken over. It will now move into the vacant John Martin's building, also in Rundle Street.

There are now only ghosts in the John Martins building. One of Australia's largest auctions of department store fixtures and fittings finished in the basement on Friday.

Bids were mainly local, but some came from interstate

More than 2,000 lots were on offer during the two-day sale, and out the door went chairs, tables, carpet, crockery, counters, hanging racks and window displays.

John Martins store manager Kevin May said that since the store closed this month, the remaining staff had been cleaning up and preparing for the sale.

There was strong interest from the commercial sector, but most locals wanted to buy just a momento.

David Jones issued a statement yesterday, saying it had issued instructions to its selling agents, First Pacific Davies, to immediately recommence its world-wide hunt for buyers of its heritage building, which was suspended when the building was first nominated for heritage listing.

But it made a strong and unequivocal commitment to redevelop the now-empty John Martins building into a four-storey department store and speciality shop complex.

"Our aim is to deliver this project in the year 2000," Mr Willkinson said.

"It is now all systems go. We are back and want all South Australians to share in the excitement and benefits this project will bring to the State."

There is understood to be no appeal process available against the Minister's de-listing. Offers on the building close at the end of the month (April 30).
