Thursday July 16, 1998

Tankard takes early curtain call
by Krista Eleftheriou

Renowned Australian Dance Theatre artistic director Meryl Tankard is going - far away.

After a day of acrimonious talks yesterday, it's now certain that Ms Tankard will cut her ties with the dance company, far short of her three-year contract.

The theatre's board and SA Arts Minister Diana Laidlaw demanded the artistic director's resignation in April after an apparent clash between the Board and Ms Tankard about objectives for the company.

Talks this morning are expected to finalise the details of the severance.

Media Entertainment and Arts Alliance state secretary Stephen Spence said Ms Tankard's three-year contract came into force in January, but he suspects that just one month later some Board members were already considering sacking her while saying publicly it was not an option.

"It seems a bit strange that after negotiating a new contract in August and having a number of months to renege on it, only a few weeks into the contract the Board decided the situation was unworkable," he said.

Mr Spence said the dispute was making South Australia look extremely foolish.

"Meryl is well recognised nationally and around the world for her talent," he said.

The Australian Dance Theatre's code of silence and a confidentiality clause in Ms Tankard's contract kept the public and arts industry in the dark until four weeks ago.

When the truth emerged a huge section of the SA arts community swung behind Ms Tankard, calling on the Board and Ms Laidlaw to resign if they reneged on the original contract.

Mr Spence has called on behalf of the arts community for a full inquiry by the Legislative Council into actions of the Board.

Diana Laidlaw announced yesterday her support for the ADT Boards' decision.

Originally Ms Tankard wanted a 12 month contract with the ADT, but the Board insisted she sign for three years.

Sources said this was because Government funding is allocated tri-annually and Meryl Tankard's role as artistic director would make it easier to ensure the ADT received the maximum finances.

The Board had said the lack of regional touring by the company was the key area of conflict with Ms Tankard, but Mr Spence said Ms Tankard was willing to perform in country areas.

Mr Spence said originally the Board had wanted Ms Tankard to go as soon as possible, but since the controversy broke have offered a lesser contract finishing in March 1999.

He said that Ms Tankard could not afford the on-going legal costs and had no choice but to accept the offer.

"She is so overwhelmed by the unfairness and injustice of it all," Mr Spence said.

Last night, Ms Tankard said she would leave South Australia, possibly for overseas.

"I don't really have the feeling I want to stay in Australia after the way I've been treated," Ms Tankard said.
