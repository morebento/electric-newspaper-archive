Wednesday July 22, 1998

Hundreds more bodies found in PNG

Burials gave way to gasoline-fueled cremations as rescue workers searching for survivors of a devastating tsunami are continuing to find corpses decaying in the tropical heat.

Crocodiles, dogs and pigs are feasting on the cadavers, prompting officials to send out hunting parties to shoot the scavengers.

With at least 1,200 dead and 6,000 missing after 10 metre-high walls of water hit the coast, fear of disease is quickly taking precedence over continued rescue efforts.

Prime Minister Bill Skate says he is considering evacuating people from the area, saying there is little hope of finding the missing alive.

A boat carrying the Prime Minister passed more than a dozen bloated bodies caught in the floating debris of houses or impaled on tough mangrove roots.

Some 700 bodies have been buried or burned, and Mr Skate estimates 500 more are floating in Sissano lagoon and the sea, festering in the heat.

West Sepik provincial governor John Tekwi expects the death toll to rise to more than 3,000.

Three waves, or tsunami, generated by a magnitude-7 undersea earthquake devastated some 12 kilometres of the north coast and destroyed at least three villages.

So far, only 2,527 people have been found alive out of the 8,000 to 10,000 believed to have lived in these remote coastal fishing and subsistence farming villages.

In one burial along the beach, soldiers, missionaries, aid workers and the healthier surviving villagers used spades to dig a shallow grave, then put on surgical gloves to wrap the corpse in black plastic.

There was no time for ceremony. The team quickly covered the body and stamped down with their feet. Then it moved on to the next grave, the next victim.

Some of the bodies were festering so much they were doused in gasoline and cremated on the spot.

The thousands of people displaced by the waves have been warned to stay away from the low-lying beach.

Most of the dead and many of the missing were children, victims of furious waters that took seconds to wreak its devastation.

Mr Skate said he would meet with emergency relief officials to decide whether to evacuate residents.

"We have to look at the environment, because it really could be infested. The water is contaminated and it is very, very unhealthy," he said.

Another problem has been the scavengers.

Along the banks of the mangrove swamp on the fringe of the lagoon, an aid worker stood at the bow of an aluminum boat and raised his rifle to his shoulder.

With one clean shot, he felled a dog gnawing on a barely recognizable human corpse.

The remoteness of the region hampered emergency officials from Australia, Papua New Guinea and New Zealand, who are coordinating a rescue operation.

Medical teams set up a tent hospital and cared for victims sent by three overcrowded hospitals. Many survivors underwent amputations because bacteria-filled coral sand had infected wounds, causing gangrene.

In Geneva, the International Red Cross said it was sending relief goods and appealed for $626,000 to provide emergency aid to survivors.

For survivors who lost everything -- children, homes, friends and livelihood -- the wounds were internal.

"For three nights I was crying for my lost wife and three daughters. I am still sad," said Fabian Tombre of Arop, a village that was totally destroyed.

But Tombre, whose son survived, said the living would rebuild the ravaged town.

"The people will go back, but to a better place," he said. "We will build new homes away from the sea."
