The Government has presented three separate arguments to support its case for the sale of ETSA and Optima. The Premier, in his ministerial statement to Parliament on February 17, indicated that the threat of losing competition payments and a potential market risk of ‘State Bank proportions' had compelled the decision to sell.

Additionally, the Government argued that a net benefit would accrue to State Treasury from sale of the electricity utilities (ie that given an adequate sale price the interest saved on debt repayments would be greater than dividends returned to general revenue by ETSA and Optima). In support of this contention the Government produced the Sheridan Report, authored by former Auditor-General, Tom Sheridan, analysing the relationship between debt, dividends and interest savings.

The three rationales for sale will be dealt with in turn. 

The Potential Loss of Competition Payments

Successful implementation of the national competition reforms will entitle the State Government to Commonwealth competition payments of $332 million and current financial assistance grants of $690 million over the next decade.

Announcing the Government's intention to sell ETSA and Optima the Premier claimed that :

 "to keep power in State hands in the face of the national electricity market and  Federal competition policy ... South Australia stood to lose more that $1  billion in competition payments." (Hansard, February 17, 1998)

Our investigation has proved this assertion is baseless. The privatisation of ETSA and Optima are not pre-requisites to qualify for competition payments. The issue of ownership is irrelevant. Further disaggregation of the industry may be necessary to ensure compliance with COAG competition principles, privatisation is not. This has been confirmed by both Graeme Sammuels of the NCC and Alan Asher of the ACCC. It is also consistent with the Industry Commission review of ETSA in 1996 and the original COAG Agreement.

 "This agreement is neutral with respect to the nature and form of ownership of  business enterprises. It is not intended to promote public or private ownership."   (COAG Agreement, 1995)
 
There is no case for privatisation on the grounds of potential loss of competition payments. 

Market Risks
In his ministerial statement the Premier also claimed the Auditor-General's Report (1997) alerted the Government to potentially massive market risks for both ETSA and Optima as a result of the introduction of the National Electricity Market (NEM).

 "However, the Auditor-General sees that joining as owners, as the  shareholders of Optima and ETSA, leaves us exposed to massive risk; in  other words, another potential disaster of almost State Bank dimensions if  operators make the wrong decisions when they are compelled to act  entrepreneurially" (Hansard, February 17, 1998)
and
 

"We could, indeed, suffer a financial disaster of State Bank proportions by  clinging to our previous policy direction in retaining ownership of ETSA and  Optima." (Hansard, February 17, 1998)

Whilst the Auditor-General's Report does acknowledge the existence of shareholder risk for the State Government due to the creation of the NEM it in no way substantiates the Premier's assertions.

 "The acceptance of corporate commercial risk by Governments is  unremarkable and a necessary consequence of government owned enterprises  operating in competitive environments." (Auditor-General's Report 1997, A.3 - 25)

Furthermore the Auditor-General states :
 "There are many opportunities available to both the South Australian  Government, the ETSA Corporations and Optima to manage the risks  associated  with the entry of South Australia into the NEM." (Auditor-General's Report 1997,  A.3-40)

The Auditor-General's Report merely encourages prudent risk management. 


The Nature of the Risks in the NEM
It is important to understand the structure of South Australia's Electricity Supply Industry (ESI) and how the market risks are qualitatively different for each component of the industry. ETSA is divided into three entities. ETSA Power Corporation is a retailer/distributor purchasing in the wholesale electricity market and the owner/operator of the low voltage distribution system.

ETSA Transmission Corporation is the owner/operator of the high voltage transmission network. ETSA Corporation is the holding authority. Optima Energy is the generating arm of the industry and is completely separate from ETSA. 


 

Market Risks to  ETSA's Poles and Wires Businesses
ETSA's transmission and distribution (poles and wires) businesses face no market risk. They are  natural monopolies. There will not be another transmission and distribution system set up in competition with the current infrastructure, a point acknowledged in the Sheridan Report:

 "While ETSA's wires businesses (high voltage transmission and low voltage  distribution) will be unaffected by competition in their own markets because of their  infrastructure's natural monopoly characteristics, they will experience indirect risks  to future dividends ..."

This is an fundamental flaw in the Government's market risk argument. The transmission and distribution network are the vast bulk of ETSA's capital value. There will be no trading losses in this area of the business.

 There is no case for sale of these assets on the grounds of market risk.  


Regulatory Risk to Poles and  Wires Income
The risk to future dividends from transmission and distribution is a regulatory risk. ie that the regulator may reduce the income ETSA currently receives from its poles and wires business. The amount of revenue ETSA receives will depend upon the valuation of the poles and wires business and rate of return deemed appropriate by national and state regulators.

Despite a measure of uncertainty regarding these calculations transmission and distribution will remain profitable sources of steady income in either public or private ownership. In 1997-98 ETSA returned $224 million to the Government. The 1998-99 State Budget estimates a return of $162 million from ETSA.


ETSA Retail Risks
ETSA Power will face genuine market competition in its retail business. It is inevitable that ETSA's retail business will lose a portion of the contestable customer market to other retailers. Contestable customers are large users of electricity and will be entitled to purchase electricity directly from the Pool. Households and small businesses will be franchise customers dealing with retailers of electricity.

It is important to recognise that retailing of electricity is a small component of ETSA overall profitability. The bulk of ETSA income is from transmission and distribution. Where the customer chooses an alternative retailer
 
"the customer still has to pay ETSA, via their new retailer, for using our poles and wires. Effectively we are only out of pocket for the very small margin we would have made on the energy." (Watt's Up? Official ETSA Publication. Issue 19 May 1998)

Consequently the reduction of ETSA's overall profitability will be minimal as a result of the loss of retail market share. 

ETSA Pool Price Exposure
There is the possibility of ETSA's retail business suffering trading losses due to NEM Pool price exposure. This would occur if ETSA was to underestimate its electricity requirements and be forced to purchase extra electricity from the pool at a time of high demand and therefore increases price. However the prospectus for United Energy, a Victorian-based distributor and retailer of comparable size to ETSA, indicates this risk is manageable.

 "If during a very hot summer the Company experienced three days of  significant  Pool price exposure where the demand exceeded the nominal contract level by  25%, and which was coincident with half hourly Pool prices that were five times  the 1996/97 average, operating profit could be reduced by approximately $1  million." (United Energy Prospectus, p66)

This illustrates the alarmist rhetoric of the Premier's market risks. 


ETSA Market Risks Conclusion
In summary the Government has greatly exaggerated the market risks facing ETSA in an attempt to justify sale of the utility. Both the Auditor-General and ETSA management have stated to the Economic & Finanace Committee that the risk is manageable. The Democrats also believe such risks as exist can be effectively managed and hence do not constitute a valid reason for sale.


Market Risks to Optima Energy
Optima will be subject to competitive forces as a result of the NEM. When the NEM begins Optima will be bidding into the Pool along with the New South Wales and Victorian generators. Optima will also be competing with interstate generators for contracts with contestable customers.

The combination of higher input costs and older generating plant puts Optima at a cost disadvantage to generators in the eastern states. Furthermore an excess of generating capacity in eastern Australia has resulted in generators charging artificially low prices. Consequently Optima could be under pressure competing head to head with generators on the eastern seaboard.

Despite this cost disadvantage Optima remains a viable entity due to the nature of the NEM. Optima's exposure to market forces is determined by the capacity of the Heywood interconnect. At present about 35% of South Australia's electricity requirements is generated in Victoria and transmitted via the interconnect. Once the Heywood interconnect is full South Australia will disconnect from the NEM and revert to being a regional market. Due to lower prices for base load generation in Victoria the interconnect is virtually always full, and this will continue with the advent of the NEM.

In the disconnected regional South Australian market Optima will effectively exercise monopoly control. By strategic bidding Optima can set the regional Pool price between 60% and 96% of the time. (Appendix 1 & 2) These graphs take into account the CUBE generator at Osborne and the Riverlink interconnect. As a result of the scrapping of Riverlink, Optima's market control is increased.


Optima Market Risks Conclusion
Optima in its current configuration, by virtue of its regional market control, faces minimal market risks. Indeed in its present form regulation may be necessary to ensure it doesn't exploit market control. Again on the evidence available the Government's argument for sale of Optima is unconvincing.

The Democrats acknowledge the circumstances surrounding Optima may change. It has been suggested that Optima should be split into two or three separate entities. Optima generates a mere 6% of the total of electricity in the three State market and is one of the smallest generators operating.(Appendix 3). Industry Commission findings state:

 "The Commission's analysis led it to conclude that division was unlikely to  reduce market power to any practical degree. Division could lead to loss of  economies of scale and scope. Such losses would disadvantage South Australian  generators in the national market, compared to much larger generators in New  South Wales and Victoria." (Research Report into the Electricity Industry in South  Australia, Industry Commission, March 1996,  p24)

Under these circumstances Optima should be maintained in its current structure and other private companies encouraged to set up gas fired generation capacity in South Australia. This would bring investment and jobs into the local economy and enhance the regional market in generation.

If Optima is to be separated it's possible that dividends would suffer. Conversely its potential sale price would be reduced undermining the Government's debt reduction arguments.

The Economic and Finance Committee was told by the management of Optima it is confident of its ability to compete in the NEM. The Democrats share that confidence. 

Dividends Versus Debt Reduction
The Sheridan Report
The State Government backed the third tier of its privatisation argument with the release of the Sheridan Report. Sheridan argues the sale of ETSA and Optima, depending on the sale price secured, offers budgetary savings to Treasury.

Sheridan makes a number of assumptions regarding future returns from ETSA and Optima to Treasury. He concludes that Treasury's forward estimate of $215 million per annum is unsustainably high and revises that figure downwards to $150 per annum. Sheridan then compares the reductions in interest payments on State debt that would flow from various sale prices with the loss of the $150 million in dividends.

He concludes that at current Government interest rates (6%) a sale price of $3.5 billion would be the budgetary breakeven point. A figure of $4 billion would return a net benefit to the State of $29 million per annum and $5 billion would improve the budgetary position by $89 million per annum. With an increase in interest rates even greater savings would be made.

The Quiggin and Spoehr Report
The Sheridan Report has been vigorously disputed by Professor John Quiggin of James Cook University and John Spoehr of Adelaide University in a report commissioned by the UTLC, CEPU, ASU and PSA.

Quiggin and Spoehr claim the Sheridan Report is defective because it seriously under-estimates the returns ETSA and Optima provide to the State. They argue Sheridan fails to take into account:

  • The statutory sales level (which has just been eliminated but is relevant to Sheridan's calculation);
  • retained earnings; and
  • the value of interest paid by ETSA and Optima to SAFA.
When the above factors are included Quiggin and Spoehr conclude that in the event of real revenue growth of 1 per cent per year a $7 billion sale price will be necessary to compensate for the loss of income from ETSA and Optima.

The Sheridan Report indicates a sale price of between $4 and $6 billion is the most likely outcome. Consequently the discrepancy between the Sheridan Report and Quiggin & Spoehr is crucial to the analysis of the debt v dividends argument.

We sought a detailed response from the Treasurer to the Quiggin and Spoehr analysis. The Treasurer replied that the Quiggin & Spoehr Report is "seriously flawed" and criticised its methodology and a number of its estimates.

However the Treasurer's response does not rebut the central contention of Quiggin & Spoehr ie that the Sheridan Report failed to take into account retained earnings when it calculated what sale price would be required for a net benefit to the State.

There is no doubt that retained earnings need to be calculated in assessing the value of a government enterprise and the benefits of privatisation. This has been confirmed by the Commonwealth Office of Asset Sales and financial commentators.

"The Budget does not take account of retained earnings and changes in Telstra's net  worth. When these are included in the figuring, the gains to the Federal  Budget from the sale of Telstra are shown to be largely illusory. Which of  course simply underlines the basic point that privatisation should occur when it  has  economic benefits. It should not be pursued for budgetary reasons."  (Alan  Mitchell, Australian Financial Review, 3 June 1998)

The failure of the Sheridan Report to include retained earnings greatly diminishes its value in the context of the debate.

The Treasurer also criticises Quiggin & Spoehr for overestimating the earnings of ETSA and Optima. The Treasurer claims that earnings (before interest and tax)  in 1998-99 are estimated by the industry to be $337 million. Even at that figure a sale price of $5 billion and a 6% nominal interest rate yielding $300 million would result in a $37 million shortfall of earnings over interest savings in the first year. Furthermore on these figure a sale price of $6 billion would result in losses accruing from 2003 onwards.

Independent economic analysis obtained by the Democrats concluded the Quiggin & Spoehr Report was sounder in methodology and analysis than the Sheridan Report.

The evidence indicates that a sale price in excess of $6 billion would be necessary to achieve a short term net gain for the State.


A Probable Sale Price for ETSA and Optima.
The Government has consistently refused to indicate what price it expects to receive for our ESI. Most commentators have estimated a likely price for South Australia's ESI of between $4-6 billion. A number of factors indicate an eventual price closer to the lower end of those estimations.

Victoria sold its ESI for approximately $23 billion. Victoria has more than three times the population of South Australia, four times the generating capacity and significant economies of scale for generation, distribution and transmission. It is also generally accepted that Victoria received a premium for its ESI.

The recent decision by the Victorian Regulator-General to reduce the pre-tax rate of return on regulated assets in that State from 10.9 per cent to 7 indicates a 35% reduction in return on regulated assets (poles & wires). This decision was made in conjunction with the ACCC.

The  Financial Review estimates this decision, if implemented in South Australia, would lead to a potential $1.4 billion write down in the value of ETSA. (Chanticleer, Australian Financial Review, 2 June 1998)

 Another consideration is the impact of the leasing arrangement entered into by ETSA Transmission and Edison Capital. Advice from Ernst & Young indicates
 "The existence of a lease increases uncertainty surrounding timing and availability  of tax deductions, and raises legal issues relating to ownership and control, as well  as transfer and termination issues. These issues are likely to result in a discount to  purchase price."

A discount to the sale price of 8-12% is considered likely as a result of the Edison Capital lease.

There is also a question as to the impact of EDS information technology contract upon the price of ETSA and Optima Energy. The Democrats have been informed that approximately 25% of the EDS contract is associated with the electricity utilities. This represents a substantial encumbrance on any future owner(s) of ETSA and Optima Energy and consequently would reduce the sale price.

We sought information from the Treasurer, on the 27th of May, regarding the cost implications. None has been forthcoming.
The Democrats are also aware of potential problems stemming from the use of easements by ETSA. Legal advice suggests that as a public corporation ETSA is not liable to pay compensation but if the corporation is privatised compensation will need to paid, hence reducing the sale price commensurately.

The above considerations indicate a price of between $4-5 billion is the most optimistic assessment that can be rationally sustained. As a consequence the sale of ETSA and Optima would lead to a medium and long term net loss to the State.


State Debt:
The Democrats sought from the Government an indication of how much debt could be retired if the Government was to receive $1 billion, $2 billion, $3 billion etc. The Democrats are aware that the structure of South Australia's debt portfolio is such that penalties would be incurred due to early retirement of a significant component of the debt. The Government failed to provided the information requested.

The Treasurer did reply :
 "I reject the claim ... that it might cost an extra $1-2 billion for early repayment of  our State debt."
This begs the question.

Whilst the Democrats support the continued reduction of debt it should be noted that State debt as a ratio of Gross State Product is at its second lowest level in the past 30 years.(Appendix 4)

This suggests the Government has an ideological obsession with the level of debt rather than a coherent strategy to reduce debt and maintain the term asset base of South Australia.


Dividends Versus Debt Reduction Conclusion:
The Government has failed to mount a convincing argument regarding the budgetary benefits of the sale of Optima and ETSA. The evidence suggests that the best case scenario is a short term net gain followed by substantial medium and long term losses.

Other Considerations
Costs to Ordinary Consumers:
Another factor mitigating against sale is the likely cost for households and small business. A study of Britain's electricity privatisation, published by the World Bank,  found UK consumers lost billions of pounds as a result of privatisation. The study found:

 "Power purchasers seem to be paying higher prices than they would have  under continued public ownership" due to the fact "higher company margins  offset lower costs"  (Public Policy for the Private Sector: World Bank Note 124  September 1997)

Furthermore whatever price is paid for the electricity utilities has to be recouped from electricity consumers. In effect South Australian's will be asked to re-purchase what they already own. The higher the price the more that will have to be returned. Electricity consumers remain a captive market.

The only freedom of choice for the vast majority of consumers will be in choosing a retailer, with limited potential for savings.

Accountability:
In a privatised future if problems emerge, as is inevitable with electricity supply, how will the ordinary consumer's rights be safeguarded? Where will the buck stop?

Currently if ETSA is tardy about repairing a fault the responsible Minister will feel the heat of public pressure. The Minister will remind ETSA's board and management of its obligations to the South Australian public. Where will the Minister ring under a privatised ETSA? Dallas? London? Tokyo?

Nor will ordinary consumers be able to exert any influence. They can't vote with their feet and move to a new transmission or distribution company. These are monopolies, not subject to market pressures. Monopoly essential services should be in public hands, susceptible to public pressures.


Reliability of Supply:
It should be noted that a large proportion of the capital value of SA's ESI is tied up in the poles and wires. Given that purchase would involve a substantial capital outlay and there will be a regulatory cap on the profits to be made, future private owners searching for increased profit may look to operational efficiencies. Reducing maintenance levels and capital investment offers the possibility of achieving such efficiencies.

Auckland's recent power failure, stemming from inadequate maintenance and capital investment, illustrates the dangers of this approach. Mercury Energy decided to invest its money in a corporate takeover rather than replacing ageing power cables. Mercury Energy whilst set up as a consumer trust was controlled by business appointees and operated as a commercial enterprise. ETSA as a public corporation has been far more prudent regarding investment and maintenance. Maintaining it in public ownership will minimise the possibility of an Auckland style blackout.


SA Relegated to a Branch Office:
The sale of ETSA and Optima will inevitably result in the loss of another two head offices from Adelaide. The relentless shift of head offices interstate and overseas has damaged the local economy and diminished employment opportunities in Adelaide. It would be inappropriate for the Government to continue this trend.


 Regional South Australia:
Regional consumers run the risk of being cut adrift by a privatised ESI. Private companies will not pick up the high cost of transmitting and distributing electricity to remote areas. It will be left to the Government to fund those subsidies, a Government that has just reduced its long-term revenue stream. Furthermore Governments change or change their minds.

Conclusion:
The Democrats are a party committed to maintaining a mixed economy and keeping essential services in Government hands. Those policies were central to our campaign at the recent State election. Nor do we recoil from being a party of review.

The Premier claimed "a disaster of State Bank proportions was looming if ETSA and Optima remained in public hands." The Premier also claimed this recently acquired information was the reason for the Government's back-flip.

It would have been irresponsible in the extreme to simply ignore this possibility and hold the Government to their election pledge whilst fulfilling ours. Consequently the Democrats undertook to thoroughly investigate the Government's claims. This involved calling for and receiving dozens of public submissions and an extensive round of meetings with industry specialists.

On the day the Premier made his announcement the Government was put on notice. It would have to present convincing economic arguments to gain Democrat support for the sale. After researching each of the Government's arguments we have concluded we cannot support the Government's decision to sell ETSA. Simply the evidence in favour of sale is slight, the evidence for retention strong.

The Government has failed to prove its case.
