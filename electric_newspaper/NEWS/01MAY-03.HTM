Mr Ingerson: positive he is in the negative about knowing earlier


ETSA chairman Mike Janes commissioned a secret report.


The Economic and Finance Committee. Ask not what the Government knew, but when it knew it.
Friday May 1, 1998

Like lambs to the slaughter
by Hendrik Gout

This morning, the office of South Australian deputy Premier Graham Ingerson issued a media statement. People bitten by rattlesnakes may soon be saved by anti-venoms produced with the help of SA sheep, Mr Ingerson said.

To make this startling announcement, Mr Ingerson had to travel to Wales. There, he visited the Welsh operations of a company called Therapeutic Antibodies, which happens to run 4000-odd sheep in South Australia, used to produce the trial anti-venom.

While Mr Ingerson is leading us to a rattlesnake-led recovery, the Opposition is sharpening its fangs.

Labor Leader Mike Rann wants Mr Ingerson to be recalled from his obviously vital Welsh wanderings, to answer questions about whether the Deputy Premier misled Parliament.

This week, before Parliament's Economic and Finance Committee, the chairman of the State's Electricity Trust revealed the Trust had last year hired an investment house to advise it on options for privatisation.

This happened early in 1997, ETSA chairman Mike Janes told the committee. The Premier, Mr Olsen, was told about the privatisation study in May, and he flew to Sydney to have a bit of a chat with Mr Janes and the investment house Schroders.

They used slides, rather than a written report, for the Schroders' presentation. Mr Janes specifically didn't want a written report. Too insecure, of course, Mr Janes told the committee. He's right. Reports can leak. Slides have a better image in the secret world of ETSA privatisation.

So we have another picture. The Premier, Mr Olsen, watching a presentation by ETSA on a plan which is clearly against Government policy - the Government, of course, was claiming right through to the election that it had no plans to sell ETSA.

Did Mr Olsen jump to his feet and tell Mr Janes to stop wasting money (Schroders was paid $50,000 for the slide-show) and reprimand ETSA for exploring privatisation against Government policy? No, he did not.

"The Premier didn't give me any idea at all in terms of the Government's view," Mr Janes said later outside the committee.

"He simply listened to what we had to say and was non-committal."

This is seemingly at odds with the version pedalled by the Premier's media secretary, Vicki Thomson, after the Schroders' meeting became public.

The Premier "told the meeting ETSA was not for sale," Ms Thomson claimed on Wednesday.

This is an interesting inconsistency.

The Opposition, of course, is using the committee not to investigate the pros and cons of selling ETSA, but to embarrass the Government.

Shadow Treasurer Kevin Foley could scarcely contain his glee.

"John Olsen's own credibility has taken a massive pounding," Mr Foley said

"We've learnt that details of the massive financial write-down of ETSA were first known in a Cabinet document in December ‘96 when the issue was canvassed, in a February Board meeting, in June, July, and August Board meetings. We now find that a copy of the draft annual report was sent to the Minister in August, given that he said that he hadn't seen it till December, and of course we now hear the startling revelation that Joan Hall, his Parliamentary secretary, was fully aware of all those documents. "

Usually, politicians get into trouble for claiming to know more than they do. This is a rare example of a Government which is in trouble for knowing more than it claims.

Which brings us back to Wales, where Mr Ingerson is talking about rattlesnakes.

The anti-venoms would mean greater jobs and significant plant and equipment investment in South Australia, the Deputy Premier said in Wales this morning.

More jobs for South Australia? Now when do you suppose the Deputy Premier knew that?
