Wednesday May 6, 1998

Videos give a healthily Aboriginal perspective

They weren't mentioned at the Oscars, but two videos produced at Flinders recently have won praise where it counts.

Produced by Flinders University screen studies lecturer Martin Mhando and a group of Honours students, the videos have been well received by both the national reference group which provided input during their creation and the Committee of Deans of Australasian Medical Schools which will now decide how they will be used.

The videos form the centrepiece of a new package of cultural awareness training materials which will be incorporated in the curricula at all 10 Australian medical schools.

The aim is to give prospective doctors a greater understanding of both the key medical issues relevant to Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander people and the broader social, historical and political issues which affect their health and well-being.

The Committee sought tenders for the project in late 1996 after receiving funding of $100,000 from the Aboriginal Reconciliation Branch of the federal Office of Indigenous Affairs.

The successful South Australian bid was driven out of the School of Medicine at Flinders and based on a collaboration between Flinders and Adelaide universities and the involvement of Flinders' Yunggorendi First Nations Centre for Higher Education and Research.

"We got what we hoped for and more," said Associate Professor David Prideaux, the Head of the Office of Education in the School of Medicine. "By putting the day-to-day management and development in the hands of Yunggorendi we have a package which puts a truly Aboriginal perspective on Aboriginal health."

The videos were shot largely during two medical conferences held at the University of Newcastle last year and include input from Aboriginal medical services and community health services and Aboriginal doctors and medical students.

They will be used to run a series of five workshops with the themes of mental health, the reflective doctor, primary health, fighting racism, and cultural sensitivity.

"We wanted to provide a national perspective on the key issues and took what you could call an anti-racist approach which looks honestly at the history of Aboriginal/European relations and the how health issues are intertwined with it," Associate Professor Prideaux said.

"And we used a wide definition of health which looked at more than just physical health and disease. The emphasis was also on understanding emotional, social and spiritual factors which contribute to an individual's well-being."

The project was managed by Ms Jenny Baker, then a Senior Lecturer in both the School of Medicine and Yunggorendi, and Yunggorendi Lecturer Mr Daryle Rigney. Their other medical school colleagues were Dr Bronnie Veale from Flinders and Ms Helen Mullins from the University of Adelaide.
