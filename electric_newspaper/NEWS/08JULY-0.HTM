Wednesday July 8, 1998

The jester is dead, long live the prince
by Hendrik Gout and Krista Eleftheriou

South Australia's new deputy Premier, Rob Kerin, was sworn at Government House yesterday afternoon, giving Premier John Olsen a valuable breathing space to consolidate his faltering leadership and head off Party discontent with his style and performance.

Mr Kerin's first job as deputy Premier was reassuring the public of John Olsen's leadership position.

"There has certainly been no leadership challenge this morning," Mr Kerin said. "What we really need in the Party is unity."

Mr Kerin indicated that it wasn't simply the public which had to be convinced of Mr Olsen's capacity to lead the Liberals, it was the Parliamentary Liberal Party as well.

He acknowledged there were a number of MP's who still wanted Mr Olsen to quit, and that he would be seeking to discuss the leadership with them.

"I would rather members of Parliament come to me or the Premier (with a problem) rather than the media," Mr Kerin said.

Mr Kerin also knows of other challenges - those which await the Government as it tries to pass its unpopular legislation to privatise the State's electricity utilities.

"The Government's got some very important Bills ahead of us. We've got to try and get those Bills through the House." Mr Kerin said.

But former Premier Dean Brown failed to rule out a leadership challenge in the future.

Some Liberals were last night suggesting that the Premier might try to offer Brown supporters some sort of sweetener to win them over. Several MP's discussed the possible outcome of the Privileges Committee (see separate story) and the likelihood of one of the Brown group being offered a junior Ministry if Mr Ingerson was forced to resign his portfolios.

Yesterday's Party room vote took less than half an hour. Rob Kerin was the only nominee for the deputy's job, the other aspirants realising that they lacked the support to mount a successful challenge.

After the election, Upper House MP's were invited in for the joint meeting (in South Australia, only members of the House of Assembly are entitled to vote for the leadership positions). MP's contacted by The Electric Newspaper said later they hoped there would now be some period of stability in Government, and that Mr Kerin was ideally placed to mediate between the Government's warring camps.

Shortly after noon, Mr Kerin, accompanied by the Treasurer Rob Lucas, Attorney-General Trevor Griffin, Premier John Olsen, and Mr Olsen's media secretary and chief of staff, walked across King William Street to meet the Governor and receive his commission.

After a short ceremony, Mr Olsen and Mr Kerin held a joint news conference - and then it was back to Parliament for Question Time and the Government's legislative program.

For John Olsen, it had been a successful morning. His future is still not assured, but as they walked back to the House, the Premier almost had a spring in his step.
