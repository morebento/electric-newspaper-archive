Friday March 27, 1998

Choo choo boo hoo

Want to go to Melbourne by train? Do it now. Next year, the Overland may be over.

Great Southern Rail, the private consortium which includes Serco - also a partner in the company which bought Adelaide airport - took over Australia National Rail last August.

G.S.R says it may be required to close down the Overland rail service between Adelaide and Melbourne unless the Federal Government spends money upgrading and servicing the track.

"Our bid was predicated on expanding services," Chief Executive John Finnin said yesterday.

Mr Finnin said that G.S.R. has no intention of backing away from its original commitment to keep the Overland going for at least two years. However, he warned that the Overland "is a product that is haemorrhaging" and unless money was spent immediately it could not provide a service that was "safe, reliable and efficient".

"We wish to expand services, we wish to expend capital expenditure on the train but in order to do that we need realistic and commercially viable track access," Mr Finnin said.

The situation illustrates the difficulties when a private company buys into a service which has much of its infrastructure and track remaining the responsibility of the Federal and State Governments.

G.S.R. welcomed a promise by Federal Transport Minister Mark Vaile to spend $ 250 million over four years, predominantly on track infrastructure. However, Mr Finnin says the money needs to be spent as soon as possible because G.S.R. is currently subsidising a "loss making service".

'Rail 2000' lobby group spokesperson Mark Carter sympathises with the difficult situation in which G.S.R is placed, saying the need for track upgrading has been highlighted for several years and is indicative of the Federal Government's approach to rail.

"The Government has not been serious about rail for decades. They would rather put $200 million into new roads than railway track," Mr Carter said.

In Parliament yesterday, Premier John Olsen responded to an Opposition question on the matter, stating that for G.S.R. to close down the Overland service would be to "default" on an agreement with the State and Federal governments.

"Of course G.S.R. must be able to reach commercially realistic track access charges and to date they have not been able to negotiate that position with Victoria. "That is not the circumstances that apply here in South Australia. So we look forward to G.S.R. honouring the agreement on which it purchased the facility," Mr Olsen said.

The Australian Rail Union's Rex Phillips believes that for the Overland to survive, track infrastructure on the Victorian side is a necessity.

"The Victorian Government did not put up a strong case for investment as the South Australian Government did of the day, and that's caused the slow running times," Mr Phillips said.

Yet Rex Phillips says that the train is viable and questions whether G.S.R. has allowed the Overland "to suffer" by not applying the same aggressive marketing initiatives, such as accessing the tourist market, as the company proposed it would.
