Tuesday March 31, 1998

The joint was packed Ralph Kogler and Natalie Crook Natalie Gust and Fil Lloyd John Bevan and Cathy Hill Angeliki Valassis and Lilla Moore Ron Hombsch, Ron Reading, Patricia Heard and Dawn Howe. A combined 140 years of service
A party to end all parties

The beer was free, the guests were invited, and the name of John Martins marched into history.

About 400 people crowded into the Clyde Hotel at Parkside, for what could have been a wake - but which turned out to be a final party for the century-old retailer.

John Martins' owner, David Jones, permanently closed the doors to the department store this month. For the past week staff have been clearing the store and preparing for a giant auction of the shop fittings.

And as of last night, they were sacked.

So the Clyde's owner, Richard Hawyard, let the beer flow and the sparkling wine pop. His family owned John Martin's for 105 years, before it was aquired and then shut down by David Jones.

"Yep, I was a manager," said Ralph Kogler, now unemployed. "I managed the second floor. That's toys, children's wear, white goods. Oh, and Santa's Cave."

"I got engaged on Friday," said Natalie Crook, showing Mr Kogler a lump of sparkling pure carbon on her finger. "I don't know where I'll get a job, though."

"Don't believe what you might read in The Advertiser. They say 90 per cent of the staff have got a job with David Jones. I know that's not true. There are 585 staff who are not going to David Jones. They're unemployed, as of now," she said.

Over near the bar, Natalie Gast is talking with Fil Lloyd.

"Hopefully I’ll be able to get a job," she tells Fil. "I'm looking."

Natalie was a supervisor in men's wear. She was with John Martins for 18 years.

Is she worried about her future?

"Well, you know... Sure. There's not that many jobs out there."

John Bevan was in manchester for 37 years. "No, 37 and a half years," he corrects himself. "I haven't decided what I'll do. I might just retire."

"I'd like to keep working," says his former colleague, Cathy Hill. "I'd really like to keep working."

"We used to get everybody in there," says Angeliki Valassis. "Pauline Hanson, Tony Modra, Stefan Edberg when the tennis tournament was one, and that bloke who commentates the Grand Prix. They all came. Everybody came to Johnnies.

"And we used to send accounts to London, to the States, and all over Australia," Ms Valassis said.

There's a pride here. Normally people who lose their job feel a sense of shame, of defeat. At the Clyde, there's camaraderie. Everyone is in the same boat, even if it's cast adrift.

"Did you know," says Ms Valassis, "that David Jones has around 30 stores nationally. And that John Martins contributed 25 per cent of the gross turnover. And that’s with stores just in South Australia."

"They’ve got it wrong," her companion, Lilla Moore, says. "There's no way they (David Jones) should go up-market. South Australia doesn't have the population to support it. It's ok in places like Sydney or Melbourne, but here most people are on average wages, and they've got average jobs. They can't all afford to shop at David Jones."

Store manager Kevin may makes a little speech. He talks about what a great team he had. He mentions sales performance figures and other retail jargon. The crowd applauds long and loud. The bar staff pour more drinks.

"Take this," said a grey-haired man. "Take a photo of the four of us. There's 140 years of experience standing right here, just with the four of us."

Yes, John Martins has gone. So has a team, carefully built up. "We're the best," says Ron Hombsch. "The best there is."

The best there was, Ron. The best there was.
