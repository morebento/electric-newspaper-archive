12.27pm, Thursday March 5, 1998

Misleading from the front
by Hendrik Gout

Liberal Party advertising in the last election was misleading - and that's official.

The Court of Disputed Returns ruled a few minutes ago that the Liberal Party had breached the Electoral Act with at least one newspaper advertisment, and that prosecution might be appropriate.

Justice Graham Prior also criticised the Liberal Party's behaviour during the case, which was brought by a failed independent candidate in the seat of Davenport in suburban Adelaide.

The candidate, Jack King, objected to Liberal ads which claimed a vote for independents or the Australian Democrats was just a vote for Labor.

This afternoon, Justice Prior found that the Liberal Party's reponses during the case were "less than frank".

The judge did not order a by-election in the seat, ruling that while the ad was misleading it was part of a wider, lawful advertising campaign in the seat and did not affect the result.

The Australian democrats have now called on the SA Electoral Commission to take action against the Liberal Party under the Electoral Act.

"When a Supreme Court Judge says that prosecution might be appropriate, it's a fair indication that something's wrong," Democrats Leader Mike Elliott said.

"The Democrats have complained for years about electoral advertising but the Electoral Office has been reluctant to move."

Mr King also criticised the Electoral Commissioner for not acting on complaints.

The seat was won by Iain Evans. Mr Evans is now Police Minister.

The Liberal Party is overjoyed with the result because there will be no by-election. The Government already relies on the support on three Independents. A loss in Davenport in a by-election - which would certainly have become a poll on the unpopular ETSA sale - would have been a crippling and possibly fatal blow to John Olsen's Premiership.

The Judge's adverse comments about the Liberal's conduct during the court case are just a minor irritation, compared with the pain of a by-election.

State director David Pigott would not comment on this aspect of the judge's finding.

Mr King has ruled out taking action against those responsible for the misleading advertising because of the further expense.

"At least at the next election the Liberals will think twice before trying to mislead the public," Mr King said.

The Liberal Party is now thinking twice.

Mr Pigott said the Liberal Government should make it more expensive to lodge a complaint. It already costs $200.
