Monday March 23, 1998

Paring down to size
by Gary Quill in Canberra

Bring more allegations against Federal Minister and Prime Ministerial friend Senator Parer is like bringing coal to Newcastle. He doesn't need more trouble.

But he'll get it today, with the Labor Opposition saying it will bring more evidence forward today, which will further implicate the Resources Minister in conflict-of-interest claims.

As well, the Australian Democrats will move in the Senate today to prevent the Minister abolishing the Energy Research and Development Corporation.

The Minister, who did not tell Parliament he held coal-mining shares worth about $2 million, wants the Corporation to close. It explores alternative energy sources for Australia.

The decision to abolish ERDC was announced in last year's Budget and is currently before the Senate as a regulation.

"It appears that this decision was taken by Cabinet at the urging of Senator Parer before his interests in the coal-mining industry were revealed," Democrats leader Senator Meg Lees said.

"How can a Government Minister contribute to a decision which effectively nobbles the development iof competition to an industry in which he has such a significant interest?" Senator Lees said.

Before its election, the Coalition gave a commitment to support the ERDC.

Meanwhile, Cabinet sources have indicated that the Resources Minister has not always seen eye-to-eye with the Environment Minister, South Australia's Senator Robert Hill.

Australia recently voted to increase national permitted greenhouse emissions at the Kyoto Summit.

This was done on the advice of ABARE which, according to the Commonwealth ombudsman, is heavily funded by the coal industry.

Senator Parer and Senator Hill have reportedly clashed over a White Paper which deals with sustainable energy.

On May 31, 1996, Senator Parer issued a media release in which he announced that he had invited energy industry producers and consumers to "participate in a Ministerial Round Table" which would work on the White Paper.

10 of the 11 seats in the Round Table then went to the fossil fuel industry.

The White Paper was to have been released at the end of 1996.

It has never been released.
