Tuesday March 31, 1998

Protestors are worried the beach will be destroyed. The special laws, gazetted at 10.00 am today, are read out by police. A protestor is escorted away. Bridgett Bannear. And within a half-hour the fence was erected.
Democracy in action, SA-style
by Hendrik Gout

Twenty years ago, former Tasmanian Premier Eric Reece stood almost 200 metres below the surface, in an underground hydro-electric power station in the State's rugged south-west, and turned a switch. Generators started turning for the first time as Reece's new hydro scheme completed the destruction of Lake Pedder.

Today, Tasmania is broke, its living standard the lowest in the country, and three out off every ten able-bodied people are out of work. Tasmania's infatuation with hydro power as an economic miracle is all too obviously over. The Gordon power station is a legacy to monumental government stupidity and cowardice.

Here in South Australia, another Government is tottering into a much smaller but equally nonsensical development - the West Beach boat harbour. If this scheme goes ahead - and it will - South Australians are going to have to pay half a million dollars a year, from now until the sun grows cold, to pay for a sand replenishment program.

That's a lot of schools, a lot of hospitals, a lot of buses and better roads.

The harbour at West Beach is being built because the State Government has handed to private developers a public boat ramp at Glenelg. There, the developers are building a marina so the boat ramp - used by average, middle-class families - is being subsumed to make way for expensive apartments and private yacht moorings.

Not everyone is wildly enthusiastic about this idea.

The Government is building a new public boat ramp and harbour at West Beach, a few kilometres north of Glenelg. The ramp will require a massive groyne, which will stop the natural northward drift of sand in the Gulf St. Vincent. It's to save the beaches further north that sand will have to be trucked around the groyne in perpetuity. If the replenishment program is ever stopped, the northern beaches will become low, rocky cliffs.

Which is why beachside residents are protesting. In a so-far unsuccessful campaign, they've formed a committee, formed a blockade, formed a community action group.

They have not stopped progress, but they've highlighted the absurdity of the boat harbour proposal. And they've embarrassed the Government.

Today, the Government had had enough.

Not long after 10 o'clock today, a cool-headed and not unfriendly chief inspector of the Government's crime-fighting force, the SA police, told the protesters blocking access to the construction site that they had 20 minutes to remove themselves.

"Under what authority?" asked an elderly woman.

And chief inspector Brenton Mart then read out long list of special regulations gazetted minutes earlier. No protesters, no media, no nothin' except construction workers could go past a line drawn in the West Beach sand. Even the Gulf is closed to ships, swimmers, and sailboards in the West Beach area.

The elderly woman was led away, shaking her head.

Twenty-seven minutes later, MacNamara Fencing Contractors had erected a two metre-tall fence across what was, until this morning, a public road.

"There are alternatives to the harbour design ," said spokesperson Bridget Bannear. "This doesn't have to be built this way. All we're asking is that the Government seriously consider the options."

Maybe, but at least 48 police, some mounted, some on motorbikes, some in paddy wagons, and some who clearly didn't want to be there, are helping the Government enforce democracy and make sure that built it will be built.

The Liberal member for the area, Steve Condous, had during the election campaign promised to lie down in front of the bulldozers. Today, he couldn't look a police Grey in the face. The honourable member was no-where to be seen.

Neither was the Opposition. Labor supported the building of the groyne, needlessly capitulating to fears that it would have been painted as "anti-development" had the legislation not passed.

When the Gordon power station was commissioned, there was no shortage of dignitaries ready to take part in the opening ceremony. Most of them are dead now. The rest are merely forgotten.

At least a groyne will be easier to dismantle than a power station.
