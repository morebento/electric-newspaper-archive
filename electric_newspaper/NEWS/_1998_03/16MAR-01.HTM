Monday March 16, 1998

Kernot smites SA
by Virginia Bosley

Labor Party cause celebre Cheryl Kernot left Adelaide on Friday, the centre of a storm about her private life partially dominating her three days of flesh-pressing around the city.

The attack on her alleged feline temperature shows how worried the Liberal Party is to Ms Kernot's vote-pulling capacity.

Her visit, part of a national tour designed to cement her support and cash in on the Cheryl-related party sign-ups which have been a documented phenomenon since her defection from the Democrats in October last year, seems to have been a mild success.

Despite obvious signs of exhaustion and a failure to recreate the oratorial style she employed so successfully in the party's Hobart national summit,

Ms Kernot enjoyed a reception one union official described as "surprisingly warm."

In many ways, this tour marks the beginning of Ms Kernot's real life in the ALP.

After the flash and dazzle of her decision and the thrill of being carried about on the shoulders of the triumphant Kym Beazley and Senator Nick Bolkus, the endless highway of ALP functions, chook raffles and workshops stretches before her, with all her new friends keen to hear an explanation of Kernot's decision and a guarantee of her loyalty.

In this visit to Adelaide - her first since campaigning for the local Democrats here before the last State election - her principal engagement was a fundraising dinner for Gail Gago, Karen Hannon and David Cox, federal candidates for Makin, Adelaide and Kingston respectively.

Kernot's address to the 300-odd gathered faithful was a carefully crafted appeal to "real values" and the need to "restructure" the language of government.

"The people of Adelaide, Makin and Kingston don't care about a GST,'' she said.

"What they care about is health, and educating their children.''

A cynical listener might predict that Kernot will find the inhabitants of other marginal electorates similarly inclined as she completes her tour, as this speech seems designed for repetition.

Short bursts of applause followed a few salutary criticisms of the Howard government, then there was a polite silence as Kernot outlined her plans for changing political language to galvanise grass-roots involvement in the political process by including more positive terms.

"We need to hear more about trust, communication and long-termism,'' she said, no doubt disappointing a few of the punters who might have preferred hearing a sincere and personal account of why the leader of a political party makes a surprise defection to the ramks of her political adversaries.

It was, as one catty observer put it, "a perfect speech for a Mt Lofty Democrats branch meeting.''

But the mood was warm and surprisingly uncritical, with hardly a sceptical eyebrow raised as Kernot prefaced her words with the claim that she had really been a Labor supporter and confidante some time before she officially joined the ranks.

Afterwards, she circulated throughout the crowd and drew a series of raffles before pleading tiredness and disappearing to prepare herself for a round of union functions and an appearance at the next day's ALP State Council meeting.

Perhaps it was Kernot's considerable personal charm that produced such a charitable response; perhaps it was the general feeling of bonhomie among the strengthening ranks of the ALP in South Australia.

But in trying to recreate a decade of networking in a matter of months, Kernot is making valuable friends and getting to know an electorate that, on the whole, seems pleased to see her.
