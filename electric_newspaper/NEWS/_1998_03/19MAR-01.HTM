Thursday March 19, 1998

Cost of living goes up in SA

The cost of living in South Australia will rise in two months - because it hasn't.

Since coming to office, the Olsen Government has pegged increases in its charges and fees to the Consumer Price Index.

This was a guarantee set by former Premier Dean Brown.

But low inflation nationally and in this State has seen the CPI rise only moderately, or sometimes not at all. Inflation is currently running at minus 1.1 per cent in SA..

Faced with huge wage increases for public sector employees, State Treasury was directed by the Premier and Treasurer to find a way of coming up with more cash.

The Government has not come up with a creative solution - it will just scrap the promise.

The Government has, in effect, arbitarily set the CPI increase at 4.5 per cent, and will increase a range of fees such as fines, car registration, and possibly public transport ticket prices, by approximately that much from June 1.

The Opposition opposed. The Democrats quietly applauded.

"State Government fees and charges are set to run well ahead of inflation as South Australians pay for John Olsen's handling of the economy," Shadow Treasurer Kevin Foley said.

"Following the ETSA broken promise and the shopping hours announcement, we have the fees and charges backflip.

"Taxpayers should mark this day down as 'Tax Wednesday'," Mr Foley said.

The Democrats supported the Budget considerations.

"The Australian Democrats have consistently said that we would rather see taxes and fees increased than a continual cut to areas such as health, education, and other essential services," said Democrats leader Mike Elliott.

But the Democrats said they had two reservations: the Government's refusal to allow proper scrutiny of multi-million outsourcing contracts by Parliamentary committees, and the high levels of secret subsidies the Government is providing to certain favoured projects.

The signs of looming pressure on the Budget were apparent to Treasury for more than six months. Under-Treasurer Gerard Bradley was so concerned it last year briefed the then Treasurer, Stephen Baker.

Unfortunately, this came about the same time as the State election, which means the news was with-held from voters.

During the election campaign, on September 22, Mr Olsen said that despite the pressure, the Government would deliver a planned $1 million surplus in 1997-98 and further surpluses of up to $6 million during a further three years.

Treasury officials said last night they had recoiled when Mr Olsen's campaign comments were reported the next day. They knew it was impossible.

Yesterday's decision is likely to raise about an extra $20 million in one year alone.

Also yesterday came the news that the Premier had agreed to pay a campaign advisor $10,000 a month.

She was Ms Alex Kennedy, a former speechwriter and media secretary to Mr Olsen.

Ms Kennedy's fee nevertheless seemed paltry compared with the free-spending credit card holders on the Government team.

Mr Olsen's current press secretary rang up a few hundred dollars shy of $10,000 on her Government credit card since the start of 1997.

The information came as a response to a question in Parliament from Labor front-bencher Ralph Clarke.

"The Premier is an extremely generous employer," Mr Clarke said outside the House.
