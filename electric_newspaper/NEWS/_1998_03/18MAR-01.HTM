Wednesday March 18, 1998

ETSA sale looks more likely

The sale of ETSA looks increasingly possible, with the Australian Democrats and Independent MLC Nick Xenaphon having not ruled out voting with the Government.

Premier John Olsen will introduce the Bill in the Lower House today.

The ALP is strongly opposed to the sale, but the Premier has warned that even if Parliament knocks out his legislation allowing ETSA and Optima to be sold through a public share float (such as the Telstra) or privately (such as with the SA Pipelines Authority), the Government will privatise through a long-term lease.

Mr Xenaphon says the Government has not yet lobbied him about the sale. The Democrats are equivocating, saying they will examine Government-provided documents before making a decision.

Government sources were "quietly confident" they could find a way to gain Democarts support.

While the Labor Opposition is campaigning against electricity privatisation, some senior ALP figures conceded privately yesterday that the Government would gain all the pain but little applause from the sale.

"We know that the sale process - and we know the Government wants to hurry it up - will still take a long time, and the money won't start coming through for years," an Opposition frontbencher said.

"But there's a few of us (in the ALP) who know if the Libs didn't sell, we'd have to. They can carry the can."

New South Wales, which has a Labor Government, is wanting to sell its power industry, but is facing strong opposition from the ALP's left-wing.

In South Australia, at least three consotiums are vying to get the nod from the Government as "sale organisers". The company which wins will charge up to $280 million, according to early unofficial Treasury estimates.

Meanwhile, the Government believes that opposition to the sale is diminishing, citing the low number of calls to the Government's toll-free information phone number.

The ALP revealed last week the calls were going to a Sydney answering company.

Ironically, SA taxpayers will save money if they tell pollsters they're not too worried about electricity privatisation.

The Government has access to a virtually bottomless chest of public funds which it says it intends to use in a television, radio, and print advertising campaign if community reaction to the sale is too strong.
