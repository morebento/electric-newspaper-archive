Tuesday March 17, 1998

Deep Thought thinks it's arrived

David Powers now runs an international competition he'd really rather be able to win.

This year Flinders University took over responsibility for organising the annual Loebner Prize, which offers $100,000 to anyone who can develop a computer which is considered to be thinking because its responses are indistinguishable from a human's.

That essentially is what Associate Professor Powers has been trying to do since, as a 16-year-old amateur programmer, he came to the conclusion that life would be much simpler if he could just talk English to his computer rather than Ada, Pascal or some other complex language.

He hasn't yet succeeded, but his work is considered at the forefront of research into artificial intelligence and his passion has seen him become co-founder (and until recently president) of the international Special Interest Group on Natural Language Learning and co-author of the book Machine Learning of Natural Language.

Over the past 20 years he has spent as much time thinking about psychology, linguistics and philosophy as he has fiddling with computers.

In fact, his interest in artificial intelligence really stems from a fascination with language and the complex and contentious question of how humans learn.

"Language is fundamental to human thinking and may in turn be the key to creating machines which can understand us," he said.

The term 'artificial intelligence' was coined in 1956 but the concept has been around even longer; since we started using computers (and in a practical sense that was during World War II) we've wondered how intelligent they can become and if they can ever really be like humans.

Associate Professor Powers' own work has focused on equipping computers to work out the structure of a given language and then the meaning of what it is saying by looking at the context in which things occur.

In this he is utilising aspects of phonology (the technique for analysing languages) and cryptography (code breaking) and even following in the footsteps of Sherlock Holmes who, in one famous case, cracked a code by looking at the frequency with which the letters appeared.

Associate Professor Powers' program can identify the structure of an arbitrary text in an arbitrary language by examining its characters or even the speech frequencies.

It locates the significant units of the language then works out from there by applying the same technique over and over again to the surrounding contacts, building up bigger and bigger chunks, like syllables, words and phrases.

The system is now basically a very sophisticated word and grammar checker but it still exists solely within a computer. The next stage is to connect it in the real world, and Associate Professor Powers is working with Adelaide-based company I2Net to build a natural language front-end which can control appliances.

"By connecting the program to a robot which interacts with the local environment we can move from simply understanding syntax and grammar to understanding the world," he said.

"That's what's needed to pass the Turing Test".

The Turing Test was developed by British mathematician Alan Turing, one of the pioneers of computing, and is the basis for the Loebner Prize.

The competition was established by American inventor, businessman and philanthropist Dr Hugh Loebner and previously was held at the Boston Computer Museum in the US.

It was held at Sydney's PowerHouse Museum this year but will move to Adelaide next year.
