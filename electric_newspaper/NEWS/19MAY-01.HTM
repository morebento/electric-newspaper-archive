Tuesday May 19, 1998

Suharto digs in, way above his head


Burn-out: incinerated mini-vans line a Jakarta street at the weekend, while a resident takes a simpler form of transport.

Indonesian President Suharto is this morning holding on to power with tanks and guns.

After two days of relative calm in Jakarta, the political temperature rose sharply last night as students marched from the University of Indonesia to Parliament in the centre of Jakarta.

In an atmosphere of deepening intrigue, the army not only said it would allow the student demonstration, but might provide trucks to ferry the students to the city centre to prevent riotous elements joining in.

Fifteen retired army generals announced they would accompany the students to show their support for their demands.

But the 76-year-old dictator's fate still lies tenuously in the hands of the army, up to now his strongest supporters. However, many there are persistent reports of rifts within the military, particularly between Gen Wiranto and Suharto's son-in-law, Lieut. Gen. Prabowo, who heads the strategic reserve command.

As military activity increased in the capital last night, the Muslim opposition leader, Dr Amien Rais, upped the stakes by announcing he would bring one million people on to the streets on Wednesday, a national holiday known as National Awakening Day. Students have said they will mark the day with a mass rally against Mr Suharto.

Meanwhile, defections are taking place among Mr Suharto's circle of loyalists. The president has lowered fuel prices and hinted at a cabinet reshuffle to pacify his critics but the measures are widely dismissed as too little, too late.
