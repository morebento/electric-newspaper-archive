Thursday June 18, 1998

Alun Leach-Jones, William Ferguson, and Laima Politis
BMGART, North Adelaide
reviewed by Dianne Gall

William Ferguson lets us view his version of the Australian landscape with dots and exclamation marks, whilst Leach-Jones transcribes the human condition through a range of symbols that form his abstracted language and Laima Politis paints the cafe society draped with modern odalisques.

Elements of the Australian landscape and the people that transverse its surface leave behind remains of campfires, footprints and evidence of foliage displaced by its inhabitants. This is where Ferguson focuses and interacts with the spiritual aura of the Australian Aborigine and the importance of the land to them. Ferguson's works are dreamy, employing subtlety and symbolic elements to form gentle studies of the broad expanse that is the Australian outback. In this exhibition he presents us with 12 paintings on paper, of which I found "Wordless Thoughts" particularly successful in its simplification of two black marks to represent features on the landscape. Ferguson uses a variety of marks as his language to express the mysteries that lie beneath the surface of the land to create a rich and varied range of works on paper.

Alun Leach-Jones shows us 17 versions of his visual language paintings that mostly seem on the surface quite repetitive. The most exciting thing for me in this exhibition is the move away from the two dimensional drawing to "Still Life (Wall Drawing)", where a steel abstraction becomes an extraction from his other works. I also enjoyed the immediacy and the simplicity of the smaller images like "Koan #2". There is a lot of energy trapped inside these works, eager to express life from its most frail and insignificant to the most powerful among us. Somehow I can almost sense a sinister aspect to this work, as some of the shapes take on the representation of spinning saw blades, whilst other more amorphous figures seem to dominate and cut into other forms. Generally not a particulary remarkable group of works but quite interesting if you have never seen his work before.

In the back gallery a change of pace, here it is time to sit down and have a cup of coffee and look at our relationships with one and other. Laima Politis presents a tight show of 17 paintings, most of them sharing a similar colour sense and tonal range, together with like features in the faces of the characters in her work. Both "A man with a happy hound" & "Cat on my lap", are particularly genial on the eye, with the latter reminiscent of a Drysdale landscape. Politis has also captured particularly languid odalisques that drape harmoniously across the canvas holding their heads in a relaxed but confident manner. Politis is in the early stages of her career and shows many promising attributes.

These three artists works are all very consistent within their own genre and prove what steady and determined artistic practice can bring. This is a short show so you had better be quick to catch it. The show closes on 27 June.
