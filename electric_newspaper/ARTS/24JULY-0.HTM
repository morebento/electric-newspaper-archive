Mornington Peninsula Regional Gallery
Agnes Goodsir 1864-1939
"A picture land over the sea"
21 June-2 August 1998
reviewed by Dianne Gall

The work of Agnes Goodsir has remained secret from public view for decades with very little ever written about her. This was until recently, when Bendigo Art Gallery Curator, Karen Quinlan took up the project of finding out who Goodsir was after a relative made herself known in the Gallery.

Quinlan then embarked on finding as many paintings by the artist that she could. This touring show is the result of her explorations.

Agnes Goodsir was born in Portland, Victoria in 1864, she studied Art for three years at the Bendigo School of Mines and followed this with another five years study overseas. Goodsir decided to reside in London between 1908 and 1918 and then lived in Paris in the 20's & 30's. A frequent exhibitor at the Royal Academies in England, Goodsir quickly established a name for herself as a portrait painter. In 1926, she was the second Australian woman to become a member of the Societe Nationale des Beaux Arts. Then in 1927, she returned to Australia to work for nine months and to hold solo exhibitions of paintings at Macquarie Galleries, Sydney and the Fine Art Society's Gallery in Melbourne. Her works were reasonably well received on these occasions but jibes at the decorative nature of her subject matter and that she was a woman, all probably influenced her decision to return to the Latin Quarter of the Paris she loved and paint where she felt comfortable.

Goodsir died aged 75 in 1939 in Paris. Thankfully, her companion and model Rachael Dunn (Cherry), then sent back to Australian relatives and friends, many of the paintings. Cherry feared the outbreak of war and hoped that by sending work back she would assure her companions place in the Art History books and at the very least preserve these most treasured paintings.

I would say it is a necessity for anyone studying painting to see this exhibition, the lessons of paint application and of layered colours are quite overwhelming. Goodsir painted women with an incredible insight into their souls, they are draped in lavish decoration and in later paintings, always looking confident. These images were of the new modern female. This is evident in such paintings as "The Cigarette Girl" 1925, where the fashionable idea of smoking as a symbol of independence is portrayed. The heaviness of the shawl around the shoulders of the woman, the delicate nature of the glass lamp, the rough texture of the tablecloth and the fineness of the porcelain cup on the table are all painted perfectly. In earlier portraits of women, we do not see the same confidence, as is illustrated by "Girl on Couch" 1915. Here the subject is politely sitting side on to the viewer, delicately reading a letter. However, Goodsir's skill is here, the sense and range of colours, the painterly application to describe patterns on the screen, the dress and pillow seen so full of depth and texture.

Works such as "The Spanish Shawl" are just mesmorising, you can almost sense the fabrics texture with you fingertips, the delicate flecks of colour in the skin and the stare of the subject are quite captivating. The portrait of "Cherry" 1925, shows the incredibly beautiful dreamy eyes of Goodsir's companion, this painting is drenched in violet and illustrates perfectly the absolute command she had over the use of colour. Again we see this skill, only this time with tone in the painting of "Cherry" 1924, where the subjects' eyes are shadowed and peering from under a hat.

I found this exhibition to be quite exceptional and felt inspired by the skill evident in these works by Goodsir. There are also many quick sketches in the show and other objects such as the medals awarded to her by the Academies, all of which provide the viewer with a further insight to her life.

If travelling around Victoria, New South Wales or South Australia and can find the time to look at these works, I am sure you will be amazed at why this highly skilled and beautiful paintings have gone unrecognised in the Australian Art History books until now.
