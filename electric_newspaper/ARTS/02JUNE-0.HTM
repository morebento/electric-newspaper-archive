Tuesday June 2, 1998


'The South coast', Hans Heysen, 1926.

'The Moon Enchanted Sea', James Ashton,1910.

'Holiday Resort', Jeffery Smart, 1946.

'Blanche Point', Ivor Hele, 1960.

'Maslin Beach, Robert Hannaford', mid 1980s.
The Painted Coast
Views of the Fleurieu Peninsula south of Adelaide.
8 May 1998 to 16 August 1998
Williams and Brack in Adelaide
8 May 1998 to 23 August 1998
both at the Art Gallery of South Australia
Both reviewed by Dianne Gall

The Art Gallery of South Australia presents us with a comprehensive history of paintings of the Fleurieu Peninsula and provides a rather questionable exhibition of two of Australia's greatest painters, John Brack and Fred Williams.

Curator of Australian Art at the AGSA, Jane Hylton has produced a broad well researched historical exhibtion of artworks produced over the last 150 years or so of the South Coast. Starting with the watercolours of Colonel William Light to the show stealer Robert Hannafords "Maslin Beach".

These earlier works show an untouched vegetation and a hint of the Aboriginals who lived there. As time passed, colonists began to visit and we see images of them strolling along the beaches and the slow population of Victor Harbor and other regions like Goolwa and Port Elliott. By the turn of the Twentieth Century, artists like James Ashton began to portray the quiet mysticism of the sea and surrounding cliffs of the landscapes around the Peninsula with works like :"the Moon Enchanted Sea" 1910. Looking at this painting you can sense the tumbling of seaweed, the crash of the waves and the stillness in the air as night falls. Artists like Hans Heysen moved from the Australian gum tree to painting the cliffs around Port Noarlunga in the mid twenties. It is then we find our most well known painter of the South Coast, Horace Trenerry who lived in the area from the late thirties. Trenerry is known for his painting technique, his colour sensibilities and his continual studies of the changing landscape in the Port Willunga area.

Moving on to the forties and the Modernist works of Dorrit Black, the serenity of the Coast is lost and is replaced by powerful, stylistic sculptural representations of the cliffs and ocean. Also around this time Jeffrey Smart painted "Holiday Resort" 1946, not much happens in this place, it's eerily quiet in this seaside town of Port Elliott. This early work of Smart's shows the start of his now well known careful placement of objects and compositional devices such as the repeating triangular motifs. Smart has given this near empty resort town some coloured lights, a solitary man reading his paper and a vacated pram to illustrate the human presence in the landscape.

Ivor Hele's 1960 painting, "Blanche Point", shows us a birds eye view of the rugged coastline near Aldinga that evokes the spirit of place, the warmth upon the rocks from the afternoon sun warming the memory of times spent gazing out to sea.

In Robert Hannafords "Maslin Beach" 1980's, a man stands firmly on Australia's first legal nude swimming beach, Maslins. A sense of light pervades the sandy beach stretching out in the warm summer sun. The cliffs hug the bay providing shelter and protection to the occupants below. The reality of everyday existence seems very far away indeed.

Although there are some paintings that are of a lower standard than others, overall a sense of tranquility and clean air away from the bustle of the world filters through and I found myself quite relaxed after visiting this exhibition.

However, I must say I was disappointed with the exhibition of Williams and Brack. There are sprinklings of good works in the show but it provides little insight into the true scope of their paintings and the standing of these artists in the history of Australian Art. I also found particularly frustrating the hanging of the works on paper overcrowded and visually confusing. I did not quite expect the retrospectives of 1987 & 1989 but would like to have seen a better selection of works to represent these two painters. I see that several have come from the Sydney dealer, Rex Irwin that helped immensely, perhaps some contact with Melbourne collectors and or galleries would have filled some of the gaps.

The pretext of this exhibition is to show these two artists work together illustrating the disparity between them and to highlight their many years of friendship. They shared a studio between 1947 and 1948 in Melbourne where one would work in the day and the other at night, then in 1967 they held a joint exhibition in Canberra and in 1976 travelled to Paris together. Brack chose to paint the Urban Landscape from interior nudes to the finest details of crowds of pencils. He takes the rose coloured glasses off and looks at the everyday puritanical journey and the whole range of humanity in its dullest form. Whilst Williams worked the vast uninhabited Australian Landscape implying its austerity with carefully chosen marks. Both artists helped us to see our surroundings in a uniquely different way.
