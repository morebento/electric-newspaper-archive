Monday April 6, 1998

Rimona Kedem at Adelaide Central Gallery, Norwood
Reviewed by Jennifer Jones

The elements of family, memory and its fragmentation, dreams and loss pervade almost all the 26 masterfully created works - two large paintings amongst smaller mixed media works - in this seriously satisfying exhibition.

Kedem's subjects, appearing as abstract, often large angular collage figures assume their various roles against backgrounds sharply delineated by colour and tone aiding in the depiction of open space/freedom or confinement. Their facial expressions, always sparingly hewn, are sometimes wistful, often sad/serious/pained and, only on a couple of occasions, appear humorous at first.

Kedem shows a fundamental concern about the human condition and how the mind can individually shape experience into memory. When we cannot deal with the memory's storehouse of sadness and pain of loss, we can retreat into fantasy, tricking the memory, however fleetingly, to metamorphose us into something lighter, perhaps ephemeral.

In Leaving My Shadow Behind, the figure runs away across the canvas to a lithe, dancing black figure, leaving her hazy shadow caught halfway across a half bright and half black backdrop. In Take Off, the floating figure, pale and gauzy, wafts just above the large red and green figure so heavy and solidly fixed, it appears to be sinking out of the frame it seems to almost fill. Claustrophobia and imprisonment (imposed by memory/mind?) are juxtaposed with the more desirable evocation of lightness and freedom.

The 'Three Stages of Life', 'My Ma and I', 'Household', 'My Sister and I' and 'A Family Circus' treat the notion of family in different ways. Family as source of core experience, moving away from, or even spurning it, back to nurturing; family as a provider of comfort and a haven against a hostile exterior world and family as the cause of grief and sense of great loss and, perhaps, even fear.

In other relationships, we see white faced, tight-mouthed, black squint-eyed anger as well as utter sadness mixed with disappointment, as in the work called, tongue in cheek of course, Valentine's Day. As if they were once joined together as one, the two heads are now back to back and only slightly apart with a seemingly impenetrable abyss separating them. This is as intense a portrayal of the souring of relationships as you are ever likely to see!

Worthy of note are the magnificent richly glazed textures of some of the works, appearing to be enameled surfaces of delicious colours and shapes. For the most part, the tones are of black and red or orange, sometimes so intricately worked with fine lines they resemble delicate etchings. There are many little treasures to be found here.

Kedem's personal experience of life must be vast, deep and intensely felt, even in memory. She uses external metaphor for internal states, obviously, but her subjects have been given their real strength by her own knowledge and wisdom of humanity as depicted by the passionate facial expressions (or lack of them) along with other elements already touched upon here. I found myself drawn into this world but not wishing to dwell there. Perhaps I havenât the strength of Ms Kedem.
