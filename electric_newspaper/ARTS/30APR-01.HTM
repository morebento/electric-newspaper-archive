Thursday April 30, 1998

Mario Dalpra, Manne Schulze, Simone Kennedy
Greenaway Art Gallery: Adelaide
Reviewed by Dianne Gall


"Aha", polychrome on wood, 102 x 65 x 60 cm


"Seed", oil on linen 91 x 91 cm
"Das Blaue Wunder" acrylic and oil stick on canvas 152 x 190 cm

Austrian-born Dalpra presents an exhibition of 19 mixed media and acrylic paintings and drawings whose images are energetic and spontaneous whilst seemingly searching for equilibrium and harmony.

Exploring the media of painting and drawing, Dalpra has attacked the surface of his paper and canvases with such vigour that they have become multi-layered and textured with underlying paint application.

Then, he often uses drawing over the painted surface to define the image. Dalpra's childlike works are complex and relay conscious as well as subconscious throughs and journeys.

The images presented have culminated from residencies in three different countries -Australia, Italy and Indonesia over the last two years.

The Australian works like "Wrestling half naked in the dirt", seem to have a sense of fun and portray two people triumphant at reaching the top of two towers.

"Archaeological Theory" also an Australian work, illustrates humanity and its relationship with the landscape with remnants of the past scratched out and disappearing whilst the present floats over the top re-writing what has been.

Many of the characters drawn in his paintings seem confused and displaced, they sometimes reappear in a different locality trying to find themselves.

This is particularly evident in "3 Possibilities".

Here the image of a woman starts off as a recognisable form and then moves to an abstracted outline where it has become transparent and onto a third stage that is dense black and unrevealing of its underlying form.

So it is perhaps a case of, What am I today? What will I be tomorrow? What was I yesterday?

In "Something Better", Dalpra illustrates his thought patterns as he journeys and pauses over the image making definite marks to illustrate moments of furious activity before running off and finding another place to rest and create a new mark.

Dalpra seems to put a lot of physical and emotional energy into his work and I imagine completely exhausts himself after each image is completed.

Other works in the exhibition seem more circumspect in their mark making and placement of objects on the canvas like "The blue wonder".

Here we see a more saturated use of colour and careful selection of revolving objects around a figure, which co-exists with all its surrounding chaos.

In Galleries 2 and 3 we see the installation of works by German-born Schulze, better known perhaps by some of South Australians as Daubist, (Daubism it is the practise of obtaining another person's artwork and re-cycling it into a new work of art).

Schulze has a PhD in neurophysiology and clearly he is interested in obtaining a particular manipulated reaction from those that view his work.

He believes in political shock value and still feels that presenting a Daubist view is the best way he can express himself and his true artist voice.

In this exhibition, however, we are presented with a group of vividly painted, same-sized patterned canvasses that are placed on the wall in an ordered repetitive manner.

In the back gallery is "Ant-Robo-Morphic" that is an installation of a group of ants circulating around a neatly leveled mound of sand.

They are very quietly going about their business and not really worried about anything else but what is in front of them.

Perhaps this is a comment an everyday mundane existence and modern life.

As is usual with Manne's work we see some interesting well-crafted sculpture that looks at modern culture and the analysis of human nature.

"Aha!" is a comical sculpture and caught in mid activity as a toy-like nurse looks into the torso of a decapitated Mr. Potato head-like robot.

On the Mezzanine floor we see the first Solo exhibition of Simone Kennedy.

For many years Kennedy has devoted her career to illustration and has recently begun to explore her creativity on canvas.

The five paintings presented are competent works that tell the story of evolution from seed to adulthood, interspersed with these works are the less successful sculptural installations of fabric foetuses suspended in organza.

I find these destroy the strength of the paintings and the subtly of touch in the compositions.

Kennedy has been seduced by colour but has learnt to use it well as can be seen in "Hybrid".

Her paint surface is smooth and calming and allows the revolution of time to play out across her images.

"Little Women" is a self-portrait that brings onto the surface text with words like 'peace' and 'stabiliser'.

This is a reflection on her life as a woman and as a mother, portraying her fears and fascinations with insects crawling around the image to emphasise mortality and the cycle of life.

The exhibition concludes on 17 May.
