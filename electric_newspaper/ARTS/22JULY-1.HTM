Wednesday July 22, 1998

Adelaide Roundup
SALA Week exhibitions (July 19-July 26 1998)
reviewed by Dianne Gall

It's official! SALA week is here, so go out and visit your local gallery for a visual feast. See Russell Morrison at Hill Smith Gallery, Staff Show at Adelaide Central Gallery, Kim guthrie & Deborah Paauwe (see separate review for Daryl Austin) all at Greenaway Art Gallery.

SALA was officially declared open on Sunday 19th July by the Arts Minister, Diana Laidlaw who was sporting a pair of wild shoes made by local artist, Deborah Miller. The throng cooked in the Art Gallery of South Australia as the temperature soared, whilst a very nervous local artist, Hossein Valamenesh gave an inspired speech about his own experiences as an artist and why he picked Adelaide for his working base. AGSA director, Ron Radford gave a brief history of the Adelaide Art scene, which reminded us all of the glory days when South Autralian Artists were at the leading edge of Australian Art.

Russel Morrison, "Breakfast time quarry" at the Hill-Smith Fine Art Gallery

Russell Morrison
"North East West"
Hill Smith Fine Art, Pirie St City.
10 July-31 July

Russell was born in South Australia in 1954 and has been working as a full-time artist since 1979. In his current exhibition he presents us with 29 works based on trips to Cape York and down the coast of Western Australia. The most successful works in the show for me are "Breakfast Time Quarry" and "Ord River". These two gouaches are more believable in their mark making and description of form than many of the other works in the show.

ACSA Staff Show
&
Damien Graham
Adelaide Central Gallery, Norwood
10 July - 9 August 1998

Adelaide Central Staff show spans the breadth of talent that the teachers of the school can provide to their students and illustrates the variety of their personal artistic practice. Of particular enjoyment is Rod Taylor's "Study of Back View of Sitting Woman", which shows a good understanding of form and tonal control with an accurate description of light falling upon the body. This is a move away from the work he is better known for, such as the painting of the nude woman wearing bunny slippers, but I am puzzled by the companion piece to this study "Composition Study for Woman and Storm". This work has outlines of crying cartoons embracing the body and is perhaps a feeble attempt at what he is trying to portray.

Anna Platten presents two studio self portraits. They are in the usual brilliant style with a few quick lines to describe light entering the room from a nearby window and the fall of her painting smock, with the focus of the study being in the more detailed drawing of the facial features.

Dianne Longley, who has just set up the printmaking area at the school, exhibits some of her new digital prints. Longley can always be relied on to be at the forefront of technology when it comes to printmaking and whilst her actual subject matter has changed little over the years, her work always remains interesting because of her constand hard work at new ways of doing things.

Lisa Youngs' still life paintings are powerful images, anchored by abstract shapes such as the very vibrant orange table that features in "Pomegranates 2". Trena Everuss works are representations of urban views, which after just seeing Ken Searles views of Ballarat at Charles Nodrum Gallery in Melbourne, I cannot help myself but to compare the two and in fact find Everuss attempts to be the more evocative images.

In the Studio Gallery, is a series of 9 watercolours by Damien Graham, which are well executed and are reminiscent of the portraits of young men by Steven Cox. These are on the surface quite attractive works, light in their appearance because of their medium but are actually in my view rather unpleasant and dark in their context.

Also in the Student's Gallery are rows of sculptures of heads supported by long posts. These are quite eerie, especially if you find yourself alone in the corridor with these works. They are interesting masks of humanity, each with their own personality. Definitely worth a look at if you are visiting the main gallery.

Debroah Paauwe, from exhibition titled "blue room" 1998

Deborah Paauwe
Kim Guthrie
Johnnie Dady
(See separate review for Daryl Austin)
Greenaway Art Gallery, Kent Town.
15 July-9 August 1998

Johnnie Dady has installed a work at the facade of Greenaway Gallery, dressing it up for SALA week, whilst inside we move through the sell-out show of Daryl Austins and into the back galleries where Kim Guthrie presents small installations commenting on himself as a regional artist working in isolation and effected by Australia's current political upheaval with Pauline Hanson's, One Nation Party.

Born in the United States and a resident of Adelaide since 1985, Paauwe presents 5 large type C photographs in the back gallery, which has been painted blue for the exhibition titled "Blue Room". I was particularly drawn to "Blue Tights" and the general sense of fun and the larger than life, saturated colour of these images.

Around the Galleries

Elsewhere about the state, you can see Karen Genoff's sculptures and Angela Kirby's paintings at Port Dock Craftworks and Gallery, Port Adelaide. The exhibition unfortunately opened at the same time as the SALA week launch at the AGSA on 19th July but it will be on until the 23 August, so plenty of time to get there. Kensington Gallery have Peter Scrymgour paintings, Jean Kent & John Geyer's underwater photographs and a selection of South Australian Artists works for SALA week.
