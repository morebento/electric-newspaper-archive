Thurssday March 26, 1998

Times Change .. or Do They?
The Department, by David Williamson. Reviewed by Ray Thomas

The expectation and the realisation were unsettling.

A Williamson play set in the 1960's. Its relevance today surely must have dimmed. Not so!

When Rodney Fischer directed the world premiere of The Department for the State Theatre Company in 1974 (it was the second play at the new Adelaide Festival Centre Playhouse), he spoke of its depiction of academic idealism foiled by institutional bureaucracy and personal interest.

A staff meeting in the laboratory of an engineering department at a tertiary college brings together a group of largely cantankerous academics, all with their own individual agendas and most at odds with the irascible and vulnerable department head, Robby, who dominates this play.

Although moving to the very edge of being over the top, Paul Blackwell's splendid portrayal of Robby, satisfies our biased perceptions of a fuzzy haired eccentric intellectual determined to have his own way and set on empire building through heavy handed coercion, pleading and downright intimidation.

Geoff Revell's initially restrained portrayal of the reluctant Al (who would prefer to be in his lecture theatre marking papers) provides one of the playâs highlights when he "loses it" after being pushed to breaking point. His departure from the set is an explosion of physical frustration that brings roars of delight from an appreciative audience.

The young 60's socialist in a hurry to change the world is perceptively played by Matthew Whittet, a stark contrast to Patrick Frost, who revisits The Department after being a member of the 1974 cast, with his carefully honed portrayal of the academic caught in a time warp while awaiting retirement.

Don Barker as the effusively reliable maintenance man seems to belong to the role and it says much for his abilities that he does it so effortlessly.

Only two of the play's ten performers are women. Bronwen James as Myra from the Humanities Department intrudes into the male domain where she is quickly told by Robbie that if he had his time again, he'd take humanities too. Why bother exercising your brain when you don't have to!

And, Mario Grocke makes two very brief appearances as the secretary.

Shaun Gurton has built a setting in which an elevated stage is engulfed by pipes, ducts, channels and conduits, appropriately interwoven in a complex pattern of engineering confusion. Below, a somewhat mysterious engineering hub, from where the characters emerge.

The revival of The Department reminds us that while the issues have changed, prevailing attitudes in the halls of academia evidently haven't.

An Adelaide professor told me after the performance that he'd overseen a meeting with his staff earlier that day which bore an unsettling resemblance to Williamsonâs play.

One final note .. and one major difference. Just about all the characters in the department huff and puff their way through the evening.

This has prompted a very precious politically correct insert to the program from concerned sponsor Living Health, reminding those of us who can't think for ourselves that "the inclusion of smoking in certain scenes of The Department is an artistic decision." It goes on to allay any of our concerns by reminding us that State Theatre endorses being smoke free.

And, as if that isn't enough, it instructs us that "a healthy culture is a healthy culture!"

I'm lost for words!

The Department
At The Playhouse, Festival Centre, until 9 April.
