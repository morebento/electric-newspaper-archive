Monday April 20, 1998

Obituary: Rover Thomas
Born Gunawaggi 1926 - Died April 11 1998 Warmun (Turkey Creek)
By Dianne Gall

It was not until the age of 60, after 40 years as a stockman, that Rover Thomas began realising his dreamings onto canvas using charcoal and ochres ground from the land. His palette was bounded by these earth colours but his strong images enhanced their sheer textural beauty.

In the early 1990's, Thomas painted abstract representations of incidents in Australian history of the 1920's and '30's. This can be seen in several series called "The Bedford Downs Killings", "Texas Downs Killings", and "Ruby Plains Killings"* that speak of the memory of white cattle-owners who murdered aborigines. The horrors of these historical facts are accentuated by the dense use of black in his paintings. The black evokes memory, contemplation and suppression whilst the use of minimal lines trace a story across the landscape, leading us to an event and realisation of the atrocities that were carried out in and around the Kimberleys.

Other paintings tell the stories of the ancient land and the region of Thomas's cultural and spiritual dreaming that merge the past with the future via the landscape.

In the short time that Thomas practised his art he achieved much success and respect within Australia and overseas. In 1994, the National Gallery of Australia held a solo retrospective of his work and in the last couple of years Rover Thomas's paintings were chosen to represent Australian Contemporary Art at the Venice Bienale.

Thomas is truly one of the great contemporary Australian painters of the 20th century, and he has left is with a unique view of the land that will influence many generations to follow. * "Blackness in the Art of Rover Thomas", Art and Australia Vol35 No.1 1997 p99
