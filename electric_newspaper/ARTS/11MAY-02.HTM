Monday May 11, 1998

Thomas Lawrence, Great Britain, 1768 - 1830, Lucy Sarah Sotherton, London, oil on canvas, 126.7 x 101.1cm

REGENCY British Art & Design 1800-1830
Art Gallery of South Australia
8 April-14 June 1998
Reviewed by Dianne Gall

In this important first exhibition in Australia of the British Regency, Christopher Menz, Curator of European and Australian Decorative Arts at the Art Gallery of South Australia has bought together a broad cross section of material covering paintings, furniture, sculptures, ceramics, toys, illustrated books and more to represent Britain and its colonies in the era of the Prince Regent. The epoch around 1800 to 1830 saw a flush of literature with the likes of Shelley, Keats and Wordsworth, whilst London was resplendent with buildings designed by John Nash and furnished with the opulence of Greek, Egyptian & Gothic revivals of Thomas Hope and George Bullock.

Regency accurately falls between the years of 1811 to 1820 when the famed patron of the arts George, Prince of Wales stepped in as Regent for his mentally disturbed father, George 111. Due to the Prince Regents influence in society of the Arts over a greater period covering his journey from Prince to King George 1V, this exhibition extends itself to the broader era until his death in 1830. A revealing statue in the exhibition shows at first glance a Neo-Classical Sculpture of the ailing "King George 111" 1813 by Francis Chantrey but actually it discloses the weakened spirit of the man who glazes in a dreamy bewildered state. Although the classical clues are there, including the royal insignia and chain of office, this a very personal portrait of a man balanced on a precipice.

Along with the opulence of this period came influential paintings in the guise of early plein air works that were a prelude to Impressionist painting. This was a case of keen observation producing paintings with a feeling of life, of the coolness in the shadows and the warmth of the sunlight, an extreme contrast to the heavy ornamentation of Hopes' furniture. To illustrate this we firstly see the work by Charles Eastlake "Cypress Trees at the Villa d'Este, Tivoli" 1817 and then by John Constable "Sunrise on the Sea" 1820's, that is a study of atmospheric effects of clouds and sky produced from observations in the open air at Brighton, England, showing a fresh approach and a recurrent rural theme throughout the Regency. Perhaps the most dramatic rural painting in the exhibition is John Glover's "Cattle Resting" 1820 painted in London, it is the image of tranquillity, it is by its size very overwhelming and thus imposing its senses of idealism on the viewer.

The portrait paintings in the exhibition are very descriptive of each subjects place in society. "Mr William Thompson" and "Mrs William Thompson and Daughter" by and unknown artist, show the dutiful wife who has just put away her sewing to play with her chid, the clues of the entire of hats and dress assume a middle class status that is confirmed when we look at the separate portrait of her husband. This painting shows a proud man in his study, dressed in Freemasons lodge garb and displaying a silver trophy inferring his successes and level of wealth in society. Other works like "Lucy Sarah Sotherton" 1825 by Thomas Lawrence, show the real wealth of a young heiress who sits comfortably and confidently with the inference of her lands stretching out beyond her. The image of this young woman evokes the elegance of the period and the Regency Style of dress and jewellery as well as portraying the social dogmas that are the real messages underlying the work. The painter later became the third president of the Royal Academy and its paintings like this one that illustrates the doctrine of the studio based disciplined portrait school.

Other items of interest in the exhibition that help create the picture of Regency, are objects from the South Australian State Library's Children’s Literature collection. These include an expanding perspective view based on the Thames Tunnel and a fascinating game called "The New Game of Virtue Rewarded and Vice Punished, for the Amusement of Youth of both Sexes" 1818, designed to help teach morals to juveniles.

Looking at this exhibition as a whole, what stands out is the creation of such an immense variety of objects to represent the revolutionary social, cultural and technical changes that were occurring in Britain during the Regency era. Illustrated with everything from lavishly decorated table pieces to the simple grace of the painted portrait of "Archibald Trotter of Bush", by Henry Raeburn 1813 Edinburgh, which is an uncluttered rendering of a man in his finest suit, seated against a dulled background, the only hint of opulence being his frilled shirt. It is worth taking the time to look at the eclectic range of items and realising what a stimulating era this was for the arts.
