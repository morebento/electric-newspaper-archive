Monday March 16, 1998

RIVERDANCE
Reviewed by Will Wood
At the Entertainment Centre

Riverdance, they say, is the show that has made Irish dancing sexy.

The audience came along knowing what to expect. After the hype of the last couple of years, who hasn't heard of Riverdance? A fair guess would be that most people in the near-capacity crowd at the Entertainment Centre would have seen excerpts on TV or video, and read about it in the popular press. An informed audience, you might say. Certainly an audience with expectations to be met.

The large ensemble worked hard to fill the vast space of the Entertainment Centre stage and didn't quite succeed. The show would have benefited from having a smaller stage, particularly for solo work and smaller combinations. In contrast with the sheer size and emptiness of the stage, the company appeared to be made up of so many leprechauns.

But talented leprechauns, to be sure. The dancers were good. Accurate, clean footwork, shown at its best in the sequences where the band fell silent and the dancers created their own percussion.

Connections were suggested between Irish, Spanish and Russian dancing; just what the basis of those connections were was not apparent to this reviewer. No doubt it was all explained in the program, but with programs at $15 a pop I felt constrained to remain programless and ignorant.

Riverdance offers spectacle, colour, and humour too. The witty sequence featuring a `duel' between Irish and Black American dance styles was a highlight of the show.

As for the band, they were the unsung heroes of the night. Two percussionists worked up a sweat behind immense drum kits adding texture to the score. The fiddler and the uillean piper had their moments of glory on stage - both eliciting rapturous applause - but for the most part the band stayed put in their corner, tight and polished and deserving more of the spotlight.

Riverdance is a Big Show. Dancers, singers and musicians alike do their jobs well, and for a good night out you can't beat being around talented performers. Make sure you get good seats, and if you can spare the extra cash hire a set of binoculars on the way in. Irish music and dancing are like Guinness: they're good for you.
