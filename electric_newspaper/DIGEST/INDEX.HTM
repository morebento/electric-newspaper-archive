Today's Media Roundup

Compiled by Ray Thomas

GST PUSH : MORE CASH TO FAMILIES

The Federal Government will boost direct family payments in its tax overhaul, wooing family support for a GST as polling by business confirms that wide inducements will be needed to lock voters in behind the planned 10 per cent tax. The inducements, also including changes to the tax scales to ease the burden on middle-income earners, will force the Government to raid future surpluses to pay for the reforms. A broadly-based GST - applied to food and excluding only health and education - would raise about $23 billion, but abolition of sales tax, and scrapping financial duties to accommodate the States and compensation the poor, will leave only about $3 billion for tax and other breaks. This would be inadequate to fund the measures necessary to make the package attractive to voters, offsetting the "pain" of the GST. Government sources said yesterday that while the package would include significant tax breaks, especially through a bigger tax-free threshold and adjustment to tax scales, families would be targeted through higher family payments. The Prime Minister has repeatedly stressed the need for tax reform to be family friendly, arguing this is the best way of defusing the political problems surrounding the introduction of a new tax. Boosting family payments - a device also used in the failed Fightback package - enables the Government to provide direct and more visible help to "principle carers". Backbenchers argue that it produces better political benefits than the more broad approach of tinkering with the tax scales. The package, which still has no firm launching date, will also affect the multi-billion-dollar sporting industry, with Australia's 30,000 sporting clubs to be subjected to a GST on sponsorship deals, entry fees and memberships. Government sources have confirmed that sport will be taxed under the GST, raising about $100 million, despite Cabinet's decision to exclude charities and churches. In other areas:

  • The tourism industry will be helped by substantial funding for overseas promotions following the decision to apply the GST to packages sold offshore.
  • The sale of existing homes will not be taxed, but new homes will be fully taxed, forcing the Government to offer some relief for first-home buyers. Real estate agents' fees and legal fees for both types of purchases will be fully taxed.
  • Local government rates and services will be fully taxed, a much tougher position than in Fightback, which only taxed services.
  • The Deputy Prime Minister, Mr Fischer, confirmed that the Government would replace diesel excise with a lower road user charge, reducing the tax burden on road transport. The excise on petrol would remain.
  • The gaming industry will be face an indirect GST burden, while also paying more than $3 billion in tax to the States. Gaming will be "input-taxed", which means the GST applies to inputs while not applying directly to the cost of gambling.
A poll commissioned by business to assess the results of its $4 million advertising campaign supporting a GST has shown that 60 per cent of voters would consider a GST if personal income rates were lowered. [The Sydney Morning Herald]

POLLSTER SEES GST AS COALITION 'SUICIDE'

Pollster Gary Morgan yesterday forecast electoral doom for the Federal Government saying Labor would "walk it in" because the Coalition was "committing suicide" by pushing a goods and services tax. Speaking at a retail industry lunch in Melbourne, the head of Roy Morgan Research was asked the percentage rate at which a GST was likely to be pegged. "You won't have any per cent because you won't have a GST", Mr Morgan said. "I think the Liberal Party is committing suicide. They're down at about 33 per cent and going south." But other prominent pollsters said Mr Morgan's judgments were too premature, because the tax package had not been released. Mr Morgan also forecast that Pauline Hanson's One Nation Party would pick up 10 to 15 per cent of the popular vote. [The Australian]

MLC THREATENS OLSEN'S ETSA STRATEGY

John Olsen's leadership took a bruising blow yesterday when a key Upper House MP said he could block crucial power privatisation legislation after losing "trust and confidence" in the Government's integrity. Nick Xenophon, the nation's sole No Pokies MP, issued a scathing critique of Mr Olsen's failure last week to discipline senior minister Graham Ingerson for deliberately misleading Parliament. Mr Xenophon said the Government's credibility had been "materially undermined" over the Ingerson affair and voters were disgusted at the Governemnt's "cumulative conduct" which had set a "dangerous and corrosive precedent" for future governments. He felt a "growing sense of disquiet" with the Government's handling of the power sale, including its $300,000 advertising campaign and the hiring of spin doctors to do its work. Mr Xenophon's attack underlines the high price the Premier is paying for his loyalty to Mr Ingerson, whose ministerial behaviour now threatens the Government's central budget strategy - the proposed $5 billion-plus sale of power utilities ETSA and Optima Energy. "Unless the Premier acts decisively in the immediate future to remedy the obvious damage to the reputation of his Government .. I will find it increasingly difficult to accept at face value the Government's case on the proposed sale of ETSA", Mr Xenophon said. Without Mr Xenophon's vote the sale legislation is effectively doomed. Supporters of former premier Dean Brown say Mr Olsen's leadership could not survive such a policy defeat. [The Australian]

$114BN IN EXPORTS AS TRADERS FIND WAY AROUND ASIAN CRISIS

Australian exporters have successfully diversified their overseas markets to compensate for weak demand in Asia, according to new trade figures published yesterday. The Australian Bureau of Statistics figures show exports to key Asian customers fell by about 3 per cent in the first six months of 1998 compared with the first six months of 1997. But exports to all other countries, notably the US and the European Union, rose by 21 per cent over the same period, which resulted in a record overall performance by Australian exporters in June. Australia exported just under $10 billion worth of goods and services in June, and $114 billion over the entire 1997-98 financial year, after seasonal adjustment. The ABS figures also show Australia's trade balance deteriorated from a deficit of $536 million in May to a deficit of $920 million in June after adjustment for seasonal factors. The trade data buoyed the Australian dollar, with the local currency settling for the day marginally firmer at US61.27¢, from US61.13¢. But there are still conflicting signs about where the Australian economy is heading, with a survey released yesterday suggesting that business investment is weakening. The Investment Monitor prepared by Access Economics and Delta Electricity shows investment projects under construction increased by a weak 3.2 per cent over the past year. "The survey thus suggests that industrial business investment is slowing," said an Access Economics director, Dr Ed Shann. Analysts were encouraged by the latest trade data, which was in line with market expectations, with some private-sector economists now claiming the worst of the Asian crisis could be over for Australian exporters. The 7.4 per cent seasonally adjusted increase in imports in June was also taken by analysts as an indication of considerable resilience in domestic demand, an important component of economic growth. [The Australian Financial Review]

CLINTON TO TESTIFY

President Bill Clinton has come to an agreement to provide testimony for Independent Counsel Ken Starr's grand jury. The president's lawyer, David Kendall, announced that Clinton will submit to questioning on Aug. 17. The testimony will be videotaped at the White House and Clinton's lawyers will be present for the questioning. "In an effort to achieve a prompt resolution of this entire matter the president will voluntarily provide his testimony on August 17, 1998 to the Office of Independent Counsel, as he has on prior occasions," Kendall said. The White House also confirmed the president did receive a subpoena on July 17 to appear before the grand jury. Now that the lawyers have reached an agreement for the president to voluntarily testify, that subpoena has been withdrawn. Sources are also telling CNN that the president's testimony will come after Monica Lewinsky begins her appearance at the grand jury. The deal between Kendall and Starr also stipulates that there will only be one day of questioning at the White House. The agreement comes on the heels of news that Lewinsky is prepared to testify that she and Clinton discussed how to conceal their alleged sexual relationship, sources say. Lewinsky's account Starr's prosecutors is that "she and the president were talking about cover stories for their relationship, as two people in such situations frequently do; how to keep it secret," one source said. [CNN]

TOUR de FRANCE CHAOS

The Tour de France has been thrown into chaos with world number one Laurent Jalabert withdrawing and other riders conducting a go-slow protest. The move delayed the end of the 17th stage from Albertville to Aix-les-Bains by several hours. It was the riders way of complaining about what they say is the heavy-handed nature of a police investigation into drug use. The stage has been declared null and void. Earlier police had searched the hotel rooms of one of Jalabert's former teams for the second time in two days. [ABC News]
