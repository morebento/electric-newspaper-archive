Monday May 18, 1998

Temple Bruer's altar ego
By Adrienne Eccles

Temple Bruer originally produced dry reds only. In response to market demand they have branched out to add two white varietals to their range.

The '97 chenin blanc is an award winner, and that's not surprising; it's a dry-finishing crisp light white with fruity undertones. Chenin blanc is not a highly flavoured grape. In the Loire Valley of France it is used for both sparkling and still wines, and its soft bland flavour have led to its use as a 'filler' in blended wines.

Wine maker David Bruer said, "This is a variety that takes Botrytis well. This particular wine (the '97) has about seven per cent botrytised grapes in it, adding a bit of sweetness and complexity to the fruit."

In contrast with the chenin blanc, which is made to drink now, Temple Bruer turns out a riesling intended for cellaring for 7 - 8 years. Drunk now, the '96 riesling is high in acid, pale coloured with light fruit flavours, just hinting at the aromatic qualities that will develop later.

After a few years in the bottle this wine is expected to develop honey and toast flavours that will reward the patient drinker.

"Restaurants in particular kept asking for whites," said David Bruer. "There seemed to be a school of thought that if you had a Temple Bruer red on the wine list you also had to have a Temple Bruer white."

The ubiquitous chardonnay? Doesn't make one. "Plenty of other people to make chardonnay," says David Bruer.

David Bruer agreed that lately more wine makers were putting grenache into bottles and treating it with more respect.

"So they should," he remarked. "It's a variety that responds well to good handling."

The Temple Bruer grenache is fuller-bodied than many. It has a delightful beetroot colour, lovely rose scent, and gently spiced fruit in the mouth. It's not big on tannin, and there's very little extra wood in the flavour.

"We aged this in old barrels only for seven or eight months. We wanted to keep the fruit dominant. We didn't want to create a shiraz look-alike. This is something different; the fruit flavour makes it on its own," said David Bruer.

Temple Bruer's biggest selling wine is its Cabernet Merlot blend. Designed for easy drinking with potential for medium cellaring, it is a popular choice. Fresh and fruity up front, it suits casual dining.

David Bruer recounted with some satisfaction that the Cabernet Merlot has been placed for the last several years in the State's top 70 "biggest selling wines" in a regular survey commissioned by Southcorp. He said, "We're the only winery with a crush of less than 1,000 tonnes in the top 70."

Also in the $15 range is the '95 Shiraz Malbec. This is a more complex and challenging wine, and a personal favourite of David Bruer.

This wine should be kept in the cellar for a while - the best of its flavours are yet to come. Flavours of chocolate and coconut, toasty oak and a firm tannin structure indicate that this wine will develop well over the next 8 - 10 years.

Tesco's supermarkets in the UK are big overseas buyers of this wine, which surprises David Bruer a little - he regards the Shiraz Malbec as the most challenging in the range.

The '96 Reserve Merlot is a first for Temple Bruer.

"We hadn't made a straight merlot before," said David Bruer. "But that year we noticed some rows of vines seemed to be developing better quality fruit than the others. We marked these vines and hand picked them separately."

The wine has matured in French oak. Its warm rich mulberry flavour is held up by firm tannins, which will soften over the next few years. Pleased with the result, Temple Bruer will continue to make this varietal. Get in early with the first one. Buy it now, cellar it four to five years, and enjoy.
