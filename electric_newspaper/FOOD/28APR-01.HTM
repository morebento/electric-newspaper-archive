Ashton Hills vineyard
By Adrienne Eccles

Back before 1982, the front paddock at Ashton Hills vineyard was growing Brussels Sprouts. The owners removed the sprouts, planted vines, and launched themselves on a journey of discovery and experimentation.

The Adelaide Hills region is a patchwork of microclimates, making for interesting variety in winemaking. Experimentation with different varieties, clones, trellis designs and vineyard management systems has led winemaker Stephen George to develop a range of cool-climate wines that capture the crispness of the Hills air.

The white wines of the Adelaide Hills can be a delight to the senses. Ashton Hills' riesling is their biggest seller. The '97 riesling is pale and delicate, clean citrus flavours with creamy stewed-apple top notes and an acid lift.

Devotees of sparkling wines should try the Salmon Brut. Nothing fishy about this wine. This pinot-chardonnay blend has absorbed the colour of smoked salmon from contact with the pinot noir skins. Salmon Brut offers a crisp and flavoursome mouthful. The acid is refreshing and subtle, the bead prickles the tongue and teases the taste buds. At around $20 the bottle, it's not a quaffer to take to a barbecue; this is a wine that deserves some attention. Full enough in flavour to marry well with food, it is an ideal sparkler for the table.

At 570 metres above sea level, the Ashton Hills vineyard is one of the highest vineyards in the Adelaide Hills. Quintessentially a "cool climate" site, with low mist and cloud, it is ideally suited for the subtle and refined development of Pinot Noir grapes. Stephen George is a craftsman, absolutely committed to and passionate about the unique qualities of Pinot Noir. He handles the 18 clones of pinot grown on the estate with a mix of traditional Burgundian methods and bold experimentation, producing a pinot that is spicy and soft in the mouth.

Standing in the warmth of the tasting room, which doubles as a pottery show-room, looking out over the tumbled-eiderdown landscape of the Piccadilly Valley to the Mt Lofty summit, I reflected that Ashton Hills is a winery worth stopping for.
