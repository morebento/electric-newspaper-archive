Monday July 6, 1998

A passion for Fox hunting
by Adrienne Eccles

If I were a betting woman - which I'm not, being too broke to gamble - I'd have some money on where the next Jimmy Watson trophy might go.

Afficionados of Fox Creek Wines are well acquainted with the talents of the ebullient Sparky Marquis and his partner Sarah Marquis, on whose divinely tuned palate the fortunes of Fox Creek turn.

Fox Creek, for all that it is a relatively new winery, has made a huge name for itself amongst fanciers of big reds.

Runners up in last year's Jimmy Watson Trophy with their Reserve Shiraz, the Fox Creek crew are now creating their 1997 Reserve Shiraz, which promises to be a cracker.

At gatherings around Australia, Friends of Fox Creek have been enjoying the rare privilege of tasting the batches of wine that will be blended together to create this year's masterpiece. I joined some sixty other eager tasters at the Fleurieu Visitors' Centre on the outskirts of McLaren Vale.

We had before us on the tasting table samples of four wines. These were the four batches of shiraz destined, in some combination, to become the latest Reserve Shiraz. We were invited to taste all four and compare notes.

Each batch yielded up its own characteristics. One big and fruity, one redolent of bitter chocolate and licorice... What would be the optimal combination in which to blend them? Argument bounced back and forth about the likely result of blending a lot of this with a little of that.

Divided into teams, we blended up small batches according to our best guesses and tasted the results. Team members with a scientific bent and a steady hand handled the measuring beaker. The white linen tablecloths took on a mottled purple and maroon appearance. Teeth and tongues turned bluish black.

It's extraordinary how combining three or four fairly straightforward components can result in complex and surprising tastes that differ so markedly from the sum of their parts.

The pinnacle of the evening was a `blind tasting' of the blends created by all six teams, with a vote for the most popular.

This exercise has been repeated at similar gatherings across Australia in the lead-up to blending time. I asked Sparky Marquis how much notice they took of the results. He replied that they take them very seriously. The exercise results in a vast cross-section of different tastes and preferences, but no matter how educated the palate, there was a fair amount of agreement amongst people about the style they liked. The results from these `Friends' blending sessions would indeed inform the work to be done at the Fox Creek tasting bench.

Sparky - who can't be described as a retiring character - takes the view that many hands are responsible for a good wine. "Good wine comes from the vineyard," he said. "Even the most fantastic wine makers have to start with good grapes. Anybody with a real passion for wine could make the wines we make, with the fruit we have. We must be the luckiest wine makers in Australia."

His enthusiasm for the high quality grapes was a tribute to his brother in law Paul Watts, vineyard manager at Fox Creek. Conscious of the contribution a fine raw material makes in the process, Paul Watts has set about establishing optimum conditions for cultivating his vineyards and oversees the quality of the product with an eagle eye.

Fox Creek's Reserve Shiraz has been its flagship wine from the start. "We reckon the '96 was the best wine we've ever made," said Sparky Marquis. "It was the runner up in last year's Jimmy Watson; it won Judge's Choice and Consumer's Choice for a '96 Shiraz in Winestate."

And the '97? Sparky and Sarah are confident it will be as good, if not better.

What will it taste like? Some of the blends that were being passed around at the Fleurieu Visitors Centre were pretty tasty. The potential is huge. Add in Sarah's subtle judgement and Sparky's firm skill and the result will be magic.

Good components made from good grapes, blended with care and skill and talent. What more could you want? The '97 Reserve Shiraz is coming soon. Watch your wine merchant's shelves.
