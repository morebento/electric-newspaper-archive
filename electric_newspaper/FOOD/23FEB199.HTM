Monday February 23, 1998

Culinary Conscience
by Random (Randy) Diner

To complain or not to complain. That is the question. Random Diner has asked himself on many an occasion after receiving less than adequate fare, service or value at some of Adelaide's 800 restaurants and cafes.

When average fare is moderately priced, it seems churlish to protest. Equally harsh if the service is off at a "cheapy". But if you are paying prices which imply good food and service, then, according to Randy's law, let the proprietor beware should standards be unreasonable.

The following account may provide impetus to fellow diners to help provide what Randy calls a Culinary Conscience for Adelaide.

Randy and partner joined a party of diners for a Chinese New Year banquet in Adelaide's Beijing Restaurant. Only nine of the promised 12 courses arrived during the course of the evening and in minuscule quantities for the table of 10. One small dish - a spoonful per person - constituted each course.

With every table packed and staff literally fast-stepping to service the crowd, Randy's concerns about a shortfall in dishes roused neither employees nor fellow dining party members - the latter preferring to adopt the famous Australian "no worries" motto and not interfere with the spirit of the Chinese New Year festivities.

No-one could reasonably complain about the quality of the food (standard Oz Chinese cuisine) but at $33 a head, the normally reasonable Randy was, by night's end, feeling short-changed and hungry!

Two days later, having debated the "complain or not to" question with his conscience, Randy decided to contact the restaurant to express his disappointment at what he felt had been a devalued dining experience.

"Where were you sitting?" asked the spokesman in a tone which brought to mind the prosecutorial zeal of the Spanish Inquisition. Randy politely replied that the geographical location of his mealtime misadventure should bear no relation to a 25 per cent rate of non-arrival for promised and paid for courses. Randy further explained that a friend had made the booking and thus he did not want to reveal the location and risk embarrassing the friend.

The restaurant man added, without addressing the absence of food issue, that the $33 fixed-price banquet was very reasonable given the inclusion of entertainment. True. A dragon dance did punctuate the evening, ending with the dancers making the round of tables asking for money from diners.

Declining to say where he had been seated Randy gave his own name and contact telephone number, reiterating that it was his (Randy's) complaint, not that of anyone else at his table.

Two days later, one of Randy's fellow diners (who also complained to Randy on the night about having left hungry) called. She reported that their mutual friend and host was very embarrassed about Randy's complaint!

Huh?

The proprietorial prosecutor had rung every person who had made a booking on that night and asked if a person by the name of Random Diner had been at their table.

He inevitably located Randy's friend and host and relayed Randy's complaint - nothing more. Randy was dumbstruck by the restaurant man's motives and modus-operandi, not to mention annoyed by the unnecessary involvement of his friend in a straightforward, one-on-one, consumer-to-business complaint.

Having been alerted to the embarrassing escalation of his initial query about not getting all the course for the banquet, Randy called the proprietor again. "Now that you know where I was sitting and who I was with, how does that help you deal with my complaint?" he asked. The proprietor's response was to hang up.

So what's the moral of this account?

Randy does not moralise, but he does know he will never return to dine at the Beijing.

He also recommends that far from being cowered by the aforementioned account, diners bear their share of responsibility for attaining a high quality of overall restaurant service in Adelaide by making proprietors aware of bad experiences. It is then up to the owners to act as they see fit.

To balance the equation, Randy can report that just such a complaint (a half-hour wait for the bill after twice requesting it) to the co-proprietor of the Cactus Mexican Cafe in Rundle Street received a courteous and professional response. After verifying the staff problem, she offered an apology and a complimentary Margarita for Randy and his guest.

When Randy next appears for you, prepare yourself for a bullet-proof dining experience. A low profile, city restaurant which consistently meets the challenge of impeccable performance for that "special" night when money is less important than the aftertaste.
