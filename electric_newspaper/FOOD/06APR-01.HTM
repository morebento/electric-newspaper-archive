Monday April 6, 1998

Promise fulfilled
by Adrienne Eccles

It's vintage time. All around the wine-growing areas, you can see the bins full of newly-harvested grapes being hauled off to the crusher. The very earliest autumn colours are showing in the leaves, and the air is full of promise.

If, like me, you're tied to your desk and can't get away to soak up the atmosphere in the wine-growing areas, try the next-best thing: make yourself a solemn promise to take a drive out into wine country sometime really soon and in the meantime head for the tastings at your local bottle shop.

In this spirit, I became acquainted this week with the new release reds from Penfolds and the new Yalumba Growers' Range. While the vines are being stripped of their fruit for the '98 harvest, new releases from past vintages are coming into the bottle shops.

Penfolds Kalimna Shiraz is one of the popular work-horses of the range, a warm bosomy wine, affordable and reliable.

Moving up the range, things can be expected to get more interesting, with more scope for vintage-to-vintage variation, and so it proves.

Last year's ('94) Coonawarra Shiraz Bin 128 was an award winner that typified the classic character of cool-climate shiraz. The new '95 Bin 128 is not one of the better years. Dry, tannic, with a sourish finish, it lacks palate weight.

Sweeter, plusher, the '95 Bin 389 Cabernet Shiraz shows a better balance between fruit and oak, with a firm structure holding up the flavours.

Predictably, the '94 St Henri Shiraz - Cabernet Sauvignon, nearly double the price at close to $40, is the pick of the bunch: a big raspberry jam scent and flavour, with plenty of rounded shiraz character evident. This wine will mellow down after cellaring and develop into a soft and sensuous mouthful.

In the lead-up to their hundred and fiftieth birthday, Yalumba has released a suite of varietal wines under the new Growers' Range label, which replaces the Family Reserve range of yesteryear. Priced around $13-$14 a bottle and soft enough for consumption now, the quality is even, if unchallenging. The range features both whites and reds, but I concentrated my attention on the red end of the spectrum.

The '95 Barossa Shiraz is strong on the pepper and spice, a medium bodied wine soft and light enough for easy drinking now. The '95 Cabernet Sauvignon - Merlot blend, with fruit from Langhorne Creek, Barossa and Coonawarra, is gentler, milder. Both are value for money wines that will twin well with meals or stand alone as conversation-helpers.

Bottled Grenache is appearing more and more frequently on wine-shop shelves these days. It's a quick-growing, easy drinking variety, cheap to produce. Once the province of blends and bulk wines it is increasingly appearing as a soft quaffing wine suitable for consumption over lunch or conversation at any time of the year. Chill it for summer, consume it by the fire in winter.

The Yalumba '96 Bush Vine Grenache is an easygoing drink-now wine: soft, plush, and surprisingly pink. At around $13 the bottle, it's an affordable quaffer.

The Penfolds Barossa Valley Old Vine grenache-shiraz-mourvedre blend is more expensive and has greater pretensions to being taken seriously. Its structure is more complex, the flavour firmer, but in comparison with the Yalumba grenache's festive watermelon character it seems slightly dour.

I'm promising myself that trip out to breathe the air of the vineyards before vintage is over. And how is the vintage doing? Gossip is that you should start saving your pennies: this is going to be a good harvest in the Coonawarra, and up in the Clare Valley, growers and wine-makers are rubbing their hands in gleeful expectation. (Not so lucky all over Australia: hands up all those who know which WA grape-growing area has had five inches of rain during vintage?)
