Monday April 20, 1998

Pinots exposed
by Adrienne Eccles

The most amazing thing about Pinot Noirs is that they are all different. No other varietal presents quite the range of surprising variations as Pinot.

Taking a leisurely wander through Edinburgh Cellars sampling some of the twenty-plus Pinots from Australia, New Zealand and France, I was struck by the contrasting tastes and aromas. Pinot enthusiasts aver that this variety is the embodiment of complexity and delicacy at the same time: softness with richness, fragrant and silky.

Certainly it is a fragile grape, and difficult to grow. Pinot Noir does best in cool, damp climates on well-drained sites. In France, it is grown almost exclusively in the chilly north eastern regions of Burgundy and Champagne. The vine buds early, the grapes ripen early. The longer the berries can stay on the vine before becoming too ripe the more complex the wine will be, but too much heat and sun cause Pinot to ripen before it has a chance to develop its full potential.

In recent times, Australian Pinots have been doing better. Cool-climate grape producing regions such as Tasmania, the Adelaide Hills and Yarra Valley are producing wines worth your attention.

Two offerings from Piper's Brook, in the Northern Tasmanian Tamar Valley, were offered for tasting.

Ninth Island '97 Pinot Noir sells for about $17 a bottle. It has a fresh-mushroom nose and bright acid finish. Its more complex cousin, the Pellion Pinot Noir ('96 vintage, with grapes drawn from the same area) offers cherry flavours, still with the acid lift. At $29 it's not a budget wine, but has enough complexity to do well at the dinner table.

Palliser Estate's '96 Martinborough hails from the south-east of New Zealand's North Island. Very different from the Tasmanian Pinots, it offers a spicy nose and the soft silky mouth feel sought after by Pinot enthusiasts.

The Mt Barker region of Western Australia is producing some interesting examples of the variety, too.

The Goundrey '94 provided a talking point as an example of a Pinot with some bottle age. Received wisdom is that Australian Pinot should be drunk earlier rather than later, and cellared for no more than three or four years. Displaying mushroom characteristics and very little fruit, the Goundrey has a surprising initial burst of flavour, but not great length.

A Plantagenet '96, also from Mt Barker, had a soft mouthfeel, slightly medicinal raspberry flavours, with a pleasing rounded aftertaste. Surprisingly, this wine tastes completely different from its aroma. This is something you'll notice about Pinot Noir: what happens in the nose is not necessarily a mirror image of what happens on the tongue. It's a variety that can offer a few surprises.

The great Burgundies of that region in France are made from this grape. There were some French Pinots offered for tasting, including two from Joseph Drouhin: '95 Laforet and '95 Cote de Nuits-Villages. Light in colour and palate weight, they were far more tannic and astringent than any of the antipodean wines, but markedly flavoursome.

I confess, gentle reader, I lacked the courage to taste my way through the full range on offer and stopped at about the half-way mark. The overall impression of my encounter with Pinot Noir is of immense variety, some wines offering surprising delicacy and elusive flavours.

Pinots tend to be pricey: it's a finicky, low-yielding grape, and growers don't produce much of it. Choose carefully, taste it if you can before you buy, and don't let the food overpower it. It's a variety that can provide a talking point at the table.
