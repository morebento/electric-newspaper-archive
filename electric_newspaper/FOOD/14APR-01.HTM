Tuesday April 14, 1998

Wine tastings
by Adrienne Eccles

In 1917, John James McWilliam brought 40,000 vine cuttings by bullock dray from Junee to Hanwood, to be planted in the new Murrumbidgee Irrigation Area. The Riverina now claims to be the second largest wine region in Australia. The climate is reliable and warm, and irrigation provides an abundant water supply. McWilliams' Hanwood winery is a large modern facility, producing a range of table and fortified wines.

At a tasting conducted by the genial Brian McWilliam, I became acquainted with the table wines released under the Hanwood label. As well as being offered for domestic consumption, this range is exported to the UK where, McWilliam says, it is meeting with a fair degree of success.

The range consists of two reds (a Shiraz and a Cabernet Sauvignon) and three whites (Chardonnay, Semillon-Chardonnay, and Sauvignon Blanc).

Fans of New Zealand and other cool-climate Sauvignon Blancs will meet a horse of a different colour in the Hanwood range. Here we have an example of a wine produced from a cool-climate grape grown in a warm area. The familiar flavours of kiwi-fruit, lime and green capsicum are there, but muted; the overall impression is of a drier, plainer wine.

The Sauvignon Blanc and Semillon-Chardonnay both exhibit light grassy flavours with an acid lift and a clean finish. The Chardonnay is, predictably, heavier on the palate and offers some melon and fig overtones. There is only a hint of oak, not the obvious wood prominent in some Chardonnays. At the table, chill these wines well to bring out their crispness, and be cautious about twinning them with strongly flavoured foods: they'll be easily overpowered.

The reds are both light to medium bodied wines. McWilliams have been cautious with the wood treatment here, too. The Cabernet Sauvignon is leafy, minty and the softer of the two. The Shiraz is light, with a herbaceous flavour and a touch of spiciness, but lacking in body and length. There is little fruit character in either the Shiraz or Cab Sav. If you buy these, drink them now. They'll do best at the dinner table with Italian foods, roast lamb or steak, or with a cheese platter.

Before leaving the Hanwood range, it's worth pausing for a word about the Aboriginal art featured on the labels. In the now-familiar `dot painting' style, each wine features a different image painted by Aboriginal artists. Each painting tells a story. The Chardonnay label features a painting by Nancy Nabarula, "Womens' Corroboree at Ti Tree", which shows women celebrating a dance during the initiation ceremony.
