Monday July 20, 1998

More than a drop of water at the vale: Stephen John wines
by Adrienne Eccles

Winemaking has been Stephen John's chosen career for over 25 years, and for much of that time he worked with the big firms: 15 years with B Seppelt and Sons and nearly as long with Wolf Blass Wines. If you can count a winemaker's worth in terms of medals and trophies, Stephen John is up there with the best. He amassed for Seppelts and Wolf Blass an enviable number of gold medals and awards, and his record of nine gold medals in one wine show (as White Wine Maker for Wolf Blass) remains unbeaten.

In 1994, Stephen and his wife purchased a six hectare vineyard just outside Watervale, and established a winery with a view to producing small volumes of high class table wines with distinctive varietal characteristics. Stephen John offers a range of six wines, all distinctively different, and elegant wines every one.

The Watervale Riesling is fresh and clean from start to finish, with that rainwater-purity of taste Watervale rieslings are known for. For those who enjoy aged whites, this one will cellar for up to ten years and can be expected to produce soft toasty characters that will reward a long wait. (Although the frequent practice is to drink white wines young, Clare Valley wine makers in particular will encourage you to keep your rieslings to develop a little bottle age. Drinking an aged riesling is a different experience, but something you should try.)

When he purchased the property, Stephen John found a planting of old Pedro Ximinez vines, dating back to 1926. He uses these to make a soft, lightly oaked, dry wine that has a silky mouthfeel, is surprisingly weighty in the mouth, but full of clean herbaceous flavours.

Pedro Ximinez originated in Spain, and vines were brought to Watervale in the very early years of settlement. Although less well known to the average wine drinker today, it is a variety with a long history. "When I was with Wolf Blass at Quelltaler," said Stephen John, "Our Quelltaler Hock was the biggest selling wine in Australia. It was made exclusively from Pedro Ximinez grapes. At one time, Pedro Ximinez was the grape that was used for Great Western Champagne."

"There has been some confusion in the consumer mind between the tastes of oaked and unoaked Chardonnays," remarked Stephen John. "Currently, there seems to be a shift toward lighter, softer styles, less wood and more fruit. Clare is not an area highly recognised for Chardonnays; it hasn't ever produced big-flavoured styles." His own Chardonnay is light, with very little wood, redolent of melon and citrus with a clean sharp finish.

Stephen John Shiraz is much in demand amongst cellar door customers. Sourced from dry-grown vines on the estate and elsewhere in the area, the '96 is a medium bodied wine, a clear purple-red colour with grassy notes, hints of spice and musky violets. Soft and velvety, it is a very approachable wine.

The Cabernet Sauvignon is made from low-cropping vines on the estate, and matured in French oak. It has a firm tannin structure and rich berry fruit, and while it is extremely enjoyable now it will benefit from some bottle age, so buy it and hide it from yourself for a few years.

Stephen John's biggest seller is his sparkling burgundy, Traugott Cuvee, named after an ancestor who gloried in the name Traugott John. A blend of shiraz and pinot noir, Traugott Cuvee has a deep garnet hue and fills the mouth with luscious berry fruit and a soft yeasty bead. Delicious!

Stephen John offered some thoughts on making and drinking wine. "I prefer to drink reds myself. When I really want to enjoy life I turn to a cabernet with some bottle age," he said. "Reds are easier to make, too. White wine making is more technically demanding. You have less time to get it right."

For Stephen John, discipline is an important aspect of wine making. He said, "You can't beat the training and experience you get working for a big firm. Making wines to order, making them to a syle, is great training. I'm now able to enjoy the luxury of making wines that I enjoy. Young aspiring wine makers who come up here sometimes say this is the kind of operation they'd like to work in. But if you start out as a winemaker with a small winery, that's all you'll ever be. Work for a big company, gain some experience, you can go anywhere."

* Stephen John Wines is located at Watervale in the Clare Valley, and is open to the public weekdays, weekends and public holidays.
