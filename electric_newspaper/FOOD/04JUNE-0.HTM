Thursday June 4, 1998

A tale of two cities: East and West pubs in Adelaide
by the Random Diner

Suburban dining out in Adelaide would appear to be a hit and miss affair judging by Random Diner's recent excursions away from the bright city lights. Word of mouth sent Randy westwards and then eastwards recently, to sample weeknight fare at two hotels which have made their dining areas as cosy as their bars and lounges.

It was a case of "west is best" and "least in the east".

Just a few blocks west of what was once the dusty, noisy, Rowley Park speedway, intrepid gourmet hunters can bag a handsome meal at The Excelsior Hotel in Brompton. On a recommendation, Randy and partner, joined by another couple, foolishly arrived one recent, chilly, Friday night without a booking.

"I'm sorry, but we're always booked out on Friday and Saturday nights," lamented the mature-age waitress.

A combination of disappointment, hunger and the cold must have radiated from the frustrated foursome.

"Wait here and I'll see what I can do," she offered with a smile.

In a spirit of "can do" which would shame most of the trendy "attitude" cafes around town, the waitress enlisted the help of a fellow employee, rustled up a spare table and chairs and squeezed the newly-arriveds in between two existing tables.

Pretty impressive considering the pressure the staff was under to wait on the full house.

What followed was equally impressive. To avoid minutiae, let it just be said that the two couples three-coursed their way through faultlessly presented and prepared cuisine, together with beer and house wine, for around $30 a head.

Randy reckoned he had hit the jackpot with his main course of smoked salmon salad, layered with eggplant tempura and covered with a raita sauce. Known far and wide for his ability to "put it away", Randy struggled to finish the extra-generous serving and wondered aloud if the chef had a salmon farm attached to the kitchen.

With its pokies parlour tucked discreetly behind the fully carpeted, dining area, The Excelsior provided a surprisingly tranquil dining environment and one which attracted a diverse socio-economic group of patrons.

Icing on the cake was provided by friendly staff who, despite their Road Runner-like footwork between kitchen and tables, made regular visits to the quartet to check that everything was in order.

In a rare gesture by Randy, he left a $5 tip in recognition not only of high quality service and cuisine, but also the extraordinary effort made to accommodate four people who should have known better.

Cut to the following week. A Thursday night. Randy and partner were hosting an old friend visiting from Hobart, together with his brother. They decided that, based upon their recent, successful, suburban hotel experience, it would be a good idea to point the compass in the opposite direction and take their Tasmanian companions to a cosy, eastern suburbs pub.

Randy's partner suggested the Maylands. She said the reputation of the Clappis family's Italian food preceded them. Done.

Entering the warm, earthy, dining room there was a sense of anticipation about a convivial night to be shared among good friends over good food. Only the dearth of patrons - just two other groups of diners - lessened the atmosphere.

Randy and co. were briefly greeted by a young waitress who pointed towards the bar to order food and drinks. She then pointed towards the table area and directed them to sit there, like a railway porter indicating the correct platform.

Drinks were self-service order at the bar. The menus were produced only after the waitress noticed the party's collective eyestrain caused by the distant blackboard list. It was to be her last meaningful encounter with the foursome for some time.

After ordering at the front bar, a basket bearing warm bread was delivered by another staffer, but there were no plates or cutlery. One of the visitors, displaying traditional, Tasmanian, pioneering spirit, ventured forth and found knives and plates beneath the distant blackboard.

The four diners then suddenly realised they were competing for the one, tiny butter dish which accompanied the bread basket. It held the equivalent of one pat of butter. With the bread losing its heat and no staff to be seen, Randy took the law into his own hands, strode to the kitchen and, in his best Oliver Twist voice, asked the milling hands for some more butter.

"Not a problem", came the reply. Randy returned victorious. Patrons 1. Staff nil.

The cavalry then arrived in the shape of Hardy's Insignia Cabernet Sauvignon Shiraz. It was to be the best spent $18.50 of the night. The red wine, open fire and warm surroundings soothed the group until food arrived.

Randy's double-take did not get past his partner who, intimately knowing Mr Diner's demeanour, looked down at his lamb and potato curry and smelt a rat.

The small dish contained five pieces of lamb which would each fit neatly onto a 50 cent piece. It was accompanied by one cup of dryish, cooked rice. At $13.50, Randy could not help feeling it was he and not the lamb which had been fleeced.

The Bass Strait emigrants fared better in the portion stakes, but the fresh salads which the menu said would accompany their meals did not arrive with the meal.

Staff were either resting from the strain of waiting on three tables or were perhaps ducking out to keep an eye on NYPD Blue - whatever the reason they were as accessible as a Friday night parking space in Gouger Street.

By the time our original waitress appeared, was reminded about the salads' non-arrival and returned with them, the Tasmanians had finished their mains.

Their politeness in the face of very ordinary mainland hospitality made the staff's indifference towards the night's sloppiness all the more insufferable.

For the record, it must be said that the food was agreeable. Randy's partner ordered kranske - two sausages which came with beetroot, cranberries, pickled goodies and vegetables as a side order. Although tasty, she thought that at $11.90 they were a bit pricey for glorified pub bangers. Randy begged to differ.

Suffice to say the party did not stay for coffee. They all agreed North Adelaide seemed safer.

Adelaide is a tough market for eatery owners. If the Maylands operators can compete with all those other hungry hotels which have renovated their kitchens and upgraded their menus then good luck to them. But indifferent service, highish prices, cut corners and servings dished out by the portion police is pushing your luck.

The moral of Randy's suburban sojourns? Go west!
