Friday May 22, 1998

Adelaide - the energy comes back
by Legh Davis, Liberal Member in the Legislative Council

For many people born in South Australia and now living interstate or overseas, a return visit to Adelaide can be something of a shock. The city has lost a lot of energy, reflecting the high level of vacant office space and years of neglect by successive State Governments and the Adelaide City Council. Adelaide has had a tired look for some years.

But the city is clearly re-girding its loins. The inspired efforts of some individuals and more critical public debate in recent years about the importance of rejuvenating the capital city of the State have achieved results.

Firstly, there are some lovely precincts which have been created. Hutt Street traders can take great credit for the transformation of their previously largely neglected boulevard. The roses, the trees and the energy flowing from the variety of eateries has made it a most attractive street for both local residents and the community at large. Rundle Street East has come a long way since I opened a kite shop in 1976. In those days people used it only as a thoroughfare to Rundle Mall. But today it is alive with activity, offering an impressive array of cafes and restaurants, retail outlets and entertainment. Gouger Street has also been transformed by some judicious streetscaping.

O’Connell Street, North Adelaide is at last looking attractive. It will be fully restored to respectability when the development of the long neglected Le Cornu site is complete. There are also promising plans for Leigh Street which should do much to rejuvenate that lovely thoroughfare.

There have also been dramatic and stylish improvements to streetscaping several key entrances to the city. Henley Beach Road has the potential in time to become the Norwood Parade of the western suburbs. The Burbridge Road upgrade is also welcome, particularly in view of the fact it is an entry and exit point to the Adelaide Airport. Port Road, adjacent to the west parklands, has also benefited from a significant capital works programme.

In the heart of the city work is currently underway on repaving with quality slate both sides of King William Street between Rundle Mall and North Terrace. Early indications are that Adelaide Council has got this project right. Quite a relief after the fiasco of the major Rundle Mall upgrade, which has been less than successful. Cracked pavers, flower boxes that look like chook houses and the Darth Vader lights have underwhelmed most critical observers. The Council’s remarkable decision to close off the slip road on the western side of Victoria Square to create a huge restaurant complex has denied any chance of adopting a holistic solution to any possible reworking of the square.

Undoubtedly the biggest challenge ahead for both the State Government and the Adelaide City Council is North Terrace. This unique cultural boulevard has been the victim of committee city. Endless consultants’ reports and committees of inquiry have amounted to nothing. For years the signage was inadequate, inaccurate and ugly. The stobie poles remain as a rusting reminder to years of inactivity.

However the North Terrace Precincts plan, just released by Adelaide 21, outlines an exciting programme to upgrade in and around North Terrace. 1998 has started well for the Terrace, with the recent opening of the Playford Hotel. It features quality architecture, with a happy combination of neo-classical detailing and modern materials.

Adelaide 21 has recommended to the Government and the Council that there should be a "strengthening .. of .. the functional and visual connection from North Terrace to other parts of the city." It argues this can be achieved through establishing new links from North Terrace to the River Torrens and strengthening existing links through ASER, King William Street, Kintore Avenue and the University of Adelaide. The report recommends a consistent approach to planting, pavement, signage, lighting, street furniture and artwork.

Adelaide 21 has again emphasised the importance of "establishing clear and inviting passages" between the commercial precincts, Rundle Mall, Rundle Street and Hindley Street and the cultural precinct of North Terrace.

Adelaide is turning the corner. Better times are ahead. The energy is returning.
