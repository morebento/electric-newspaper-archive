Friday May 22, 1998

The East Indies: an artificial republic
by Hendrik Gout*

The Dutch were the world's major maritime power in the 16th century, and their merchant ships took the aptly named Trade Winds to the Spice Islands. Here, the Europeans discovered an archipelago of 13,000 islands, at least a dozen of which had spawned empires which had come... and gone.

The first of these empires was Srivijaya. It arose in southern Sumatra, and like the Dutch who came so much later was maritime in character. In the fifth, sixth, and seventh centuries AD, Srivijaya controlled the sea routes of the Malacca and Sunda Straits. In time Srivijaya extended its influence into the coastal areas of western Java and the Malay peninsular.

In part simultaneously with Srivijaya, a series of kingdoms emerged in Java. Whereas Srivijaya was a maritime empire, these were based on the control of irrigated rice fields. The first was Mataram, in central Java, which flourished at the beginning of the eighth century. This was followed in the 10th century by Airlangga, whose control extended to Bali. At least 18 greater and lesser kingdoms followed, including the Madjaphit, Djanggala, and finally the Mataram re-emerged as a power in central Java. Its sultan extended power from Kalimantan to Sumatra, and things were looking good for the Mataram expansionists until they kept bumping into European traders, who were buying cloves and selling them in Amsterdam for a profit of 2,500 per cent.

But the Dutch weren't the only Europeans. The Portuguese came first, at the beginning of the 16th century, and by 1511 Malacca was in their control. Next came the Spaniards in 1521, and the first Englishman was Sir Frances Drake, who circled the globe in 1577-80, visiting Halmahera and Java. The Dutch were late arrivals in 1596.

The Portuguese wanted only to establish fortress trading posts. This they did at Ambon, Ternate, and Timor. The Spanish were simply passing through on their way to the Philippines. The Dutch, however, had more serious intentions. After four years of preliminary voyaging, they too set up trading bases, and then sought to develop a monopoly. They forced out the Portuguese by 1641, fought an unofficial war with the English, and then encountered difficulties in enforcing their desires for monopoly on the local rulers.

By force, and by the end of the 17th century, the Dutch were the undisputed masters in the archipelago.

Even so, the Dutch East India Company's policy was trade, not military incursions. Military action was rightly seen as expensive and interruptive. Local rebellions still broke out sporadically and the Dutch had still, reluctantly, to crush them. As often as not, the Dutch intervened in local wars between rival Javanese kingdoms, and there were also Chinese migrant troubles in Java. Chinese settlers had been in the archipelago since before the coming of the Portuguese, but their increasing numbers brought violent reaction from the Javanese. In 1740 some 10,000 Chinese died in ten days of bloody massacre, which the Dutch could have halted.

The British came in next. The King of the Netherlands had fled the war with France - arriving in England with a suitcase and a few vassals. Britain took over in the Far East. Thomas Stamford Raffles became Lieutenant Governor of Java and its dependencies. The Dutch moved back in 1815, but for the next 40 years sporadic resistance broke out in Sumatra, Java, southern Kalimantan, southern Sulawesi, Ternate, Aambon, Lombok, and Bali.

Then Japan marched in, and things got really bad for everyone - for the Dutch there (some families had lived in the Indies for three hundred years, longer than whites have been in Australia), for the Chinese, the Sumatrans, and the people of the other islands.

When the Japanese slunk back out, defeated, the war-time puppet Government headed by Java's Dr Sukarno was poised to fill the vacuum. This he did, against Dutch wishes but with Australian support, and then followed not a federation of independent States, but a new Javanese empire, more ruthless (the Japanese excepting) than the region had seen for many centuries.

The various parts of the archipelago had never felt ‘Indonesian'. They saw themselves as Sumatran, for example, not Indonesian. The stone-age hunter-gatherers in West Irian had nothing in common with the Balinese, or the people of the Malay peninsular.

The Javanese warriors took over an entire archipelago and its different peoples, its different languages - there are 25 major languages and two to three hundred variations spoken - its different religions, and its different cultures and histories.

Local rebellions against the new colonialists began, and were suppressed as they still are in East Timor. While Timor is the latest and best known example of Javanese military domination of the region, it is certainly not the first, nor the second, nor the third.

Indonesia is an artificial empire, forged from the ruins of the Second World War, and ruled from Jakarta with only token local autonomy.

And yet, it is generally at peace.

Autocratic rule has not meant complete misery, as the region has known at other time of its long history. Bribery and official corruption has not meant crime in the streets. ‘Indonesia' is a Greek word, meaning East Indian Islands. It is a mixture of East and West. It is a vast land, of polluted cities and vast rainforests, of sparkling coral reefs and canals which run as cesspools, of magic and mystery, violence and blood.

Indonesia is a lesson in nationalism, international intrigue, and survival.

*Hendrik Gout was Made in Indonesia
