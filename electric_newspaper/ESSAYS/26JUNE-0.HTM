Friday June 26, 1998

Tom Playford can rest easy on ETSA
by John Spoehr *

The South Australian Democrats have made an historic decision to oppose the privatisation of ETSA/Optima. After months of consultations and painstaking care in the preparation of their case, the Democrats have sent a clear message to economic rationalists and free market ideologues - the public sector should continue to play a key role in economic and social development.

There were many good reasons to oppose privatisation in this case. The Government had no mandate to push the sale through the Parliament. Before last year's State election the Liberals consistently opposed the sale.

Upon re-election, the Liberal Government predictably reversed its decision. It did this by distorting the findings of the Auditor General.

The Government claimed the State risked the loss of $1 billion if it did not sell ETSA/Optima. The reality was very different. There was no threat to the loss of $1 billion worth of competition payments, nor any compulsion in National Competition Policy to sell publicly owned enterprises such as ETSA/Optima.

As the debate on ETSA proceeded the Government claimed that there would be a net gain of around $150 million per year from the sale. This claim was based on a flawed report commissioned by the Government and undertaken by former Auditor General, Tom Sheridan. The eight page report appeared to be hastily put together. A closer analysis revealed that Mr Sheridan had omitted some key elements in his calculation of the impact of the sale.

In a report commissioned by the United Trades and Labor Council, ETSA unions and the PSA, Professor John Quiggin and I demonstrated, that once the omissions in the Sheridan report are taken into account, the sale would result in an $880 million loss over 10 years if the sale price were around $5.5 billion. Indeed a sale price of over $7 billion would be required to achieve a small net financial benefit.

In the end the State Government never successfully challenged the arguments that were put to them. Their responses were hollow and poorly researched.

The decision to retain ETSA/Optima in public ownership is a victory for fact over fallacy, reality over rhetoric. John Quiggin and I are pleased to have played a role in this victory. The Democrats are to be congratulated for putting the longer term interest of South Australian ahead of short term political interests. Indeed the irony at this time of great political disaffection may be that the applause for the decision lasts much longer than the predictable headlines in the Tiser over the next week.

It is well to remember that the debate on privatisation is set to intensify rather than abate in the wake of this decision. The Government's failure to provide a convincing case for the sale of ETSA/Optima will stiffen its resolve to push through other proposed privatisations such as the sale of TAB/Lotteries Commission, Homestart, WorkCover and the Ports Corporation. In all of these cases the arguments provided by the Government are as unconvincing as that developed around ETSA/Optima.

The reference point for the debate should be sound research as well as crafty politics. The formula for successfully arguing the case for public ownership of the energy industry may be clearer but there is no room for complacency. The case for public ownership is not automatic. It must be carefully argued in each case. An historic opportunity now exists to make this case more powerfully than ever.

A few months ago I said that Tom Playford would be turning in his grave over the possibility of the sale of ETSA. He can now rest easy.

* John Spoehr is deputy director of the Centre for Labour Research at the University of Adelaide.
