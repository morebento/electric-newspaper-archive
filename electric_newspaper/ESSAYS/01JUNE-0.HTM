Monday June 1, 1998

The pokie, the net, and the addict
by Caroline Schaefer, MLC

The Social Development Committee will always be locked into contentious enquires. I am a recent appointee, but the previous Presiding Member certainly had more than her share of controversy when the Committee reported on prostitution. Eventually, the Committee of six brought down three separate sets of recommendations.

Hopefully, the present Committee will reach consensus when it reports on the enquiry into gambling in South Australia.

Our actual reference is to inquire into and report on the extent of gambling addiction in South Australia and the social and economic consequences of that level of addiction, the social, economic and other effects of the introduction of gaming machines into South Australia, and any other related matters.

So it becomes clear that we are not, as is popularly believed, 'an inquiry into gaming machines', but must make much more wide-ranging recommendations.

Certainly the current climate (ably assisted by the Honourable Nick Xenophon!), is to blame poker machines for all the evils of society from bankruptcy to bunions. Certainly there has been a heavy emphasis placed on the gaming machine section of gambling by many of the 69 witnesses who have appeared before us, but as a committee, we would be derelict in our duty to look at only one section of an activity which has become very much a part of the Australian culture, gambling.

We have taken evidence from a broad range of representatives from all legal codes of gambling - racing, lotteries, TAB, etc as well as psychologists, counsellors and members of the general public and must now try to bring down findings which balance the rights of the individual with the social and welfare needs of society. How many problem gamblers are there? Does their plight justify a "nanny state" attitude? Are the businesses disadvantaged offset by those that make a handsome profit? What of Government revenue and what price human misery?

It would be improper for me to make any comments which would in any way pre-empt the decisions of the Committee, so really I am severely restricted in what I can say. I merely wish to point out in your illustrious publication that most parliamentary inquiries (and this one in particular) are considerably more complex than the daily press would have you believe.

Perhaps one of the most concerning aspects of gambling is the opening up of interactive and internet gambling.

Last month I attended the conference "Gambling, Technology & Society: Regulatory Challenges for the 21st Century" in Sydney which was sponsored by the Australian Institute of Criminology in conjunction with the Australian Institute for Gambling Research with Committee Members Mr Joe Scalzi MP, Mr Michael Atkinson MP and two staff members. Regulators, law makers, the gambling industry, churches, charities and counsellors in fact, most of the key players, were represented and the findings were a revelation.

We were quickly informed that internet and interactive gambling - even virtual casinos - are already available and will be widespread throughout Australia within a year or two.

Armed with a 'smart card', it will be possible to bet on anything, anywhere, 24 hours a day, without leaving home, with no guarantees and (at this stage) no limits. Can you imagine coming home from work to find your partner had bankrupted the family before lunch? Suddenly poker machines seem obsolete and quite innocent!

My view is that the personal tragedies we now hear about will be terrifyingly multiplied by this form of gambling - at least playing the pokies, patronising the TAB, going to the races, even buying a lottery ticket all require some form of human interaction, electronic gambling does not.

It was generally agreed by speakers at the conference that Australia has an international reputation second to none for its responsible regulation of all forms of gambling and the consensus seemed to be that there needs to be a collaborative effort by all States and Territories to regulate.

An international collaborative effort would be even more effective, but is in fact, unlikely.

There appeared to be three schools of thought on what form regulation should take:

  1. Those, like Senator Grant Chapman from South Australia, who spoke passionately for total prohibition. This is the stance being attempted by the United States of America with its recently introduced Kyl Bill and most would agree that on the surface, this is an appealing solution. But the young technocrats with whom I spoke assured me that prohibition simply means using off-shore facilities with no method of control. They say prohibition is impossible.
  2. Those who would go down the 'laissez-faire' path of total deregulation (which mainly appeals to those who believe they can make a fortune out of others' gambling). And
  3. 'Carpé Diem' - seize the day - build on our world-wide reputation to introduce fair regulation.

Regulatory controls suggested included:

  • Outlaw credit betting
  • Ensure games are fair, profitable and publicly acceptable (thus enticing gamblers to stay on-shore)
  • Regulate for self limits, that is individuals nominate a weekly/monthly spending limit
  • Regulate for on-line counselling
  • Monitor website advertising.

Certainly, there were more questions raised than answers given. I am somewhat heartened by the knowledge that gaming ministers throughout Australia are attempting to address the problem with draft regulations, but there is little doubt that, as with information, the Œweb‚ will open a floodgate of gambling opportunities. Legislators can only try to keep up.

As for the Social Development Committee Report on Gambling - watch this space!

* Caroline Schaefer, a Liberal Member of the Legislative Councill, is Presiding Member of the Social Development Committee
