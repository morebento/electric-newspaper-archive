Monday July 20, 1998

Rann - a song and dance man without the music
by Legh Davis, Liberal Member in the Legislative Council

The media has had plenty of politics out of the South Australian Parliament this year but has shown a curious reluctance to probe behind the scenes in the Labor Party. It's not a pretty sight.

At the moment Labor Leader, Mike Rann is facing plenty of critics in the Parliamentary Labor Party who are questioning his cynical stand on ETSA privatisation. As one Labor MP whispered in my ear recently, "He's playing King Canute with his trousers rolled down!"

Mr Rann is at his best darting and weaving with clever if not superficial one liners which are great for the eight-second grab on television news. But he is a bit like a scratchy ticket. If you scratch him, more often than not, there's nothing there!

Just look at Mr Rann's record on economic issues. As I have mentioned on more than one occasion, he has no credentials and credibility in economic and financial management. Mr Rann railed against the startup of Roxby Downs and he lashed out against Liberal Party probing on the State Bank before its ignominious collapse in February 1991.

A lack of financial smarts - that's been the problem with the Labor Party in South Australia for a long time. Most of its members have been union officials, teachers or research officers with Labor Members of Parliament. Can you think of any Bannon/Arnold ministers who had any practical business or financial background? You wouldn't worry the scorer on that question.

Now ask that question about the crop of 13 new ALP Members who came into Parliament after the October 1997 State election. The Labor Party is still running on empty, except for Terry Cameron MLC, who has a healthy understanding and appreciation of how business works. He has had some practical experience in the private sector and it shows in his questions and speeches in the Parliament.

Apparently this background is anathema to Mike Rann and Mr Cameron is very much on the outer. Anyone observing proceedings in the Legislative Council would readily agree that Mr Cameron is by far and away the most effective and pugnacious Parliamentary performer for the Labor Party by a country mile.

Mr Rann's lack of financial savvy shows. Anyone bothering to read his nine page speech in reply to the recent State Budget would roll their eyes in disbelief. There is plenty of rhetoric but no serious attempt to articulate an alternative budget strategy. Mr Rann apparently is under the delusion the State is travelling easily. He still has not recognised the financial hurt that South Australia suffered through the $4 billion of losses in State Bank and SGIC. Anyone who has had a severe financial setback knows how tough it is to fight back. For some it may take years and for others it will be a lifetime of misery. The options for fixing a major financial problem, whether it be for an individual, a business or a State, are limited. In the case of debt, the solution is to massively increase income and/or slash expenditure, or to sell an asset. Mr Rann doesn't seem to understand that point. He has attacked the Liberal Government for increasing income and/or slashing expenditure and is now castigating them for selling assets to reduce debt. Somehow Mr Rann thinks you can just wind the altimeter back to zero on this sky-high State debt and step out without any parachute being required.

Since the Liberal Party came to government in late 1993, developments around the nation have meant that the State's difficult financial position will be exacerbated unless remedial action is taken. South Australia already has by far the highest State debt per head of all mainland States. Queensland is debt-free and Victoria is heading that way in a hurry. In New South Wales you can write your own ticket on the fact that a decision to privatise its electricity industry will be made after the March 1999 State election irrespective of which party wins government. New South Wales will also be debt-free.

So dear readers, you don't have to have an economics degree or even be an ex-journalist like Mike Rann, to understand that South Australia will be fighting the other States with one financial arm tied behind its economic back. Our mainland State competitors will be able to devote every dollar in their budgets to vital services and support for their communities and business sectors. But in 1998/99 the South Australian Government can only spend 83.6 cents of every dollar it raises in taxes and charges because 16.4 cents of this income goes in interest payments on debt. These interest payments for 1998/99 are estimated at $728 million.

I can't write any slower than this, but I think Mike Rann might get the drift. He can always ring me to discuss it if he doesn't understand.

And it is not only in the debt stakes that the world has moved on since 1993. The National Electricity Market has become a reality and will start on November 15, 1998. It is a market, you will remember, created by, yes, a Federal Labor Government. South Australia, Queensland, New South Wales, Victoria and the ACT will join the National Electricity Market when it opens.

Victoria has already privatised its electricity industry. It will happen in New South Wales next year and Queensland is also likely to follow.

Initially this National Electricity Market will allow the 27 major consumers of power in South Australia, such as WMC, General Motors and Mitsubishi, (which together account for 17% of the State's electricity) to join the national grid and buy power from any source.

But in time everyone, including Mike Rann, will be able to buy power from this national market, which is expected to have up to 30 companies retailing electricity in South Australia in competition with ETSA.

Mike, the big questions which I would like to see you respond to in The Electric Newspaper are -

  • Can Rann guarantee that ETSA's current profitability will remain undiminished in this new competitive National Electricity Market, which will be dramatically different from the monopoly position which ETSA now enjoys?
  • Can Rann guarantee that ETSA and Optima's sale value will remain undiminished in the face of this new competitive National Electricity Market?
  • Will Rann guarantee categorically that he will not sell ETSA and Optima in the unlikely event of him becoming Premier after the next State election?

Mmm. Very interesting questions, but the answers will be even more interesting and I am sure the readers will look forward to Mike's response next week.

And while he's about it, Mike may also care to explain why he has refused to call a convention of the ALP in this State to discuss this important issue, given that it is nearly two years since the matter was last addressed.

Mike, you might be a song and dance man, but there's no music playing.
