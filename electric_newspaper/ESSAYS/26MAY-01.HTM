Tuesday May 26, 1998

When a leak is pissin' in the wind
by Hendrik Gout

Beware the carefully orchestrated Budget leak. Reporters at this sensitive time of year are hanging on to every word from the Treasurer's office, and on every figure from the Premier's office, as they become willing footsloggers in the general's war.

Treasurer Rob Lucas will begin delivering his Budget speech on Thursday afternoon, after Question Time in the House of Assembly. Protocol dictates that the Budget will do three things. Firstly, it will give an accurate snapshot of the State's economy at the moment. Secondly, it will give an accurate forecast of where we, as South Australians, will collectively be in a financial sense in 12 months' time. And thirdly, it will be a map - a chart - of how we'll get to this destination: it will detail all spending, all tax collections, all capital works, loans, and receipts.

It will be nothing of the sort.

Budget predictions of State growth have consistently been over-optimistic. And these days, Governments use Budgets as part of their bullshit, rather than as a financial plan.

For the past three weeks, selected journalists have been quietly taken aside and deliberately given Budget details. There was a time in politics, not so long ago, when this sort of behaviour from Ministerial staffers would have been a sackable offence. In contemporary Adelaide, it's done with Ministerial imprimatur.

So far, reporters have been told details about increases to car registration, poker machine tax, boat registration fees, and land tax. We have been spoon-fed stories about health funding and capital works. We have been leaked details of a $23 million, four-year mineral exploration program.

And all those stories contain the phrase 'to be announced in Thursday's Budget'.

This isn't very clever reportage. It doesn't involve any more effort ringing the Ministerial staffers and be willing to be used. But it is clever politics. It means that come Thursday, much of the bad news will be out of the way, and the Adelaide media will feast instead on potato and gravy rather than on potage and gristle. It will be a Good News day. At least, that's the Olsen strategy.

So what's in the Budget? It is a tacit admission that the State of South Australia is in pretty crook shape. But it doesn't put it that way.

The Electric Newspaper understands from people unconnected with Ministerial staff that Budget forecasts will acknowledge that State growth is lagging behind every other mainland State. In its forecasts relating to State growth, the Budget will tacitly concede that employment prospects remain bleaker in SA than in any other mainland State, and that prospects for business and investment are dimmer here than on the eastern seaboard. State tax receipts have been lower over the past financial year than Treasury would have liked, because economic activity is in the doldrums. This means that charges have to go up (if there were thousands of new boats being registered, or tens of thousands of new cars being registered, then there would be less need for increased fees. The extra money would come from a more robust economy).

Furthermore, the Budget will not increase spending on 'quality of life' expenditure - to things like Living Health, rural reconstruction, or the urban or natural environment - an a fiscal acknowledgment of our declining standard of living.

The Budget will not tabulate monies granted to major corporations as inducements, nor will it say how much the payroll tax exemptions, for example, to failed enterprises like Galaxy have cost the State.

Sounds grim? Well, South Australia is a small State in the national economy, and getting smaller. Our position compared with other States (with the exception of Tasmania, which is in ruins) has been getting worse for more than a decade, regardless of which Party has sat on the Treasury benches, and there is so far no reason to suggest that a change of Government would lead to a sudden - or even gradual - improvement.

And this is the real message in Thursday's Budget. But you won't find it in 'confidential' phone calls from Government media. As well as making headlines the Budget sets an economic direction, and South Australia is walking in ever-decreasing circles.
