Firday April 17, 1998

A changing world for Women
by Legh Davis, Liberal Member in the Legislative Council

Life was not exactly a bowl of cherries for women in colonial South Australia. Phillipsâ "Emigrantsâ Guide to Australia" (1852) warned women settlers that "the drawing room accomplishments of singing, dancing, painting and crochet would stand no shadow of a chance against the highly prized virtues of churning, baking, preserving, cheese-making and similar matters".

Caroline Chisholm was arguably Australia's first social worker and has deservedly received credit for her pioneering role. However, she was on record as saying that women should certainly be paid less than men because "high wages tempt many girls to keep single while it encourages indolent and lazy men to depend more and more upon their wivesâ industry than upon their exertions, thus partly reversing the design of nature".

The New South Wales Parliament also had something to say on the matter, when in 1835 a select committee reported that women should not be brought into the workforce except "either as houseservants, or in some of the more ordinary occupations". Feminist historian, Dr Katrina Alford, in tracing the economic history of women in Australia in the period 1788-1850, noted, "the limitations imposed on married womensâ property ownership during the period, and the legal uncertainty whether they were entitled to bequeath their own property, discouraged female entrepreneurship". Indeed she highlighted the example of one Eliza Walsh, a single woman of independent means, who took her case to the British Government after Governor Macquarie had refused her a land grant on the basis of her sex. The good Governor argued that his policy was based on the fact that women were "incapable of cultivating land".

It was not until 1894 that women in South Australia received the right to vote. This State was the first in Australia to give women the right to vote and in fact ranked as the third country or State in the world after Wyoming in 1868 and New Zealand in 1893. In 1897 Catherine Helen Spence, at the age of 72, became the first woman political candidate in Australia when she stood unsuccessfully for election to the Federal Convention.

However, South Australia was the last State to elect a woman to its lower House. The year was 1959 and the member was Joyce Steele, who later became the first woman to achieve cabinet rank in the South Australian Parliament. Jessie Cooper was the first woman elected to the Legislative Council, also in 1959. Both women represented the Liberal and Country League, as it was then called, but still had to face a court challenge to the validity of their election on the grounds that the State Constitution only referred to "he's" being elected!

In the Commonwealth Parliament in the 80 year period following Federation in 1901, only 4 women were elected to the House of Representatives and 12 to the Senate.

For most of this century women were discouraged from entering the workforce. During World War II they did men's work for a fraction of the pay. After World War II women received only 54% of the men's basic wage and this was then increased to 75%. Eventually equal pay for equal work was recognised by the Commonwealth Arbitration Commission in December 1972.

Only recently a woman doctor who graduated in 1960 told me there were only 6 women in the first year medical studentsâ class of around 160. All 6 graduated without failing any years. They were among only 70 graduates, many of whom would have been students who had failed earlier years. These women still have a clear recollection of being discriminated against in lectures and feeling that they had to perform better just to be on level terms with their male peers. But in their first year as interns these female doctors were paid around £10 per week, while the males were paid £12 per week. Another woman from the same era walked into the first lecture in a law subject with a girlfriend and the professor lecturing them said "what are you two girls doing here?"

In the early 1960's it was still common for parents to discourage their daughters from attending university, although they may have been more gifted than the sons who did attend university.

Until quite recently women have been discriminated against when seeking housing or business loans. Single women with jobs found it difficult to obtain the necessary finance from banks.

We have come a long way in the last generation but there is still some distance to travel.

In the recent history of this State and nation women were denied the right to own property, and the right to vote, were discouraged from working and if they did work were paid significantly less. The glass ceiling has made career advancement difficult. Should the government or men of Australia apologise to today's women for the discrimination against past generations of women?
