Friday June 12, 1998

When silence is not golden
by Andy Alcock *

It is a scandal that not one world leader in any democratic country is standing up to Indonesia as it continues its illegal occupation of East Timor and West Papua.

There are talks of troops being sent to Bosnia Herzogovina to resolve the situation there, but there is total silence from the democratic world as Indonesia's military continues to commit abuse human rights.

Those who know the real story about what is happening in East Timor are sickened by Indonesian propaganda claims about what it has done for East Timor. The East Timorese have been pushed out of their own economy. This has also been the case in occupied West Papua.

The reason for the silence about what has happened in these two countries over the past two decades is to allow international mining corporations to have unfettered access to their mineral and oil resources.

Former Australian Ambassador to Indonesia and head of Foreign Affairs, Richard Woolcott, admitted as much in his then secret cables to the Whitlam Government in 1974. He also advised that when East Timor was invaded, the Australian Government should do nothing, but try to understand Indonesia's position. He even had enough insight to admit, this was not a principled position. This is the one opinion of his with which we can agree.

The extra bonus that mining corporations operating in East Timor and West Papua have is that they don't face certain costs such as reasonable wages, good health and safety standards, environmental safeguards, and compensation to people moved off their lands.

The Indonesian military tortures or murders those who get in the way. The Indonesian dictatorship knows that it can always call on certain western leaders (especially the Australians) to handle the public relations side for them.

We all remember Gareth Evans "aberration" comment following the 1991 Santa Cruz Massacre in Dili. In more recent days, we also had Alexander Downer suggesting that the Indonesian army weren't using real bullets in the early days of the democracy rallies in Jakarta streets. Indonesian students soon put him right on that score, but only after the letting of much of their blood.Then there was the sickening cacophony of Australian leaders telling the world what a great leader Suharto was. "He brought stability to the region."

It seemed that Howard, Beazley and Downer were vying for the airwaves to get across their misleading message about Asia's other Pol Pot.

Some of our journalists also help the dictatorship in its difficult public relations problems. East Timor is frequently referred to as the 27th province of Indonesia despite the fact that this has never been accepted by the UN. Oil companies operating in the Timor Sea are said to be drilling off the coast of Indonesia.

There are many in the region who want the lies and distortions of politicians and journalists to stop. They want real peace and justice to prevail in our region and the abuse of human rights to cease. The voices of young Indonesians have recently joined this chorus. How long will it take for leaders who claim to be democratic to hear?

* Andy Alcock is information officer for the Campaign for an Independent East Timor
