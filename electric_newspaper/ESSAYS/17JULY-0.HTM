Friday July 10, 1998

One Nation and the 'Mafia'
by Carmel Zollo, Labor member in the Legislative Council

This is my first contribution to the 'newspaper' of the new millenium, now only just over a year away (millenium bug or no millenium bug!). As a newly-elected MLC in last October's State election and the first Italian-born woman to be elected to the Legislative Council, I guess it is natural for me to have strong views concerning the current race and multiculturalism debate.

The title of my article is not just designed to attract attention - it is in fact a paraphrase of a headline from the 13 July edition of Il Globo, Australia's largest foreign language newspaper. The title of the article was 'One Nation party and a certain mafia mentality' and it suggested that students of internal Italian and Australian politics might be struck by the coincidences and similarities of the ideas and tactics of the Italian Lega Nord and the Australian One Nation Party. Il Globo suggested that both movements have grown on a mixture of populism, racism, and a widespread malaise of democracies which goes under the generic term of intolerance.

The latest article from Il Globo is representative of the consistently strong stance they have taken against Hanson and One Nation. Previous headlines have included "Queensland election: a triumph for racists", "From banana republic to the kingdom of the Ku Klux Klan", "One Nation danger: the coalition trembles", "Too little too late from Howard in the struggle against racism in Australia". Whilst I am only familiar in some detail with the Italian ethnic media, at least this normally conservative ethnic newspaper has taken the Hanson factor much more seriously than the rest of Australia's popular press. As I have jokingly suggested, groups are being singled out by One Nation in alphabetical order - Aborigines and Asians today, Greeks and Italians tomorrow?

In Adelaide this week were two women of widely disparate backgrounds, with the media paying special attention to only one of them, One Nation's Parliamentary Leader. It is not exactly difficult for Hanson and her Party to get publicity. Hanson's latest gaffe (she should stick to her script) concerns the apparent folly of the Australian people in 1967, in overwhelmingly supporting a referendum to count Aborigines in the census and to give the Federal government power to make laws for the benefit of our indigenous Australians, and not to give them the vote as she falsely claimed.

The other person in Adelaide who did receive some media, mostly via ABC radio, was Dr Mary Kalantzis, who was here as a guest speaker for the Hellenic Conference - a conference held to debate issues of importance to the Hellenic-Australian community. It is the first time that I have heard in a public forum such strong criticism concerning the manner in which political parties and in particular the Liberal Party and John Howard, the Prime Minister, have handled the Hanson issue.

Steve Condous looked a bit uncomfortable, even though he had criticised the Prime Minister himself in the South Australian Parliament. Dr Kalantzis believes not enough has been said by the major parties because we are all running scared. As a Member of the Party that gave migrants in South Australia a sense of belonging and a sense of diversity through the concept of multiculturalism, I took some exception to that particular comment.

Whilst in South Australia we have strong bi-partisan support for multiculturalism, people are nonetheless fed by the popular press a daily menu of what at best can only be described as sensationalism - the woman who was taken as a joke has turned the joke on Australia's vulnerable: our indigenous population, migrants, single mums, to name but a few.

Hansonism has been called the politics of grievance. It was the excuse given by Liberal politicians in particular in relation to the manner in which semi-rural Queensland voted in that State election. The bush may be hurting but so are many other people in our community. The overwhelming majority of people who have migrated to Australia have worked hard and enriched this nation. During difficult economic and rapidly changing times, pointing the finger at people who are not breaking any laws, but who look different, speak differently or behave differently - is not only misguided but is also dangerous as history shows.

The important question that needs to be asked is 'who has given One Nation respectability?' I guess there is only one answer to that - the many thousands of people who have supported Hanson and to some extent the popular media.

In my Maiden Speech last year, I said that I believed that the concept of multiculturalism and the post-war migration program have been Australia's great success stories, and asked why were they suddenly under threat? I went on to say that all of us would accept that most individuals and ethnic groups are to some extent prejudiced against others and that it has probably been the cause of many wars throughout history. I believe however that our 'Australian experiment' was unique because of the bipartisan commitment and support of both the migration program and the concept of multiculturalism over the last 25 years.

I called on the federal coalition Government and in particular the Prime Minister to follow South Australia's lead in its support of multiculturalism and added, "We need a strong, clear, unequivocal statement from the Prime Minister as our national leader in full support of a non-discriminatory immigration program and the concept of multiculturalism," and further "There must also not be any equivocation about Party preferences for any candidate who espouses racist views."

I am proud to say that my Party, the Australian Labor Party, did and will continue to stand on principle regarding preferences for Hanson and the One Nation Party. If the coalition had had the courage and the decency to put One Nation last in the Queensland election, the ALP would probably have won a few more seats and the end result would have been the same in terms of forming a government. But the result for One Nation would have been dramatically different as they would have won only a couple of seats and therefore would also likely have had only a minor impact on the coming federal election.

Helen Sham-Ho's resignation from the Liberal Party seems to suggest that the sublime racism that one comes across from time to time is on the increase. It appears that Mrs Sham-Ho took her stand because of suggestions from some of her former colleagues that a person of Asian background standing as a candidate for the Presidency of the NSW Upper House might not be acceptable to One Nation supporters. She also expressed her disappointment at the Prime Minister's lack of leadership concerning the distribution of preferences by her former party.

What the One Nation phenomenon has done is bring the underlying subtle racism that exists in most communities to the fore, almost made it acceptable. Subtle racism is in many ways a more insidious form of racism and often difficult to prove.

All Australians should be justly proud of both our Aboriginal and European heritage, traditions, language and way of life - but I think there is room for people who have come to Australia to call this nation their home to also be able to express their customs, traditions and languages, within the laws of this nation, as well as to embrace those of their new home, Australia.
