Monday March 16, 1998

MAI lie
by John Rau

It is pleasing to see the beginnings of an informed public debate on the subject of the proposed Multilateral Agreement on Investment Treaty.

The range of issues thrown up by this debate will hopefully demonstrate the complexity and potential seriousness of MAI for Australia.

As I see it, the legal issues alone are sufficient to warrant grave concern. Many of these issues come down to looking out for "the devil in the detail".

Other issues are ones of principle or conceptual architecture. I will illustrate these different types of issue by way of example. Before doing so however, I would first like to set MAI in some historical perspective.

We are now passing through a period of great economic, social, political and technological change. In retrospect, this will be seen as one of the great transitions in human history . The new world which is rapidly emerging is stripped bare of old beliefs and values . Cultures and individuals are being cut loose from their roots. Globalism intrudes into all facets of life.

The evidence is all around us. It is only by looking back that the extent of change becomes obvious. A new economic dogma based on the blind wisdom of the market is in the ascendancy. A popular culture of "the self" corrodes our sense of community and Nation. Business is not trusted. Politicians are viewed with contempt. Corruption in high office is viewed with resignation by a powerless population. Institutions of democratic Government are increasingly seen as ineffectual apologists for big money. It would be foolish to suggest that any individual amongst us can halt this process. For the time being, at least, our collective voice is however powerful enough to make a difference.

In the years since the end of the Second World War, the global economy has expanded beyond recognition. In 1945, Germany, the powerhouse of Europe, was a pile of rubble and ashes. Japan, the economic engine of Asia, was occupied, broke and starving. How things have changed. These countries have substantially achieved through commerce what they failed to achieve through conquest.

The rise of the Trans National Corporation (TNC) has occurred since the War. TNCs are based overwhelmingly in First World Countries. The OECD is the "Club" of first world Nations.

477 of the global fortune 500 corporations have their home bases in OECD countries. In other words, 95.4% of the largest Trans National Corporations are headquartered in member countries of the OECD.

TNCs are large enough in many cases to have interests and objectives quite independent of those motivating a host country. TNCs are the most powerful institutions yet devised for the exploitation of resources and the accumulation of wealth. Their power is proportional to their wealth. History shows that they have few moral scruples when the pursuit of profit is at strake. Bribery and corruption have been weapons in their armory.

TNCs have ready access to governments at top level. Through lobbyists and other agents of influence , they quietly work on politicians and bureaucrats alike. Their agenda is being advanced simultaneously across the globe.

Within the OECD, the distribution of wealth and power is very uneven. The United States, the European Union and Japan are the big players. Other member countries, including Australia , are basically along to make up the numbers.

The agenda of the TNCs and their partners, the global banks, is to set in place a series of international guidelines which will produce a secure environment that is "good for business". TNCs want a "big stick" to guarantee them certain rights as against Nation States. This is MAI.

OECD Nations will be encouraged to sign up.In due course, other poorer Nations will be obliged to join on the same terms . MAI will not be easy to get out of either. Upwards of 20 years notice could be required to extract a Nation from MAI.

MAI will give TNCs enforceable legal rights to recover damages from Nations which do not observe the Treaty. Damages will be determined by an International Tribunal. Failure by a Nation to comply with an order of this Tribunal, from which there is no appeal, would result in ostracism and possibly, sanctions.

It is proposed that the MAI Draft will be finalised by May of 1998. It appears that the Federal Government is seriously considering signing.

The potential consequences for Australians of submitting to MAI are enormous.

I would now like to illustrate a principle or conceptual architecture issue, mentioned in my opening remarks.

MAI treats Nations and TNCs essentially as equals. In terms of their sheer economic power , this is a recognition of reality . TNCs are given the right to sue Sovereign Nations for damages if those Nations exercise their Constitutional powers in a way considered to offend the terms of the Treaty. It is beyond argument that this compromises National Sovereignty .

It is arguable that in extreme cases, for example expropriation of assets by a despotic government, this may have merit. This depends of course on how you define "expropriation".

There may be sound reasons for a Nation to elect to compromise itself in this way. Most International agreements involve some degree of compromise. Whether such a compromise is warranted in any particular case, comes down to a cost benefit analysis.

MAI is unusual in that it gives rights not to Nations but to TNCs. Nations obtain no enforceable rights in return. At best they are offered the woolly promise of an environment that is "good for business"and all the usual empty assurances about more jobs .

As a very minimum, MAI should be a reciprocal charter of rights and responsibilities. TNCs responsibilities might for example include payment of proper taxation in a host country, observance of ILO Labor regulations, or environmental standards. At present it is a one way street. All rights for the TNCs, but no responsibilities.This is quite simply a bad deal.

Examples of the "devil in the detail" are potentially limited only by one's imagination. Take for example exemptions, or the lack of them, under MAI.

MAI is designed to apply in its entirety unless a specific exemption is sought by and granted to a Nation. Circumstances, unforseen at the time of negotiation of the Treaty, will not be exempted. Even exemptions sought and granted will have to be continually reviewed and if possible, wound back.

Take a very local example. SANTOS is one of the few major corporations with a major presence in SA. This is no accident .The South Australian Parliament passed the SANTOS [Regulation of Shareholding] Act in 1979 to protect SANTOS from a then threatened take-over by Alan Bond. Just as well as it turned out!

This Act was amended in 1989, but the law remains that so long as SANTOS or any subsidiary, engages in the recovery and production of petroleum in SA, no person or corporation may own more than 15 % of the shares.

MAI is designed to remove all impediments to investment and restrictions on the flow of capital. Assume for a moment that Exon wanted to buy SANTOS. As far as I am aware, there is no proposal to exempt this Act of the SA Parliament from the operation of MAI. It is impossible to know how many other pieces of legislation, passed in the interest of South Australians, to which no one has yet turned their mind, are lurking out there.

MAI may oblige the Federal Parliament to pass a law overriding the SA Act, or risk leaving Australia open to litigation and a possible claim for damages by any TNC, denied full access to the SANTOS share register.

Similar questions could be raised about a privatised ETSA, or the Commonwealth Bank, or Qantas.

Similar problems emerge for cross-media ownership and foreign investment guidelines in other sectors of the economy .

Australian content rules, restrictions on violent films and any cultural protection from total Americanization may also be threatened.

These are all complex issues and they require public debate. Make it your business to raise this debate. Silence now may be repented at length.
