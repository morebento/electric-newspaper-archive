Tuesday June 16, 1998

Prisoners of the word
by John Rau

For at least two decades, Australians have been told by commentators and political leaders that ours is a "multi-cultural" society. This terminology was initially descriptive. Australians came from many parts of the world. Migration had expressed itself in our cities. Our tastes had expanded beyond meat and two veg. The term "multi-cultural" was a simple adjective.

Over the last twenty years, intellectual elites, recognising the broad acceptance of this adjective, have colonised it by stealth. They have injected it with new meaning. Colonisation has many advantages over the more difficult task of introducing unfamiliar terminology. For a start, you don't have to sell a new concept. You pick up adherents by inertia.

This colonisation process has now gone so far that adherents of "multi-culturalism" cling defiantly to highly individual personal definitions. "Multi-culturalism" now has so many facets as to have no specific meaning. It has as many meanings as it has proponents and detractors. The term serves to shroud debate in analogy or code. This language should be retired from the political lexicon and replaced with crisper concepts about which debate can be openly focused.

The "multi-culturalism" conceptual bag is full of political dynamite. Because the bag was stuffed by linguistic stealth rather than debate, each volatile strand must be gingerly removed. Each must be examined, in some cases, for the first time. The biggest strands are labelled "population policy", "immigration policy" and "cultural identity". If any evidence of this fact was required, the Queensland election result of last week-end bears consideration.

Australia has not had a debate about population policy since the end of World War II. This debate raises a number of subsidiary issues, including sustainable levels of population, distribution of population over the continent, national and regional development, and family, employment, immigration and economic policies.

Australia has not had a proper debate about immigration policy since the War either. A blanket of elite consensus has been thrown the subject. The issues have not gone away - they have festered in the dark silence. One Nation seems set to destroy the elite consensus by raising issues directly with the masses.

Immigration policy may or may not be debated in the broader context of population policy. There are a number of sub-plots here too, such as levels of immigration, sources of migrants, selection policies (eg. refugee intake) and international obligations.

Cultural identity is the real sleeper. It is hard to define but it resonates in an electorate besieged by uncertainty. Ultimately this may become the defining issue in the looming political contest. What does it mean to be an Australian? What are our universal icons? Are we capable of articulating an inclusive culture based in reality?

"Multi-cultural" jargon has outlived its purpose. A new crisp focus, borne of genuine community values must emerge. Political leaders who cling to the redundant language of multi-culturalism will be side-lined in the coming debate. The major parties will continue to vacate the field of ideas until this is acknowledged.

HOWARD IS A SLOW LEARNER

John Howard hit the air-waves bright and early on Monday to put an acceptable spin on the devastation of his Queensland branch by cyclone Hanson over the week-end. He had "heard the message" that people were distressed by the pace and direction of change.

BUT there was still a good chance of a double dissolution, he was still going to go ahead with a GST and he was still going to press on with the sale of the remaining two-thirds of Telstra.

Obviously John Howard needs to hear the message again and a lot louder. Paul Keating was thrown out in response to his policies of economic rationalism. It looks like John Howard wants to go the same way, and to make One Nation a major player on the national stage in the process.

It takes a big man to admit that he is wrong and to make changes. It seems that John Howard is a small man after all.
