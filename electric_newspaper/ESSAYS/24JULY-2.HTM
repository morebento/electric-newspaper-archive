Friday July 24, 1998

Oils ain't oils *
by Carmel Zollo, Labor member in the Legislative Council

"The picturesque beaches were being ruined by bombs, the mountains and the forests were being consumed by the flames of napalm bombs, the dawn quiet was rudely disturbed by the crackling of machine guns and reports of cannons." (Meres Orgis, A. Coutas )

The chilling scene described above is neither from Bosnia, Rwanda, Kuwait nor Vietnam. It is in fact July 20, 1974 on the Mediterranean island-republic of Cyprus. It describes the invasion and subsequent occupation and partition of the island by Turkey. The island remains divided to this day and stands as the only forcible partitioned nation in Europe. This is not the place nor am I in a position to present a complete and detailed examination of this on-going tragedy; rather I will focus on Australia's involvement in the issue and whether it has a role in the solution.

As readers of the News section of the Electric Newspaper would have noted, last weekend the Cypriot and Greek communities marked the 24th anniversary of the invasion and occupation. A memorial was conducted for those who lost their lives and to pray for a peaceful resolution to the ongoing issue. The ceremony was attended by politicians from the Federal and State Government and Opposition, and the new "not really a political party person" my colleague Nick Xenophon MLC, who is of part Cypriot heritage. Some of the more cynical readers might well say this is what politicians usually do, because they are concerned with gaining sectional votes. I can assure them that this is not the case.

The issue of Cyprus is one of justice and human rights. It is one of the enduring tragedies of the last quarter of the twentieth century. The Cyprus problem has been with us now for some 24 years. Turkey, as the attacking and occupying force in Cyprus, has been condemned in international fora with many United Nations and European Union resolutions. Australia and Australians are also involved in many ways with Cyprus and as such, the Cyprus issue is a concern to this nation.

Australia has a long involvement in Cyprus. It has had a peacekeeping police force (UNFICYP) on the island since 1964. The Republic of Cyprus is also a member of the Commonwealth of Nations, a fact I suspect is not that well known by many Australians. Thousands of Cypriot people have migrated to Australia and become citizens, as part of post second World War migration. Australia has also provided a home to many Cypriot refugees since the 1974 invasion.

Like many Australians, I remember being horrified at the so-called 'peaceful intervention' in Cyprus by Turkish troops in 1974. To place some of the chilling statistics in context, Cyprus has a population about half of South Australia's, but is only 0.37% of its area. The 'intervention' led to the 'ethnic cleansing' (well before it was a popular term) of over 200,000 Greek Cypriots from their ancestral homes - some 40.5 percent of the Greek Cypriot population became refugees. Turkey occupies 37 percent of the island, 6,000 Greek Cypriots lost their lives and over 1,600 persons remain missing. Over 500 Orthodox churches and other sites of antiquity were pillaged. The 1974 value of the land and property lost by the invasion is valued at $AUS 6.75 billion.

Since the invasion, Turkey has systematically manipulated the population mix on the island by importing over 100,000 Turkish mainland colonists to Cyprus. Some 35,000-40,000 Turkish troops are stationed on the island, representing part of Europe's largest post Cold-War armed force. The troops, together with the colonists, now make the Turkish Cypriots a minority in occupied north Cyprus. About 50,000 Turkish Cypriots have emigrated to other countries since 1974, casting doubt on the premise of the Turkish invasion, which was meant to protect and assist Turkish Cypriots.

To some, these may appear as just the usual statistics and figures of events that occurred some time ago and a long way away, but to those Cypriots and Australians who have lost loved ones and homes, the pain and loss must be unbearable.

Unfortunately, it seems that despite the efforts of United Nations delegations, Turkey appears to be unmoved. Instead of approaching a solution, the problem is being made harder to resolve. An escalation of tension on the island recently, including the much publicised and horrific bashing and murders in the UN demilitarized zone and the Cypriot moves to purchase an S-300 ground-to-air defence system, is rapidly making Cyprus a real international 'hot-spot'.

The United States has a major role to play in any cessation of the illegal Turkish occupation, particularly as both Greece and Turkey are NATO members. Some people fear that an increase in tension in Cyprus and continued violence may cause further tragedy between two historic combatants in the Aegean.

The Labor Party, and in particular in South Australia through Mike Rann, the Leader of the Opposition, has always stood up for the Cyprus cause and to bring the injustice to the attention of the public. Importantly, the issue has bipartisan support in Australia. As politicians we can all perform a useful function by continuing to encourage discussion and seek re-unification of the island through the international community. In particular, Australia must maintain its peacekeeping presence in Cyprus, and continue to seek the implementation of United Nation's Resolutions on the problem.

History has taught us that people with passion and commitment for justice can change the course of events. Just as with the fall of the Berlin wall and the collapse of the communist Soviet empire, it can take longer in some cases than others. Greek and Turkish Cypriots lived and worked side-by-side for over 500 years until the 1974 invasion. With perseverance, patience and diplomatic pressure, Cyprus may reach a just and peaceful solution to the scar that has divided the island.

* The title for this piece "oils ain't oils" reflects some interesting comments made in Adelaide last week by visiting international speaker Dr Chris Ioannides from the Vryonis Centre for Hellenic Studies in Sacramento California. Dr Ioannides ironically remarked on the lack of expediency by the international community and the United States in particular, to resolve the issue of Cyprus. Unfortunately for Cyprus, it has an abundance of golden olive oil and not black crude oil. Perhaps if it did, similar international resolve and outrage as seen in the Iraq-Kuwait conflict may have prevailed.
