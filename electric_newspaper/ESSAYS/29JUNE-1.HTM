Monday June 29, 1998

A vision splendid
by Jack Hoogland

A while ago, (Electric Newspaper, June 2) I wrote an article highlighting the difference between illusion and reality, between mirage and substance. As an example, I referred to a piece by Legh Davis MLC regarding the Roxby Downs copper and uranium mining development.

To make my point, I chose some specific and key parameters and compared the predictions made for them and the achieved reality some 14 years later. I'll reproduce them here. Note that dollars are 1997 dollars. Note also that the job figures relate to direct production only. Predicted: 

Production by 1993		150 000  tonnes per annum
Direct (I say direct) jobs	2268 to 2815
Annual value of production	$1,200 million
Annual royalties		$50 million

These figures were taken from the Environmental Impact Statement (EIS) produced for the joint venturers. I didn't invent them, nor did I modify, obscure or shuffle them. These sorts of figures were central to the campaign to establish the mine. In the end, the issues of waste disposal and nuclear weapons proliferation, and the dodgy environmental record of past uranium mining operations were secondary to the lure of thousands upon thousands of jobs, and millions upon millions of dollars for us lucky South Australians.

So what was achieved by 1997? The outcomes were: 

	Production by 1993		85,000  tonnes per annum
	Direct (yes, direct) jobs	946
	Annual value of production	$350 million
	Annual royalties		$9.5 million

To compare jobs figures including construction and multiplier effects with direct production jobs forecasts is to misrepresent the facts, as is comparing dollar amounts across a span of years without allowing for changes in value of the dollar over that time.

Since the economic aspects of the project were considered the most important, it would seem doubly unsatisfactory that the reality had fallen so far short of the predictions.

A lot was made of the importance of this project to the economy of SA, in fact, reading the Advertiser, back in 1982, one could be led to believe that the state's whole future existence depended on it.

"$40m. (pa) in royalties likely" shouted the front page headline of the Advertiser at the time. Job estimates of variously 7500, 10,000 11,250 and even 15,000 and so on were made so often as to become a sort of pseudo fact. Full page advertisements paid for by the Chamber of Commerce appeared almost daily, claiming "Build Roxby Downs or the sign of the times will be ... NO WORK".

Mr Davis's response (Electric Newspaper, June 5 1998) was thunderous and contained a plethora of large numbers. By describing my article as "ideological claptrap", and then presenting a series of "facts", he presumably thought that a wrong had been righted, and truth restored. The facts I presented were perfectly correct of course, but in rushing to stomp out any alternative points of view he missed the point, that being that major and important decisions affecting the state and its citizens, must be made in an environment of informed debate, with an informed electorate. Indeed, good democracy requires an informed electorate, not a misled one.

Democracy also relies on the exercise of due process. Environmental Impact Statements serve to shed light on the nature of an intended development. The draft EIS is published so that the public can be informed, and gives an opportunity for the public to present submissions that seek clarification or modification or even outright opposition. When an EIS such as the one prepared for the Roxby Downs development has been shown to be so wrong, one could be forgiven for losing a little faith in that process.

A further example of illusion versus reality involves the EIS prediction of achieving full output from the mine in five years. Mr Davis suggests that I might expect the mine to reach its projected output "from day one". Well, one day is a bit short, but since the mine began operation in 1988, I would have thought that nine years (about 3,300 days) would have been adequate, particularly when the DEIS of 1982 actually predicted full output by 1993.

Mr Davis also misses the point when he suggests that I "lament" the fact that the value of production reached only $350m. What I lament is the fact that such an inaccurate set of predictions could be put forward as a basis upon which to make rational decisions on such a contentious matter, and that our parliament lacked the capacity to critically assess these claims.

Of course, Western Mining Corporation, (WMC) being the ideal corporate citizen, it naturally is greatly concerned about the public's perception of its environmental credentials. For that reason, it is a contributor to AMEEF, the mining industry's environmental PR foundation. That such a body would bestow an environmental award on the town of Roxby Downs, a WMC town, should come as no surprise to anyone.

Giving itself awards might impress some folk, but I was less than impressed when WMC announced in 1994 that 5 million cubic metres of liquid had leaked from its tailings dam over the previous years. I was also less than impressed to learn that the tailings storage facility described in the EIS had been substantially modified without any public consultation. One wonders why bother with the EIS in the first place. After all, our leaders know best. The whereabouts of the escaped waste is not known, notwithstanding the "enormous amount of environmental knowledge of arid Australia" possessed by WMC. Radioactive wastes have half lives of tens, hundreds or even thousands of years. A mine operating for less than ten years is already having trouble containing that waste, one wonders at how such long lived wastes will be successfully managed for generations to come.

The Draft EIS's for the proposed Honeymoon and Beverley In Situ Leach uranium mines are due to be published shortly. Given the somewhat extravagant claims made in the Roxby EIS, and the way in which it was in part ignored after the event, these DEIS's must be scrutinised thoroughly, the public kept informed, and due process followed.

Lest you think that information, debate and scrutiny lie exclusively in the domain of the uranium mining sector, even as I write, the battle for the hearts and minds of the public begins on another contentious and important decision, the selling of ETSA. For all the shouting, precious little in the way of fact, reason and light is being directed our way.

Irrespective of the issue, the better decision will inevitably be made if the maximum number of people are adequately and correctly informed.
