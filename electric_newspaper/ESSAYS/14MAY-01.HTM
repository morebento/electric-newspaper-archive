Thursday May 14, 1998

Surplus to requirements: more jobs go
by John Spoehr

The $2.7 billion Budget surplus has been achieved at the expense of significant reductions in Commonwealth General Government outlays and grants to the States. In particular, this is reflected in the significant decline in public sector employment - around 30,000 nationally over last two years.

Reflecting these job losses, public sector administrative costs are forecast to decline by around $2.4 billion over 1998-99 as the result of these cuts. This is largely due to the axing of the CES and the creation of the JobNetwork and CentreLink. These changes alone account for around $1.8 billion of the decline in running costs. As the result of these and other substantial cost cutting measures, running costs for 1997-99 are forecast to decline by around 15 percent from $13.9 billion to $11.9 billion.

In 1997-98 public sector staff cuts under the Public Service Act were around 12,200. The creation of a surplus in the current Budget has not stemmed the tide of job loss with a further 9000 jobs set to go between June 1998 and June 1999.

Average staffing levels across the public sector are set to decline by around 9,000 from 163,000 in 1997-98 to 154,000 in 1998-99.

Key areas to be effected include DEETYA - 7400 to 3000; Finance and Administration - 3000 to 2500 and Social Security - 21,000 to 20,000.

Appointments of permanent staff over the last four years have declined significantly with male appointments declining from around 4300 in 1994 to 2200 in 1997 and female appointments declining from 4900 to 2700 over the same period. Overall there has been a 40 percent decline in appointments over this period (APS Statistical Bulletin, 1997).

Commonwealth sector retrenchments increased dramatically over the 1995-97 period. Male retrenchments rose by 150 percent from around 1900 in 1994 to 5200 in 1997. Female retrenchments rose by nearly 100 percent from 2400 to 4700 (APS Statistical Bulletin, 1997). Over 1995-97 period there were around 18,500 retrenchments compared to around 7,400 in the previous three years.

Public sector outlays have declined by around three percent since the election of the Howard Government and are set to decline a further percentage point over the next financial year. Outlays have declined from a peak of 31 percent in 1985-86 to 25 percent in 1997-98. A further sharp decline to 22.8 percent of GDP is forecast by 2001-02

Rather than improve Australia's economic position, these substantial reductions in outlays and employment and the likely acceleration of privatisation will continue to stifle our growth prospects. Put simply, Australia could have achieved higher rates of economic and employment growth if public sector investment levels, particularly in physical infrastructure had been higher. Cuts in government expenditure have had a direct effect on demand for goods and services as the once of effect of separation packages wear off and unemployment becomes a reality for many of the 'separated'.

At a State level the most obvious symptom of under-investment by the Federal government is chronic and sustained high rates of unemployment during the recovery period. With unemployment forecast to be around 8 percent nationally throughout next year, South Australia is unlikely to get below 9 percent. An additional $347 million over four years for an expanded work for the dole scheme will do nothing to improve this situation, nor will the chaos currently being created by the introduction of the JobNetwork. Additionally, any further public sector job losses will simply erode the full-time employment base of the State, adding to population outflow and obscenely high rates of long-term unemployment.

The $2.7 billion surplus should have been utilised to provide support for the 750,000 unemployed Australians. The Federal Government has failed the test of compassion through it's misguided pre-occupation with debt reduction and inability to develop coherent industry and employment strategies. The Budget contains nothing to reassure the Australian community that unemployment will be seriously tackled over the next year. Without this reassurance and practical policy solutions the modest inroads that have been made into unemployment may be quickly eroded if the Asian financial crisis further undermines our domestic outlook.

All of this will have serious negative implications for the South Australian labour market over the next twelve months. Given the current parlous state of the SA labour market, the prospect of a downturn in the national economy at the beginning of the next century should be of great concern for us all. The Federal Budget offers little hope to the 42,000 unemployed Souht Australians - it appears they are 'surplus to requirements'.

John Spoehr is deputy director of the Centre for Labour Research at the University of Adelaide.
