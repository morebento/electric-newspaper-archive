Friday July 31, 1998

Beware the false prophets
by Carmel Zollo, Labor member in the Legislative Council

"In 1948 I shared with thousands the gift of false prophecy. I was satisfied Tom Dewey would win the (U.S.) presidency; which goes to show what extraordinary results can happen in a country like the U.S. so backward as not to have compulsory voting." (R. G. Menzies, reported in New York 1960)

The above quote made at the end of my contribution to the Abolition of Compulsory Voting Amendment Bill 1998, elicited a number of interjection from Government members. They seemed to find it incredulous that none other than the founding father of the Liberal Party (and longest serving Australian Prime Minister) was a supporter of compulsory voting.

For the fourth time since 1993, the Government has introduced a Bill to abolish compulsory voting in South Australia. This is hardly the issue of our time and there are certainly more pressing issues which should be addressed by Parliament. The One Nation inroads in the Queensland election has strengthened my views that now more than ever, there should be a moral and legal obligation on all citizens to exercise their hard fought right to vote at every election.

Compulsory voting, I believe, allows every citizen to decide whether to vote for a particular person or party or vote for no-one at all (by simply attending, having their name crossed off the roll and placing the blank ballot paper in the box). It does not leave the process to, for example, people motivated by whipped-up self-interest or populist doctrine that is often lacking in substance. In other words, it provides real choice for all citizens.

No doubt a large number of complex factors are involved as to what motivates people to vote or not where voting is voluntary. The mix of those factors may vary from country to country. No doubt apathy and disillusionment are major factors. But more importantly, however, a large number of voters become marginalised by political parties targeting only the people most likely to vote.

In the United States voluntary voting leads to the poor, pensioners and the young (especially those voting for the first time) being wiped off the data base or to never make the data base - as politicians work out who is not likely to vote and hone in on those who are likely to.

In the recent Constitutional Convention, just under 40% of electors bothered to vote, even though it was a relatively convenient postal ballot. The ballot was the brainchild of South Australian Senator Nick Minchin, the champion of the voluntary voting system, and showed again what a sham voluntary voting would be for the democratic process.

The amount of money expended on administrative and court costs are given as a reason for abolishing voluntary voting. But this cost is only a small part of the overall cost of elections - but in any case the question should be: what price does one place on democracy?

More importantly, I am sure that the overwhelming majority of the approximately 95% of people who normally vote, do not do so because they are terrified of being fined if they don't. They vote for the most part because it is a civic duty and part of the democratic process, even if there is a legal requirement in the Electoral Act to do so.

This is an important point - democratic communities do not function because of compulsion, but because of cooperation and the sense of community and civil responsibility. Yes, we have our myriad laws which regulate virtually every aspect of our society, including the obligation to vote. But it is not compulsion which makes it work, otherwise we would need the proverbial police presence on every corner.

I like to use the analogy of the Adelaide City Council erecting a 'keep off the grass' sign in order to maintain an area of lawn. In our community 95% will respect that sign not because of the threat of sanctions, but because of our civic pride and that there would not be any lawn if thousands of people walked on it. There would probably be little impact if a few people ignored the sign, but occasionally Council inspectors will question people walking across the lawn and if they don't have a legitimate reason or keep doing it, will issue an expiation notice.

Unlike the Liberal Party which is split on this issue, the Labor Party has always had a strong history of supporting compulsory voting. The divisions in the Liberal Party were demonstrated by the Federal Member for Hindmarsh in a recent newspaper column. She listed the usual arguments regarding the low voter turnout at voluntary local government elections, the United States example where the President of that country is elected by less than a quarter of US citizens, and the temptation for political parties to prioritise their policies to those more likely to vote. The member concluded: "Those who advocate voluntary voting should remember that compulsory voting has served us well. Despite the growing gap between the rich and the poor, it has kept us together as a cohesive society, and it has kept at bay special interest groups who would seek to control the political process and the politicians."

Even more telling comments were made at the Victorian Liberal Party's October 1997, 126th State Council held in Shepparton and reported in The Australian - 90% of the 500-strong delegates carried a motion which labeled the concept of voluntary voting a "disaster" for democracy. The immediate past president of the Victorian branch of the Liberal party was quoted as saying that "compulsory voting was a fundamental part of the nation's political culture and heritage, and he believed the majority of Liberal members recognised it should be strongly upheld."

Australia shares compulsory voting with well over 30 other democracies in the world. Significantly, Greece which invented democracy thousands of years ago, has compulsory voting. There is no doubt in my mind that voluntary voting increases the likelihood of inducements and undue influences being offered to voters to get them to vote. I believe it is important for parties to concentrate on what they can offer to the electorate, rather than how to induce voters to get them to the polls.

Although there are minor differences between Federal and State voting systems, they do have a common electoral roll. Given that the Federal government does not appear to have any intention of changing the current system, one can imagine the voter confusion if one had a voluntary system and the other a compulsory system.

We should remember that only 25 years ago the Liberal Party was still fighting to ensure that Legislative Council voters needed to be property franchisees. Voluntary voting could produce similar unrepresentative results prior to the reforms by the Dunstan Government.

As a woman it also concerns me that if the Government is successful in this legislation, it would no doubt reduce the number of people who would play a part in the community life of this State. I regret to say that there are still women in our community in 1998 who may not be given the opportunity to go out to vote if it is not considered essential for them to do so. This would certainly be a retrograde step for a State which introduced the franchise for women in 1894.

One is led to conclude that the reason for persisting with this legislation is nothing more than Liberal Party self-interest, given the lack of community interest or support for this issue.

For a democracy to be a democracy, it means majority rule and the expression of opinion by the majority of its electors. Compulsory voting has and does serve our democracy well.
