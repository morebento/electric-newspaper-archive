Friday May 29, 1998

Work, work, and then no jobs
by John Spoehr*

The most leaked State Budget of all has turned out to be one of the most unpopular in the State’s history. The last Budget was heralded by the Government as the end of austerity, a time for self-congratulation because debt was under control. This Budget was to offer some relief and hope for South Australians. Instead it has heightened insecurity.

Ironically this is a Budget that is big on austerity and low on sharing the fruits which the Government claimed would grow from its debt reduction strategy. Perversely this is a Budget that rejects hronically high unemployment in its rhetoric, but enshrines it into its projections.

With the State Bank and SGIC crises behind us and State debt at levels not experienced since the mid-1980s, the Government had the opportunity to loosen the purse strings and introduce a stimulus package which made some contribution to a reduction in unemployment. Debt management now requires a high growth strategy rather than a tight fiscal strategy. This shift is absolutely justifiable and sustainable with debt as a percentage of Gross State Product set to decline steadily over the next few years to well below 20 percent. The consequences of a debt first strategy are clearly spelt out in the Budget papers.

Employment and economic growth estimates in the Budget paint a frightening picture for those looking for work. At 0 percent this year and 1 percent next year employment growth will not be adequate to bring down unemployment to the national average over three years as the Government promises. Indeed South Australian unemployment is set to hover around 10 percent over the next few years.

At a time when a substantial fiscal stimulus is necessary to help kickstart the South Australian economy the Government has done nothing to accelerate the States economic growth rate. This is a formula for sustained decline and a further widening of the gap between South Australia's performance and that of the nation as whole.

A measure of the Government’s hypocrisy in the Budget is the claim that infrastructure expenditure has been increased when in fact the allocation has declined by $130 million. By failing to invest more substantially in infrastructure investment and end public sector job losses the State Government will face chronically high unemployment and poor economic growth over the next few years.

If the Government is serious about tackling the jobs crisis an additional $1 billion should have been allocated to infrastructure investment. This could have been accommodated by easing debt reduction. Additional capital expenditure would flow through to higher growth and reduce debt. This is the high road to economic development which the Government is so reluctant to take,

With the poor outlook for national jobs growth over the next twelve months it was vital that the State Government introduce additional job creation measures to help offset the negative impact of the Asian Financial crisis on South Australia.

One of the great disappointments in the Budget is that the 4500 jobs to be created by Jobs Package are likely to be offset by further job losses arising from cuts to education expenditure and further outsourcing of services. The Jobs Package does contain some positive initiatives however which if linked to a much more substantial industry and infrastructure development package would begin to make some inroads into unemployment.

Higher levels of productive investment and additional support for local business development are necessary to break the cycle of decline and chronic long term unemployment in South Australia.

Claims that privatisation of ETSA will have financial benefits to the State are illusory. Likely sale prices will result in a net loss to taxpayers of around $880 million over ten years. Tying the Budgets forward estimates to the sale of ETSA is a cheap political trick, likely to fuel further cynicism about the proposed sale.

While many of the increases in revenue in the Budget are regressive changes to land tax, gambling taxes are positive. In general the strong acknowledgment in this Budget of the need to broaden the States revenue base points to the need for a public review of taxation and revenue options for the future.

Perhaps the most disappointing aspect of the Budget is the Government has failed after four Budgets to deliver a coherent economic and employment development strategy for South Australia. The costs of this will be measured in the monthly ABS jobs figures. They will provide no comfort for the Government as it prepares for the next election.

* John Spoehr is deputy director of the Centre for Labour Research at the University of Adelaide
