Friday July 24, 1998

Cameron pulls lever, Rann lights up
by Legh Davis, Liberal Member in the Legislative Council

On Thursday night 23rd July just before 7.00pm Terry Cameron at the end of a long speech analysing the State budget, flicked the switch and relit tensions within the Labor Party about the privatisation of ETSA and Optima.

Cameron, in talking about asset sales, said that the community opposed them if they were "based on deceit, lies and stupidity and had been shown not to be in the public's economic interest."

At this point I interjected, "Well what about ETSA then?"

Cameron responded by saying that he had read material about the proposed privatisation and had discussed the matter with Professor Cliff Walsh and John Spoehr. And then he said, "If the case that has been made out by the Premier of New South Wales and the Treasurer, Michael Egan, to sell off their electricity assets in New South Wales is a compelling one, then one can draw their own conclusion from an economic analysis of South Australia."

Cameron then drove a stake through the heart of Mike Rann's anti-privatisation argument when he said, "The only conclusion one could come to is that the case must be more compelling in South Australia than it is in New South Wales because the economic fundamentals in South Australia are much worse than those in New South Wales. Our per capita debt is much higher, and it does not matter what economic statistic one looks at: we are performing at a worse level than is New South Wales. As I have said, the only conclusion to which I can come to date on the material at which I have looked is that it would be in the best economic interests of South Australia and its people for ETSA to be sold."

Cameron continued ".... if Bob Carr and Michael Egan want to sell the New South Wales electricity authority (for $22 billion) with a State debt of $13.2 billion ... one would have to be an economic idiot to come to any other conclusion. If they have made a compelling case in New South Wales, then we must have even more compelling reasons in this State for getting rid of ETSA."

So there you have it - the genie is out of the bottle. Cameron has had the courage to say publicly what I have been told at least 11 other Labor Members of Parliament have been saying privately.

And what was Labor Leader Mike (Also) Rann's response to Cameron's statement? The Advertiser of Friday 24 reported that Rann had warned Mr Cameron he faced automatic expulsion from the Labor Party if he crossed the floor to support the Government. Mr Rann in a statement said that Mr Cameron had "not raised his concerns during the ALP policy convention which unanimously confirmed our position on the ETSA privatisation or during the Parliamentary party's discussions on the ETSA Bill."

Let me just remind readers what's happening here. The Labor Party in South Australia set down their opposition to the privatisation of ETSA at an ALP convention in October 1996 - that's nearly two years ago. The world has moved on since then. In New South Wales the Labor Premier and Treasurer are committed to privatising power and you can't get enough money on the fact it will happen after the next State election, whichever Party wins office.

So how can Rann threaten Cameron with expulsion for agreeing with Labor Premier Carr and Treasurer Egan? It's a neat point.

While on the subject of Rann, you might remember that in my column last week I asked him to respond to three simple questions. Now the Labor Members tell me Rann is an avid supporter of The Electric Newspaper and he certainly would have read my column or at the very least have had it drawn to his attention.

The three questions I asked last week were:

Question 1 Can Rann guarantee that ETSA's current profitability will remain undiminished in this new competitive National Electricity Market, which will be dramatically different from the monopoly position which ETSA now enjoys?

Question 2 Can Rann guarantee that ETSA and Optima's sale value will remain undiminished in the face of this new competitive National Electricity Market?

Question 3 Will Rann guarantee categorically that he will not sell ETSA and Optima in the unlikely event of him becoming Premier after the next State election?

The sad thing about Labor's opposition to the privatisation of ETSA is that in their hearts the leadership team knows it is right. Impeccable Labor sources tell me that Rann and his Deputy Leader, Annette Hurley, along with Treasury spokesman, Kevin Foley, and Finance spokesman, Paul Holloway, all support privatisation. Of the leadership group, only Carolyn Pickles is against privatisation.

But Terry Cameron has flicked the switch and the arc light is now firmly swinging over the Labor Party's position on this critical legislation - unarguably the most important the Parliament has debated since the Roxby Downs Bill in 1982.
