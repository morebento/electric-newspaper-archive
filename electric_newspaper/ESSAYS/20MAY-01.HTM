Wednesday May 20, 1998

Risky business - the impact of the privatisation of public enterprise in South Australia
by John Spoehr

'Risky Business - the impact the of privatisation of public enterprise in South Australia' is a contribution to the debate on privatisation in South Australia. This report is provided in the hope that the South Australian community has the opportunity to compare our findings with the results of scoping studies commissioned by the Government on privatisation.

It is vital that all of these scoping studies are subject to public scrutiny to ensure that vested interests do not drive the sale process - this is particularly critical where consultants are likely to receive substantial fees - up to $30 million in the case of the ETSA/Optima sale. The stakes and the risks are high. Given that future Governments' hands will be tied by any decision to privatise, the South Australian public must be made aware of the longer term consequences of any privatisation. The South Australian public have the right to demand an assessment process which is transparent and subject to public scrutiny before any decision to privatise is made.

The great risk to the South Australian community from the current wave of privatisation proposals is the likelihood that significant medium term financial losses from privatisation will be ignored in favour of short term political gains. If driven by this ideological blindness the privatisations of TAB/Lotteries, ETSA/Optima and the Ports Corporation will create a $1250 million blackhole in the budget over the next ten years. In this context it is disturbing that the South Australian Government's approach to privatisation appears to be grounded in a combination of political pragmatism, a pre-occupation with short term debt reduction and an antipathy towards the public sector.

A more sober assessment of the likely impact of privatisation is required - such an assessment from government appointed consultants is unlikely given substantial vested interests in privatisation. While short term revenue raising from privatisation of public enterprises would enable some public debt to be retired, the interest savings from debt reduction would be insufficient to offset the revenue lost to Government arising from privatisation. The loss of revenue to Government from privatisation of the Lotteries Commission/TAB, Ports Corporation, HomeStart and ETSA while appearing to offer some short term financial gain in terms of debt reduction will on the basis of our analysis, harm the financial position of the State.

If partially utilised for the purposes of debt reduction, the revenue streams of these enterprises under public ownership will do much more to improve the financial position of the State than privatisation. On the basis of modest enterprise performance and likely sale prices, the privatisation of ETSA/Optima, TAB/Lotteries Commission and Ports Corporation will result in a $1250 million 'black hole' in the Budget, a $1250 million net loss to South Australian taxpayers over a ten year period. Interest savings are not expected to exceed the loss of revenue from the enterprises unless sale prices of $7 billion for ETSA/Optima, $2 billion for TAB/Lotteries and $500 million for Ports Corp are achieved.

Sale prices for ETSA/Optima are estimated to be between $4-6 billion while TAB/Lotteries is likely to attract around $1.4 billion based on interstate experience. On financial grounds alone the privatisation of the enterprises in question is not justifiable unless the total sale price exceeds $9500 million. This is highly unlikely.

Much of the momentum for privatisation in SA over the last four years was generated by an escalation of public sector debt arising from the SGIC and State Bank bail-outs. This problem was reinforced by the gradual decline over 1980s of Commonwealth grants, the relative severity of the recession in SA and, arguably, an insufficient taxation effort.

While State debt increased sharply due to the financial shocks of the early 1990s, debt as a percentage of GSP has declined significantly from nearly 28 percent in 1993 to 21 percent in 1997. Debt levels are now at historically low levels. They also compare favourably to benchmarks set in the mid to late 1980s when economic growth rates were considerably stronger than those of the mid 1990s.

A significant risk of privatisation is that it will actually harm our financial position. Rather than lower State debt, privatisation is likely to result in an escalation of debt. In this way the proposed privatisations would be smaller in scale but have similar effects to the recent financial crises already experienced in South Australia.

Evidence from the OECD only reinforces this concern. While some short term economic gains appear to be made through debt reduction, the loss of revenue streams and assets, often results in a net deterioration of Governments overall financial position as the OECD suggest.

"Each sale of a public asset only provides the government with a once-and -for-all source of revenue. The treatment of the proceeds of such sales as deficit reduction, or even as negative outlays, may lead to the incorrect impression that the Government's budget position has improved when all that has happened is that its financing has been made easier. Moreover, continual disposal of the Government's assets leads inevitably to the depletion of its wealth. Once the stock of assets is depleted, alternative financing must be found. The danger of bringing revenues from asset sales on-budget rather than keeping them off-budget is that, by running down their stock of assets, Governments will eventually find themselves even more constrained either to raise taxes, accept further increases in net debt, or monetise future deficits in order to finance the flow of expenditure" (OECD, 1986).

This report has revealed that rather than minimise risks to the taxpayer the sale of Government enterprises can be very Risky Business - resulting in huge financial losses. In our assessment of the public enterprises in question the risks outweigh the rewards of privatisation.

John Spoehr is deputy director of the Centre for Labour Research at the University of Adelaide.
