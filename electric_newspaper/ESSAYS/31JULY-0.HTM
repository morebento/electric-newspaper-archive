Friday July 31, 1998

A pox on both their houses
by Sandra Kanck, Australian Democrat MLC

Australians' former easy going political apathy long ago transformed into a general scepticism towards politicians. The combined impact of the Keating Labor and Howard Liberal Governments has been to transform this scepticism into a hard edged cynicism.

The two old parties have no one to blame but themselves. Labor's litany of broken promises on privatisations, tax cuts and economic rejuvenation eventually saw it swept from office by the least inspiring Opposition in many a long year.

The Howard Liberals promised a relaxed and comfortable Australia, one in which no one would be worse off and a Government that would have higher ethical standards than its predecessor. It then proceeded to create a new standard of political deception by defining its electoral promises as core and non-core, sacked tens of thousands of public servants and slashed Government services. So much for ‘Honest John'.

The impact of having two deeply distrusted Federal Governments back to back was highlighted in a recent Bulletin poll. It found a whopping 66 per cent of voters are unhappy with the Liberal and Labor parties.

Furthermore 55 per cent of electors want to see more independents and minor parties in the Senate. Given the Democrats, Greens and Independents already exercise the balance of power in the Senate neither of the old parties will enjoy the heady power of being an elected dictatorship in the foreseeable future.

Equally disturbing for the old parties was the revelation that 49 per cent of the electorate want to see more independents and minor parties in the House of Representatives. Currently an unprecedented eight Independents sit in the House of Representatives. That number is set to increase with 30 per cent of Coalition voters and 41 per cent of Labor voters considering voting independent.

Perhaps the most startling result was that 38 per cent of respondents want independents and minor parties to exercise the balance of power in the Senate and 36 per cent want them to have the same function in the House of Representatives. More than a third of the electorate doesn't trust Labor or Liberal with free reign in either House of the Federal Parliament.

This seismic shift in voter loyalty has already been witnessed in the recent Queensland and South Australian State elections. In Queensland One Nation grabbed 11 seats and the Beattie Labor Government rules with the support of an independent. In South Australia the Democrats hold the balance of power in the Upper House whilst independents and the National Party exercise that role in the Lower House.

Curiously, despite the emergence of a hung parliament in the South Australian Lower House, that old-chestnut of abolishing the Upper House has re-emerged. The logic goes something along the lines that, in the Upper House, minor parties (read Democrats) are in a position to frustrate the processes of Government and hence the Upper House should be abolished.

One problem with this line of reasoning is that both the Lower Houses in Queensland and South Australia would also need to be abolished. After all both Houses are at the mercy of independents.

Thoughtful readers might also be wondering how minor parties and independents manage to ‘frustrate' the business of Government. The secret is they don't perform that ‘trick' on their own. No minor party or independent has ever defeated Government legislation without the support of the Opposition of the day.

In fact both the major parties, when in Opposition, routinely oppose government legislation in search of political gain. Policy considerations often come a distant second to political calculations. In the South Australian Parliament these tactics have consistently left the Democrats as the arbitrators of the merits of Government legislation.

Rather than obstruct the legitimate business of Government I believe we have consistently and honestly reviewed Government legislation, holding it to account when necessary. This is the great value of an independent Upper House.

Consider the words of Labor Leader Mike Rann during debate on the Electricity Corporations Generation Bill:

"I'm not surprised that this Bill is being rushed through without the usual notice and consideration. That is why the Upper House will be so crucial in putting in amendments to stop the privatisation of ETSA." (Hansard, 29 May 1996, p1583)

Without the Upper House the Government's deceitful attempt to sell ETSA would have succeeded. And the ALP wants to abolish it!
