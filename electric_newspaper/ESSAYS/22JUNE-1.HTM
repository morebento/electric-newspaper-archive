Monday June 22, 1998

One redhead sparks a media fire
By Sandra Kanck, Australian Democrat MLC

One Nation's 23% of the primary vote and 10 seats in Queensland's State election is an impressive debut for the New Nationals. How did a fledgling party with little money and only one elected member in another parliament achieve this result?

Unprecedented amounts of media coverage for a third party provides is a significant part of the explanation. Deep dissatisfaction with the Coalition and Labor parties accounts for much of the rest.

The media coverage began when Pauline won the Labor seat of Oxley as the dis-endorsed Liberal candidate. The Liberals dumped her in the middle of the campaign for making racist remarks. The media's interest was immediately aroused.

Pauline's red hair, gender and simple style of speaking fuelled this interest. But the match that lit the Hanson media bonfire was her cunning inaugural speech Federal Parliament.

So what did she say to the House of Representatives on Tuesday the 10th of September 1996? Briefly, Pauline told us she entered Parliament not “as a polished politician but as a woman who has had her fair share of life's knocks."

She then preceded to outline a series of grievances that pretty much encompassed contemporary Australia's fears and prejudices. In turn Pauline attacked Aboriginal welfare, unemployment and official attempts to disguise its real level, the Family Law Act, the privatisation of Telstra, immigration, multiculturalism, foreign aid, tariff reductions and the level of interest rates.

In between we were told “to survive in peace and harmony, united and strong, we must have one people, one nation." After listing the respective populations of Japan, China, India, Indonesia and Malaysia, Pauline then told us we are in danger of being swamped by Asians. Exploitation of fear of the ‘yellow peril' is alive and well.

It wasn't all invective and insinuated dangers. The Adelaide to Darwin railway line was promoted as the type of project needed for national development and she advocated national service for all males and females reaching 18 years of age.

The Member for Oxley finished by exhorting Australians to ‘wake up'.

From that moment on the media has displayed a prurient interest in the fortunes and utterances of Pauline. It's notable the media coverage of the inaugural speech focused almost exclusively on her views about Aborigines and Asians.

The Prime Minister did his bit to keep the ball rolling, proclaiming an end to political correctness and the virtues of free speech. Australian's love of a sideshow did the rest.

Given the list of grievances dealt with in Pauline's inaugural speech it's easy to understand the oft-heard suggestion that ‘whilst I don't agree with everything she says she's right about some things'.

Indeed the Democrats share common ground with One Nation. We too support a reduction in immigration although not for fear of being swamped by Asians. The Democrats believe this ancient dry land can only support a limited population no matter what their colour or creed.

The Democrats are also in the forefront of calls for greater regulation of foreign capital and have been instrumental in alerting Australians to the dangers of the Multilateral Agreement on Investment.

We also oppose the privatisation of Telstra and have consistently opposed the imposition of extremist competition policy.

But the Democrats also stand for a racially tolerant, multicultural, inclusive Australia. Not very sexy in media terms. Nothing new or unusual or provocative, or divisive in that stance. Not like beating the race drum.

As a result of her inflammatory views, for a time, Pauline seemed to be everywhere - television, radio, newspapers and magazines. When the media's interest did wane Pauline appeared on national television telling us “when you see this you will know I have been murdered".

I read that as a political suicide note. It wasn't and, courtesy of a cynical preference strategy by Queensland's Coalition parties, One Nation again found itself the centre of media attention in the lead up to the Queensland election.

Again the Prime Minister weighed in on behalf of One Nation's vote when he entered the campaign to call Pauline ‘deranged'. That ill-considered outburst only added fuel to the electoral bonfire that was consuming the Old National Party.

At the last South Australian poll the Democrats received 16.4% of the primary vote. That was achieved with minimal media coverage. With just a skerrick more media attention we too would have broken into the Lower House. What we won't do is to peddle nonsense and promote division to increase our vote.

Australia can't afford it.
