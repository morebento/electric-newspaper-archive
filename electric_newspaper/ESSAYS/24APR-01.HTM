Friday April 24, 1998

Obligation - An old fshioned word
by Legh Davis, Liberal Member in the Legislative Council

Call me old fashioned, but whatever happened to the word "obligation"? The dictionary definition of "obligation" is "a moral or legal duty, or indebtedness for a service or favour". In law school students are taught that for every right there is a corresponding obligation. But while the media is full of stories of rights being taken away or denied, there is very little about that unfashionable word "obligation".

Let's take the example of smoking. There have been recent cases in the United States where individuals have taken action against tobacco companies on the grounds that smoking has been injurious to their health. That may not surprise the intelligent readers of The Electric Newspaper, but if this proposition is carried through to its logical conclusion, no-one is responsible for anything. The warnings about the link between smoking and cancer have been around for decades. But some of the people suing come from a generation who would have been heavily exposed to publicity about the dangers of smoking.

Closer to home, there is currently a debate raging about the evils of gambling and in particular the social and economic impact of poker machines. It should be emphasised that poker machine legislation was a product of the Bannon Labor Government which was desperate to raise revenue from anywhere and anyone following the State Bank and SGIC debacles. It is a matter of record that in the House of Assembly the poker machine legislation passed narrowly. Of the 21 votes in favour, 17 were cast by Labor Party Members.

In the Legislative Council the legislation was passed by the barest of margins and only because Premier Bannon spent the very early hours of one morning talking to a Labor Party Member under a rather bright light! I voted against the poker machine legislation, as did the majority of my Liberal Party colleagues, because I did not like the model proposed and I was uneasy about the social and economic consequences.

Although there is now widespread public concern about poker machines, it should be remembered that gambling takes many forms. There are people who may be hooked on blackjack or the roulette wheel at the Casino. The racing codes (horse racing, trotting and greyhounds) while providing significant employment in South Australia, also inevitably play host to chronic gamblers. Hundreds of people have ruined their lives and those of their loved ones by excessive gambling.

And the hospitality industry through hotels and clubs obviously has catered for a large number of alcoholics. Drinking has the added dimension that it not only causes financial ruin and human misery, but also death through drink driving.

And in leisure, Victoria experienced a spate of drownings in the recent summer months, almost all due to the fact that swimmers flaunted the safety flags and swam in unpatrolled areas.

There have been horrific examples over recent years of parents partying all night or being absent during the day and leaving young children at home alone to fend for themselves.

Perhaps of lesser consequence but still relevant to the argument is the irresponsibility of people who leave broken glass on a beach, litter the pavement with rubbish, or desecrate or vandalise public or private property.

The vast majority of the community could plead not guilty to any of the above. But the social and economic costs of people flaunting their rights and privileges while ignoring their obligations are enormous.

There is also a growing tendency to blame governments for anything and everything that goes wrong. Individuals or the community at large increasingly expect governments to find solutions to social or economic problems not of the government's making. In some cases these problems may have been avoided or solved if individuals had a clearer recognition of their obligations.

It would be nice to think the big O word might enjoy an increase in popularity as we scramble towards the millennium. But perhaps I am just old fashioned!
