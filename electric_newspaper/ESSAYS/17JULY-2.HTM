Friday July 17, 1998

Will Ingo finally get caught in a burning warehouse?
by Ralph Clarke, Labor MHA for Ross-Smith

Some three months ago, prior to Graham Ingerson's (or "Ingo" as he is widely referred to) most recent difficulties involving the dismissal of a former Chief Executive Officer of the South Australian Thoroughbred Racing Authority, Mr Merv Hill, a feature article appeared in the Adelaide Advertiser on Graham Ingerson.

In that feature article I am quoted as saying that if you were caught in a burning warehouse make sure you were standing next to Graham Ingerson, because if there was a way out he would find it. My comments related to the fact that despite seemingly insurmountable odds Graham Ingerson has managed to, in the past, not only survive, but indeed be promoted, when many other Ministers would have been forced to resign.

Graham Ingerson has had a history of falling foul of the Opposition and the media with respect to incidents which have raised questions as to whether or not he misled the Parliament in his answers to various questions. Without going through all the details of them, they relate to his handling of the tourism portfolio, as to whether or not he sought to use political influence with respect to the employment and attempted dismissal of certain public servants within that portfolio. This led to the celebrated public stoush he had with the former CEO of the Tourism Commission , Michael Gleeson, who accused him of political interference in the running of that Department.

That in turn led to a no confidence motion against him in the House of Assembly, which he survived on Party lines. In addition of course, there has been the ETSA $97 million write-down, which he claimed he had no knowledge of before reading its Annual Report in December 1997, despite being its Minister when the ETSA board took its decision.

Ingo outside of the Parliamentary Chamber is quite an amiable person, and his company is enjoyed by a number of Members on the Opposition benches. However, his handling of his portfolios and in particular his predilection to put his foot in his mouth at crucial times has bought a great deal of joy to the Labor Opposition, and despair to his own Party Members.

Indeed it is difficult to imagine why Ingo answered the questions put to him by Labor's Opposition Treasury spokesperson Kevin Foley, on the Merv Hill issue, the way he did. He is an experienced politician and he must have known that Kevin Foley already knew the answers to the questions he was asking, and was waiting to see whether or not he would receive straight-forward answers to those questions.

Ingo could have legitimately said that as the Minister for Racing, he wanted his views about Merv Hill to be taken into account by the Racing Authority and that if the Racing Authority suffered as a result of Mr Hill's appointment as the CEO, then he (that is, Ingo) would "cop" the flack for it as the Minister for Racing, and therefore he had every right to at least voice his views on whoever the successful applicant might be as CEO of SATRA.

Whilst that answer may have got him into some trouble for deliberately involving himself in the appointment of a person to a Statutory Authority, when he has no responsibility or right at law to do so, at least it would have been a straight answer, and one which would have barely caused a ripple in the morning press.

Of course the rest of the story is history, in that as a result of Ingo's answers, both in the Estimates Committee of the House of Assembly, and in the Parliament itself, a Privileges Committee has been established by the Parliament to determine as to whether or not Ingo misled the Parliament.

This is the first time in 140 years of self government in South Australia that a Privileges Committee has been set up to investigate such a matter with respect to a Minister of the Crown.

Ingo, effectively, was required to resign as Deputy Premier. Some Liberal strategists believed that they would be able to sell to the Liberal Independents and the National Party, who hold the balance of power in the House of Assembly, that that was a sufficient penalty for Graham Ingerson.

That of course isn't the case. Whilst the loss of the Deputy Premiership means a loss of status for him, the fact of the matter is he remains a senior Minister, with a senior portfolio in the Olsen Government, (Industry, Trade and Tourism) and as far as his pay is concerned he has only suffered an $8,000 per annum pay cut, which is mere postage money for a person with Mr Ingerson's financial standing.

The Labor Opposition could barely contain its glee that the Liberal Party again failed to act decisively to stop their internal haemorrhaging. Keeping Graham Ingerson on the front bench mortally wounded is the best thing any political opposition party could have going for it.

Can Ingo get out of the burning warehouse this time? It would look unlikely at this stage, the loss of the Deputy Premiership meant that he got singed by the fires raging around him, but he has not been consumed within that burning warehouse yet and I am sure that he is furtively, but frantically, looking for that one last escape hatch which he has always found in the past. However on this occasion I think the trap door is firmly latched.

From rumours around the corridors of Parliament House, it would appear that no deal has been done with the so called Independents and therefore Graham Ingerson and the Liberal Party would seem to be steeling themselves for an adverse finding against him, by the Privileges Committee, at least by a majority of them, when they report to the House of Assembly next Tuesday when Parliament again resumes.

If Graham Ingerson resigns or is forced to resign from the Ministry, where does he go then? He could of course whittle away his time on the Government backbench until the next State election. However, that seems unlikely in my view, knowing the person.

If he is to leave the Parliament at a convenient time to the Olsen Government, it may be to assume some other public office or sinecure befitting a person of his standing within the Liberal Party. If that happens there would be a by-election in what would normally be regarded as a rock solid safe Liberal seat of Bragg.

Bragg, which covers the eastern suburbs such as Burnside, is hardly likely to revolt against the Olsen Government in sufficient numbers to see a Labor Party member elected in that seat. However, what would be interesting is the internal faction fighting which will inevitably engulf the Liberal Party as to whether a wet or a dry, an Olsen supporter or a Brown supporter or whatever other shade of colour of opinion they have in the Liberal Party, will succeed Graham Ingerson, as the Liberal Party standard bearer in Bragg.

What should also worry the Liberal Party is not only the opening again of all the factional rifts that they have within their Party, but as to whether or not an Independent Liberal or just an Independent running in Bragg might not make a significant dent in the Liberal Party vote in that seat. We have seen Independent Liberal's in formerly safe Liberal seats of McKillop and Gordon in the South East, and we have seen in Jeff Kennett's heartland of Victoria in Gippsland West that a formerly safe Liberal seat was lost to an Independent, who was formerly the Labor Party candidate for that same seat in the 1996 State election.

A large swing away from the Olsen Liberal Government in such Tory heartland, even if the Liberal Party wins a by election in Bragg, would be a major blow to an already morale deadened Liberal Government and would put further pressure on John Olsen's leadership.

A win by an Independent of any particular political flavouring would be an absolute nightmare for John Olsen and the Liberal Party, and would certainly end Olsen's leadership.

Whilst it may be premature to be writing Graham Ingerson's obituary, I should also in fairness to him point out that when he was Minister for Industrial Affairs, he got through a number of significant pieces of legislation, such as a major overhaul of the old Industrial Relations Act, giving rise to non union enterprise agreements being able to be made in the State award system. There were significant changes to the WorkCover legislation which saw benefits to injured workers significantly reduced, at a cost saving to employers, and he introduced changes to the shop trading hours legislation which allowed for the extension of Sunday trading in the central business district of Adelaide.

Each of these pieces of legislation were hard fought over, I should know, I was the Opposition Shadow person responsible for opposing those pieces of legislation, and Ingerson achieved those changes by badgering, cajoling, threatening and flattering the Australian Democrats in the Legislative Council.

When I first entered State Parliament in December 1993, Graham Ingerson gave me a wise piece of advise which I should have listened to with greater attentiveness, when he told me that "Ralph, not all of your enemies sit opposite you in the Chamber". This statement is a political truism as relevant today as any other time in our political history.
