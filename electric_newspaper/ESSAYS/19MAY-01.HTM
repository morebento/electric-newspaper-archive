Tuesday May 19, 1998

West Beach: more than just a picket line
by Chris Warren*

It's time for the West Beach boat harbour protesters to move on.

Confronting amiable cops with a community picket line, and resisting attempts to be man-handled away from trucks loaded with rocks is wearing a bit thin and is doing nothing to educate the public about the real issues surrounding the Glenelg/West Beach mega-development.

Any casual visitor to Glenelg must be wondering what's happening to South Australia's most famous tourist beach. It looks and sounds like a bomb site - mounds of bluestone boulders, a huge hole where there was once a car park, and the incessant clank of a pile-driver forcing shafts of steel into the sand.

Of course it's no longer a natural beach but a construction site, and as any developer will tell you, to make a souffle you first have to break eggs.

In the next few weeks a seven storey apartment building and marina pier will start rising from the sands, and protected from the waves by an “artificial reef”, which is a euphemism, for another pile of boulders dropped into the sea.

The shape of the Glenelg beach has changed forever, and 80 apartment owners will have the privilege of living on an engineered foreshore which used to be accessible to hundreds of beach-goers who parked their cars at the end of Anzac Highway.

For several months every year these apartment dwellers will have a dredge at their front door-steps pumping sand to the north of the Patawalonga entrance, and another dredge to remove seaweed, an unexpected nuisance which is clogging up the channel.

Already $420,000 has been spent in the past six months removing seaweed, and that's on top of the half a million dollars budgeted for sand management every year.

The biggest folly of the Glenelg development is that after spending millions of dollars removing the notorious Patawalonga sandbar which jeopardised the safety of small boat-owners for 30 years, those same boaties are being shut out of the new marina.

The marina will cater only for 59 large yachts and launches, owned of course by the apartment dwellers.

Dozens of small boat owners who currently use the north Glenelg launching ramp will be forced to pay a substantially higher launching fee at the new West Beach boat harbour, which is the target of community protests.

So the message to the community picketers is that while concern for the erosion of your beaches is quite justified, it's only a small part of the big picture.

Building too close to the beach and interfering with natural coastal processes has caused huge problems in the past.

Now we're watching the dramatic re-shaping of Adelaide's most precious piece of coastline, putting our faith in engineers and developers, and relying on State and Local Governments to come up with the millions of dollars needed to guarantee our beaches will always be there.

*Chris Warren is environment reporter with Seven National News
