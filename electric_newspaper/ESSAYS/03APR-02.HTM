Friday April 3, 1998

Parliament can make life better - sometimes!
by Legh Davis, Liberal Member in the Legislative Council

Many South Australians fondly remember the good old days and believe life was better then than it is now. But in many ways our lives have been improved and sometimes, perhaps to people’s surprise, laws passed by Parliament have helped make that happen.

Take, for example, deaths on South Australian roads. In 1970 there were 349 deaths on South Australian roads. Our population in that year was just 1.16 million. However last year only 149 people were killed on South Australian roads, 200 less than 27 years earlier. In that period our population has increased by nearly 30% to about 1.5 million.

What are the reasons for this dramatic reduction in road deaths? Firstly, car design has improved the safety of the vehicles in which we travel. Secondly, legislation in 1971 which made the wearing of seat belts compulsory, is now well accepted by the community. Thirdly, random breath testing has had a significant role in minimising drink driving, particularly amongst the young.

Initially there were many opponents to seat belt legislation. I remember one woman who threatened to chain herself to a stobie pole because this legislation would eliminate her freedom of choice. But there has been overwhelming evidence that the wearing of seat belts significantly reduces the possibility of death and/or serious injuries to both drivers and passengers. A recent example was the tragic death of Princess Diana. Several experts were of the strong view that she could have survived the accident in Paris had she been wearing a seat belt.

When random breath test legislation was first proposed in South Australia in the early 1980's, it created a furore in sections of the media and the liquor and hospitality industries. The News, Adelaide's afternoon newspaper at the time, ran an eight page venomous attack on this legislation. Of course it had nothing to do with the drinking habits of the journalists at the paper!

I was a member of the two select committees of the Legislative Council which examined random breath testing (RBT). The statistics were both compelling and frightening. Roughly half the deaths on South Australian roads at that time were due to drink driving. And about half the people killed as a result of drink driving were innocent victims.

The first select committee inspected RBT in Victoria, which had been a pioneer in world terms in introducing testing in 1976. On a wet and misty night near Moonee Valley we watched the RBT unit in operation. The gnarled sergeant who had been with the unit since it had been established, recounted many amusing moments. On one occasion they were packing up for the night when a car sped past the RBT caravan, which was lit up like a Christmas tree, at around 100 kilometres per hour, well in excess of the speed limit. They heard a screech of brakes and the car dangerously reversed up the highway to the caravan. The driver staggered out and approached the caravan. He slurred, "A chest X-ray unit! What a bloody good idea! About time I had one of those." They duly did give him one of those, but it wasn't an X-ray and he blew 0.18 and was charged!

The South Australian Parliament quickly appreciated the benefits of RBT laws and introduced further legislation requiring first time drivers to have a zero blood alcohol content. There has been a dramatic reduction in the number of deaths, injuries and accidents among young drivers as a result of this law.

Random breath testing is not about catching people, so much as changing perceptions and attitudes towards drink driving. If you drink, don't drive and you'll stay alive.

So sometimes politicians do have the satisfaction of seeing legislation which they have supported, working to the benefit of a community which often had been initially sceptical or hostile.
