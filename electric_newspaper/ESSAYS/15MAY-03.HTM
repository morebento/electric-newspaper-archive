Friday May 15, 1998

Will you still need me when I'm 82?
by Sandra Kanck, Australian Democrat MLC

If you reach the age of 82, and made your contribution to your family, community and country, will you hope to step back a little and at last be the one who's waited on? We may justly expect it; for some that respite never comes.

Volunteers Week has been and gone, and we've sung the praises of that enormous band of people who are the glue of our society, those who make the connections between people that an economy is unable to make.

One of those unsung heroes is an 82 year old woman - a partially self-funded retiree. Recently, prior to going away for a fortnight, she sought to find a replacement for the volunteer work she does.

It was no mean feat, and it must have taken more than one person to fill her shoes, given the level of her commitments. In a suburban retirement complex she:

  1. edits a monthly newsletter for residents
  2. delivers the completed newsletters and other information to the 200 plus letterboxes within the complex
  3. plays the piano for church services in the nursing home
  4. wheels in nursing home residents from and to their rooms when entertainment is available
  5. assists wheelchair bound residents in their shopping centre excursions
  6. helps hostel residents with their meals
  7. escorts residents to medical appointments
  8. works in an on-site Thrift Shop and
  9. sorts and puts away washing in the nursing home once a week.

One of her 'off-campus' activities involves work in a Lutheran Community Care second hand shop one a month, although some other tasks had to be curtailed when she turned 80 years of age as she lost her way getting there in her car!

I do not even know this woman's name. I have never met her. She is somebody's mother, and I was told about her when I recently had my quarterly meeting with SACOSS (South Australian Council of Social Services) to discuss forthcoming budgets at both the State and Federal levels.

No doubt these undertakings keep active the mind and body of an 82 year-old, but one has to question the fact that her voluntary work is essential for the wellbeing of so many in that retirement complex and in other endeavours. And one wonders how many others are in similar situations.

Funding cutbacks at both State and Federal levels have become the rule as governments of all persuasions attempt to produce balanced budgets. This occurs against a background of paranoia about debt.

But Governments are failing to distinguish between what I call "good" debt and "bad" debt. When you or I go into debt to buy a house so that we will have somewhere to live at relatively low cost when we reach retirement, that debt must surely be seen as a positive. But the Treasurer viewing the same financial situation would surely see it as a negative for no other reason than that it bears the word "debt".

So, in the flurry to reduce debt, funding to welfare organisations is cut with a resulting increased dependence on volunteers. Lutheran Community Care, for instance, receives $58,000 p.a. to run an emergency relief scheme, but the Government seems not to recognise that it costs that organisation $40,000 p.a. - basics for renting office space and equipment and having one fulltime clerical person to administer the money - to deliver the service.

As they (somewhat laughingly) suggested to me, savings could be made if they simply put a bucket of money out on the verandah once a week - although the money might not get to the people most in need. While some volunteers are already assisting in delivering the service, more volunteers may be required when the fine details of the budget are revealed.

In the past an 82 year-old was honoured and cherished, and given time to catch breath and reflect on life. Now she could find herself working as a volunteer to keep an essential community-based organisation afloat.

As the song says "the times they are a-changin". The question is whether that change is for the better.
