Friday May 15, 1998

Shock horror! Federal Budget delivers good news
by Legh Davis, Liberal Member in the Legislative Council

It is not easy to read good news these days . The reporting of good news is distinctly unfashionable. Why should people be entitled to know good news, when there is so much misery in the world? The old adage for television news, which is practised religiously by commercial stations, is "If it bleeds, it leads".

Therefore it may have been disturbing to some people to see that the Federal Budget delivered by Treasurer Peter Costello, actually contained some good news. The underlying budget surplus was $2.7 billion, which is a dramatic turnaround from the $10.3 billion deficit which the Liberal Party inherited from the Keating Labor Government. You might remember for a long time Labor was adamant that there was no such black hole, although in recent days Shadow Treasurer, Gareth Evans, has overcome his earlier Alzheimer's attack and admitted there was indeed a black hole of our own making.

Gross domestic product (GDP) will increase in 1998/99 by 3%, having been shaved by 0.75% to reflect the Asian financial crisis. Unemployment is predicted to fall to 7.75%.

The rate of inflation is expected to edge up to 2.5%, reflecting the inflationary impact of the weakening Australian dollar and the increase in imports and import prices.

Over the next three years the Government plans to slash the Commonwealth debt by two thirds as a result of asset sales.

Even Paul Keating would be forced to admit this was "a beautiful set of numbers". However, the real challenge for the Howard Government is tax reform. The Fraser Government of the late 1970's squibbed the opportunity of introducing a goods and services tax (GST), even though it had control of the Senate - the last Federal Government to do so. The Hawke Government's tax summit flirted with a Keating-driven GST proposal, but backed off under pressure. In 1991 Liberal Leader, John Hewson's Fightback package won over the voters for a period of time until it was savaged by Paul Keating following his elevation to the Prime Ministership.

So 1998 will be fourth time lucky for major tax reform.

The facts are compelling. Average earnings in Australia are now $39,000 per annum. But the income tax rate is 43 cents in the dollar for over $38,000 in earnings and 34 cents in the dollar for between $20,700 and $38,000. These are extraordinarily high and punitive marginal rates for average income earners. 6.2 million of the 8.2 million taxpayers in Australia (75.6%) earn between $5,000 and $38,000. Obviously the main benefits from any tax cuts should flow to people in these income brackets.

The optimistic outlook for the Australian economy over the next 12 months is encouraging, particularly in view of the fact that Australia in comparative terms has a greater trade exposure to Asia than any other country.

The major wild card in this otherwise promising economic future is unexpected economic and/or financial dislocation in Asia. Japan, which accounts for 20% of our exports, has at best a flat economy and perhaps could be entering into a period of recession, which reflects the internal structural problems which have remained unaddressed over the last 5 years. The forecast for the Indonesian economy is grim, with an 8% decline in GDP expected in 1998. South Korea is also forecast to slump, with a 4% fall in GDP in the current year. On the other hand, China has emerged as the financial leader in Asia, holding its currency firm in the face of sharply depreciating currencies elsewhere. Its highly focused leadership is determined to drive the transition from a communist state with inefficient and bureaucratic government-owned instrumentalities, to a market economy. Economic growth is forecast to be in the order of 8% in the current year. Taiwan, up 5.5% and Hong Kong, with a 3% increase predicted, are also performing well despite regional uncertainty.

It is interesting to reflect that during the 1980's and 1990's (except for the last 8 months) Australia has piggy-backed on annual Asian economic growth in the order of 8-10%. The basic strength and stability of the Australian economy is underlined by the fact our economic outlook is still excellent, notwithstanding the damaging impact of Asia. Now that's what I call really good news!
