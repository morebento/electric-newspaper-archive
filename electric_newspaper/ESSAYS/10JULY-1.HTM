Friday July 10, 1998

Wedge politics
by Sandra Kanck, Australian Democrat MLC.

The Prime Minister is probably second only to Tim Fischer in his relief that a double dissolution on Wik has been averted.

John Howard must have had more than the occasional sleepless night recently. The rise of One Nation, biting a huge chunk out of the Conservatives' primary vote, is enough to give any Liberal leader heartburn. The prospect of a Wik double dissolution consummating the transformation of Howard's battlers into Hanson's converts would have sharpened the pain deeply. The fact Howard nurtured the conditions that encouraged the growth of One Nation must have had the Prime Minister tugging at his collar gasping for breath.

Make no mistake: Howard ploughed the ground, sowed the seeds and watered the field of the Wik dispute. He did so imagining that the Labor Party would wither on the vine. It's proved a bitter harvest.

The Liberal's strategy on Wik has been lifted straight from the U.S. Republican's handbook on how to politically exploit racial animosity. The Republicans call it wedge politics.

Ironically the Republicans' strategy had its genesis in the successes of the civil rights movement in the sixties. In response to the appalling inequality exposed by the movement the Democrats sought legislative remedies. In particular quotas were introduced for public sector jobs, the letting of public contracts and for admission to educational institutions.

The initial popular support for quotas waned as the costs became apparent. Those costs were borne predominantly by working class whites. It was in the low-skilled low-paid occupations where the quotas bit.

By the end of the sixties the Republicans noticed a strategy of opposition to racially determined quotas was a potent political weapon. It drove a wedge between two core constituencies of the Democrats, working class whites and blacks hopeful of securing those jobs they were previously excluded from. It is classic divide and conquer. It played on deep seated prejudices.

The language of Republican wedge politics in the USA is temperate. "Racist" remains a pejorative term. Hence the end of quotas was about returning equality of opportunity no matter the colour or creed. Never mind the reality of life before quotas.

The strategy led to the creation of a voting block later known as Reagan's Democrats. The 1996 Federal election produced a remarkably similar phenomenon with a flood of traditional Labor voters crossing to the conservative parties. These voters even got a label - Howard's battlers.

The Keating Government's commitment to native title and reconciliation was widely supported by the middle class left and much resented by many working class voters. Howard's Wik manoeuvres were designed to exploit this division amongst Labor voters.

The issue in Australia is different, native title rather than quotas, but the pitch is similar. John Howard talked of equality when he proposed pulling the teeth from native title rights. Howard knew that forcing the ALP into opposing his position would continue to alienate many of its traditional supporters.

On one level Howard's strategy has been reasonably successful. The ALP's primary vote has barely budged from its disastrous showing at the last Federal election. The failure of the strategy is found in the plummeting Coalition vote. Howard's battlers have rejected both the Coalition and the ALP and welcomed the new party of the right, One Nation.

Letting the racial genie out of the bottle wasn't the only reason for the rise of One Nation but it sure helped. Each time Howard twisted the knife of racism he strengthened One Nation.

Remember Howard holding up the map of Australia showing native title would apply just about everywhere and his podium thumping performance at the national reconciliation conference? Each incident was laden with deceit and double meaning. Tim Fischer did his bit claiming that freehold title was under threat from native title.

It took the Queensland election to conclusively demonstrate that the Howard strategy had bitten the Coalition hardest. That's why Howard was beaming to the cameras after the passage of the Wik legislation. Escaping the gallows must be an exhilarating experience.

He didn't deserve to be let off so lightly. In courting the racist vote Howard exposed his own character deficiency. He demeaned the office of Prime Minister and set race relations back 30 years. Australia has lost a golden opportunity to become a fairer country.'
