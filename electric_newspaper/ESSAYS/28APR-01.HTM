Tuesday April 28, 1998

Strange - some people don't believe him
by Patrick Conlon, Labor MP for Elder

The Deputy Premier, Graham Ingerson, is in trouble again. To say that Mr Ingerson is accident-prone is to note the Titanic took a bit of water after hitting the iceberg.

He has survived against the odds in the past, but he looks at the present very likely to sink under the weight of his own shortcomings.

Mr Ingerson is currently in trouble because of the answers he gave in parliament about the date he first learned of an extraordinary $96 million ETSA write-down. The timing is significant because late knowledge of the write-down was an important element in holding together the Government’s paper-thin story that it decided to sell ETSA only after getting new information following the State election.

His initial claim was that he didn’t know about it until he read the ETSA annual report in December last year. Subsequently he told Parliament that he learned about it when he received the annual report during the election campaign - plainly an inconsistency.

He was let off the hook on a charge of misleading Parliament when the Speaker accepted his apology and allowed him to return to his original answer.

Recent developments in the Parliament’s Economic Development and Finance Committee have, however, placed his likely knowledge of the write-down much earlier even than the election campaign, and put him right back on the hook again.

The Under-Treasurer, Gerard Bradley, in oral and written answers to questions from the committee, allowed that the write-down had been considered to be a significant commercial matter and thus had been reported in briefing notes to Mr Ingerson twice prior to June 1997.

The Deputy Premier now concedes the existence of the briefing notes but explains they were read by his advisers who, apparently keen not to excite the sensitivities of their Minister with news of such a large loss, decided that Graham would do his job better if his mind was not cluttered with knowledge pertaining to his portfolio responsibilities.

Oddly enough, some people don’t believe him.

Graham has flown off overseas with the matter still hanging over his head and with further witnesses to be called before the committee, including the heads of ETSA and Graham’s own former chief of staff.

It is difficult to see things getting any better for him or for John Olsen’s leadership, which will be seen by many to be linked to his deputy’s struggle for survival.
