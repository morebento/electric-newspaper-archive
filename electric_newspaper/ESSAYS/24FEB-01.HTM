Tuesday February 24, 1998

Olsen's five-month credibility gap
By Hendrik Gout

There were amazing scenes at Parliament House yesterday. Just as some of us were once told that there is no Santa Claus, no Tooth Fairy, and no reason to be silly, today we were told there was no such thing as a Secret Report.

Quite a shock.

You'll remember that a week ago today, Premier John Olsen announced to his own Liberal colleagues - who may nominally be expected to represent their electors - and to the rest of us that he was putting $2 to $4 billion dollars of our property on the market.

He had earlier been asked if he was going to do this.

He said "no."

That was during the election campaign, when to have answered "yes" would have seen him kicked out of office. Big way.

But he didn't say "yes". Not then.

Last week, he not only said "Yes"; he also asked us to believe that he didn't lie when he said "no".

It is a good thing that we believe him. Otherwise we'd have to kick him out - not for selling ETSA, but for lying. Big way.

Last Tuesday, Mr Olsen said he had no plans of selling ETSA and Optima until he received the Auditor-General's report in mid-December.

It has now been revealed that the State's top government advisors, including ETSA itself, the Premier's chief executive officer, and the Office of Energy Policy, as well as the Under-Treasurer, Gerald Bradley, had had the report since mid-1997.

In a Parliament House committee room yesterday the urbane and unflappable Auditor-General, Mr Kenneth Ian MacPherson, LLB, FCPA, was before the Economic and Finance committee.

Now Mr MacPherson is as straight as they come. His credentials are impeccable. He is an independant officer of the Parliament, and even his critics concede he is scrupulously honest.

Here's what he said in questioning from Labor's Trish White:

MS WHITE: Are you saying that on 28 July the advisor to (Minister) Graham Ingerson was given a copy?

MR MACPHERSON: Mr Longbottom.

MS WHITE: Of your draft report?

MR MACPHERSON: Yes, that is correct.

MS WHITE: Anyone in Treasury?

MR MACPHERSON: The copy went to the Under-Treasurer.

MS WHITE: The Under-Treasurer?

MR MACPHERSON: Yes.

MS WHITE: So the Under-Treasurer plus Graham Ingerson's advisor had a draft copy of this report on 28 July?

MR MACPHERSON: That is correct.

MR FOLEY: Three months before an election when they said they wouldn't sell it.

South Australians are therefore being asked by the Premier to believe that some of the State's top public servants were given shock-horror details of a potential loss of $1 billion, but declined to tell him or the Minister for Infrastructure.

The Premier's department chief, Ian Kowlick, says this is exactly what happened. He says this was due to "the fact that a State election was about to be called".

We believe that. Don't we?

But what about immediately after the election?

According to what Mr Olsen said in Parliament seven days ago, he was not made aware of the Auditor-General's report until mid-December, and it was then that he decided ETSA must be sold.

If this is the case, then one would imagine a conversation like this in the Premier's office immediately after the election.

PREMIER: Good morning, Ian. Nice day. Hey, did you see in the papers that I've won the election?

MR KOWALICK: Oh, yeah, I think I read that. Congratulations.

PREMIER: Well, we'll have to do lunch sometime. Meanwhile, is there anything new?

MR KOWALICK: Oh, a billion-dollar exposure thing on ETSA came up a few months ago.

PREMIER: Tell me about it in mid-December, will you?

Yes, that's plausible for sure. Far more plausible than the Premier being told either before the election, or immediately after.

Because it was a Secret Report, wasn't it?

Because we still believe in Santa Claus, the Tooth Fairy, and not being silly.
