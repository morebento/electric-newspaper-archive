Tuesday June 2, 1998

Roxby: Illusion and delusion in the desert
by Jack Hoogland

Mr Legh Davis (Electric Newspaper Thursday April 30 1998, 'Roxby Downs - the world's biggest mirage'), rather appropriately refers to the Olympic Dam uranium mine in terms of "mirage".

A mirage is a sort of shimmering optical illusion, a pool of inviting water in the hot desert sands. Classically, our intrepid heroes, crawling over these blistering sands under a merciless sun, and within a few croaks of their demise, are completely and as it turns out, cruelly convinced of the reality of this apparition and expend precious reserves of energy to stagger to the location to dive into, to drink of ... more sand.

Mirages, it would seem, can manifest as little puddles of water amongst the ripples in the sand right through to the full blown oasis complete with palms, camels and spacious Arabian tent. In the early 80's, the proponents of Roxby Downs cast their spell and weaved ever such a shimmering sight - a glorious city in the desert the size of Mt Isa, a stream of gold and riches into the State treasury, and yes, there would be work for all! How could our intrepid heroes resist such splendour in the desert?

At the time, of course, there were the usual collection of fringe dwellers and obstructers of progress who would have it that this shining arrow to the future was just a mirage. Furthermore, they opposed the participation of Australia in the world nuclear fuel cycle, opposed the use of our uranium in "weapons of mass destruction", opposed the release of concentrated by-products of fission into the environment where its safe handling would be landed in the laps of the next thousand generations - ironic isn't it, that without uranium, the Indians and Pakistanis wouldn't be engaging in this absurd nuclear chest thumping exercise - but I digress.

So let's look at the oasis, that glittering promised land, that "biggest thing to happen to SA in the last 20 years":

  • Jobs. By 1997, 946 direct jobs relating to production (not construction) existed, compared to the illusory 2268 to 2815.
  • Production. The value of production estimated at $1200 million reached only $350 million by 1997.
  • Royalties. The State's coffers would have resembled Aladdin's cave but alas, the $50 million annual royalties promised in 1982 have only come to $9.5 million by 1997. For the 20 years 1988 to 2008, the Roxby joint venturers predicted royalties of $2010 million. By 1997, this figure had reached $60 million.

And what of the "magnificent contribution to the South Australian economy" from Roxby? The costs to the State in infrastructure, by the end of the '95/6 financial year, exceeded royalties by $10 million. This fact was revealed by the Government itself, which starts to give one an insight into either the quality of the mirage or the state of mind of our heroes.

Now as it happens, it seems the desert is awash. Yes, this is an exciting time for our heroes as the Draft Environmental Impact Statements for both the Beverley and Honeymoon uranium mines are about to be presented. No doubt our unemployed will all find work, our deficits will be paid and the flowers will all bloom in winter, because mirages are real, if you really truly believe.
