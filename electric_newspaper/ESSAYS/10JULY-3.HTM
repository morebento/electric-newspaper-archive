Friday July 10, 1998

Cheryl Kernnot: the Kim Philby of Australian politics
by John Schumann *

The campaign for the political beatification of Cheryl Kernot, it would seem, is attempting to regain some momentum after being derailed by media politics and the Member for Oxley.

The idea that Ms Kernot embodies all the values and aspirations lost to Australia in the nation's headlong rush into the arms of economic rationalism is one which amuses those of my colleagues who were around before she joined the Democrats.

It is fair to say that while she travelled with the Democrats she espoused those values and aspirations with sincerity and conviction. It is also fair to remind people that Ms Kernot was at great pains to lambast the ALP and the Coalition for sneering at the Democrats for holding to their core values. That she finds herself in a different ideological context is now a matter for Ms Kernot and her apologists.

Economic rationalism has been embraced with equal fervour by both the Coalition and the ALP. No amount of rewriting of history can change that and, frankly, it beggars belief that Ms Kernot will be able to single-handedly inoculate an organisation like the ALP against the ravages of an electorate which has finally had enough of economic rationalism.

I have been around the Democrat camp for some months now and in my view Ms Kernot is the Kim Philby of contemporary Australian politics. Almost every utterance of hers and every strategic move by the ALP indicates that, after her defection, she was and remains a willing participant in a very thorough debriefing process.

Recently there has been a spate of articles that indicate that Ms Kernot's media minders are cranking up again. Mr McKenzie Wark's recent contribution to the Australian (She who must be obeyed 1/7/98) is a case in point.

The attempt to hijack the recent work done by the Democrats in developing a credible and achievable strategy to counter the threat of One Nation in the Senate is patently obvious and rests on the very dubious proposition that the choice for the electorate is between Hanson and Kernot.

There are a number of problems with this theory. In the first place, to propose that the entire Australian electorate will orbit around either the Member for Oxley or an as yet unelected ALP candidate in a marginal Queensland seat implies a lack of the most elementary political knowledge on the part of the electorate at large. Politicians who have traded on this supposition in the past have had their careers cut very short.

Secondly, the proposition assumes that, even if elected, Ms Kernot will be in a position to have a significant influence on national affairs. She has to be elected in the seat of Dickson, the ALP has to win government and she has to be appointed to the Ministry.

In the event of this happy conjunction, Ms Kernot still faces a degree of hostility and resentment from within the rank and file of her new Party before she can start to wield anywhere near the sort of political clout that has fallen into One Nation's lap. We know that the ALP is a very tribal, ‘blokey' organisation and an ALP powerbroker remarked recently, with reference to Ms Kernot: "If they rat on ya once, they'll rat on ya twice."

It is onto this sea of suspicion that Ms Kernot hopes to relaunch her political career and set sail for the Prime Minister's office. If the ALP remains true to form, she faces very heavy weather. The storm clouds tower even now.

As much as the two older parties hate the thought, over the past 20 years their legislation has been scrutinised by the Democrats in the Senate for justice, compassion and environmental values. Time after time, the two tired, old Parties have predicted the demise of the Democrats, often working in concert behind the scenes to bring try to bring that about. The Australian people, however, have thought and voted differently. Australians do not trust the two old Parties and the evidence for this is that the last Prime Minister to have a majority in both houses of the Parliament was Malcolm Fraser.

Neither the Coalition nor the ALP enjoy the generational, "rusted on" support they had in years gone by. There has been a massive cultural shift and today's political landscape is a brave new world. The political reality of this brave new world is that minor Parties and independents hold, and will continue to hold, the balance of power in the Senate. The recent Morgan Senate poll, the first such poll for over 18 months, tells us that - and also that the Democrat vote has been maintained. Clearly, Democrat supporters are holding fast and not deserting to One Nation. The same cannot be said for the Coalition's voters. It is also clear that disenchanted small ‘l' liberal voters are drifting away from the Coalition to the Democrats.

The fact of the matter is that the battle for the kind of Australia we take into the future will be fought in the Senate between the Australian Democrats and One Nation. In the coming election, the choice will be stark. The battle will be between tolerance and intolerance, balance and chaos, reconciliation and confrontation.

The battle will not be fought in the ‘no man's land' between the Federal seats of Dickson and Oxley. No amount of spin doctoring, media features and carefully placed articles will change that.

* John Schumann is senior media adviser to the Leader of the Australian Democrats, Senator Meg Lees.
