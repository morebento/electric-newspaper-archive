Tuesday May 5, 1998

A walk on the mild side - Why I took part in the May Day parade
by Jack Hoogland

Saturday's May Day rally was, amongst other things, a pleasant amble through the streets of the city on a beautiful, classically autumn day. It's not just why I went though.

Throughout the world, this is a day of celebration to workers, that is, people who work for a salary.

On this day, workers celebrate the achievements of their efforts, through their unions, in matters of work conditions, hours and pay. We take our four weeks annual leave for granted, as something as sure as Sunday. We naturally expect to work 40 hours, or thereabouts, in a week and our salaries are set in awards arising from negotiations entered into between employers and workers represented by their unions. Even if you're not a union member, your pay and conditions are nonetheless set by the same awards.

Even so, it's not just why I went. The MUA was out in force - people on megaphones - "MUA, here to stay" - even as I mentioned to a friend, "that may be, but it's not entirely why I'm here".

All of these reasons were perfectly good reasons to be out on the streets and in truth, they were partly why I was there. I was there because of the damage that was being done to the whole relationship between workers and employers. Workers throughout Australia were exposed to the possibility that the company or organisation with whom which we had entered into an employment contract could, at the stroke of a lawyer's pen, absolve themselves from any obligations that we thought they might have had, and sack us willy nilly, as they please, if they chose.

The legal chicanery carried out by Patrick Stevedores, were it successful, would have provided the template for every other employer to follow, even if they were opposed to the implications underlying that course. It would have been commercially necessary.

When a company, the government and the unrepresentative executive of the NFF appear to conspire with each other to sack 1400 workers simply because they belong to a union, then one feels compelled to express opposition. Howard's "no worker will be worse off" promise in the last election campaign was obviously "non core". For an elected government to engage in this anti- constituent behaviour is deplorable and insulting. Fortunately, we have such things as the separation of powers and consequently a relatively independent judicial system. When a judge of that system describes the government's behaviour as "ignoble", then it's time for that government to hang it's head in shame.

This war against the MUA was simply a first step in the systematic removal of workers' rights across the board, and the removal of corporations' responsibility for and obligations to the workforce. It has exposed some ugly attitudes held by some employers regarding their employees. The NSW president of the Chamber of Commerce made it clear where his sentiments lay regarding the workforce. How can a trusting, mutually respecting and harmonious industrial relationship exist when these attitudes prevail? How can good come of divisiveness? How can workers and employers trust each other?

I guess I was there not only to show my support for the right to organise, but to say that our work is more than just exchange of labour for money, it's often a large part of our lives; through it we develop trade and career skills, we develop personally and socially; through it we have a chance to contribute to our society, and these things transcend legal and idealogical constructs.

I was there in support of a workplace where the humans in it were recognised. I hope that the High Court decision yesterday marks the beginning of the return of commonsense and decency to the industrial landscape.
