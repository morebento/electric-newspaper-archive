Wednesday May 27, 1998

Hundreds gathered in Victoria Square today for National Sorry Day.

Speakers both Aboriginal and non-aboriginal, addressed the crowd.

Individuals were invited to record their signatures.

Not to apologise - to know
by Danial Dollard*

Sorry Day emanated from the Stolen Generation inquiry. One of two recommendations in the report was for a national sorry day.

Saying sorry would help people recognise the effect on the stolen children and the aboriginal communities.

Some political figures in this country have turned it into a political football game, and made the stolen generation and saying 'Sorry' a guilt thing. It was never meant to be this way.

I believe many people feel they should not say 'Sorry' to a race of people because of the way they perceive that race, or the prejudices they have toward them. Inside, they think they should not have to say 'Sorry' because, they think, some people benefitted by being stolen.

Saying sorry has nothing to do with guilt, but it is about acknowledging what happened to aboriginals so that we can move and come together as one people.

The nation can not more on until the stolen generation and the current state of aboriginals is understood by all Australians.

The leadership of this country and the Opposition have contributed to the misinformation and guilt. All the Government needed to do was come clean on the issue, but because of its largely anti-aboriginal, anti-minority groups agenda, it has not done so. The conservative Government has fueled the perception that aborigines get special treatment - this simply is not true. The Prime Minister has been to only one aboriginal community in the past two years, and that was very recently, and then only because of community division about Wik. And it happened because of the approaching, which is largely based on race.

Sorry day is about awareness - not guilt.

There is only one true history and it starts 40 000 years ago and we need to teach this to our children. The community needs to understand the true history and the policy making that has bought us to today's thinking. Only when people are informed can we debate these issues.

The children of our country are hungry for the truth they want to know about other cultures.

The older generation grew up in a culture of exclusiveness or white supremacy in the post war periods. A children are growing up in an era of inclusiveness and of a thirst for understanding of the truth and other cultures.

We are at a crossroads in the relationship between indigenous and non-indigenous people. We are living on the Asian rim with whom we want to increase our economic ties with, they will be viewing us on the way we deal with our indigenous Australians as a measure of how we treat other cultures.

We think we are so progressive in our thinking, but in 50 years time Australians will look back and think how primitive we were in our basic inability to acknowledge history and feel not guilt, but sorrow that it happened.

* Danial Dollard is Aboriginal aged care coordinator in Ceduna
