Tuesday February 17, 1998

Welcome, to the machine.
By John Rau

The Constitutional Convention sitting in Old Parliament House during the first two weeks of February 1998 is unfit to be compared with the Constitutional Conventions held in the 1890's.

The 1890's conventions, which achieved the fusion of six distinct British Colonies into the Commonwealth of Australia, tackled the enormous task of creating a functioning, democratic, politically stable nation, from scratch.

This task was infinitely more complex than the present narrow Republican debate.

The participants in the debates leading to the drafting of the Australian Constitution were the finest legal and political minds of their age. They were selected for their ability to do an important and complex job.

They were driven by the high ideal of building a nation. By their labours, they largely achieved the impossible act of Constitutional Alchemy.

Starting with the British prototype of a functioning ,evolutionary democracy, they blended elements of the American Federal model and novel solutions of their own to create a complex, subtle web of constitutional law, institutions and conventions. These have served us well for nearly a century.

The Australian Constitution may be likened to a complex machine. Each component has its function. Each component relates to other components in a particular way. No component can be removed or changed without disturbing the function of the whole.

Some components are so vital that their removal or modification can render the whole machine useless. These vital components may not, at first glance, appear to be significant. What they seem to be in isolation may not reflect how they operate in a dynamic setting.

The new Constitutional Convention consists of a group of people, mostly chosen like Noah's beasts, on the basis of what they are, or where they come from. They reflect the current western democratic fad of selecting samples from arbitrarily created subsets of the populous, locking them together in a building and expecting wisdom to emerge.

Never mind that these subsets are not themselves representative in any sense. Never mind that the selection process generally throws up the loudest, or the pushiest, rather than the best. Never mind that this process generally produces leadership of the uninformed, by the ill informed.

From the Government's point of view, this convention is a stunt. It is an abdication of political leadership. No wonder the result has been to let a thousand flowers bloom. Flatulent egos the size of zeppelins compete for top billing on television news services. It is difficult to imagine a process less likely to produce an intelligent or helpful result.

The proof of the foolishness of this whole process, if any were needed, lies in the central debate about an elected Presidency. The difference between a popularly elected President and the present system is vast. It is the constitutional equivalent of taking a sledge hammer to the current machinery.

Whether the machinery would continue to work afterwards is an open question. What is beyond debate is that it would be vastly different. Put simply, a popularly elected President could properly claim to have a 'mandate' directly from the people. Presumably, the President would act accordingly.

It may be that there are good reasons to consider such a major overhaul of the machinery of Government. It may be that a major overhaul would produce real benefits for our democracy which is so obviously in crisis.

The problem is that this convention is not equipped to oil the current machinery, let alone to redesign and reconstruct it.
