Friday July 3, 1998

In his dreams
By Sandra Kanck, Australian Democrat MLC

What a farce - the State Government's American privatisation advisers hauled into the spotlight to argue the merits of the case for the sale of ETSA and Optima.

Gee, don't these guys stand to pocket a cool sum should the sale go ahead? Oh, wouldn't that colour their opinion? Isn't it scary listening to a rich Americans tell you they know what's best for you?

To add insult to injury we had the spectacle of the hired guns waving a wad of notes under the noses of the gathered journalists. I guess that represents the money they hope to ship out of the country.

I wonder if those other highly paid consultants, the odd couple, Geoff Anderson and Alex Kennedy had a hand in this little piece of nonsense. Bad call.

When I announced that the Democrats wouldn't be supporting the sale of our electricity utilities I was warned to anticipate a campaign of hysteria and deception. The warning was prescient.

The editorial of the Advertiser kicked things off the next day. A combination of scorn and abuse was deemed to be a substitute for argument.

The Premier chipped in on morning radio. He told the listeners that our electricity utilities would be swamped in the National Electricity Market. To prove the point he said that the majority of our electricity is generated interstate (!) and hence Optima would never be able to compete.

This is simply wrong but the Premier obviously has no intention of letting the facts get in the way of a good scare campaign.

The problem for the Government is that the scare campaign isn't working. My office has been inundated with expressions of support. ‘Don't let them bully you' has been an oft-heard sentiment.

By Monday afternoon the Government had produced a counter argument to my report on the proposed privatisation of ETSA and Optima. It is as unconvincing as all the arguments it has so far mounted to support the case for sale.

At the same time the Premier issued a list of 15 rhetorical questions and demanded they be answered ‘immediately'. The Premier seems to be deluded into imagining he can snap his fingers and others will jump.

The Government's misinformation campaign continued with promises that country consumers would pay no more than 1.7% above the city price for electricity. The Premier failed to indicate there would be a time limit on this. News leaked out that this was in fact a transitional arrangement and that within five to ten years country consumers would be left to absorb the full costs of transmitting electricity to the door.

Then on Wednesday the Treasurer misquoted an article I had previously contributed to this very journal. Not satisfied with attempting to mislead the Parliament about what I had actually said he then repeated the stunt in a media release. It was an extraordinarily limp performance from one of the Government's better operators.

All of this is indicative of how desperate the Government is about the proposed privatisation of ETSA and Optima. How did they ever get themselves into such a mess?

I have a theory.

Having suffered inexcusable losses at the last state election the Premier desperately needed to make a stand to reinforce his shaky hold on the Liberal leadership. Selling the power utilities was the obvious choice.

The Premier is clearly a true believer in economic rationalism. Furthermore the injection of funds would allow the Government to point to a substantial reduction in State debt and provide a war chest for the next election.

The major problem for the Government was that it had categorically ruled out privatising ETSA and Optima during the recent State election. Given that the Democrats and Labor also opposed privatisation the Government faced the real possibility of being defeated on the floor of the Parliament.

No problems, the Government had an escape hatch; a long term lease on the utilities. That would circumvent the need for Parliamentary approval and allow the Premier to look like the strong leader of his dreams.

Alas, like Liberal voters at the last election the Government was mislead. Later it emerged that the lease option would result in approximately a 20% discount on the expected sale price. I doubt whether even John Olsen would be desperate enough to sell South Australia that short.

After our investigation exposed the Government's shoddy reckoning on the reasons for and benefits of sale the Government, caught in its own trap, turned nasty.
