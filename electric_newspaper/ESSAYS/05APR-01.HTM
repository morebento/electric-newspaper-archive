Sunday April 5, 1998

A.B.C., tumble down-D. And E-ducation, too.
by Nicole de Bree, in York, U.K.

Last week, Australian university students took to the streets of all your capital cities to protest against your seemingly fierce cuts to education.

You're not alone. All over western Europe, the debate about how to run a school system and a university system, and how to pay for it, is raging simultaneously with Australia. Perhaps there are lessons for both of us here.

Your readers might remember (if not, it's still in your paper in the All Viewpoints section) E.H. de Bree's article about tertiary education in the Netherlands - or Western Europe for that matter. As a student at a British university, I am becoming aware of the universality of limitations of university education.

The relevance to Australian readers is twofold. Firstly they can have a good laugh about the mess those colonising (and often patronising) bastards in Western Europe are in. Secondly, they can learn a little something from those mistakes, and perhaps benefit.

One of the problems the West faces is costs. Every country in Western European has and is continuing to drastically cut education spending, even though in Germany for example (the country with the largest student population on a per capita basis) all a student has to pay is an average yearly administration fee of DM 65 (approx. As$ 80). All over Western Europe (if not beyond) people are becoming aware of the need of changes in the education system, but their approaches and attitudes towards it vary.

In the Netherlands, the educational system is compared to a dilapidated bus that ought not to be on the road anymore. In Britain, however, the system is considered traditional and for that sole reason worthy of maintenance, regardless of costs.

In Britain, a tuition fee of up to £1000 per year of university education will be introduced from next year. A major difference between The Netherlands and Britain seems to be, however, that students will only have to pay these fees AFTER they have graduated, and not even 10% of the students will have to pay the full £1000, as it is a means-tested fee.

What this comes down to in reality is that those who will be paying the full fee are those people who gladly pay £2000 a TERM for their children's secondary education at public schools.

Public protests concerning the introduction of these fees are largely unfounded. It is not true that university education will only be possible for the rich, because there are other selection criteria that decide whether or not students will be accepted at university. This is not the case in (Germany and) the Netherlands, where student numbers are ridiculously high, and where selection on the basis of income COULD be a much more realistic threat.

The British also fail to recognise that students cannot receive their education for free, as another reader's response to Ms de Bree's article rightfully pointed out. Facilities for British university students are much better than those of, for example, Dutch students, who have been paying for their education for quite some time.

Another question facing universities, their students, and governments is to define a university's position in the world - is it purely a research institute or does it need to be linked to the job market? At present, universities are trying to be both, arguing that the need for large numbers of students (i.e. more funding!) and sponsorship from industry interferes with the 'strictly research' climate.

What this means, though, is that humanities and arts studies, the traditional university disciplines, receive the smallest amount of money available, as they are the areas of study that are considered least relevant to modern society. It therefore becomes very unattractive to study human sciences, and to focus instead on studies that will get you rich - or maybe will just get you a job.

I don't think there is any reason to presume that the situation is any different in Australia. Needless to say that as an Arts students (without blinkers!) I believe that this is a dangerous development, as they concentrate on people and their position IN the modern world.

But now, at last, I arrive at the core of my epistle. The point I am making is that if Australia looks to Western Europe to redefine its tertiary education structure, it should try to prevent making mistakes that have been made over and over again. It should not be afraid of changing its structure radically, should that be necessary.

The Australian Government should not be afraid of imposing quality restrictions on the students they let in, because student numbers will then be checked in a fair way. If student numbers are reasonable, that means that the amount of money necessary for their education and the maintenance of universities will be kept in check, and general costs for the students will be lower as well.

It could also mean that an even distribution of money along the several disciplines would be possible again, and with that a reasonable attitude towards the humanities. The government could provide and PROMOTE other forms of education for those students who simply want practical training.

In order to pay for all of this one-time, big but worthwhile investment, the Australian Government would not have to be afraid of imposing means-tested tuition fees that do take into account the 'raw material' aspect of students, as your another reader pointed out. It could allow many visiting students to study at its universities. The scientific disciplines could sell their research findings if they are useful for industrial purposes.

In short, I am projecting all my frustrations about failures and mistakes of western European tertiary education in the hope that Australia, as a relatively young nation, may not be as stuck in its ways as we are. Although it has probably already constructed its university 'bus', it might not be too late to change some of its essential parts BEFORE embarking on a long journey.
