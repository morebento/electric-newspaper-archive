Friday May 22, 1998

A message in a bottle
By Sandra Kanck, Australian Democrat MLC

I don't know if I've ever met an anonymous sperm donor. It's not something blokes crow about. I'm told the financial inducement is the prime motivation for donors. Poor students and the like looking for some supplementary income. I also suspect the biological imperative to reproduce also has an influence, at least at a sub-conscious level.

Until recently I've not considered the implications of being the progeny of a sperm donor. A meeting with Louise and Lea from the Donor Support Group (DSG) changed that.

Louise and Lea approached me to air their concerns about the lack of rights of those conceived from the sperm of a donor. They informed me that some of the offspring of anonymous sperm donors would like to track down their genetic dads. This is a difficult task to accomplish as there is no central register and no legal requirement that reproductive clinics provide details of who the donor was.

Louise's son, who was conceived by donor sperm, wasn't to be deterred by the difficulties placed in his path. He eventually managed to get a message to his genetic father that he would like to meet him. No was the answer but he was told that longevity is a characteristic of the family line.

Why should these people have a right to know who was the male provider in their creation? Plenty of other people don't know. Indeed some who could feel relatively confident are certainly labouring under misconceptions. A 70's Chicago study of paternity within marriage discovered that 18% of children born in wedlock were fathered by someone other than the husband. I'm sure few of those offspring ever find out who their father is.

Isn't the absence of certainty about paternity simply a fact of life. Indeed have not desperate attempts by men to guarantee they are the fathers of the children in line for the inheritance led to enormous injustice and suffering. I think of female circumcision as but one dreadful example.

Shouldn't we just say it's better not to be too concerned about who the genetic father is and concentrate on encouraging whoever does the parenting to do a good job?

No. I think the imperfect world argument is a poor excuse for leaving things the way they are.

Louise and Lea produced some extremely persuasive arguments for setting up a central data base matching donors with conception and enabling the offspring to access that information.

Louise's son discovered his donor father came from a family blessed with longevity. What if they were cursed with poor tickers? Shouldn't he have that information available to enable him to make the right lifestyle decisions? Cutting fat and salt from the diet and taking more exercise makes a great deal of sense if you know your genetic father died from a heart attack at 50.

The possibility of incestuous relationships amongst the male progeny of a donor is slim but real all the same. Indeed given resemblance to a parent is a powerful attraction for many people the likelihood of inadvertent incest is genuine.

The situation is comparable to that surrounding adoption until recent changes made it possible for the birthmother to be tracked by an adopted child, should the mother so choose. I think a similar system is appropriate for sperm donors.

At the very least a circumscribed biography and a health profile should be available to the offspring. Although this will scare some donors away and will require an increase in the fee paid to donors for their services, I think that is more than appropriate.
