Tuesday June 23, 1998

Pauline Hanson's Alien Nation
by John Spoehr *

The rise of Pauline Hanson and One Nation should be no surprise to critics of economic rationalism and long-term observers of the idiosyncrasies of Queensland politics.

Pauline Hanson and her advisers have tapped into a deep wellspring of dissatisfaction and desire for change. One Nation is largely born of alienation from the major political parties and frustration at the effects of corporate and economic rationalism.

In Queensland, this frustration has been compounded by the experience of widespread drought over many years and some of the highest rates of regional unemployment in the country.

In Liberal and National Party heartland where other political alternatives are not readily available a lurch to the 'right' could be expected. Equally Labor Party strongholds were not immune from inroads by One Nation with the memories of high interest rates and the recession we had to have still stuck in many minds.

The magnitude of the lurch to the right has been fuelled by One Nation's racist and xenophobic propaganda, which is reminiscent of the worst kinds of nationalistic political opportunism. Indeed it is not difficult to draw some parallels between the rise of Hanson and that of Adolf Hitler. Pauline Hanson's criticism of assistance for Aboriginal people and her tirades against 'multiculturalism' are the sort of racist and nationalistic politics which underpinned Nazism in the 1940's and which more recently have fuelled the persecution of Chinese Indonesians.

The equivocal response from the Howard Government and the Liberal and National Parties to the rise of Pauline Hanson has at best fuelled support for One Nation and at worst legitimated its role in national politics.

The Prime Minister's reference to Pauline Hanson as 'deranged' was politically clumsy and very likely to have produced the opposite effect to that intended. He will find this and his other actions in response to One Nation very difficult to defend.

The problem giving rise to One Nation, according to the Prime Minister, is the need for better communication about its policies. The response of voters in Queensland suggests otherwise. It suggests that many of the policies associated with economic rationalism are the problem. These are problems originating in the economic liberalism of the Keating Labor Government and compounded and amplified by the radical free market agenda of the Howard Government.

A combination of persistent drought, the highest rates of regional unemployment in the country and the rationalisation and withdrawal of regional services have rightly forced many Queenslanders to look for political alternatives.

The challenge for liberals, social democrats and socialists alike is to break out of the straight jacket of economic rationalism and begin the process of developing coherent policy responses which accept as most pragmatists acknowledge, that there is no such thing as a level playing field in the global economy. The response to this should not be unbridled nationalism leading to insulation of Australia from the international community, but cautious pragmatism which recognises that democracy and the economy are inextricably intertwined. Naive pursuit of level playing fields in a predatory global economy has local effects which voters are increasingly beginning to identify and understand.

What is less understood by many voters and One Nation are the alternatives available.

The struggle for political legitimacy in Australia is well and truly on. For those of us looking for a social democratic alternative the Thatcherite dictum 'There is no alternative' reminds us of another emperor suggesting that 'While Nero fiddles, Rome burns'. Behind the flames the economic rationalist Emperor like One Nation has no clothes. On the other hand the leadership strategy of One Nation's national director, David Oldfield, may have been more inspired by Peter Sellers portrayal of the political rise of Chauncy Gardner in 'Being There', than Roman history.

History will no doubt reveal all.

* John Spoehr is deputy director of the Centre for Labour Research at the University of Adelaide.
