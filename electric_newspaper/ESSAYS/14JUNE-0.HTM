Monday June 14, 1998

Making history
by Legh Davis, Liberal Member in the Legislative Council

In 1988 Australia celebrated the bicentenary of European settlement. However, in that year it was revealed that in South Australia only 25% of junior school students would study Australian history.

At a recent State history conference the vice-president of the History Teachers’ Association of Australia, Peter Price, made some timely observations about the state of history in schools. He said, "It is sad to say that history is far from thriving in our secondary schools, and it is a serious issue as to how we can keep it alive and significant as part of school education." Mr Price said that New South Wales was now the only State where history teaching is mandatory for all students in the junior secondary years. Mr Price suggested, "There are many good reasons for the changes to curriculum that have been introduced this decade - schools are under pressure to both teach the basics, literacy and numeracy, more determinedly than ever, and at the same time to ensure that modern students have drug education, consumer education, health and sex education, computer education and so on."

As we approach the centenary of Federation in 2001 it is appropriate to reflect on the importance of history.

Currently the Howard Government is supporting a major curriculum development in schools for the teaching of civics and citizenship with an emphasis on using history as a vehicle for knowledge and understanding.

The revered Australian author, David Malouf, once noted, "when I was growing up, history was what happened somewhere else".

Australians owe a great debt to Manning Clark and Geoffrey Blainey, who were largely responsible for making Australians more aware of the rich history of this country. Even though they were of different political persuasions, they had a great respect for each other and were united in their passion for history.

But many Australians remain strangely ignorant of recent history. For example, how many would know that in 1850 the South Australian copper town of Burra, with 5,000 residents, was the seventh largest population centre in Australia? It had a larger population that Brisbane or Perth and ranked as the largest inland city in the country.

How many know that General Douglas MacArthur on his retreat from the Philippines during World War II made his famous declaration "I shall return" at the Terowie railway station in the mid north of South Australia?

If Australian National Railways and the community at large had a better appreciation of history, the Terowie railway station would now be a popular shrine for American and Australian tourists. No prizes for guessing what happened to the railway station!

The centenary of Federation gives us an opportunity to put history back on the map. Each State and the Federal Government has established a Centenary of Federation Committee to plan and manage either State or Federal based projects and events. The Federal Government is allocating $1 billion to a Federation fund ... $900 million is being allocated to major infrastructure works such as the Alice Springs to Darwin rail link.

Around $30 million has been earmarked for distribution by Federal Members of Parliament for community projects in their electorates. This means that each electorate will receive $200,000.

The National Council for the Centenary of Federation will manage a $35 million fund from the Federal Government of which $10 million will be allocated to a History and Education programme.

The South Australian Government is also committing funds to mark the centenary. It is to be hoped that some of these funds will be used for historical research and writing appropriate histories relating to South Australia.

As Cicero observed over 2,000 years ago, "to be ignorant of what occurred before you were born is to remain always a child".
