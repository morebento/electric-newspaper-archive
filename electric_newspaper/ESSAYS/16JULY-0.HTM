Thursday July 16, 1998

Getting things in perspective: road testing the Porsche 964 - C4
by Nick Beaton *

To the uninitiated or passing observer, it is the 911. To the cogniscenti it is the C4. In German it sounds harshly like "Tsay-fear". In soothing French it is the "Say-quatre". In the owners manual it is the 911 964 - C4. Prototyped as the legendary 959, proven in the Paris - Dakar and universally held in suspiscion because of its permanent four wheel drive...

Its strange, but somehow it feels very small, yet heavy, solid, weighty. Yes its small, smaller than I remember.

All the mechanical movements have a heavy action about them. Turn the key in the door and the lock offers a slow, well oiled, sliding resistance. The door lock buttons with their winking LEDs lift with a heavy thud.

The ignition key lights up a 747 array of lights on the dashboard. Differential lock check, seat belt warning, ABS check, fan belt warning, brake wear warning, rear spoiler interlock, 4-wheel drive check. The engine rumbles, the lights go out. Only the spoiler interlock stays lit. The clutch is heavy with a long travel. In fact it's very heavy, but you can still feel the spring assist. The gear change is short, heavy and precise. The brakes are just a short push to a rock wall.

At 7 kmh the spoiler light goes out - speed sensor functioning. At 40 kmh the 4 wheel drive interlock winks on and off - the system changes to automatic load monitoring. It will now only light if the 69-31 torque distribution is altered. Useless information, really. If that's happening under hard driving you have a much more to do than ponder the dash lights. Much, much more.

At 80 kmh the spoiler lifts and extends - if you are looking in the rear mirror it comes into view at the bottom of the rear window. If the engine is hot, the road clear and your nerves strong, 100 kmh comes up in 5.2 seconds. If you've got the energy for the whole process, 160 kmh comes in another 8 seconds. The most gratifying point is the noise when you change gears, and throttle off for the change - the overrun is a deep throated, animal bellow. But then 3.6 litres is big, and size matters.

Two German governments have fallen trying to put speed limits on motorways. Freie Fahrt fuer einen freien Buerger - "a free drive for a free citizen" was the catch-cry when they tried the first time in the early seventies.

In Germany the main manufacturers have an agreement to limit their engines to run at no more than 250 kmh. 155 miles per hour. Porsche do not do this. A fox doesn't run with the hounds and pretend to be a sheep. On the A5 between Frankfurt and Darmstadt there is a long straight stretch that turns into 4 lanes and crosses a valley and rises up the other side after 6 or 7 kilometres. It is here the faithful go to drive the free drive.

At one in the morning on a dry Winter's night the traffic is thin. At 265 kmh there is still more throttle left, and the beast is willing, but I cannot handle it any more. It is just too much input. Sensory overload. The rush and the noise is exhilarating, but the cars and trucks 3 lanes over are still too close, getting too big to fast.

I have proven my point. I lose my nerve. I am too tired. Its been a long day of meetings and presentations. Throttling back to a more comfortable speed, I realise I have been holding my breath for the last few minutes. I breath in and out counting the seconds, relax, do some finger exercises on the wheel, skip the CD forward. That feels much better. I check the oil temperature and pressure gauges. And since I am there and have the time, I check the speedo. Two hundred and twenty. I'm relaxing at 220 kmh.

If you want to get things out of perspective, this is the car to do it in.

* Nick Beaton is a motoring writer with a fascination for the unique. His colums will appear occasionally in The Electric Newspaper.
