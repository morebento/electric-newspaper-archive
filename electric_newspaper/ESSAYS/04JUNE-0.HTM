Thursday June 4, 1998

Tax cart rumbles again
by John Rau *

In the mid-1980's, Treasurer Paul Keating coined the expression "Tax cart" to describe his proposals for tax reform. Commentators picked up this imagery and ran with it. Specific proposals were "on the cart", or "off the cart". At the climax of the Tax Reform Summit, "the wheels fell off the cart" altogether. Keating's preferred position, "Option C", was dumped.

The key element of "Option C", was a broadly based goods and services tax. In other words, a GST. The Liberals opposed "Option C" for reasons of political expediency. Business also opposed a GST, as did the welfare lobby and the ACTU. It was the GST that bogged the Tax Cart's wheels and eventually, tore them off.

In 1992, Dr Hewson put a new Tax Cart on the road. This time Paul Keating opposed the GST. The one clear thing about the 1993 General Election, is that it was a referendum on the GST. It was overwhelmingly rejected. More overwhelming, when one considers how hated Paul Keating was.

Dr Hewson was destroyed. John Howard eventually rose from the Liberal ashes. To put a definite end to the lingering ghost of 1993, Mr Howard promised that the GST was dead, for good. So much for that promise. Within 18 months of being elected, Mr Howard and Mr Costello were busy talking up "Tax Reform" and putting the wheels back on the GST Cart.

Isn't it funny that this GST proposal keeps coming back like a bad penny.

Governments like the GST because it promises untold revenue to the Treasury. The money comes from sources that won't disturb the filthy rich who now pay little or no tax. These rich people are hard to take on. They fight back. They employ smart lawyers and accountants. They seem able to influence Public Opinion. A lazy gutless Government finds it much easier to tax rent money paid by pensioners. Much easier to tax food and school fees. Much easier to turn every small business in Australia into an unpaid outpost of the Australian Tax Office.

Governments also like the GST because once it is introduced, it can be increased, little by little. It is the ultimate weapon of lazy Government. Its impact is greatest on the ordinary citizen, the little people who lack the information and resources to fight back. Meanwhile, the spotlight comes off the big dodgers of income and corporate tax. If we vote for a GST, we are admitting that it is just too hard to tax the rich and the powerful. We will be inviting them to please themselves even more. Only a fool would believe that any Government would chase these big fish when it has the easy option of taxing defenceless small fry instead.

The Tax Department loves the idea too. It amounts to the "contracting out" of tax collection to over one million small traders. The best bit is that these people won't be paid for their endless hours filling in endless forms. They will just face criminal prosecution if they fail to collect the tax. This virtually amounts to civil conscription of small business.

What Government would you trust with a new tax that they can increase at will? You would have to be a moron to believe Mr Howard's promise that the rate of tax will "never "go up. Even if he keeps his word, (which recent history shows is unlikely), he won't be Prime Minister in five years.

This GST proposal is a con-job. The easy money from a GST would come from pensioners, families and the least well off. They would pay new taxes on essentials. Meanwhile, the lazy rich would be let off the hook. A GST is not good policy, it is a substitute for it. A GST would submerge small business in paperwork and expose it to new criminal penalties.

The distant rumble of the GST Cart is growing louder. It will be given a new name, but it remains what it has always been. Bad policy for ordinary Australians. All of us have a part to play in making sure the wheels fall off GST Cart this time too.

* John Rau is an Adelaide barrister and commentator on social, legal, and economic affairs. His column appears every week in The Electric Newspaper.
