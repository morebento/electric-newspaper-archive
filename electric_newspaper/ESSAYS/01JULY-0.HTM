Wednesday July 1, 1998

On the right track
by Neil Andrew *

South Australians should welcome the announcement of a feasibility study for the proposed Melbourne to Darwin rail link.

This is despite the negative responses which followed the Prime Minister's endorsement of an investigation into the potential of an eastern rail link, and the perception that the eastern track would detract from the proposed Alice Springs to Darwin rail link.

Let's face the facts.

First of all, the Commonwealth Government has committed $100 million to the Alice Springs to Darwin proposal and not a single dollar into any alternative.

John Howard is the first Prime Minister to commit funds to this railway; Bob Hawke said in 1983 that only a Labor Government could be trusted to build such a rail line, but in 13 years of Labor Government no funds were ever committed.

The Commonwealth contribution of $100 million has been matched by similar amounts from both the South Australian and Northern Territory Governments, and the Premier of South Australia, Mr John Olsen, is confident that private funds can be found to underwrite this historic scheme.

All that the Prime Minister has done is to welcome the news of a feasibility study into a major project of national significance.

I contend that the proposal for an eastern rail link to Darwin could well enhance the viability of the Alice Springs to Darwin line.

The eastern states link will join the Alice Springs to Darwin line at Tennant Creek, thus putting additional freight loads onto the Tennant Creek to Darwin section of the line.

Melbourne to Darwin operators would have to pay to use the line.

Engineers have suggested that the most expensive part of the rail link through the centre of Australia is the Tennant Creek to Darwin section, primarily because of the number of river crossings.

I believe that getting a major investor to increase the freight load on the northern half of the Alice Springs to Darwin line is a real plus for South Australia.

Many detractors have said that the Melbourne-Darwin proposal will take rail traffic off the planned Alice Springs to Darwin line.

The fact is that the eastern link will pick up freight from Sydney, but most freight from Melbourne may well take a more direct route through South Australia, rather than the relatively mountainous eastern states route.

All that happened last week was the announcement of a feasibility study into a major piece of transport infrastructure initiated by private investors.

The success of the eastern link depends on a viable Alice Springs to Darwin link, and all South Australians should welcome the reality that their line will benefit from the additional usage generated by the eastern rail link, if the feasibility study vindicates it.

* Neil Andrew is the Liberal Member for the Federal SA seat of Wakefield.
