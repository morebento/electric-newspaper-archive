Friday May 29, 1998

The State Budget - an exercise in reality
by Legh Davis, Liberal Member in the Legislative Council

The Treasurer of South Australia, Rob Lucas, made history yesterday by becoming the first Legislative Councillor to deliver a State Budget.

But, in his speech Lucas had to deal with some unpalatable history. He noted that when the Liberal Party came to Government in December 1993, it "faced the daunting prospect of a budget in which we were spending over $300 million a year more than we were earning, a State mortgage out of control and ballooning towards $9 million and unfunded superannuation liabilities estimated at $4 billion and growing alarmingly."

The Treasurer noted that over the past four years the debt has been reduced by nearly $2 billion. This means that debt is now less than 20% of Gross State Product, compared with 28% in 1992. In addition, the State's superannuation liability has been reduced by close to $900 million.

The Treasurer outlined a four-year financial plan, which commits the Government to balanced budgets, a continuing reduction in debt and the elimination of all unfunded superannuation liabilities by 2024. However, the key to implementing this programme is the privatisation of ETSA and Optima.

The Lucas message was blunt. If the sale of ETSA and Optima was blocked in Parliament, the Government would be forced to introduce a mini-budget in October to provide for up to $150 million in tax increases or expenditure cuts, to take effect within the four-year financial period.

This is financial reality in South Australia in 1998.

The Labor Party, which vehemently opposes the privatisation of ETSA and Optima, does not have a crooked leg to stand on. The Hawke/Keating Governments privatised the Commonwealth Bank, Australian Airlines, Qantas and Commonwealth Serum Laboratories and were in favour of at least a partial privatisation of Telstra. The State Government, in which current Labor Leader, Mike (Also) Rann was a key player, privatised the State Bank and sold the the Government's dominant shareholding in the South Australian Gas Company.

In my Electric Newspaper article of May 8 ("Take Your Money and Rann") I offered a free lunch and tour of Parliament House to any reader who could provide me with a speech of Rann criticising the Federal and State Labor Governments' privatisation initiatives. My e-mail "In"mailbox on this subject has been, not surprisingly, empty and I have placed the lunch in the deep freeze!

The reality is that if the ETSA/Optima sale does not proceed, South Australia will continue to have a debt of over $7 billion which requires interest payments of $2 million a day and no AAA credit rating. If the sale does not proceed, South Australia will be forced to compete against other electricity authorities in the Eastern States, which are already or likely to be privatised. The State will remain vulnerable to increased interest rates and other adverse economic trends. If the sale does not proceed and the debt remains, you do not have to be a financial whiz to realise there will be less money available to be spent on health care, education, the environment and other important community services.

A key component of the budget is a $99 million commitment to provide traineeships for more than 4,600 people, including 1,500 in small business. There has also been a strong recognition of the environment, with spending on State parks up to $22 million - $5 million more than 2 years ago and a doubling in new State funding for the Natural Heritage Trust to $6.8 million.

Significant wage increases in the public sector have flowed through to a 4.5% increase in fees and charges in the 1998/99 budget. However, Lucas noted that "State taxation per capita in South Australia will be $130 lower than the national average and the third lowest of all States and Territories." There will be a differential tax rate on gaming machines for clubs and hotels. Clubs and community hotels will benefit from a drop in the tax rate because the Government recognises they return a significant proportion of their revenue to the community.

There is a pleasing increase of 8% in real terms in capital works expenditure of $1.24 billion. This includes $43 million for the redevelopment of the Queen Elizabeth Hospital, a $55 million upgrade of the Adelaide Convention Centre, $100 million for the Alice Springs-Darwin rail link and a refurbishment of the State Library.

This is a responsible budget which reflects financial reality. Ironically, its critics include the very same people who were responsible for recklessly wrecking the State's finances. It will be interesting to see how they now confront reality.
