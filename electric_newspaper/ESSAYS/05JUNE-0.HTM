Friday June 5, 1998

Two Bob on the Nose
By Sandra Kanck, Australian Democrat MLC

Each Friday I engage in an activity of which my father would deeply disapprove. I squander $2 on the Parliament House football tipping competition. Actually given two payouts already I retract the word squander.

I mention both my father’s strict prohibitionist beliefs and my own modest flutter to lay my cards on the table. Whilst I enjoy the football tipping competition I find most forms of gambling dull as dishwater.

Sitting in front of a blinking poker machine repeatedly hitting a button in the hope of striking the jackpot is my idea of purgatory. Casinos, the track, poker games and bingo tickets have about the same appeal.

Whilst these activities leave me cold I have a relaxed attitude towards those that choose to spend their time and money seeking the thrill of a win. Each to their own is a reasonably good motto provided that others are not hurt in the pursuit of pleasure.

This leaves me out of step with prevailing hype against gambling. Poker machines in particular are now generally portrayed as a pestilence upon our society. Those who play the pokies are seen as helpless victims and those who profit from them as ruthless exploiters.

Pokies have been blamed for everything from the closures of retail stores, second hand car dealers, restaurants, even the Advertiser’s declining circulation. It will be the dog dying at the end of the road next.

The hysteria has got out of hand and it’s time for a little perspective. In an attempt to provide a better picture of gambling in all its manifestations the Social Development Committee has been conducting an investigation into the gaming industry in South Australia.

Committee rules prevent me from discussing evidence that has been presented only to the committee, but there is plenty of information on the public record about gambling in South Australia.

South Australians spend an estimated $1 million a day on gaming. That’s roughly 75 cents a day for each man woman and child or an average of $66 per week for each South Australian household.

When my $2 per week wager is lined up against these figures a discrepancy is immediately apparent. I’m not wagering my share; out of football season I’m not contributing at all. Small children, prohibitionists and non-gamblers don’t pull their weight either.

Hence some South Australians are gambling more than the per capita average of 75 cents a day. Indeed some are gambling enormous amounts of money each day. But how many South Australians fall into this category?

A Harvard Medical School study has found that only 1.29% of the adult population in the United States and Canada has "serious pathological problems incident to gambling". The study further concludes that "the majority of Americans and Canadians gamble with little or no adverse consequence".

The figures for South Australia are comparable. Hence a relatively small number of South Australians are caught in the madness of compulsive gambling. Well short of the plague proportions that have been suggested.

This is not to suggest that Government is exonerated from acting to reduce the damage being inflicted upon compulsive gamblers and those around them. The Government has become increasingly reliant upon revenues from gambling.

The 1998/99 State Budget estimates that the State Government will pocket $329.3 million in gambling taxes. This is projected to grow to $352.2 million by 2001/02. Poker machines make up the lion's share of this tax grab.

The time has come to wean the Government from its growing dependency on gambling revenues. One means would be to increase the percentage returned on each dollar gambled.

At present on average 88 cents of each dollar wagered is returned to the punter. By increasing that amount and consequently reducing returns to the Government and poker machine operators three birds are killed with one stone.

At the end of the day the punters would have more money in their pockets, the Government be forced to find more appropriate source of revenue and the operators could shake the tag of blood suckers.

I shall revisit this subject when the Social Development Committee has concluded its report. As for the secret to success in the footy tipping competition - always compare the rucks!
