Friday July 24, 1998

And in the losers corner, South Australia
by Ralph Clarke, Labor member for Ross-Smith

Industry Trade and Tourism Minister and former Deputy Premier, Graham Ingerson, survived a vote of no confidence in him by the House of Assembly on Wednesday the 22nd July 1998. He survived with the narrowest of margins, with a tied vote of 22 votes all, the issue was decided on the casting vote of the Speaker, Liberal Party Stalwart, and ex Minister under Dean Brown, John Oswald M.P.

What makes Mr Ingerson's survival all the more curious is that he survived on the vote of so called Independent Liberal M.P. for Gordon, (based around the City of Mount Gambier in the south east of the State) Rory McEwen. He was not only one of the five person Privileges Committee set up by the House of Assembly to investigate as to whether or not Graham Ingerson had misled the Parliament on the issues surrounding the dismissal of the former Chief Executive Officer of the South Australian Thoroughbred Racing Association Merv Hill in 1997, but he was also one of the majority members of the Privileges Committee who found that Mr Ingerson had deliberately misled the Parliament.

Rory McEwen is prone to jumping to his feet in Parliament and beating his breast, calling on all Members, and in particular the major political parties to "lift their game" and to "lift their standards of behaviour and ethics in the Parliament". In the debate on the Privileges Committee's report on Tuesday the 21st July 1998

Rory first indicated that he was about to do a backflip on Graham Ingerson when he complained about the culture of the Parliament where Ministers past and present, of all political colours had, in his view, been sloppy with the truth in their answers to questions in Parliament, and that they had allegedly suffered no penalty. Therefore he reasoned that Graham Ingerson should be judged by the culture of the Parliament of the day, rather than on the principles that had been adhered to in the past by the Mother of Parliamentary democracy, the British House of Commons, where if a Minister intentionally misleads the House, they resign from the Ministry or are dismissed by the Prime Minister or Premier.

What Rory McEwen conveniently overlooked was that if the standards expected of Members of Parliament, and in particular Ministers of the Crown, is as low as he states (in many respects I could agree with that statement), then that in itself is no excuse to accept those lower standards. Rory McEwen should have drawn a line in the sand. In a case where a Minister has been not only found guilty by all members of the Privileges Committee for misleading the House, but in a majority decision found that he had deliberately misled the House, and that that report is adopted unanimously by the House of Assembly, then there is only one penalty sufficient to restore the sorts of standards and ethics of Parliamentary behaviour that Rory McEwen so often says should apply, namely Graham Ingerson's resignation from the Ministry or his dismissal from Office by the Premier. If the

Premier refuses to act, as he has consistently refused to do so in the Ingerson affair, then the House of Assembly should pass a vote of no confidence in that Minister.

What Rory McEwen did by not voting for the motion of no confidence in Graham Ingerson, was to say to all future Ministers of whatever political persuasion, that it is acceptable to not only mislead the Parliament in answers that they give, but it is acceptable for Ministers of the Crown, not withstanding their oath that they swear when they are made Ministers, to deliberately mislead the Parliament. Try and explain the morality of that situation to any primary school child.

From a purely party political position the Labor Opposition is far better off in having Graham Ingerson where he is, on the front bench, mortally wounded, strung across a barbed wire fence, with the Liberal Party in the State bleeding. The credibility of the Olsen Liberal Government and his Ministers has been much diminished by the Ingerson affair, in particular the lack of leadership shown by the Premier in this matter. He has allowed his own personal considerations of retaining the leadership of the Liberal Party in the short term, to override the moral imperative of dealing with Ingerson in a straight forward way by sacking him.

To be frank, if I had my choice between having the Labor Party in its current position, where politically it is better for Ingerson to remain where he is, slowly bleeding the Liberal Party to death, or having the institution of Parliament reinvigorated with the standards of accountability of its Ministers being reinforced by having the Minister forced to resign his Office, I would prefer the latter.

It is precisely because of the way politicians deal with themselves in these sorts of matters which brings discredit to all Members of Parliament and to Parliamentary institutions. It further alienates the public from the Parliamentary process, who cannot understand why a Minister of the Crown is allowed to get off scott free after having deliberately misled Parliament, when they themselves know that if any school child deliberately lied to their teacher, or to their parents, that that child would receive greater punishment than a Minister of the Crown received. It is because of this alienation of the public from the Parliamentary process that allows the rise of “Hansonism” in this country, because people become disillusioned with main stream politics and mainstream political parties.

Rory McEwen did himself and the Parliamentary system in the State no good whatsoever by his back down over the Graham Ingerson affair. He will never ever again be able to rise to his feet in the State Parliament and complain about the standards of Parliamentary behaviour, or Parliamentary ethics. He had the opportunity to draw the line in the sand and to demand that all Members of Parliament in the State, adhere to a certain minimum acceptable standard, and he failed that test.

The failure of the State Parliament to carry a vote of no confidence in Graham Ingerson has taught current and future Ministers of the Crown that no matter what your sins, provided you are prepared to "tough' out public opinion and have something that you can hold over either your Party or your Premier, then you can ride out the storm and suffer little or no penalty at all. The losers are the public of South Australian and the Parliamentary process.
