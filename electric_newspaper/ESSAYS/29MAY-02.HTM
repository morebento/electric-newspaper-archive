Friday May 29, 1998

Does society exist?
By Sandra Kanck Australian Democrat MLC

When Baroness Thatcher was the Prime Minister of Great Britain she made a startling admission - she didn't believe in society. I've always interpreted this to mean Margaret Thatcher didn't believe in the concept of community, that when all the artifice was stripped away each fared only for themselves in the economic jungle.

This philosophy sits uncomfortably alongside another of her stated beliefs, that private charity was better than public welfare - why if community means nothing would anyone willingly give to support others in the community? More importantly both positions released the Iron Lady from some of the more basic policy requirements that characterise social democratic government.

I come from the other side of the street regarding community. I find it expressed in my family, my street, my suburb, my city, my society. Whilst recognising that some benefits stem from competition I believe the fundamentals of human civilisation are built upon cooperation. A balance between the two imperatives is necessary for a healthy society.

Unfortunately the myth of the unmitigated benefits of competition has gained ascendancy in the late twentieth century. It is expressed in the Thatcheresque withdrawal of government from many functions that were once considered necessary for the creation of the good society. Universal access to free education, professional guidance to those actively seeking employment, the management of water supplies, the provision of power, the maintenance of well resourced public libraries.

This shrinkage of government has had unforseen consequences. Notice how conservative bleating about State's rights has faded away. It doesn't ring true to rail against Federal interference after you've taken the pea out of your whistle.

Other consequences are less welcome. I fear a decline in political activism especially amongst the young. What's the point of being involved in politics if all the important decisions are taken by boards on the other side of the earth? Boards immune to political pressure and drawn from a tiny elite. Boards that are connected to the needs and wants of the people only in so far as they can turn a buck or two. Political activism can seem futile in these circumstances.

This malaise is also affecting membership of pressure groups. It was recently reported that the Australian Conservation Foundation faced dwindling support amongst people under 30. ACF's membership is down from 20,000 in 1990 to around 15,000 now and people over 40 make up half of the existing membership. This is despite the fact that environment remains a powerful issue amongst people of all ages.

The implications of this are profound. If a Government is only as good as its opposition then a democracy is certainly only as good as its dissent. Pressure groups fulfil a crucial function in this process and youthful zeal is an important component of that dissent.

It's younger activists who do much of the leg work so essential to successful organisations. They climb the trees, storm the barricades and post the bills. They also offer a vital symbolism for any group - hope for the future.

The current apathy of so many isn't terminal for the future of our society. It will require innovative responses from political parties and pressure groups to reverse the trend. We will need to discover the ways and means of breaking through the corporate cocoon that has engulfed communities. We need to relocate the connection between politics and our society.

I'm confident this can be done. I believe that Baroness Thatcher was profoundly mistaken when she dismissed the concept of society.

I have circumstantial evidence to support my case. "Anyone who thinks the ANC is going to run the government of South Africa is living in cloud-cuckoo-land." (Margaret Thatcher 1987)
