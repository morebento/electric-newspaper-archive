Friday July 31, 1998

A matter of preference
by Bernice Pfitzner *

At the last State election in 1997, preferential votes made the difference by not returning the first and only Australian of Asian origin back into South Australia's Upper House.

The Asian community began to realise the importance of preferential votes.

Again, at the recent Queensland election, preferences made a key difference and the 前ne Nation Party', whose comments are seen as racist, made a significant impact on that State's Legislature.

Many Australians, especially Australians of Asian background began to feel apprehensive, insecure and unwanted. Others felt angry and many raise the question, "What have I done wrong?"

Against this background, the Signing Ceremony was born.

This week, community and religious leaders gathered together to sign a letter that will be sent to all candidates who stand at the next Federal election. The letter urges candidates to put the 前ne Nation Party' last in their allocation of preferences.

Eighty people attended this Ceremony, and as each leader's name (below) was called, there was a quiet sense of determination and hope felt by all present, that this is really what Australia stands for.

Respect for the individual, whatever his or her cultural, racial or religious background, and regard for the diversity in modern day Australia, lie at the heart of the letter.

Although the major political parties in South Australia are now indicating an intention to put 前ne Nation' last, the letter should serve to reinforce this intention and, more importantly, the reasons underlying it. These reasons include the need for candidates and the electorate also, to understand how misinformation and simplistic and divisive statements by leaders of a political party can fuel prejudices and division.

If the community and religious leaders failed to speak out, the electorate might be forgiven for thinking that the major parties were putting 前ne Nation' last, not for basic humanitarian reasons but for their own political self-interest.

In the eyes of those attending the Signing Ceremony, the need to overcome misinformation, prejudice and division, and the need to allay the fears and insecurities produced in the hearts and minds of the members of marginalised groups, are far more important that party politics.

* Bernice Pfitzner is a former Liberal MLC
