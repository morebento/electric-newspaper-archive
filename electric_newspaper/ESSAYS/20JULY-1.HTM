Monday July 20, 1998

A cup of tea, a Bex, and a good lie down
By Roy Franklin

Where on earth is Graham Ingerson? The accident-prone former deputy Premier, who faces a no-confidence motion when Parliament resumes tomorrow, hasn't been seen publicly for almost a week.

One story is that he will return to work today, after a week's holiday driving through SA's south-east Coonawarra region. There., so the story goes, he dropped in for a social chat with Independent Lower House MP's Mitch Williams and Rory McEwen.

He may as well not bother stopping for tea and scones at chez McEwen. Rory has made his decision and is understood to have made the casting vote in one of two adverse decisions arrived at by the Privileges Committee investigating whether Mr Ingerson misled Parliament.

But Mitch Williams might be a different story, and if Mr Ingerson is serious about plans to dig himself in, he's certainly wise to be looking to Mitch and the National Party's Karlene Maywald for a bit of loving-up in advance.

What will happen when Parliament sits tomorrow?

The Opposition is warming up for the maximum humiliation for the Government. The ALP is praying that rogue Liberal MP Peter Lewis will cross the floor to support a no-confidence motion against Mr Ingerson in the House.

True to fashion, the Opposition's gleeful anticipation is shared by some Liberal backbenchers, whose weakness for a spot of below-stairs Schadenfreude where Mr Ingerson is concerned has made for plenty of delicious sniping in the wings.

With guilty delight, these observers now contemplate the unthinkable: that Ingo will try and tough it out.

Rumours are rife of legal teams, of ancient precedents and dusty tomes on the finer points of the Westminster system.

The case of poor old Denver Beanland, the former Queensland Attorney General who last year stuck out a no-confidence motion and then limped home to an ignominious electoral defeat, is being hauled out and examined anew. Could Mr Ingerson do the same thing?

The prospect of a post-censure Ingerson on the Government front bench is a potential Liberal disaster beyond the happiest dreams of the Opposition.

With the ETSA debate kicking in and Labor looking for any diversion from its own superficial policy position, Premier John Olsen might as well start selling advertising space on the shirtfront of Graham Ingerson, Minister.

With ETSA so pivotal to the Premier's own future, Mr Olsen is unlikely to be keen on the distraction provided by his bellicose former deputy.

One of the funniest solutions to the what-to-do-with-Ingo conundrum was canvassed last week by The Advertiser's Phillip Coorey.

He reported that a deal was being prepared by various backbenchers and "at least one minister" to offer Mr Ingerson the presidency of the Environment, Resources and Development Committee.

According to Mr Coorey's story, this would entail the elevation to a junior ministry of the incumbent ERD president, rural MP Ivan Venning, while allowing Mr Ingerson to keep his government-provided car and taking about a third of the sting out of his pay cut.

Mr Ingerson proffered no response to the prospect of steering the low-octane ERD committee into its next terms of reference - an investigation into rural obstetrics.

Quick to confirm that he had been approached on the issue, the staunch Mr Venning nevertheless maintained that his first priority would be keeping Mr Ingerson on the front bench.

Touting Mr Venning as a supplement to the junior ministry is a novel idea. Prudent observers might well have expected solid backbenchers Rob Brokenshire or Martin Hamilton Smith to be a more appropriate choice.

Mr Venning's blustery loyalty and lack of media savvy were obvious the day after Mr Ingerson's disastrous "elevator-stop" resignation, when Mr Venning was cornered by journalists in a routine leadership-story-fishing-expedition outside Parliament.

He dismissed the suggestion that Mr Ingerson's departure would threaten the Premier, but then substantially undid that good work by stoutly declaring that Mr Olsen had the support of "at least 75 per cent" of the party room.

If Mr Venning were ever to be a serious candidate for a junior ministry, he would need to satisfy his colleagues that there would be no chance of such howlers becoming a regular occurrence.

Either way, this week will provide some welcome belly laughs for those who have grown to love Mr Ingerson's punchy approach to delicate situations.

The Opposition will be needling him mercilessly to provoke an injudicious tirade, and with the media likely to set up camp permanently in his back yard, the question may not be whether Ingo will crack, but when.
