Thursday February 5, 1998

Investment Agreement bad news for some
By Murray Chambre

Since the demise of the Soviet Union, the smug view has arisen in some quarters that Big Capital has now got the game to itself.

Big Capital and its alter ego, Big Money, have been pushing hard for their "global market" agenda. Globalism involves seeing the world as a single place - a place without national borders, without national governments and without national culture: simply a single market to be manipulated and exploited to maximise profits.

The phenomenon of globalism is best viewed from the differing perspectives of the winners and the losers. It is very difficult to assess it in absolute terms.

The undisputable fact is that most people, most nation states, and most cultures lose from this globalism. People have less democratic influence on developments in their own countries.

The winners are the wizards of international finance, global corporations, some shareholders and of course the obscenely rich corporate executives.

For the winners there remain many frustrations though. Present stratospheric profits are not enough. Big money has now fixed its sights on the nation state as an inconvenient, annoying and sometimes (yes only sometimes) substantial barrier to unlimited exploitation of the market.

After all, nation states do crazy things. They pass laws to protect occupational health and safety, they pass laws to protect the environment and worst of all they sometimes limit the access of big money to portions of their national economy. Nation states have historically had the gall to protect their citizens, their institutions and their culture from unlimited predation by Big Capital.

No wonder the nation state is a gross inconvenience.

Luckily for Big Money, there is some light at the end of the tunnel. The Organisation for Economic Development and Cooperation (OECD) has been persuaded by these international financial predators to promote an international agreement designed to demolish the sovereignty of national governments and to remove their capacity to act in the interests of their citizens. This agreement has been given the benign title "Multilateral Agreement on Investment" (MAI).

This agreement is presently being concocted, in secret, by Big money. When the work is complete, any nation signing up would relinquish its sovereignty. Whole nations will place themselves in the position of being answerable in damages to a specially established international tribunal if they fail to comply with Big Money's agenda.

Economic rationalists and other flat earth believers get into quite a lather thinking about the theoretical purity of this proposal. Sane, rational people all over the world however are deeply concerned.

Our Prime Minister has on many occasions expressed his dissatisfaction with the behaviour of the former Labor governments in signing up to unnecessary and restrictive international agreements. His government, recently rejected terms of a treaty with the EEC because it contained a rider clause presuming to cast judgement on Australia's human rights record. We can only hope that the Prime Minister and his government retain their healthy dislike for these international agreements and refuse point blank to have anything to do with MAI.
