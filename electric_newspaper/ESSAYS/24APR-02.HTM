Friday April 24, 1998

Trust will be hard to find
Sandra Kanck, Australian Democrat MLC

On February 25th this year the Hon Dean Brown, the Minister for Human Services, announced a change in direction for the South Australian Housing Trust.

The Minister acknowledged the Trust had a long history of providing low-cost housing to low-income tenants but argued that changing circumstances demanded a stricter criteria for assessing eligibility of public housing tenants.

He pointed out that changes in Federal funding had progressively shifted resources from the Commonwealth-State Housing Agreement (money to build houses) to the Commonwealth Rental Assistance Program (money for low-income private renters).

Furthermore he claimed as the Federal Government doesn't provide rental assistance for low-income public housing tenants and South Australia has a high percentage of public housing the State was doubly disadvantaged by its previous policies.

Consequently “our social responsibility as a community requires us to better target housing assistance to those with the greatest need - including those with severe disabilities, mental illness, extensive poverty, those fleeing domestic violence, and the homeless - rather than more general housing provision.”

Unfortunately for the cogency of the Minister's argument he then proceeded to point out that the number of trust tenants paying full market rent had fallen “from 75% in the early 1970s to around 18% today.” Obviously the policies of the Trust are already achieving the Minister's stated aims.

The Minister's unstated goals, the sale of our existing public housing stock and the winding back of the Trust's building program, are particularly worthy of analysis.

The Trust has provided housing for one in five South Australians since its inception 60 years ago. That startling figure is indicative of the importance of the Trust in South Australia's development.

There are sections of the community who may lament the stability of housing prices in South Australia post WW11. Bank shareholders, real estate agents and conveyancers come to mind.

Conversely for those hoping to fulfil the Great Australian Dream of owning their own home the Trust has been the equivalent of a benevolent uncle. By building affordable low-cost housing throughout metropolitan Adelaide and regional South Australia the Trust effectively suppressed the price of all housing in the state.

The provision of public housing for sale hitched private sales to a cost structure that minimised profiteering and eased the boom bust cycle in real estate sales. Equally private renters benefited from having a large public rental system that effectively held down prices in both sectors.

For those believers in Chicago School economics this wasan unmitigated disaster. Only the market can determine the true value of resources and any interference by the state distorts the process displacing costs into other areas.

Like most fundamentalists they often can't see the wood for the trees. Keeping a lid on housing costs is an excellent way of improving the standard of living of the community as a whole.

Not that the Trust is above criticism - not by a long shot. During the autumn session I alerted the Parliament to the fact the Trust had been ripping out gas heaters from trust properties whenever there is a change of tenants, irrespective of whether the heaters are in working condition.

Apparently this vandalism was prompted by concerns that if the heaters were to breakdown in the future the Trust would be responsible for repairing them. If there is no heater installed when the tenant moves in then the tenant is responsible for providing and repairing heating appliances.

This type of mindless decision is a throwback to the Trust at its paternalistic worst. The new tenants should have been allowed to choose. Bureaucratic efficiency always needs to be tempered with an intelligent commitment to providing options.

The Minister inadvertently declare his hand when he gave his ministerial on the Housing Trust. Rather than going into bat for the Trust, detailing its achievements and guaranteeing its future he meekly deferred to Commonwealth policy.

When that is contrasted with his persistent attacks upon his Federal colleagues over health funding I can only assume he approves of the gutting of the Trust.

In the long run that will benefit the few and disadvantage the many.
