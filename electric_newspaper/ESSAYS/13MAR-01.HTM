Friday March 13, 1998

South Australians have their work cut out for them
by John Spoehr

The South Australian labour market is in crisis and the outlook for any significant reductions in unemployment is bleak. There are simply not enough jobs now, and economic growth is too low to generate enough jobs for those who want them.

At the beginning of 1998 there were around 58 unemployed people for every job vacancy advertised in South Australia. The number of job vacancies is less than in the late 1980s. Vacancies remain around 45 percent below the peak in January 1990.

South Australia is the only mainland state to have made no inroad into unemployment over the twelve months to January 1998. Indeed the South Australian unemployment rate has steadily increased over this period rising to 9.9 percent (seasonally adjusted) in February 1998. While the national unemployment rate eased down by 0.5 percent in January, the state rate increased by 0.5 percent. This signals the collapse of jobs growth and an economy which is ailing.

Over seven per cent of the South Australian labour force, or 52,000 people, would prefer to be working more hours than they currently are. If added to the 71,500 people currently unemployed (January 1998) the combined number of people seeking work or more work could increase the competition for available job vacancies by up to 75 percent.

There were fewer people employed in South Australia in January 1998 than there were in January 1997. The foundation of secure jobs in South Australia is being undermined by the loss of around 720 full-time jobs per month over the two years to December 1997. In addition to public sector workforce reductions averaging more than 6,000 a year over the last four years nearly 14,000 people per year were laid off or retrenched over the last four years. Over the 1994-98 period the annual average number of people laid off or retrenched has steadily increased.

In 1991 around 18 percent of employed people in South Australia were in state public sector employment. Over the 1992-97 period the state public sector share of total employment declined to 14 percent, representing a 22 percent change in just four years. Combined, the large numbers of public sector and private sector losses have directly contributed to higher rates of long-term unemployment in South Australia.

Perhaps one of the most disturbing features of the current crisis is the very high level of long-term unemployment in South Australia (those people unemployed for more that 12 months). Nearly 42 percent of unemployed South Australians have been unemployed for twelve months or more. This is by far the highest level in Australia, exceeding even Tasmania, which historically fares worse than South Australia in the unemployment stakes. At nearly 70 weeks the average duration of unemployment in South Australia is only exceeded by Tasmania.

South Australian employment growth should be rising with the tide of national economic recovery. Instead it is sinking. At this stage of the economic cycle, it is reasonable to expect that inroads would be made into unemployment. Indeed at the end of the 1980s recovery period South Australia's unemployment rate had declined to around seven per cent.

With each recession during the last ten years the unemployment rate has ratcheted upwards. If significant gains are not made during the current recovery period unemployment in South Australia is set to ratchet ever upwards. Under these circumstances double digit unemployment is likely to be the norm rather than the exception in South Australia. This social and economic crisis is not inevitable. I believe it can be averted by governments which have the courage and the wisdom to embrace and implement new ideas.

The performance of the South Australian economy over the past four years has been poor relative to other mainland states and the nation as a whole. Growth rates during the current recovery period have not been sufficient to bring down unemployment to levels experienced during the late 1980s recovery period. Deteriorating economic growth rates over the 1995-97 period have resulted in much lower employment growth than was expected. The outcome for Gross State Product (GSP) growth for the 1996-97 period was nearly 50 percent less than the forecast rate of 2.25 percent. In contrast national rates of growth in Gross Domestic Product have risen over 1996-97 by 2.8 percent.

The challenge for government at all levels is clear. Their performance over the next two years will be measured against the extent to which they makes significant inroads into unemployment.
