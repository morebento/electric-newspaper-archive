Tuesday April 14, 1998

Knickers to you
by Sandra Kanck, Democrat Member of the Legislative Council

Fellas, are your underpants a little tight? Did you opt for the cheaper pack of polyester knickers last time you restocked the jocks and socks draw? Or perhaps those new jeans are pinching your crotch as you read this.

If so, you are probably contributing more than your fair share towards an alarming decline in male fertility. A review of 61 different studies has concluded that average human male sperm counts dropped by almost 50% between 1930 and 1990.

A Danish study has shown that in 1940 only 6% of men had extremely low sperm counts. By 1990 this had increased three-fold, to 18%. A French study quoted in Our Stolen Future found that if the downward trend in sperm count was to continue

"... the 30 year old man in 2005, who was born in 1975, would have a sperm count of roughly 32 million sperm per millilitre - about one-fourth the count of the average male born in 1925 (at the equivalent age)," it said.

Studies have also found that deceasing sperm counts are matched by an increasing incidence of sperm abnormalities. These alarming results have prompted some novel experiments.

Rams have been kitted out in the equivalent of modern underpants and dogs have been subjected to the wholly unnatural experience of wearing polyester underpants. These experiments detected lower sperm counts and an increased rate of pregnancy loss in their female counterparts.

It's clear modern fashion has failed to take into account a design peculiarity of the male gender. Male testes have been placed outside the body because they require a lower temperature than the rest of the body. Tight garments and those made from polyester have the effect of increasing scrotal temperature.

It seems the connection between tight, polyester jockettes and male infertility hasn’t been made in some places where it should. Dr Judy Ford, an Adelaide-based genetic scientist, author and designer of the animal experiments, suspects that when couples present themselves to a fertility clinic with sterility problems they usually go straight into an assisted conception program and that male partners are not routinely quizzed about the type of underpants they wear.

Given the effects of inappropriate underwear can be reversed within about three months by simply dispensing with the offending garments, underpants type and tightness should always be addressed before people enter a reproductive program.

I also believe the South Australian Council on Reproductive Technology should conduct a public awareness campaign on the issue. A smartly targeted advertising campaign would be a simple, cost effective means of increasing male fertility generally. This is definitely a better use of the stretched health dollar than blindly leading people into expensive reproduction programs.

So, fellas if you've got any problems in this area it might be time to consider the Scottish option.
