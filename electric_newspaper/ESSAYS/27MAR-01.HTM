Friday March 27, 1998

Rethink Riverlink, and more power to you
by Sandra Kanck, Australian Democrats M.L.C.

Adelaide can be as hot as hell on blistering summer days. As the temperature soars so does our consumption of electricity. Not surprisingly heatwaves generate peak electricity demand.

At peak consumption it’s estimated there is a one per cent shortfall in supply. In the trade it’s known as a 'brown out'. Consumers know it as no lights, no airconditioning and a fridge full of food on the turn.

The State Government’s proposed solution is Riverlink, a 330 kilometre electricity interconnect between Robertstown in South Australia and Buronga in New South Wales. It will cost approximately $100 million. The promise is of cheap, abundant electricity on tap. But the problems are manifold and complex.

Consider the implications of allowing a flood of cheap electricity into South Australia at the very moment the Government is hoping to sell Optima (SA’s electricity generating company). A substantial depreciation in the price of Optima is guaranteed. Business strategy is not a strong point of this Government.

Riverlink could also drive SA’s electricity generating industry into the ground. NSW’s currently has lower input costs and an excess of supply capacity, conditions ripe for driving competitors out of business.

The excess in supply will last a maximum of five years. In that time, with aggressive price cutting and marketing, Optima could be driven out of the market. If that happenes South Australia would be dependent on electricity produced exclusively on the eastern seaboard.

Aside from the loss of jobs and investment in South Australia, that scenario would lock South Australia into a permanent price disadvantage with the eastern states. Intractable inefficiencies in the transmission of electricity result in a 17 - 25 per cent loss of energy delivered, and consequently the cost disadvantage will be of the same order.

South Australia simply can’t afford to lose its generating capacity. Indeed in the long-term a relative advantage may swing to SA. With large reserves of natural gas, South Australia will be well placed to take advantage of the looming need for greenhouse friendly energy. Coal-fired generation produces three times the amount of greenhouse gases as gas-fired generation.

Despite the Howard Government’s notorious support for the coal industry a carbon tax will be added to the list of inevitabilties in life. When that happens it’s imperative South Australia has the generation infrastructure to take advantage of the changed circumstances. Green energy is the way of the future

Riverlink has other detrimental implications for the environment. Both the alternate routes for the transmission line will cut through the Chowilla and Calperum Stations. The stations are part of the UNESCO Bookmark Biosphere Reserve and contain the habitat of several species listed under the Endangered Species Act. The rare Black-eared Miner is particularly at risk. It is only found in old growth Mallee and is highly susceptible to habitat disturbance. Other endangered species include the Mallee Fowl, Red-lored Whistler and the Scarlet-chested Parrot.

Little of Australia's old growth Mallee remains. Driving a transmission line through what little is left will only increase the incidence of feral animals and plants further eroding the capacity of these areas to sustain indigenous flora and fauna. Indeed the transmission route may become a conduit for plant disease to pass freely.

These are powerful arguments against the construction of the line. The Government counters with an argument built around cost. The cost of increasing South Australia’s generating capacity is greater than the cost of Riverlink hence Riverlink is the way to go.

But I've been provided with information that indicates the total installation cost of a pair of gas turbine generators with sufficient capacity to fill the peak demand shortfall is $92 million. Whilst that is double the State Government’s share of the cost of Riverlink, the gas turbines would provide a healthy return on the capital investment.

The Government's decision to build Riverlink is connected with the creation of a national market. Whilst the national market is inevitable SA must not slip into the situation where it sources its base load of electricity from the eastern seaboard. To do so would expose business and households alike to ruinous prices for electricity.
