Friday March 27, 1998

I beg your pardon, it's a rose garden!
by Legh Davis, Liberal Member in the Legislative Council

The State Government is currently amending legislation relating to the National Wine Centre and International Rose Garden, to be located on about 7 hectares (17 acres) of land in the Hackney precinct, adjacent to the Botanic Gardens.

The Premier, John Olsen, has honoured his election promise of a major rose garden containing 10,000 bushes. This will be planted immediately east of the Bicentennial Conservatory. The Goodman Building will become the new administrative headquarters for the Botanic Gardens and Tram Barn A will be modified and used as the State Herbarium.

The national wine centre designed by award winning architect, Philip Cox and the well respected local architect, Steve Grieve, will now be sited close to the North Terrace Hackney Road intersection.

This major development will ensure a second cluster of tourism attractions for the city of Adelaide. Visitors will now be able to feast on the Botanic Gardens, the Bicentennial Conservatory, the Adelaide Zoo, the National Wine Centre and the International Rose Garden. These attractions are adjacent to the North Terrace precinct, a unique cultural boulevard in Australia which offers a range of interests all within easy walking distance - Ayers House, the Art Gallery, the Museum, the University of Adelaide, the State Library, the Bradman Collection and in Kintore Avenue the Migration Museum.

Also importantly, the Government has announced a major international rose festival in October 2000. This will build on and complement two highly successful events - Tasting Australia and Gardens Alive.

South Australia is the undisputed national leader in both wine and roses. The synergy between the two is obvious. We have achieved international recognition for our wine. Fifty percent of the nation's wine is produced in South Australia and 65% of wine exports derive from this State. Our Mediterranean climate makes South Australia one of the world's great places to grow roses. We produce 60-65% of all roses in Australia. David Ruston's rose garden at Renmark is the largest in the southern hemisphere and Walter Duncan in the Clare Valley is the nation's leading grower of heritage roses.

Over recent years some grape growers have planted roses at the end of each row of vines - not only for the visual appeal, but also because roses act as an early warning for the fungal problem of mildew. In the coming weeks the Premier will be encouraging wineries to plant roses to add colour to their vineyards and as a visitor attraction. The planting of roses on both sides of the highway through the famous Coonawarra wine district demonstrates the potential of this initiative.

So how does Mike Rann feel about this proposal? He is always talking about consensus, although I think the emphasis is really on the first three letters. But he is really happiest when he is nit picking and this is highlighted in the debate on the National Wine Centre legislation in the last few days. Mr Rann responded to John Olsen’s announcement on January 30, 1998 about a international rose garden being incorporated into the wine centre site, by saying "The rose garden was added to help disguise the fact there had been a huge stuff-up" (with the wine centre).

Mr Rann totally ignores the fact that the Premier in announcing the rose garden in October 1997, had flagged that one of the site options was the Botanic Gardens precinct. This modification to the original plan has been widely and warmly endorsed by all wine and rose industry leaders and also the Adelaide City Council. This small town whingeing style helps explain why some of Mike Rann's Labor mates now reckon his middle name is "Also".
