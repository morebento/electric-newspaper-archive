Thursday April 2, 1998

The air is free, the free-to-air isn't
by John Rau

Even economic rationalists would accept that there are some things that belong to everyone, or to no-one, depending on your point of view.

Sunshine, rain and air fall into this category. The economics text books used to classify these as "infinite". Perhaps the Ozone hole and pollution have modified this theory a little. Perhaps one day, a method will be devised to privatise these too, but for the time being, we seem safe.

Fresh water has always been seen as a "finite" resource. It has been managed and distributed by Governments in the public interest. There is after all no substitute for fresh water. Users have paid according to formulae which have balanced their level of consumption against the productive needs of industry and agriculture. Only South Australia has been foolish enough to pass control of this natural monopoly to a private Company.

The air-waves are another "finite” natural resource. Given any level of technology, limited channels are available for broadcast purposes. The existing analogue technology is quite limited. Even if there was the desire to do so, new entrants would be hard to fit in.

Digital technology now offers an explosive increase in the carrying capacity and broadcast versatility available to broadcasters. To pursue the water analogy, it is like finding another River Murray, or even an Amazon.

Digital technology allows the broadcast of several TV signals in the same bandwidth which, today, carries just one analogue channel. High definition TV, another digital option, can deliver Cinema quality pictures and sound into your home. Online services like the internet can be provided, as can interactive services. This technology represents an advance comparable to the leap from radio to TV.

The Federal Government has the power to call the shots for all Australian broadcast communications systems and operators. Digital technology has presented the Federal Government with an historic opportunity to manage this finite public resource in such a way as to introduce "market forces" into Australian television broadcasting.

The advent of digital TV is also a potential gold mine for the public purse. More channels, more users and more services, should mean more license fees and more taxation revenue.

What would an honest Government, committed to "user pays", even to the extent of chasing frail, aged, people in nursing homes, do?

What would an honest Government, committed to lower levels of personal income tax, tax reform and a GST do?

What would an honest Government, so committed to fiscal restraint that it is prepared to gut our Public Hospital system, to produce a current surplus, do?

While we consider these questions, let me tell you the answer that our Federal Government came up with. The Government has decided to allow the existing free-to-air networks to use digital TV free of charge and without competition from another network, until 2008. Think about it.

If, like me, you are still scratching your head trying to work out how our Federal Government could possibly come up with such an idiotic solution, lets look at the apparently unrelated issue of Media ownership rules in Australia.

The former Federal Government set the present rules in place some years ago. The rules do two things. They regulate market dominance across media sectors,(i.e. print and broadcasting), and they limit foreign penetration of the local media market generally. This is a pretty cosy deal if you happen to be one of the existing big players. No competition from outside and very limited opportunity to compete inside.

Mr. Murdoch's News Corporation is confined to a position of massive dominance of print media. He is unable to get into TV. He is now an American citizen. No doubt he finds this very inconvenient. He would love a digital network to call his own.

Mr. Packer has a different problem. He has almost all of his eggs in the Nine Network basket . He would love to acquire John Fairfax, instantly snapping up the Melbourne Age and The Sydney Morning Herald cash cows.

Fairfax is of course the last remnant of a major independent print media in Australia. It provides some choice other than a Murdoch paper, at least in Sydney and Melbourne. Mr. Packer wanted John Howard to change the rules last year, but a public outcry from people including Malcolm Fraser and a backbench revolt saved the day. Mr. Packer now employs both former ALP heavy Graham Richardson and former Liberal Party National Director Andrew Robb.

Speaking no doubt with a healthy sprinkling of self-interest, Mr. Murdoch is reported in The Australian of 28-29 March as saying of the Government's decision to see the merit in Robb's case on behalf of his client.

"It was unthinkable that anyone would have the gall to ask for it and that anyone would have the gall to give it."

It is hard to disagree with him.

Having denied Mr. Packer once over Fairfax, the Government has handed him a license to print money until 2008. Unlike all the rest of us, existing free-to-air broadcasters are to be sheltered from competition. They are to be given exclusive access to a public asset.

I am still scratching my head, wondering. Could it have anything to do with the fact that a Federal election is expected this year ?
