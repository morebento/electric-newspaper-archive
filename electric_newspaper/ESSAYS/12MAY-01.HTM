Tuesday May 12, 1998

Poker machines: illusion and delusion
by John Rau

In the agony of its final days, a Labor Government without hope of re-election sponsored a desperate measure. The Government assumed responsibility for the introduction of poker machines. A so-called "conscience vote" was allowed, but the result was never in doubt. Sufficient pressure was applied to enough of the undecided to ensure passage of the Bill.

The poker machine lobby added its formidable resources to the advice of bean-counters in the State Treasury. In an act of political stupidity which rivals Neville Chamberlain's Munich Agreement, Labor soiled its moral credentials just to provide a windfall tax base for the incoming Brown Government. Both Government and Opposition Parties were compromised.

It should be noted that there were some brave individuals, on both sides, who foresaw the misery to follow. Labor's Murray Delaine and Michael Atkinson had the courage to say no. In the Upper House, Mario Fillepa was worked over until he agreed, in good faith, to a "compromise". We are now living with the true consequences of that "compromise".

The arguments put in favour of introducing poker machines were the most dishonest and transparently contrived political humbug I can ever recall hearing.

One senior Minister argued that those opposing Poker Machines were all "snobs". Why ? Well, rich people went to the Casino to gamble. The lucky devils! The poor were missing out because they couldn't afford to come to the City or play black-jack. This was an equality issue. Poker machines would redress an injustice. This is a bit like arguing that everyone has the right to be exposed to the AIDS virus!

Then there was the tourism angle. All the countless millions carried by pensioners across the border at Wentworth, would stay in SA. The fact that these elderly folk went on these trips only once or twice a year was not mentioned. The fact that the whole event was a holiday of which this was just a part was also not mentioned.

Then of course our old friend employment was trotted out. No show without punch. Jobs, yes, thousands of good jobs. What a bonanza this has turned out to be. Jobs loading automatic teller machines with money in pubs. Jobs collecting ash trays from around machines. Jobs for social workers, lawyers and accountants dealing with marriage break-ups and work for the Salvo's.

In fact what has happened is a tragic indictment of our system of Government. The State has been reduced to collaborating in a joint venture with publicans and other purveyors of poker machines. This is done in full knowledge of the social misery it causes. This is like the State encouraging smoking or alcohol abuse, just to pick up a few shekels in tax. Publicans can't believe their luck. Pubs are now like gold mines.

The State is now hooked on gambling too. This easy money from community misery is hard to replace. Who speaks for the people? Certainly not the politicians, except Nick Xenophon.

The current tax reform debate has an important State dimension. The State must be weaned off gambling taxes. Until this occurs, none of the overdue regulation and winding back of gambling will occur. The fast buck is an illusion for the State as much as it is for the State's countless punter victims.
