Friday April 17, 1998

Voluntary euthanasia: the good death
by Sandra Kanck, Democrat Member of the Legislative Council

Moments before Janet Mills became the second person to take her own life using the Northern Territory's voluntary euthanasia law she uttered 'Peace at last'.

Although she was suffering from a rare form of skin cancer most media reports were circumspect about detailing the physical agony she was enduring.

Janet Mills dreaded waking up in the morning. For overnight her torn, weeping skin would stick to the blood stained sheets in which she slept Each morning her bedding would be doused with water before it was gently pulled away from her tortured skin.

Aside from the relentless pain Janet Mills was constantly engulfed by the stench of decay. She lived with the smell of death - her own death. All she wanted to do was to meet that death.

For a brief period of time the Northern Territory gave her and others the opportunity to legally do so with much needed professional assistance. The Federal Parliament closed that door when it passed the Andrews Bill.

Against the overwhelming tide of public opinion a majority of federal parliamentarians chose to turn back the clock. Most cited religious beliefs although I suspect more than a few were swayed by electoral considerations. Fear of the voters wrath is a more probable motivation for contemporary p

oliticians than the threat of eternal damnation. This raises an interesting question. Why with public support for right to choose VE running at between 70-80% should any politician fear an electoral backlash?

The answer lies in the nature of the opposition to voluntary euthanasia. It is largely the religious faithful who object. As it’s a matter of faith they are more likely to break their traditional voting patterns over VE. Particularly if the churches were to actively campaign on the issue. Indeed the Catholic Church pulled no punches in an expensive anti-VE crusade waged in California.

Suddenly that 20-30% opposed looks like a very powerful voting block indeed. Few seats would be considered safe in the face of concerted attacks from the churches. All lower house politicians are acutely aware of the dangers. I've had conversations with lower house members confirming this fact.

As a result of proportional representation upper house members are less easily targeted by pressure groups and consequently more likely to support VE. A fact borne out in the voting for the Andrews Bill. The Senate narrowly passed the Andrews Bill, the House of Representatives voted overwhelmingly in favour of it.

Prior to the last state election I proposed a referendum on VE to be held in conjunction with the October poll. My Bill was stone-walled by both Labor and Liberal. Too divisive, too dangerous.

In place of the people being given a direct say it was decided to refer the issue to a select committee. The Legislative Council Select Committee on the Voluntary Euthanasia Bill was convened and received some 3000 submissions before the October election formally ended its terms of reference.

Carolyn Pickles MLC attempted to revive the VE Select Committee in the autumn sitting of State Parliament. The Council voted to refer the matter to the Social Development Committee rather than re-establish the VE Committee.

"Gutless wimps" I screamed.

Progress on this vital issue has been effectively stymied once again. The Social Development Committee is dominated by conservatives opposed to VE. Even if it was to start the inquiry now it would take 12 months to complete.

Hence the Social Development Committee won't be making any recommendations on this matter until well into the Parliamentary term. By then it will be too late - too divisive, too dangerous, and preselection could be at stake.

I feel passionately about VE. It seems perverse that a person in dreadful pain should be denied the right to choose the time of their death. It is equally perverse that doctors are placed in the agonising position of breaking the law to comply with their patient’s reasonable request.

Medical technology has extended the boundaries of what we called 'life'. As such, politicians should be required to grapple with the implications of these changed circumstances. At present too many of my colleagues are more interested in the concept of life than life itself.

Sadly, VE legislation is a long way off.
