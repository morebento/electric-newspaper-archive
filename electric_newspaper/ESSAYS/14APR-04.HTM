Tuesday April 14, 1998

Hurley-burley in the Labor Party
by Legh Davis, Liberal Member in the Legislative Council

On Friday April 3, there was a meeting in the Medindie Gardens office of Labor Senator Chris Schacht. The notice convening the meeting was headed 'Independents' and stated that the purpose of the meeting was "to discuss the formation of a working relationship between 10 State Labor MP's (who collectively represent the Non-Aligned, Independents, and the so-called Duncan Left)".

This notice did not mince words. It stated "the action was taken to balance the aggressive behaviour of the Machine. (A grouping of the Bolkus Left and the Right). Over the last several months the Machine has made it clear that if any State MP is not a member of the Machine, they have a doubtful future in the parliamentary party."

Although the autumnal temperatures in Adelaide are falling, that's certainly not happening in the Labor Party in South Australia.

The notice bluntly concluded, "If the arrogant behaviour of the Machine within the party is to be checked, action such as that taken by the 10 State MP's was essential. The Machine's arrogance has reached the stage that their power brokers have told many party members who would like to seek preselection for winnable seats that they have no future unless they join the Machine."

This notice was signed "Ralph Clarke, Convenor". Yes - the same Ralph Clarke who until recently was the Deputy Leader of the Labor Party in South Australia.

Now Ralph has had a pretty tough time over recent months. He has a well deserved reputation as a knockabout fighter in the parliamentary arena. Ralph has been thrown out of the House of Assembly six times in his career - more than any other person in recent years. He is the quintessential deputy leader who can handle the rough stuff and dish it out and be a backup for the parliamentary leader.

Of course the Machine saw otherwise and following the State election in October 1997, the blood was knee deep in the Labor Caucus room. Poor Ralph was from the centre of the party (some call it the lost centre). He was challenged for the Deputy Leadership by Machine candidate Annette Hurley from the right of the party. And in a bitter battle Hurley rolled Clarke.

Where was Mike Rann in the days leading up to the contest? Well he was out bushwalking. The sort of thing you would expect from a leader who is anxious to ensure the best candidate for the job is elected.

As the new Deputy Leader, Annette Hurley has refocussed attention on the long-lost art of the ventriloquist's dummy. The most polite description of her performance has been benign. And in a newspaper which is surely scanned by thousands of young eyes, I cannot mention the colourful adjectives attached to equally descriptive nouns used to describe Hurley. Her questioning is lacklustre and never spontaneous and she is mute in the heavy exchanges that inevitably accompany question time.

But the Machine rules OK? All is not forgiven Ralph, even though the Machine knows deep in its heart (is that a contradiction?) you were the best choice for Deputy Leader.

Now Ralph has to skulk around organising meetings for the Independents who are still very much in the minority.

As Ralph himself confirmed in the notice calling the meeting of Independents, the Machine has threatened the preselection of party members who do not belong to the Machine. Sources in the Labor Party claim at least 7 Members of Parliament have been intimidated by Machine members and told their preselections are at risk.

The Liberal Party has not been without its challenges in recent months. But put in perspective, the Labor Party's deep-seated factionalism and hatred dwarfs any difficulties on the Liberal side of politics.
