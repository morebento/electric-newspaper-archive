Thursday May 28, 1998

Sorry Day raises difficult questions
by John Rau

On the 26th of May 1998, most Australians awoke to the news that the day had been set aside for them to say "sorry" to aborigines. Exactly what the expression of contrition was to be for, varied according to the spin put on it by each news service. A consensus of opinion seemed to focus on the experiences of the so-called "stolen generations".

For many Australians, aboriginal and others, this day stirred deep emotion. For these people, the day had a clear meaning and purpose. For most, however, I suspect that the event was one in which they took little interest. For some, it was the cause of private indignation. For a few, not concerned with observing the Australian convention of silence in preference to affront, it provoked critical public comment.

The well-intentioned involvement of primary school children in the events of the day was a sad low-point. The spectacle of young children, struggling to read prepared statements of remorse, is evocative of unsavoury totalitarian regimes. It is not fair. Let's leave our children out of the loop until they are able to fully comprehend the issues and formulate their own views.

Being sorry is not a simple business. Who should be sorry ? To whom, and for what ?

Since the arrival of Europeans in Australia, (now described in some school texts as "invasion day"),the aboriginal way of life has been under siege. By the turn of the century, most dispossession of aborigines from their land had already occurred. In Tasmania, aboriginal culture and language were essentially lost. In the south-east of the continent, a traditional lifestyle was destroyed by European influences, foods and diseases. Aborigines lived in conditions of squalor. They had little opportunity for education, employment or participation in our society.

Well-intentioned people saw these problems and sought to find solutions. At the time, assimilation into the cultural main-stream was viewed as the best answer. This same philosophy was applied in a different form to "new-Australians", escaping a war-torn Europe. We were all to become part of the core Australian culture. In doing so, all would be accepted.

State welfare agencies had the power to remove children from their families. This occurred when it was deemed to be in the interests of the child. This policy was applied to all children, not just aborigines. It still is. In this context it should not be a surprise that Australian Parliaments permitted the removal of aboriginal children from their mothers. In passing these laws, it was thought that these children would get a better chance in life. They would be educated. They would eventually be employed. They would not die in infancy from utterly preventable illness.

Foster parents came forward. No doubt, there are tragic horror stories of abuse. These however, were not the rule. Most foster parents were ordinary mums and dads. They were inspired by the best of motives, to give a child a good home and a chance at a better life.

Of course today these policies are judged to be "genocidal". Values have shifted. In these days of multi-culturalism, to have been "snatched untimely" from one's cultural roots, is a serious personal loss. Our society has taken to the celebration of difference as enthusiastically as it once demanded conformity. It is hardly surprising that many aborigines, taken from their mothers, should deeply feel a loss of belonging.

Our community values have evolved and will continue to evolve. In time, they may even come full circle. "Sorry Day" has exposed the real difficulty of obtaining a sincere apology from all Australians, for well intentioned deeds of the past. In the end, surely we must be judged and judge our ancestors, by the mores of the times. The nation needs a focus on the here and now, not on the then and if.

We allow aborigines to live in abject squalor in 1998. We still tolerate aborigines dying younger than the rest of us. We still allow aborigines to swell the ranks of our unemployed and to fill our prisons. Most shameful of all, our Governments refuse to accept the self-evident truths embodied in the High Court's recognition of Native Title. These are the elements of a heartfelt national shame for which all decent Australians would gladly express contrition.
