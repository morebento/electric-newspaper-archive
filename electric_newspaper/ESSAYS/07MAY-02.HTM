Thursday May 7

Who knew what, when?
By Sandra Kanck, Australian Democrat MLC

When the Premier John Olsen announced in a ministerial statement to Parliament on February 17 his about-face on the sale of ETSA and Optima, the Labor Opposition asked just one question.

Mike Rann, Leader of the Opposition, inquired of the Premier whether it was he or the Deputy Premier who had told the truth before the State election regarding plans to privatise the electricity utility.

The Premier characteristically ignored the question and attempted to go on the front foot with attacks upon the ALP for being a policy-free zone, but the nature of the 'debate' to come was foretold on that first afternoon: who knew what when.

I understand the intense media interest as to precisely when the Government decided to privatise the public utilities. The Government's no-sale protestations before the last election and subsequent u-turn after the poll have all the hallmarks of deception on a grand scale.

Politicians are generally considered economical with the truth, but the suspicion of calculated dishonesty further rents the fabric of trust between Parliament and the people.

As a fox smells its own hole, the ALP recognises the whiff of deceit and has locked onto this issue. The ALP and media working in concert on this issue has provided quite a spectacle. There's little doubt political scalps will hang on collective belts at the end of this particular chase.

Whilst it is valid the issue of deception is thoroughly explored questions of greater importance lurk off-stage. In particular, is the proposed sale of ETSA and Optima good policy? Will the people of South Australia benefit from the privatisation of their electricity utilities?

To answer these questions I've called for public submissions on the sale and have been conducting a series of meetings with industry specialists both here and interstate. The submissions I've received have been many and varied, ranging from simple declarations for or against sale to detailed analysis of the economic implications of the proposed privatisation.

I've made continual requests to the Government for detailed information to assist in the decision making process. I've also been subject to sustained lobbying from business, unions and academics regarding the sale. One of the benefits of meeting with people of such diverse perspectives is the opportunity to test one assertion against another.

The Government has gradually developed a three-limbed argument in support of sale. The February ministerial claimed that the State faced risks of State Bank proportions. The Premier identified threats to $1 billion worth of competition payments and unspecified market risks associated with entry into the national electricity market.

Later the Government began arguing that selling the utilities will reduce interest payments on State debt by a greater amount than the dividends the utilities are returning to the State budget. This position was outlined in the Sheridan report.

The argument regarding competition payments is erroneous. To qualify for competition payments the electricity industry needs to be disaggregated ( separating generation from transmission from distribution from retail). Whether the industry is publicly or privately owned is neither here nor there.

Disaggregation should be a priority of the Government. Not only would it guarantee competition payments but it will also result in greater efficiency within the industry. The Government can disaggregate by regulation.

The market risks argument is real and ties in with the interests rates/dividends argument. It is a bottom line question and the answer will be significant in my final determination. Equally I will be considering what will be best outcome in terms of price for all consumers, security of supply and levels of employment within the industry and state as a whole.

As to who knew what when, I think the Government lacked the courage of its convictions in the lead up to the last state election. But given the importance of getting this right the electoral deception will have only marginal weight in my final decision.

(Ms Kanck is currently conducting a comprehensive investigation into the Government's proposed privatisation of ETSA and Optima. The Democrats will cast the deciding vote on the Privatisation Bill in the South Australian Upper House.)
