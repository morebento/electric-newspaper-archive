Friday March 27, 1998

Australian submarines hit by bugged iceberg
by Keith Jacob, B.Sc.

The problems at the Australian Submarine Corporation (ASC) are not only unsurprising, but just the tip of an iceberg. What is more, we are not talking about a small iceberg, like the one which mortally damaged the tragic "Titanic", but one which threatens to sink entire economies.

Societies will increasingly discover that computer-related glitches are a daily hazard. It may be easy to point fingers at electronic and software engineers, but even the deepest of deep sea dives won't reveal the pertinent issues.

On the eve of the so-called "Millennium Bug", it is time for corporate and governmental executives and management at all other levels to put aside their greed, and embrace the need to understand the real cause.

Put simply, dubious design principles and poor management, are the major impediments to better software outcomes. Similar things can be said of the various hardware engineering forms. However, their related annoyances are generally much better understood.

What is not so well understood, if at all, is the special relation between software and hardware design in major projects, and especially, the particular issues unique to software development.

Some organisations, in various countries, have just begun to understand the importance of "mature" software engineering processes. Many others have not. It is quite common, if not the norm, for software development projects to run over time, and, not surprisingly, over budget. This is extremely damaging: many jobs are either lost, or not created, due to these budget blow outs.

It is certainly true that the imperative must be software that does what it is intended to do, but a failure to use good design practices often leads to missed deadlines. Throwing money and time at a software project that is behind schedule, may achieve the designated result, but it does not solve the underlying problem.

Overhauling project management practices, to properly encompass the special requirements of good software development, is an excellent start. Simultaneously, educational institutions must diligently prepare future software engineers, so that they are able to perform their tasks using a mature process.

With the increasing complexity of today's products comes the need to introduce some aspects of computers, to those interested, at a much earlier stage in their education.

One example of what has been happening in the U.S. may be of assistance. There, some software companies, in an effort to beat the competition to market, are content to ship faulty product. Deadlines are set, not by software development teams, but by marketing personnel and management, and product is sent to be packaged before it is completed.

Fortunately, for some users, "patches" for said product are made available on the Internet, and can be downloaded for "free". Consumers without access to the Internet are, of course, disadvantaged by such inappropriate behaviour.

The resulting poor relations between users and the company are not the end of it. If the company loses market share, and needs to spend extra money on customer support, the chances of it moving to a more mature process become less likely: a classic vicious circle .

One saving grace for the ASC, in its current predicament, is that it has a monopoly. Clearly though, the certainty that came, from knowing a sale was inevitable, did not guarantee the best process.

For instance, the software systems required to operate the weapons system should have been completed much earlier; they should have been a higher priority from the outset. The submarines cannot be handed over until their weapons system works, so the (completed) hardware is, at present, useless.

The hardware tests at sea are moot without a fully operational weapons system. Obviously, some sea trials are necessary, but the test commanders would be learning much more if they had the option of utilising (or not, as the case may be) all of the various software systems. There is a limit to how much can be gleaned from testing those systems in isolation: the systems will, ultimately, have to coexist.

It was a commendable decision to try to create a new Australian industry, arguments about relevance aside. However, the (apparent) mismanagement casts doubt on similar future commitments. Lessons must be learnt, and obviously will, but whether the big lesson is learnt will only be known in time.

More immediately, the actions of management in overcoming today's computer date problems, such as year 2000 inaccuracies, should be closely scrutinised by all. The "Millennium Bug" is not the only computer date issue requiring attention. The use of "9/9/99" to indicate "Out of date", and the fact that 2000 is a leap year, are just two that spring to mind; there are others.

All managers must take heed from the timely warning the ASC woes provide. A review of software education in Australia is much more important than the political scapegoating currently taking place. So, too, is better management of software as a whole.
