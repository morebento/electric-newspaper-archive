Monday June 22, 1998

Australia, Indonesia, and racism
by Andy Alcock *

In the past two days both the President of Indonesia, Dr Habibie, Australian Prime Minister John Howard have spoken about the need to combat racism in both countries.

Dr Habibie called on Australians not to support the One Nation Party because of its anti-Asian policies, and, at the same time, promised to combat anti-Chinese racism in his country. He said that he did not want to see the Chinese community suffer again as it did during the recent upheavals.

John Howard said that he was also concerned about the One Nation Party because it made Asian Australians feel unwelcome in this country.

Australians and Indonesians of goodwill would surely welcome both statements as they express the desire to see social justice in the region. However, in both cases, they just don't go far enough.

The virulent form of racism demonstrated by the Indonesian dictatorship against East Timorese, West Papuans and some Indonesian minorities has led to genocide and gross violations of human rights. Even though people in Indonesia, Australia and around the rest of the world are calling on Indonesia to give the East Timorese and the West Papuans their freedom, Habibie is refusing to do so.

If John Howard is truly sincere about his concerns for Australians of Asian descent, he would allow East Timorese refugees to remain in this country and not add to their insecurity by threatening to deport them. Further, his Government would be prepared to give them humanitarian support as they attempt to recover from the racist terror they suffered because of the illegal and brutal occupation of their country.

If the Australian government was truly concerned about peace and justice in the region, it would be doing more to press the Indonesian regime to allow self-determination for East Timor and West Papua. Earlier in the week Immigration Minister, Philip Ruddock and Foreign Affairs Minister, Alexander Downer, showed that they lack the compassion and the courage to implement the policies needed to ensure that the East Timorese and West Papuans achieve freedom and justice.

It is not just Pauline Hanson who is giving Australia a bad name. At least Ms Hanson is prepared to speak out against the crimes committed by the Indonesian dictatorship in East Timor. The callous attitude of the Australian Government towards the victims of this regime leads many in the world community to believe that Australians are prepared to profit from suffering of others.

* Andy Alcock is infotmation officer of the Campaign for an Independent East Timor.
