Monday May 4, 1998

One and a half billion good reasons not to sell ETSA
By John Spoehr

If ETSA is sold at a price of around $5.5 billion as appears likely, there will be a net loss to South Australian taxpayers of somewhere between $660 million and $1500 million over ten years.

The interest savings over this period from retiring debt are simply not enough to compensate for the loss of revenue the Government currently enjoys as the owner of the electricity industry. In narrow financial terms the sale of the industry does not begin to make any economic sense unless the sale price exceeds $7 billion.

Even at this price losses can be expected if the industry grows in line with State GSP - a not totally unreasonable expectation. If this were the case taxpayers would make a net loss of nearly $900 million. These figures have not been plucked out of the air. They are the result of a detailed economic analysis undertaken by Professor John Quiggin and I and released this week.

The study has been largely ignored, with the media preferring to focus on the more colourful question of whether the Government had plans to privatise ETSA/Optima before the election. Well of course it did. It has been politically and ideologically committed to the sale of ETSA for years, as have been a few key Labor Party figures.

In political terms the sale of the industry would provide a warchest to suit short term political needs. Some debt could be retired to make the books look a bit better and some spending could be undertaken in politically sensitive areas such as health and education. This could be just what it takes to re-elect the government unless a serious ETSA induced implosion within the leadership occurs over the next few months.

The key to the sale of ETSA is in the hands of the Democrats. They are carefully weighing up the evidence and will be faced with a difficult political decision. The economic evidence is clear - the sale will be a net loss to taxpayers in the medium term. Equally compelling is the fact that privatisation of electricity has almost uniformly resulted in an increase in prices for domestic consumers.

Over the 1989-91 period, domestic electricity tariffs in Britain rose by 28 percent or 4 percent above the rate inflation. Perversely the cost of coal in 1991 was 27 per cent cheaper than it was in 1988. If this decline in coal prices had been translated into a reduction in prices, the unit price for domestic consumers would have been around 8 percent less in real terms.

A 1997 World Bank study on the restructuring and privatisation of the UK electricity industry found that despite estimated efficiency savings between 6-11 billion pounds which translate into potential price reductions of 3.2 to 7.5 percent, prices did not fall as fast as costs. Indeed consumers were found to have experienced a significant loss from the reforms of between 1.3 and 4.4 billion pounds.

On the other hand shareholders were found to have gained significantly with National Power and PowerGen share prices increasing by around 300 percent since public float. The study also found that around 25 percent of the net gains from privatisation were transferred out of the country through the repatriation of profits.

In New Zealand, while the wholesale price of electricity declined by 17 percent in real terms domestic costs have increased by 20 percent in real terms over ten years since corporatisation in 1987 (Smith, 1997:36).

Since privatisation of the electricity industry in Victoria there has been a doubling of the connection fee for domestic users from around $16.00 to $34.00. Since privatisation in 1995 electricity prices show no sign of declining.

Recent evidence on water prices following outsourcing of Adelaide’s metropolitan water and sewerage supply can only reinforce concern about prices under privatisation. In this case claimed savings of around 20 percent from outsourcing have not been passed onto customers. Since outsourcing of the service in 1996 bills for average water use have risen by 6.7 percent.

The sale of the South Australian electricity industry makes no economic sense and it will inevitably lead to higher prices for domestic customers.

Futhermore the argument that the Government runs the risk of loosing $1 billion in competition payments from the Federal Government if it retains ETSA in public ownership is a fallacy. The Auditor-General confirmed in his 1997 Annual report that there is no immediate risk to the payment of the first tranche of competition payments due in 1998-99.

The question of ownership of the industry is irrelevant to compliance with competition policy as the 1995 COAG agreement itself makes clear. It says, "This agreement is neutral with respect to the nature and form of ownership of business enterprises. It is not intended to promote public or private ownership."

This view on the ownership of the industry was also reflected in the Industry Commission’s review of ETSA in a 1996 report for the State Government, which said, "The South Australian Government would not be prevented from continuing to own the various businesses which would be created by restructuring ETSA along the lines proposed."

The final word on whether ETSA will be sold or not remains with the Democrats. If the sale is blocked the spectre of a 99 year lease looms. This is equivalent to a sale and must be rejected as such. The Labor Party and the Democrats have the capacity to ensure that this does not proceed by firstly opposing a lease and secondly by publicly proclaiming that they will take all steps to bring the industry back into public management when political circumstances change. These are the political challenges ahead. In the end the Liberals, Labor and the Democrats will be judged against the test of whether they have put short term political gains ahead of the longer term public interest.
