Friday July 10, 1998

Rann: live wires exposed
by Legh Davis, Liberal Member in the Legislative Council

Mike Rann during the election campaign portrayed himself as a reasonable man. He would be bipartisan in his approach on major issues. Since the election he has continued to huff and puff about bipartisanship. It's just a pity he doesn't practise it.

Premier John Olsen flew out late Wednesday afternoon, July 1 for talks in Melbourne with Premier Jeff Kennett on taxation reform and later as number one ticketholder for the Adelaide 36'ers he supported the team in its bid for the national basketball premiership - its first since 1986. However, Leader of the Opposition, Mike Rann, had refused to give John Olsen a pair for this trip to Melbourne. As the Premier quite rightly observed in the House of Assembly, it was "petty, small-minded".

In Parliament there are some genuine friendships between Members on opposite sides. If a Member has relatives who are seriously ill, a pair is readily granted. There is also a respect for the conventions of the House. Protocol demands that the Premier of the day should be entitled to absent himself for events or activities which are deemed to be of interest and importance to the State. Premier John Bannon was never denied a Parliamentary pair to attend an important sporting event such as the Grand Prix.

Mike Rann does not have a pretty record when it comes to making the right decisions on big issues. He was violent in his opposition to Roxby Downs and encouraged people to boycott BP petrol stations as at that time BP was a joint owner of the project. Today Roxby Downs is one of the State's great success stories of this century, boasting 4,000 people. Ironically, the 1996 ALP platform, in discussing development in country areas of South Australia, notes "other centres, in particular, Roxby Downs, are enjoying major expansion". Mike Rann was wrong on Roxby Downs- dead wrong.

In the last few days we have witnessed Mike Rann's legendary bipartisanship on the big issues. The privatisation of ETSA and Optima is currently being debated in the Parliament. In October 1996 the Labor Party Convention in South Australia revised its State platform. Page 60 of that platform unambiguously notes "Labor is committed to maintain ETSA's generation, transmission and distribution assets in public ownership." In other words, Rann is handcuffed to a policy of opposing ETSA privatisation because of a decision made nearly two years ago.

Just pause a moment and think about how the real world works. Can you think of a business or an organisation that would not review a decision made two years ago in the face of dramatically changing circumstances in its industry? I certainly can't. And yet that is what the Rann-led Labor Party is resolutely doing.

This is in sharp contrast to the Labor Government in New South Wales, where Treasurer Michael Egan is actively campaigning to change Labor Party policy to allow the Government to privatise the power industry and raise an estimated $22 billion which would slash the State's debt to zero.

In a paper distributed to the power and electricity unions last year, Egan made the following comments:

"The provision of electricity is not redundant; however in my view its provision by government is. When large-scale electricity generation was first established in the late nineteenth century, capital markets were primitive. Government had to do the job themselves or give monopolies to private companies to generate power.

"This is no longer the case. As demonstrated the world over, a competitive private sector can now produce and distribute electricity effectively."

Egan claimed "there is no sustainable argument that the government, the taxpayers, the environment, consumers and the economy would be worse off if we had more than one owner of the major electricity utilities in New South Wales."

In an excellent summary of the arguments for privatisation of power Egan noted "in the long term our electricity businesses will be facing only increasing commercial and competitive pressures. In the short to medium term, the sale of the businesses will provide additional budget flexibility for the government as well as potentially funding a number of major capital projects."

Rann, whose ostrich-like attitude to Roxby Downs was matched only by his naivety and indifference to persistent Liberal questioning over the State Bank, should take particular note of Egan's observation that "it is naive to think that the great economic and social changes over the last few decades will soon cease ...Governments need to reap the benefits of these changes while softening the impacts of change and protecting the essential values of the community ...New South Wales either had to gear up for these changes or risk losing a competitive edge against other States or other countries."

Egan made the point that "no longer is electricity a monopoly business with guaranteed returns to government. It is an increasingly commercial and competitive business with increasing risk to its profits. The choice for government is whether it regulates and oversees this industry to secure good social and economic outcomes, or whether it owns the industry, thereby risking millions of dollars of taxpayers' money in commercial business enterprises, rather than investing those funds in social and economic services and facilities that are the core areas of government responsibility."

Egan concluded by noting that "the social and economic benefits of selling our electricity utilities, I believe, are very clear. It is now up to the opponents of sale to join the debate by identifying any specific social and economic advantage which they might argue can only be gained by retention."

Well Mike, I couldn't have put it better myself. How do you argue against that logic? I would like to hear why the power industry in South Australia is different from that in New South Wales.

With Rann's track record on Roxby Downs and the State Bank, you would think that he might be a bit more careful the third time around. But no, Mike Rann last week refused to attend a government briefing on ETSA offered to all Labor Members by Premier John Olsen. How extraordinary! Here was an opportunity to have a briefing from international experts and Treasury officers on the biggest issue facing the State since Mike Rann refused to face the questions on the State Bank nearly a decade ago, and what does he do? He refuses again. Rann said he would only attend the briefing if Premier John Olsen agreed to a television debate on the issue and also provided various reports on the privatisation.

However, former ALP State Secretary, Terry Cameron, who is arguably the only member of the Labor Caucus with any real business background, accepted the invitation and issued a challenge to Mr Rann "to direct me not to go".

And so it came to pass that just a few days later 15 Members of the Labor Caucus were at the lunchtime briefing on Wednesday July 8. What on earth happened? Did Mike Rann receive a blinding flash from a stobie pole? No. It was simply that some Labor Members genuinely wanted to attend the briefing. Mike, who had his finger in the dyke, suddenly found he couldn't prevent the flood of responses and so he followed his followers to the briefing. How farcical!

Aren't the voters of South Australia entitled to expect the Opposition should take such an important matter seriously? Particularly when Mike Rann silently acquiesced to the privatisation of QANTAS, the Commonwealth Bank and the Commonwealth Serum Laboratories (CSL) by the Federal Labor Government, which also had in principle supported a partial sale of Telstra. Rann, as a member of the Bannon government, put his hand up for the privatisation of the State Bank of South Australia and the sale of the government's controlling interest in Sagasco.

Mike, I've looked at the sky and QANTAS hasn't fallen out of it. I've just rung Information and confirmed that Telstra's still around. And how many complaints have you heard from Mike Rann about the supply of gas to consumers in South Australia since he was a party to privatising it a few years ago?

It's all a bit sad. We live in a State where people get excited about opposing things while the rest of the world sweeps by. Rann, whose financial and business acumen would not bother the scorer on his efforts on Roxby Downs and the State Bank, is doing South Australia a disservice by refusing to convene a special Labor convention to revisit the issue of ETSA privatisation. If New South Wales Labor Premier, Bob Carr, and his Treasurer, Michael Egan, want to do it, and Premier Jeff Kennett in Victoria has already done it, and the whisper is that newly elected Labor Premier, Peter Beattie in Queensland also wants to do it, why can't Mike Rann do it?
