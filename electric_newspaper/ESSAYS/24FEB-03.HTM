Tuesday February 24, 1998

History on Hussein's side
By John Rau

As the Middle East situation remains dangerously poised, let's reflect briefly on Iraq's historical relationship with the West.

There is no doubt that Saddam Hussein is a violent, ruthless tyrant.There is no doubt that in most civilized countries, he would be an organized crime figure or in jail.The man is a 24-carat villain.

Hussein has always been thus, but he has not always been in the international bad books.There was a time, not so long ago,when he was America's pal in Asia-Minor. In those days, the great Satan was Iran and Hussein was locked in a bloody, pointless war of attrition.The Americans were happy to arm him.The British and the Europeans were busily selling him equipment, materials and know-how.His capacity to produce and deliver weapons of mass-desruction dates back to this time.

During the conflict with Iran,Hussein used mustard gas on Iranian civilians. In some cases,even hospitals were hit. At the time, nothing much was said. Iranians did not seem to matter, and he was our buddy in the Gulf.

Eventually, both countries accepted that neither could win and this pointless war ended.The war had suited the U.S. as it was punishment by proxy for the Iranian hostage fiasco.The war also suited Israel, as it tied up two potential enemies in a debilitating struggle and split the loyalties of the Arab world.

Hussein was not quiet for long .He had learned his lesson, not to pick on a country big enough to defend itself. His next victim, Kuwait, was too small to resist any invasion.

Occupation of Kuwait gave Hussein immense oil reserves and associated economic clout. It also placed him in a leadership role in the Arab world. The U.S. was not going to put up with any more Arab oil shocks like those in the 1970's. Israel was justifiably scared of a belligerent Hussein dragging the Middle East into another war against the Jewish State. Something had to be done.

As luck would have it, Russia was particularly in need of American largesse at this time. China, in transition from Deng, was also focused elsewhere. Britain, as always, was at the ready. Even France, normally keen to advance its own interests in the Arab world at the expense of its allies, showed uncharacteristic solidarity.

The community of Nations approved of operation "Desert Storm". The insincere justification of " restoring democracy " to Kuwait by returning the autocratic El-Sabah family had nothing to do with it. Bosnia is the classic case of a far greater human disaster, largely ignored for years. The critical ingredients here were oil and Israel.

"Desert Storm" should have finished Hussein off. The fact that he is still there and firmly in control is testimony to the ruthless Police State he has under him.

If he is killed in any military action taken by the U.S., there is no one other than his son (who by all accounts is worse!) to take over. This would destabilize the whole region, probably making way for the Syrian strongman, Assad, to fill the vacume. This would be no better for the U.S. and arguably, a lot worse for Israel.

The other consideration is how Hussein would react to any military strike. He does not have the conventional weapons required to inflict massive losses on the U.S. What he probably does have is enough chemical or biological weaponry to devastate any densely populated area. All he requires is a method of delivery. The Israelis are fully aware of the shocking effect of low-tec delivery methods. What is more low-tec than a car or a truck? How vulnerable is Israel to an attack of this kind?

The US has rounded up the "usual suspects", Canada , Britain, New Zealand and Australia. It is hard to see how it is in our interest to be involved in this brawl on the other side of the planet. Sucking up to Uncle Sam is the only credible explaination. As Australians we should be a lot more concerned with the meltdown in Indonesia and its direct implications. Let's hope that our SAS contingent is home and safe before we need them.
