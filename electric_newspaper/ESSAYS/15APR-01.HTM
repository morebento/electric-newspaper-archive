Wednesday April 15, 1998

The blind leading the deaf
By John Rau

The last State election plumbed new depths in public despair. The electorate really wanted none of what was on offer. The Government presented as hopelessly divided, bereft of talent and without ideas. The Opposition was not warmly embraced as a realistic, preferred alternative. Polling figures show that the bulk of the increase in the ALP two-party preferred vote came via minor party and independent preferences.

The Democrat vote rose to such an extent that the Democrat candidates outpolled major party candidates in many seats. There were very nearly a number of Democrat members sitting in the House of Assembly. Two Democrats and one independent were among the eleven members elected to seats in the Legislative Council. Democrats such as Ian Gilfillan claimed that this was a massive pro-Democrat vote. In reality, the huge rise in Democrat first preference votes had a lot more to do with an expression of frustration at the incapacity of the major parties to tackle problems actually facing South Australians.

The election predictably solved none of these real problems, nor did it bring a solutiion even one step closer. The sad realisation is dawning on South Australians that it is more than just a case of our political leaders not being up to the job. Our present system of Government seems incapable of throwing up the leadership required.

Our Westminster model of Government was borrowed from England. It should not surprise anyone that this model, developed over centuries to govern an Empire upon which the sun never set, is not meeting our local needs in the twilight years of the 20th century.

South Australia is basically a city-state with a vast hinterland. Our State is not blessed by nature with great agricultural or mineral wealth. We lack fresh water. We enjoy no geographical advantage associated with being on the way to somewhere important. Unless we do things a lot better than other Australians and people in other lands, we are doomed to fade and die as a community.

We require direction and leadership in order to tackle and overcome the massive impediments to our future prosperity.

Our present Constitutional arrangements encourage and entrench the instability and weakness in our Government.

The xxecutive arm of Government so dominates the legislature that the only check on excesses or stupidity comes from the judiciary and certain statutory officers, such as the Auditor-General. The power of the executive has corrupted the system to such an extent that the wishes of the people are ignored in substance all of the time and in form, at all but election times.

The role of the Parliament as a protector of the people, willing and able to take on the executive, has been turned on its head. The Parliament is now little more than a bizarre private club. A club in which those who yearn for executive office, hunt and attempt to destroy incumbents. In so doing, they hope to prize the levers of executive power free for their own use. This predatory mentality dominates the Parliament to the exclusion of its proper role as our watch-dog over the Executive.

The problem is particularly serious in South Australia where such a high proportion of Government members in both chambers already hold executive office, whether as a Minister or a Parliamentary Secretary.

Ironic as it may seem, this also corrupts the proper functioning of the executive too. Recent history shows how a Premier, swept into office with a record majority in the House of Assembly, can be destroyed by venal self-interest on the part of otherwise insignificant members of the governing party. This gives no confidence in the system.

The old objective of a Government of the people, by the people, for the people, has become a reality of Government of the people, by the powerful, for the powerful.

Things can not continue in this way. Already contempt for our system and our politicians has reached dangerous levels. The public is so disillusioned that we almost expect corruption, lies and deceit from Parliamentarians.

The cure for this complex of symptoms requires radical constitutional surgery. The executive arm of Government must be separated totally from the legislative arm. The executive should be directly elected by the people. The legislature should be directly elected as it currently is, but it must be impossible to hold both legislative and executive office at the same time.

This is hardly an untried, crazy idea. This is essentially the same system that operates in the American States and at the Federal level in the US. The governing party members in the Parliament would have their hands untied from the responsibility of blindly supporting the Premier, for fear of bringing down the Government. As it is now, they are under enormous pressure to support him, no matter how manifestly silly he may be.

The Premier, or in my preferred model, the elected Governor, would have a secure, fixed term in office. He could only be removed by impeachment proceedings such as are presently available to remove judges. The Governor could select whomsoever he wished to be in his executive team, the "Ministry" if you like. This ministry could be selected on the basis of need and the talent available throughout the community. Contrast this with the present situation where the choice is limited to the tiny pool of talent in the Parliament at any given time.

In practice, a candidate could stand for the position of Governor, with or without the support of the major political parties. Either way, we would be given a chance to vote directly for our Governor for the next four years.

This reform would restore some faith in the political process and bring the executive closer to the people. The Governor would have a personal mandate to implement the policies upon which he was elected. The Parliament would be free to keep a critical eye on the executive, without fear of destroying the Government by expressing a difference of opinion, or properly scrutinising executive conduct.

Of course, no system of government has yet been devised which is immune to abuse or corruption. I am sure that my proposal would have its faults and weaknesses too. But surely the time has come to seriously overhaul our present system. It is not working. This proposal would be a great leap forward.
