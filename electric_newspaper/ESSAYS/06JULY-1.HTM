Monday July 6, 1998

The lights go out on the Democrats
by Legh Davis, Liberal Member in the Legislative Council

There are three Australian Democrats in the Legislative Council. Mike Elliott is the Leader and Sandra Kanck is the deputy Leader, which must leave Ian Gilfillan, the former leader of the Democrats, just a little bit miffed.

Sandra Kanck has formulated the Democrats' attitude to the proposed ETSA privatisation. The Kanck Report which opposed the sale of ETSA was made public on June 25. However, last week in Parliament, the Treasurer, Rob Lucas blew the Democrats' stobie poles out of the ground and left them wire-less!

Ms Kanck made much of the fact that the Democrats' decision had been reached after 1,000 hours of research. But the lack of logic and errors contained in this report indicated that 1,000 hours was not enough.

In South Australia Optima is responsible for the generation of electricity. The Kanck Report stated "... Optima should be maintained in its current structure, and other private companies encouraged to set up gas fired generation capacity in South Australia." In the following paragraph Kanck argued "if Optima is to be separated, it's possible that dividends would suffer. Conversely its potential sale price would be reduced, undermining the government's debt reduction arguments."

In other words, Ms Kanck was clearly against breaking up Optima into two or three companies competing against each other. There can be no other construction put on her statement.

The intelligent readers of The Electric Newspaper (aren't they all?) will no doubt remember that on May 7 Ms Kanck wrote an article about ETSA. However, in that article she was singing a different tune. Kanck wrote "disaggregation should be a priority of the government. Not only would it guarantee competition payments, but it will also result in greater efficiency within the industry."

The Treasurer also noted an article which had appeared in The Advertiser on July 1, where Ms Kanck was quoted on the Democrat position on disaggregation - "Ms Kanck said that, while she had concerns about how Optima would be split, she felt ‘comfortable' with seven government owned corporations competing in the National Electricity Market."

The Kanck Report reaffirmed that Optima should not be broken up when it noted "In the South Australian market Optima will effectively exercise monopoly control. By strategic bidding Optima can set the regional pool price between 60% and 96% of the time. As a result of the scrapping of Riverlink, Optima's market control is increased." Rob Lucas made the very obvious point that if Optima, as a monopoly, can set the price of electricity for up to 96% of the time, there will logically be a higher level of prices under the Democrat model than if there is a disaggregated Optima with a number of companies competing against each other.

And yet the Democrats claim that ETSA should not be sold because the government will lose the dividend which it currently receives from ETSA. And how is that dividend obtained? Yes, from the profits achieved by ETSA. As Rob Lucas told the Legislative Council, "if you are going to allow Optima, as one Optima, to exercise monopoly control over price for up to 96% of the time and you also want to maintain or increase dividend flow to government, the only policy option as a result of that is that the long suffering consumers out there will have to pay higher electricity prices under the Democrat or Kanck model of the electricity industry here in South Australia."

But that was not the only stumble in the Democrat grumble. An official Democrat pamphlet outlining the arguments against the ETSA sale claims "A World Bank study has shown UK shareholders are up to $4 billion worse off because of electricity privatisation." That sounds like a pretty good argument, except it is totally untrue. It is not a World Bank report at all, but rather the precis of a report by some academics which happened to be published in a World Bank journal, which carries a clear disclaimer that "the views published are those of the authors and should not be attributed to the World Bank or any of its affiliated organisations. Nor do any of the conclusions represent official policy of the World Bank ...".

If Sandra Kanck wants to properly quote the World Bank view she should be advising the South Australian community that the World Bank has in fact consistently recommended that governments should privatise utility services and has spent millions of dollars in supporting this objective.

And why didn't the Kanck Report tell the truth about electricity privatisation in the UK, where both residential and industrial electricity prices in real terms have fallen by around 20% over the last 8 years and where complaints by consumers have fallen by nearly 60% since 1991?

With the pressure mounting against these flaws in the Democrats' argument, Ms Kanck simply lost it in the Legislative Council last week. On Wednesday July 1 at the end of Question Time Ms Kanck claimed she had been misrepresented. She suggested that in my question to the Treasurer I had taken quotes from the Kanck Report out of context. But I had not even quoted from the Kanck Report. She claimed that Rob Lucas had got it wrong when he said he had examined the six page Democrats' analysis on ETSA appearing on the Internet. However, I was the one that had made the statement, not Rob Lucas. Kanck claimed the analysis ran to 10 pages plus appendices.

Dear readers, if you would like to check Kanck's numeracy, take a trip with me to http://www.electric.on.net/news/26June-democrats.html and count the number of pages on the Democrat analysis. Yes, it is six.

A thousand hours of research by Kanck has produced sloppy and inaccurate information and the Australian Democrats' position has fluctuated from week to week on the important question of Optima's dissaggregation. Kanck's performance in the Legislative Council last week was an embarrassment. She couldn't even recall accurately what had been said minutes earlier.
