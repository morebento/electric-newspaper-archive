Tuesday April 14, 1998

State jobs Budget vital
by John Spoehr

More bad news on the jobs front must be met by a State Budget which delivers jobs growth and hope for South Australia's 72,000 unemployed people.

South Australia's seasonally adjusted unemployment rate has jumped to its highest level for two years. Once again the rate is in double digit figures, rising from 9.9% in February to 10.1% in March.

Further deterioration in the South Australian labour market at this stage of the economic recovery is alarming. Over the last twelve months the SA unemployment rate rose by 0.6 % while the Australian rate fell by the same margin. A rise in unemployment nationally may be a signal that the Asian financial crisis is taking it's toll on jobs growth. A crisis of this magnitude on top of large scale public sector employment reductions is of great concern. A jobs policy vacuum at a State level will further compound the jobs crisis South Australia faces.

To restore confidence and security and in the SA labour market the tide of public sector job losses must be stemmed. The Government should dismiss the scorched-earth economic solutions of the SA Center for Economic Studies and announce a freeze on any further public sector workforce reductions. It should establish a Job Retention Task force to assist local businesses experiencing difficulty and call on the Federal Government to restore industry networking assistance.

Rather than privatise public enterprises such as ETSA and the TAB, cutting off an important revenue stream to the budget, the Government should begin a process of boosting public sector infrastructure investment leading to increased jobs in the private sector.

A detailed import replacement strategy should be developed to support local manufacturers and boost jobs growth and provide a long term base for boosting exports.

South Australia's ailing regions, many with unemployment rates double the State average must have special assistance through the development of regional employment pacts, bringing industry, government and the community together to resource projects which lead to long term sustainable jobs and support diversification into new industries such as aquiculture, eco-tourism and growth industries such as community services.

The State Government must intensify pressure on the Federal Government to provide structural adjustment assistance to South Australia to help compensate for the negative effects of the tariff phase-down on employment levels. These funds should be directed towards infrastructure development and strategic assistance to small and medium sized businesses.

The forthcoming State Budget must deliver on the need to develop an employment strategy for South Australia. It is incredible that after four years in government the State Government still has no coherent employment or industry development strategy. Any strategy must contain a target unemployment rate of around 7 percent by the year 2000 if we are to claw back the gains made during the previous recovery in the late 1980s.

John Spoehr is Deputy Director of the Centre for Labour Research at the University of Adelaide.
