Monday June 29, 1998

The world changes, but not in South Australia
by Legh Davis, Liberal Member in the Legislative Council

People reading this newspaper are participating in the global revolution in communication and information technology. The world is shrinking. The way we do things is changing.

This weekend many South Australians will watch World Cup Soccer matches live from France on SBS television or race meetings around Australia live on Sky television. Others will surf the net and buy books from the Amazon Bookstore in the USA. People planning holidays may book through the net. All these examples have an impact on the economy and employment. The ability to watch races in the comfort of a pub or with the aid of teletext betting at home has put pressure on attendances at race meetings. What happens to bookstores in Adelaide competing against the growing percentage of books being sold through the net? Indeed, it is suggested that the net accounts for up to 20% of all books sold in America. And what happens to South Australian travel agents faced with this cyberspace competition? And how do governments collect taxes on cyberspace transactions?

You haven't heard the Australian Democrats talking about these things. And why not? These changes in information and communication technology will destroy jobs in this State. And if South Australia is not alert to this danger and does not work hard to create new jobs , then it will slide off the map of Australia.

Put simply, South Australia has to be competitive. And power costs can be a very important component in creating a competitive economy.

So there is a paradox. The Democrats embrace and recognise change in global communications by using the net to proudly trumpet their decision against the proposed privatisation of ETSA and Optima. But they ignore the reality of what is happening in the commercial world. Yes, change is happening there too. The National Electricity Market, established courtesy of a Federal Labor Government, will see State boundaries disappear. Initially large South Australian employers such as Mitsubishi or WMC will be able to buy electricity from interstate if the price is right. In time electricity will be like a supermarket commodity. Merv and Melba Metro of Melrose Park may in fact be able to buy electricity through the supermarket.

In many countries electricity has long been the province of the private sector. Labor, shortly before it lost government in South Australia in 1993, flogged off the State's controlling interest in the South Australian Gas Company. A de facto privatisation of an energy utility. Mike Rann was there for that decision. At least he got that one right! So why now is he against the privatisation of ETSA, when all other States are moving in that direction? And why are the Australian Democrats ignoring the reality of the National Electricity Market? Do they really think that South Australia should hang on to ETSA, which ultimately may be the only government-owned power utility on the eastern seaboard, and expect it to compete against private sector owned power utilities? And if ETSA remains in public hands, where will the money come from for additional capital expenditure required to upgrade or refurbish our power sector assets? The Democrats' analysis of their decision to reject the privatisation of ETSA and Optima includes no discussion of that very significant point.

The Democrats reject the arguments of the well-respected Tom Sheridan, a former Auditor General and Deputy Under Treasurer in South Australia, and instead prefer to accept the report of Professor John Quiggin of James Cook University and John Spoehr of Adelaide University, who were commissioned to provide a report by the United Trades & Labour Council, the Public Service Association and CEPU and ASU. No-one has yet showed me any evidence of Quiggin and Spoehr ever saying anything nice about privatisation. And why don't the Democrats quote the views of the business community in this matter?

The Democrats, in publishing their reasons for opposing privatisation, claim the South Australian Liberal Government "has an ideological obsession with the level of debt". Some facts may help put the matter of debt in perspective. Victoria has slashed its debt through privatisation and has achieved a AAA credit rating, which reduces the interest rate it pays on any borrowings. Queensland's debt is minuscule. The New South Wales Labor Government is also committed to massive debt reduction through the privatisation of its power companies. So dear reader are you as relaxed as the Democrats at the prospect of South Australia competing against those three eastern States, which each will have dramatically lower debt per capita, better credit ratings and much deeper purses to attract and support business and provide monies for health, education and other vital services? The irresistible conclusion is that the lentil soup is talking!

So globalisation to the Democrats is terrific if it involves the internet or the South Australian wine industry breaking into new markets overseas and clobbering other countries.

But when it comes to the reality of arguably the biggest game in town, they put on their knitted cardigans, light the candles, reach for their quill pens and draw a moat around the map of South Australia.
