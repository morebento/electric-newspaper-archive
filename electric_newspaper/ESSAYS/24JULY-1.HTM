Friday July 24, 1998

My Tribe Right Or Wrong
By Sandra Kanck, Australian Democrat MLC

When my brother Dennis was eight years old and I was just 11, he achieved all that Year 2 local boys could aspire to: he was granted admission to Warwick Cramey's 'gang'.

Warwick's gang was the most prestigious going and Dennis immersed himself in it totally. I was driven balmy day after day as Dennis arrived home bragging about his gang's exploits. It was a steady menu of what the gang had done, what it was going to do and how great Warwick was.

Dennis' loyalty was only broken when Warwick's family left town. While it was going on, I didn't understand just how important this was to my brother and the other members of Warwick's gang. We girls didn't have the same structures.

In my teens I started going to the football with my father. Those Saturday afternoons helped me gain a better understanding of modern tribalism. Joy and disappointment were shared experiences based on team loyalty. Winning came first and unfair play, dodgy umpiring decisions and dumb luck meant little unless you were on the losing team.

Taking up politics and entering Parliament has completed my education in modern tribalism. It is rampant in the large grey building on North Terrace. Members openly boast of their tribal loyalty.

Ralph Clarke, the Labor Member for Ross Smith, recently placed his commitment to tribalism on the public record:

"We in the Labor Party have great tribal loyalty. We might like to club one another occasionally, but what those in the Liberal Party do not understand is that we enjoy clubbing Liberals more than we enjoy clubbing ourselves - unlike members of the Liberal Party, who enjoy clubbing themselves more than clubbing members of the Labor Party."

Ralph is a little ingenuous here. Inter-party rivalries are particularly vicious within both of the old parties and great pleasure is taken in the fall of a factional rival in either the ALP or Liberals, as he well knows.

Some ALP members couldn't contain their glee at the fall of the once powerful centre-left faction. I also remember reports of a notably jolly gathering in Dale Baker's office the night Dean Brown's premiership was sacrificed to John Olsen's leadership ambitions. I'm reminded of Winston Churchill's quip that in Parliament the Opposition is in front of you, your enemies are behind you.

That said, the main game is inflicting damage on the Opposition. Conversely the most effective means of limiting damage is by presenting a unified front in the face of political adversity. Both parties practise this principle as evidenced in the recent storm as to whether the former Deputy Premier and Minister for Industry, Trade and Tourism, Graham Ingerson, had misled the Parliament.

The brouhaha began when the Opposition lured the Minister into a statement that was ultimately exposed as false. The Opposition in concert with the Independents then forced an historic Privileges Committee to investigate the claims. The Committee was comprised of two Government members, two Opposition members and an Independent, Rory McEwen.

The Committee found unanimously the Minister had misled the House. On the crucial question of whether he had deliberately misled the House the committee split along party lines. The deadlock was broken by Rory McEwen supporting the Opposition members finding that the Minister's words were deliberately misleading.

In the face of a majority finding the Minister had deliberately misled the House the Government's dilemma was acute. Stand by the Minister and sanction dismal parliamentary and ministerial standards or force the Minister to resign and fracture party unity. Tribal unity was preferred over parliamentary standards and a decision was made to stand by the Minister.

Not all Liberal members found this easy. One Liberal member, Steve Condous, told the media "I have my principles, I live by those principles and I value them very much." He then bowed to the tribal bonds by acknowledging he'd vote with the party whether "he believed in it or not".

Another Liberal member, Peter Lewis, actually broke ranks and voted for the no confidence motion in the Minister. At that moment Peter Lewis tore himself free of the chains of tribal loyalty.

To my mind his act was the most significant of any member of the Assembly. Even more so than Rory McEwen's ‘backflip'. I suspect Peter Lewis will pay a heavy price. I respect his commitment to principle.
