Wednesday February 18, 1998

Would the last one leaving, turn off the lights
By John Spoehr

Tom Playford would be Turning in his Grave - but he may have the last laugh.

If John Olsen has is way on ETSA, he will be remembered as the Liberal Premier who shattered what remains of the "Playford" Vision. Half a century after former Premier Tom Playford nationalised the power industry, the return into private hands of the $2.5 billion asset is a possibility.

It was the failure of the privately-run electricity provider, the Adelaide Electric Supply Company, to exploit local reserves of Leigh Creek coal by continuing to import coal from New South Wales which determined Tom Playford to bring private electricity provision under public control.

For Playford, public control over electricity provision was an essential pre-requisite for the industrialisation of South Australia. Under private control, electrification of the state was proceeding slowly and industry needs were under- provided.

Playford regarded electricity provision as a "natural monopoly" where the best interests of the State were served through public control. This would enable him to provide the necessary electricity infrastructure to support the development of the automobile and mining industries in South Australia. It would also ensure that regional South Australia had access to affordable power supplies.

Private providers on the other hand saw the electrification of country areas as unprofitable. Playford and his comtemporaries had a much longer term view on what was profitable and in the broader public interest.

Tom Playford's objective of nationalisation was achieved because, unlike John Olsen, he had the support of the Upper House. Indeed in 1946 there was a rare bi-partisan support for nationalisation of electricity provision, with Conservative and Labor forces combining to achieve the nationalisation objective.

Bi-partisan political support was necessary at every stage. To fund the takeover of the Adelaide Electricity Supply Company, Playford built an alliance with the Federal Labor Government, winning the support of H.V. Evatt and Chifley to use Commonwealth Bank finance.

Without the support of the State Labor Party and the Democrats, privatisation appears to be an impossibility. A long-term lease to an international utility service organisation like United Utilities is probably the real objective.

Aside from the political realities, the sale of ETSA in the context of it being a risk to taxpayers is hardly a sound footing on which to begin a sale process.

The return on the sale of ETSA is rumored at around $1.5 billion. This is around 40 percent below the value of the assets, not taking account the value of intellectual property.

While ETSAs profitablility is likely to decline as it becomes part of the national electricity grid, it’s recent high levels of profitability indicate that it has the capacity to continue to make solid contributions to government revenue. ETSA has consistently paid dividends to the State Government and may have the capacity to continue payments of around $100-200 million per annum.

The sale of ETSA will reap only a once off-injection into the State Budget whereas ETSA’s continued operation under public control will provide up to $1 billion every five years. It makes no economic sense to privatise ETSA under these circumstances.

The privatistion of electricity utilities in Britain and Wales reaped less than half the asset value of the utilities and led to significant increases in both domestic and industrial tariffs. Over the 1989-91 period, domestic electricity tariffs in Britain rose by 25 percent, while tariffs for industry rose by 19 pecent. The proposal to sell ETSA represents a victory for the economic rationalists within the Government. However, there will be many in the Liberal Party who question this ideological zeal and call for a more pragmatic, Playfordian approach.
