Monday, June 1, 1998

Postcard: Poiti'n in Ireland
by our uniquely own foreign correspondent, George Aldridge

Have you ever had to leave a whiz-bang party just as a Kingswood load of your dearest friends spill through the front door? Or had to exit a raucous and rollicking, swinging and swilling pub just as the first pint on Dimms kicks in? Well that's what it was like leaving dear sweet Ireland!

Yep, it may seem odd to start off travel-logging Eire by describing the feeling of leaving it, but I truly confess desperately wanting to teetle off into just one more pub, cakeshop, boozer, bakeshop or whatever and hear that beautiful song of an Irish voice. The Irish are gems - like Colin Hannagan who runs the Skerries Post Office B&B, whose greeting and welcome was that of a long lost buddy who never writes to you but has never changed.

Colin, bless his soul, advised a small wander to the Black Raven Hotel for a lovely meal and a wonderful ale and, later in the evening, my most pleasant discovery to date -a small cheek-full of the Leprechaun's or little green men's secret spirit "POITI'N" pronounced (here goes!) PUTCHEEN.

Well, no wonder this mob sing all right - this be the secret ingredient. No secret really; prior to the 1600's, one in two Irish households distilled their own. I'm not sure what the number is now but the recently-released legal batch I was drinking under company name "Hackler" (said with a gravelly voice like Rev. Paisley), is lovely. Try the clean spirit with friends and you will sing alright, or as recommended by one Irish woman, "Rubbed on tired limbs and muscles does them a power of good". I kid you not, I prefer to do the inner-muscle work.

Now while discussing the great subject of travel and the odd tipple, I must say Ireland has the most extraordinary number of beautiful pubs. It's like looking at the distribution map of the common housefly in Australia. Being a froth-and-bubble, soil-grubble-cold one connoisseur, I noted in one townâs main street over about 300 metres long that every third to fourth establishment was a tavern pub, or free house. This is the honest truth. Now I've heard some oz-trepreurs have embarked upon the new craze of "theme pubs" but I have to advise for the "Irish theme pub" you'll definitely need 200 Irish men and women to go with it. There seems little point otherwise, and I met quite a few willing to fill the positions.

Back to the map, hire car loaded, trusty navigator and junior navigator strapped in, and off through Dublin City maze and medium-level chaos towards the west coast target of Fimerick on Motorway MII. Mind you, the M11 changes from motorway to minor motorway to country lane to main street to small alley- way to motorway to whatever, to who cares-way! It's grand - the sun is shinning, the scenery is soothing and the stone walls whizz past in endless noisy rhythm. Ireland is a soft green velvety gift-wrapped landscape. No great excess in outlandish farm/town architecture - instead, subtle and simple dwellings, with stock appearing to inhabit the major part of the farm motels. Well, heads pivoting and tyres turning we eventually cobble-stoned to Cahir Castle, a major dwelling of the Erectus Irish Species.

Cahir Castle apparently dates back to that time when we came down from the branches and started to chisel away at the very land. The castle is on a rocky island in the river Suir, on the main road from Dublin to Cork. This is real old, real good, and for 13 year-old Viking son Jack, the first real experience of buzz-blast, armour-clad, sword-slashing proportions.

This is home building on a serious level. Mind you, they had some people knocking at the door with very serious business, or actually wanting to knock the door down to discuss the business. But dealing with these mostly unwelcome castle-to-castle salesman was easy. A feature of all (at the time) modern castles was "the murdering hole". From the guide: "the original entrance was from the South. This entrance is guarded by a murdering hole overhead, so arranged that a defender could drop a large weight or some noxious substance substance on the head of any unwelcome intruder."

Imagine your Hickinbotham Home with this feature, "I want you kids to keep away from that murdering hole today. I havenât got an ounce of good hot cooking oil left!" or some clever oz-treprenur with the new-easy fit, Better Homes Murder Hole Kit. "Get rid of all those messy, unwanted and unpleasant household scraps, concrete hot ash, and obsolete white goods, install one now!" There's more - Cahir Castle offers the damp dungeon experience, clambering through the tiny tunnels with armour-clad travel bag and battle reflex camera made me realise that at 187cm tall with a well Guinness'ed waistline, I possibly would have been the large weight they would have dropped down the murdering hole. The first line of defence on ground level, or counter balance for the portcullis, these corridors were small! Wall thickness was more important than corridor width. You could still hear the clatter of armour along these tiny tunnels.

Our Irish jaunt or jig, though excruciatingly short, was pure delight. It left me convinced firstly that I would be happy to cancel my fondue night with the Pope for extended time in Ireland, and secondly even more convinced I would return, soon. Guinness, I was told, does not travel well and should be consumed in hearty quantities in Ireland. Thatâs good enough for me.

Cheers,
George.
