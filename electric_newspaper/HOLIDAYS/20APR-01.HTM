Norway - way north!
By Aussie Kanck

There have been many articles written about the beauty of Norway - this is another of them, but this one is written by a person who emigrated from the country as a little boy and went back for the first time after 45 years to meet relatives, and to be a tourist.

I have seen many sights around the world, but I had not previously encountered a place where on a daily basis I would be left speechless and in awe at what I saw. Around me there was nature's intensity - the sheer size of the granite mountains or the massive scars gouged into the landscape by glaciers in past times.

Some years ago the Norwegian Government had hired artists from all over to create sculptures to symbolise various aspects of Norway's culture and history. These sculptures have been placed in towns and locations right across Norway, and wherever I went, I was sure to get strong comments from the residents about the sculpture in the local area. Two of the sculptures that I still recall are the head that almost magically inverts as you walk around it (near a place called Eggum) and a 15-metre statue of a man in the harbour at Mo-I-Rana. This last statue was most striking as, on the morning I saw it, there was a soft mist on the harbour and the water was glass smooth, creating ghostly reflections right across the harbour.

One of the most popular tourist spots in Oslo is Vigelands Park. In this park are hundreds of carved statues of naked people. The vast majority of the statues are of people entwined together, and in the centre of the park is a massive column made up of naked people. Over the many years these statues have been on display, thousands of people have placed their hands on certain anatomical parts of the statues to be photographed. It is fascinating to see which parts have become the darkest over time as they have been handled by so many people.

At the other end of Norway, in Kirkenes, I had the great fortune of being able to see over 100 works of art from churches in Russia, that were being restored by the new "historical centre" in the city. All of these paintings had, over the centuries, become blackened with soot from fires in the churches and buildings in which they were kept. Most of these precious paintings had been restored to their original brilliance and are shortly due to be returned to the Russians.

One of the natural beauties that is impossible to miss in the "top half" of Norway during July is the midnight sun. To sit on top of a hillside and watch the sun gliding horizontally along the horizon is a experience to remember. I was also intrigued to find that I needed no more than 4 hours sleep each "night" during this visit, and did not end as tired as I thought I might. Another of the natural sights to leave you speechless are the glaciers - especially the Svartisen Glacier, which is the second largest glacier in Norway. This glacier covers some 375 square kilometres, and it can be viewed from many vantage points. If you are very keen it is possible to arrange walks on the glacier with the Norwegian Mountain Touring Association! I was lucky enough to have a trip onto the top of the new Storglomvatn dam, which takes water runoff from the Svartisen glacier for hydroelectricity generation. It's not often one can sit at the top of a massive dam on top of mountains, looking at the truly mind-blowing sight of a glacier and sheets of ice in the dam, all being reflected in the glassy still surface of the water. The day I was there was such a day - and it was warm enough to wear T-shirts.

Now, for those considering a trip to Norway, some basic house-keeping tips. Living costs in Norway are higherr than in Australia. Be prepared for food costing 50% more than here. If you want to drive, then petrol costs can be as high as $2/litre out in the countryside. Bus, train and boat costs are fairly good - especially if you get a Scanrail ticket. This rail ticket will also give you concessions on many boats and buses. There are concessions of 25%-50% for group tickets with most transport companies - and a group is 2 or more people! Accommodation costs will vary considerably - depending on style, time of year and time of week.

Norway may be an expensive place to visit if you don't have relatives or friends to stay with, but if you want to be awe-struck on a daily basis - what a place to go!
