Wednesday, February 4, 1998

Why Thai?
By Amanda Lynch

We did not originally intend to go to Thailand, but the signposts and the baht all pointed in that direction and in the end it was inevitable.

What really sealed it was a conversation with a returned traveller. Thailand was different, it was exotic, I was told. It was more than a country of temples, the Pat Pong Road, a line of Australians on death row, and delicious aromatic food. "Trust me," I was told, "there are only a few simple rules to remember in Thailand."

I have summarised them for other travellers:

1. Remember that Thailand has never been colonised by a foreign power. The people are very proud of their culture and religion. Don't point your feet at anyone, particularly while carelessly crossing your legs. That is an insult. Treat the head as sacred, it is closest to the God or Buddha, so never pat a child on the head.

2. Don't insult the King, Queen or the Buddha. They are venerated by the Thai people. Coming from a country where dinner parties die unless religion, Charles and Camilla, and our freedom from imperialist apron strings are chewed over, I wondered what people chatted about. We soon found out they liked gossip as much as everyon - just look at the features in the Thai English-language newspapers.

3. Don't get angry. Defuse a situation by smiling. If you show anger you have lost face and are embarrassed.

4. Use your spoon to eat. Push the food onto it with your fork. Eating with the fork itself is akin to us putting a knife in our mouths. Leave some food on your plate. Try to eat a little rice first, to show respect for what is the staple of life in Thailand.

All this advice was intriguing. It explained something about a country of diversity and fascination . Thailand is a deep stream I from which I could not drink, but rather gazed into with Australian eyes. Besides, you can't trust the water in Thailand.

And so we found ourselves at The Oriental Hotel in Bangkok. In the Authors' Lounge writers such as Somerset Maugham and Joseph Conrad penned their experiences of Asia. (Maugham also used Raffles Hotel as a base.) They observed and wrote of life from their colonist perspectives - of the bright fish that dazzled and jumped above the surface, as quick as mercury. They knew of the darkness too, particularly Conrad, but it was frequently unfathomable and deeply mysterious.

But this was a holiday of only two weeks, so we too chased the colourful phantasmagoric photos of our dreams. We saw chained monkeys in Koh Samui performing tricks for tourists and wondered, where were the Abolitionists? We saw elephants playing soccer and picking up lines of beer bottles by their trunks when there are only an estimated 500 wild elephants left in Thailand. We saw a man who earned his living by having his photo taken while being bitten by centipedes and with his face covered with scorpions. We saw destitution and poverty right next to opulent gold temples.

We also saw heart-wrenchingly beautiful night skies stained with scarlet and gold leaf, experienced kindness towards (us) clumsy foreigners who could not speak the language, we ate wonderful seafood - huge white prawns and small but delicately flavoured lobsters, and had a massage on a marble slab overlooking the beach.

The quirkiness was probably what finally caught us in Thailand's magical net. The James Bond Island off Phuket, where the Man with the Golden Gun was filmed, has developed a whole tourist industry. The rocks on Koh Samui, carved by a bizarre act of nature to resemble male and female genitalia. This is a major tourist attraction and one which transcends cultures and shock-value. The bars on Koh Samui, where you can visit a shrine to Jim Morrison at the Doors' Pub, worship the Blues at the Blues Brothers, or get into Reggae-man at the Reggae Bar. The way to the nightlife is via a rickety bridge which gets worse as the night wears on. Not even the cockroach who took a liking to our nibbles dampened proceedings. The Buddhist monk who died while praying is preserved in a glass case in a temple with his sensible spectacles still on is worth a visit and tourists are encouraged to take photos. We had no doubt where his soul went.

If you too are tempted to go, here are some final tips...

5. Visit the island of Samui. The best way to get around the island is by renting a small motor-bike. Visit the three spectacular waterfalls. Stay at the Imperial Boathouse in Samui - it is fabulous - and eat at Charlie Chaplins opposite the hotel. The staff own the motorbike shop so you can kill two birds with one stone, or one spoon. And a huge meal is only around $5 each, though steer clear of wine - it is very expensive.

6. Bangkok is overpoweringly seductive: the silks, the restaurants, the bargains such as fake polo t-shirts for $5 and the beauty of the river. Go on a river cruise and a one-day trip to Ayuthaya, Bangkok's ancient capital is well worthwhile. There are examples of ancient ruins and temples like the giant bronze statues of Wat Chiya Mongkoland Wat Panangcherng.

7. Have a massage on a beach at sunset. It may or may not change your life. Thailand will let you leave but it won't easily let you go.

(Amanda visited Bangkok and the islands of Phuket and Koh Samui)
