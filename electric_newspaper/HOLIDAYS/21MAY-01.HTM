Thursday, May 21, 1998

We're all going on a houseboat holiday
by John Schumann

The bride wasn't keen.

First of all, it was a succession of dinner party conversations about the sorts of holidays you could take without either renewing your passport or driving for days. Every one of our friends had been on a houseboat holiday and every single one of them had returned raving. That was hardly a good start as we're the sort of people who bitterly resist doing whatever is fashionable amongst our friends. We haven't even seen "The Piano" yet.

Then it was the safety aspect. "Children drown in the river," she observed every time I brought the subject up. "Yes," I thought to myself, "but it does not follow necessarily that ours will." I did not venture this aloud.

"And it might be a holiday for you but I'll be cooking and cleaning and watching the children. Anyway, I'm not sure I want to be cooped up on a houseboat with other people for a week or more with no hope of getting off until we get back to wherever we started from."

So it was with some apprehension I heaved the first box of provisions onto the boat moored alongside ten or twelve of its siblings at the Liba-Liba Houseboat base just outside of Renmark.

How we came to book a Liba-Liba houseboat was quite another story. I'd researched the houseboat thing quite thoroughly, I thought, in the South Australian Tourism Commission's Shorts book and the Yellow Pages among other places. There were some houseboats available for hire which seemed to be the inland maritime equivalent of a four bedroom suite at the Hyatt. Both the bride and I were drawn somewhat in that direction. Dad, on the other hand, had hired Liba-Liba houseboats before and he was persuasive.

"Son," he said (he always calls me ‘son' when serious advice is about to be dispensed), "the Liba-Libas have paddle-wheels on the back - they're much quieter - also much cheaper to run. And the Murray is sensational upstream from Renmark. Lots of places to moor and the cliffs are fantastic." Actually, the cliffs are awesome - but awesome is not a word which springs easily to the lips of my 70 year-old father.

So it was one of those warm-bordering-on-hot river days under a steel-grey sky when we manoeuvred our houseboat out of the berth, along the creek and into the Murray River proper. We'd just had a 30 minute crash-course in river navigation, elementary chart reading, maritime courtesy, tying-up and radio operation. We were also vaguely acquainted with the pumping system, the gas system, the generator, the dunnies and the lighting system. Quite frankly, for the first five minutes or so, the complete lassitude of a resort island holiday was looking pretty good.

Moments later we chugged into the main channel, lined up on right side of the river and headed upstream to Whirlpool Corner. The mighty Murray cast its spell and to this day the spell remains unbroken.

The Murray River is incredible. 2,820 kilometres miles long, it rushes out of the high country and eases itself down to the sea with the confidence that comes with maturity and power. Even after 200 years of abuse by white man, you can't help the feeling that human beings are just carpetbaggers in the presence of royalty. The Murray River irrigates a goodly part of Australia one way or another and whatever else you might say, one thing is true. The Murray dies and we die. It's that simple.

Upstream from Renmark though, the Murray looks in pretty good shape. And we were starting to get in pretty good shape ourselves. The expansive front deck of our houseboat was an entertaining area complete with outdoor furniture, a gas barbecue and a broad sunshade. Everything had been thought of - including a sliding window in front and at the side of the wheel, which was inside the cabin on the starboard side. This meant that whoever was "on watch" could join the conversation. The sturdy safety-rail which ran around the entire boat convinced the bride that she could relax. The male members of the crew needed no convincing at all. "Drinks anyone?"

The Liba-Liba navigation system consists of series of sequentially numbered large signs attached to trees at regular intervals along the banks. These numbers correspond to numbers on the simple but effective chart provided by the Liba-Liba people. We took a pair of binoculars (most important!) and the kids literally fought with each other to spot and report the numbers thereby plotting our passage.

The navigation system gave everyone on board a way of really discovering the stretch of Murray we had chosen to traverse. Between Renmark and where we (very reluctantly) turned back, we steamed past an old Japanese POW camp, a sunken barge from the river days of old, an aboriginal canoe tree and a wildlife reserve. Yawning creek mouths beckoned but our chart was very firm on the subject of creeks. These were navigable only in the aluminium dinghy tied to the rails on the back deck.

The houseboat was a dream to handle. I had thought, in my characteristically pessimistic fashion, that steering would become a chore. Not so. The gentle humming of the motor and swish-swish of the paddle-wheel aids contemplation and reflection. You do need to concentrate a little but it's the sort of concentration that's inherently relaxing. Like building a model. In any case, there is always a queue of people wanting a turn. I came to quite value my "watches" as times when I could lose myself in my thoughts. It was easy -and a lot of fun - to imagine life on a Murray River paddle-steamer at the turn of the century.

Dad was right about the paddle-wheels. We were overtaken, quite often, by many of the more modern houseboats powered by twin outboards. You could hear them coming for miles. But ours was a gentler noise, much more in harmony with the river and the life that teemed in and around it.

Take a book on Australian native birds. We didn't and we regretted it. The Murray's abundant bird life is source of endless fascination and the most frequently asked question during our trip was "What sort of bird is that?" In the face of seemingly endless variety our ornithological knowledge, such as it was, wilted.. A family of tiny swallows had nested underneath our boat and we became quite protective of them as the days meandered on - mainly, I think, because we all knew what sort of birds they were. And above, squadrons of pelicans banked and turned in the pale pink sunsets. We could pick pelicans. We'd seen "Storm Boy".

The next most frequently asked question was "Cold beer, white or red?" And, oh, what a joy to be in South Australia! We had thoughtfully stocked up on some crisp little whites for the afternoon, some pugnacious reds for our many barbecues and a range of beers to cleanse the palate as required. All South Australian produce and all of a standard the rest of the world can only dream about! We spent most of our early afternoons sitting on the front deck, nursing a cold beer or a wine, chatting, listening to some of our favourite tapes, nibbling on savouries and all the while chugging away in the middle of a picture-perfect watercolour.

Our practice was to be tied up to the bank by about 3.00pm. All along the river there are any number of perfect spots at which to moor and the procedure is quite simple. Once you have identified your spot and you're sure there's enough water underneath the boat, you nose the houseboat very, very gently into the bank. You tie up to the trees (which are a necessary feature of the perfect mooring spot), lower the gangplank and shut down the motor. You can then explore the bush, or prepare for a night-time barbecue on the bank.

Dusk throws a blanket of stillness over the Murray. It is a time when the river-world takes on deep shades of blue and brown. The slow-moving river becomes a mirror to the banks and the sky and the brilliant sheen is broken only by the soft plop of a jumping fish or a puff of evening breeze scuttling across the water.

There is something very consoling about a standing around a fire on the bank at night with your comfortable houseboat only metres away. The kids love a fire too. But please, dig a hole for your fire, don't make it too big, don't chop down trees for firewood and bury your fire thoroughly and take your rubbish with you before you move on. Too often houseboaters leave the remains of their campfires on the bank for others to clean up. Unless this practice changes, it won't be too long before the simple pleasure of a fire on the bank at night is denied everyone on account of the thoughtlessness of a few. Our Liba-Liba houseboat had a shovel on board. I noted that with approval.

I can't begin to describe the river. There's just too much. There are ancient forests of drowned eucalypts, standing in patient silence until the end of time. There are towering cliffs, whose ochre-like colours vacillate in the ever-changing light. There are kilometres of reeds and trees weeping gently into the river. And above there's the sky, changing a thousand times a day. There's no end to the beauty and there's no point in me talking about it. You simply must go yourself.

The thing is that our houseboat holiday was tremendous fun and tremendously relaxing at the same time. Cooking was very much part of the fun and there was very little on board that needed more than a wipe with a sponge every now and then. The bride loved the houseboat holiday as much as I did and remarkably, both kids can't wait for the next one.

This story is about a Liba-Liba Houseboat holiday but there are many, many equally fine houseboat operators. It's really down to what you want. Look for the Houseboat Hirers Association logo if you're unsure.

Take a book on Australian birds, a pair of binoculars, comfortable clothes, some insect repellent and some 15+ sunscreen lotion. A shrimp-net and a couple of hand-lines will entertain the kids for hours. Make sure the people you go with are good friends. As large and comfortable as our houseboat was, it would have been hell-on-water with the wrong people. We were lucky.

And yes, we went upstream from Renmark but there are many, many other beautiful stretches of the Murray River to explore. It's 2,820 kilometres long. Enjoy.
