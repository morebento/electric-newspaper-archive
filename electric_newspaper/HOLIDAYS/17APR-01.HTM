Port Adelaide - the far side
story: Jennifer Jones
photo-essay: Tania Liemareff

A sparkling blue day. Auto-suggest - drive. Drive where? Old Port Road,
15 minutes from the city.

Port Road was once planned to be the route for a canal; it is now one of Adelaide's widest roads. Century year-old palms and the tallest flagpole in South Australia loom large from water-guzzling green lawns on which no one ever sits or uses, dividing the dual carriageway and random blocks of deserted commercial conglomerates, pockets of abandoned narrow shops spilling tears of rusted iron and shards of glass onto weedy footpaths.

End of the line, swing right into the Port. The antique cantilever bridge in the distance on the left. Backdrop of eyeblinkingly beautiful architecture on both sides of the streets, not the sporadic remnants of the era we see in Adelaide city’s square mile.

We already know about the new restaurants, the maritime, historic military vehicles, railway museums, the Sunday markets sites, the theatre, nice galleries. But not today. Something beckons, indefinable, suggested by the expanse of multi-textural sky and wharves and objects upon them. There’s a suggestion of mystery here and who amongst us doesn’t enjoy a good unravelling?

Drive on, a few minutes past the hub and its gentle hustle and bustle, straight through the next available gap in suddenly obvious and unfriendly cyclone wire fencing, right past the signs which say "No entrance to unauthorised personnel and vehicles". Aaaahhh , now this looks interesting. What would you imagine 26 metre highsilver metal objects closely resembling giant dairy cans with yellow nylon webbing across their mouths might be used for?

Crawling pace past a huge rust-brown barge, larger than any I ever saw gracing Amsterdam canals. Flat bow that could be used for a double helicopter pad is laden with suburban accoutrement, toys, bicycles and laundry happily flapping in clean brisk breeze. A man steps outside. We wave and smile.

Attracted by the sight of a crow’s nest atop a small metal sailing ship, modelled on the Endeavour and moored on the opposite side of this little inlet, we pass a lone fisherman whose dark Middle Eastern looks and silhouette contrast amazingly against the brightness of the white ship, almost to suggest he’s been shadow painted in black on the hull. Only his slow movement belies the mirage. Closer now, a sign is visible: "Half-share for sale".

Further along, a Dutch ship’s bowels offer up 12 metre long, 300 cm wide red plastic plugged metal straws to a crane’s webbed talon. Below, two dock workers, sporting impressive 50 centimetre wide biceps, deal with the poles as they splatter noisily onto the wharf. Then, deftly wiping aside web and talon, they send them back down for the next haul. One metre wide forklifts cart off their very precariously hitched wide loads to a more distant dock where we will follow.

But not for long as there’s now an urgency to reach the next promontory where tantalising hills of twisted giant ropes, each one wider even than the navvies’ biceps, must, I think, represent a very large proportion of hemp crops grown and processed long ago. Now they look like the washed-up and dried out eviscera of some extinct sea monster. The death of any really large animal holds at least a little fascination, if not a melancholic sense of loss of something far greater than we mere humans.

Perhaps spurred by this last thought, it’s now time to search for refreshment. When the red-headed, almost toothless attendant of the drive-in bottle department of a lonely hotel hidden from anything resembling a beaten track is asked what sort of sparkling burgundy might be had, the short answer is none. "Thparkling burgundy? We’ve only got burgundy in a cathk! Eh, Thid, ‘ave we got any cathkth of that burgundy left?" Enquire, "What about champagne?" "We’ve only got two thort-th - Carrington Bluth and Theaview." Hold the champagne. No, well, after all, we’re only here for a short while. Remember the eviscera!

A little later we’re back in a more inhabited area and, look! There’s a wingless Canberra bomber in that yard over there! My first visit to the Historical Aviation Museum. Here you might be fortunate enough to meet a really good, blue-and sharp-eyed white-haired bloke, part of the dedicated volunteer group who keep the place going with uncountable hours on missions of air wreckage retrieval, restoration, display, museum attendance and so on. This man is a gem of helpfulness, full of stories and knowledge of his first passion, aircraft. Here you can actually board a troop of its time; marvel at and calculate with awe how many mini-bombs there are inside a mean looking cluster bomb; wonder at the destructive power of black, incredibly thin 3 metre tall rockets and generally have an awesome time.

But these are distractions. Avanti! Onwards! Back to meandering through the far side. Didn’t really come for structured, especially military, activity although it was pleasant enough.

After several more minutes of peering at the vast lanes of dull grey corrugated iron warehouse walls, I remember now that, many years before, I travelled away from even less developed tourist trails to find examples of once elegant, intriguing buildings. Vandalised, burnt out and otherwise gutted remains left no hint or glory of their former purposes. Some, however, contained twisted pieces of metal, rubble and corroded antique machinery suggesting heavy manufacture.

Well, blow me down! Here, right in front of me now is one of the very best of them! Not too badly burned and still sturdy of mortar, brick and bluestone. On closer inspection, old masonry, rubble and gnarled metal have vanished, new pale flooring divides the lower and upper stories, shiny new roofing appears. Approval of the retention of the slim cast metal art-nouveau columns now assist to support the floors. Suddenly, on cue, the workmen arrive to answer my question before I had even asked them (how did they do that?) Yes, new owners were renovating to live here themselves, amongst an expanse of two- and three-storey iron warehouses set in fenced off bitumen, strewn amid acres of weed covered stony soil. Nice quiet neighbourhood for the most part.

Now I am satisfied with hope for the regeneration of one of the most fascinating of our earliest settled areas. Without the strangulation of my current mortgage, I could be easily tempted to buy into this and be one of the first of a new generation of Old Port settlers.

In the span of a few kilometres and a couple of hours of fossicking in the almost deserted back blocks of the Port River wharves, it’s quite possible to stumble upon more provocative images than one could experience in a normal week of furious activity in the city of Adelaide, Festival times excluded of course.

In anticipation, I apologise to the ‘wharfies’ for any incorrect estimations of biceps, poles and canisters.

Next time, I’m going to leave the city earlier so I can have a cup of tea with them at the stevadore’s deli on the wharf, if they’ll let me.
