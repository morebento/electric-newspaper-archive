Tuesday, February 3, 1998

London : Exceptional, exhilarating... and expensive !
By Ray Thomas

The hands are out all over London! Or, so it seems to an unsuspecting tourist.

Not merely content to charge what seem to be outrageous prices for almost everything you purchase in this exhilarating, expensive city, the impression is quickly formed that they're after every pound and penny that you've converted from your hard-earned Australian dollars.

It begins very soon after your Qantas flight touches down at that ungodly hour of 5 a.m. At around $7 each to the heart of London, the underground beckons! But beware !

After the exhausting 22-hour flight from the peace and quiet of placid Adelaide, it can be more than a little daunting to battle with early morning commuters as you defend your bulging cases and bursting backpacks while clutching warily onto your prized duty-free.

An alternative might be a London cab. But that will put a dent of around $60 into your first day's budget.

Worth considering is booking a mini cab before leaving Australia -- you will still part with more than $40 for the journey. The current rate is UK/pound 18.50 and when you hand the driver a 20-pound note at the end of the journey you can be sure that he won't be able to find any change!

With an air of reluctant inevitability, you relent. You sense the start of a smirk on the cab driver's face, but he's long gone before you can protest.

It's still only 7 a.m. The B & B you've booked yourself into doesn't want to know you before midday, so after depositing your luggage in the foyer it's time for breakfast.

But, being early on a Sunday morning, nothing's open except McDonalds. Prices seem almost as high in pounds are they are in Australia in dollars!

Our budget for London was $250 a day for two. It cost us close to $350 and that included staying at a B & B in Kensington ($116 a night), two nights at the theatre and visiting the usual tourist attractions.

Some experiences, though, are worth every penny.

The hour or so you'll spend inside Buckingham Palace for $20 is a steal! The sumptuous splendour of Nash's architecture and decoration defies description.

At the other end of the scale, both aesthetically and fiscally, is a two-hour journey up the Thames to delightful Richmond. You will travel on a boat that's seen better days and sit (if you're lucky enough to get a seat) on uncomfortably sagging plastic chairs.

For the experience you will hand over $30.

And if you think that the somewhat inadequate commentary along the way from the captain is an unexpected bonus, think again. He eventually passes around the hat so that you can "show your appreciation in the usual way."

Eating out in London can also cut a swathe through the average Australian's budget. A 3-course meal at Simpsons-on-the Strand (listed as moderate) costs $65 a head plus wine!

But, there are now cheaper Italian eateries everywhere. Nevertheless, that bowl of pasta which might cost $7 in Adelaide is closer to $18 in London.

Travelling is a thirsty business and a visit to Safeway or Tesco at the start of each day for a large bottle of spring water is a worthwhile investment. It makes it possible to avoid those ready to rip you off at tourist attractions where even the smallest bottles of water can cost anything up to $5!

And in every snack bar, coffee shop or patisserie, the saucer sits on the counter suggestively urging you to leave a bob or two. But bargains do exist. Clothing is decidedly cheaper than at home,

chocolate bars also cost less and Coca Cola from the supermarket is good value.

But wearing a new outfit while munching a Mars bar and downing a coke is only one fulfilling holiday experience.

There are two rip-offs (one of them, ironically Australian) that you can do something about...

When your travel agent urges you to book your hire car before leaving home, do it! A three-day rental of a small car (when booked in London) will set you back around $250 - substantially more if you want to drop if off at a different location from where you collect it! Pre-booked before you leave home will bring it down to around $140.

One of the more insidious rip-offs involves theatre tickets. Going to the theatre in London is a special and, often, inexpensive experience. The half-price ticket booth in Leicester Square is particularly worth locating.

But the pre-booking of theatrical attractions from Australia can be a trap.

A ticket for a premium seat at a blockbuster musical (the currently running Martin Guerre is a good example) costs $75 at the theatre box office. Purchased via the Australian agent before leaving home, the very same tickets are priced at $120.

In these days of credit card bookings and cheap international phone calls, an astute Australian traveller can easily book by phone before leaving home, saving as much as $90 on a pair of seats.
