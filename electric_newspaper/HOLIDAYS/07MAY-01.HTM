Monday, April 27, 1998

A Hawkers view of London (Part II)
by George Aldridge in the Mother Country

Our very own foreign correspondent, George Aldridge, has just been to the greatest icons of shopping in the known universe - Harrods and the Victoria and Albert Museum.

Well readers, in my last euro-mail in the saga of Mitre 11 Man of the North and Flinders region, I fleetingly farewelled with mini-mention of the great Museum of Harrods, superstore, supersecurity and superprice. How nice can a store be?

Mind you, if you’re a teenager unaccompanied by adult... not real nice; you won’t get in! Apart from the front door pectoral boys this place is worth the wander even for just a breath of the perfumed air. And of course it’s worth it for a sight of the magic cave masticators Mecca, the food hall! Never before have I set eyes on food that looks so good. This is the tattoo of taste - miniature wild strawberries from France, oysters costing one pound each from some unmentioned, unpolluted ocean. Stirring grin from Smoky Bay SA? Harrods is opulent, silver service cash registering at its best. Just look at the laugh lines around your wallet when you go into Harrods and then when you come out! So do this tour, shopaholics. Use the warm washcloth’s at the end of the elevator handrails and meet the heritage-listed shop assistants.

This seems an appropriate time to mention the Brits' unrivalled position as Top Shoppers of Planet Earth in the field of antiquities. The Victoria and Albert Museum is testimony to this. The great pioneering shoppers of the U.K. did some serious homework over the past few hundred years. The Victoria and Albert is a gob-smacking reminder of how rich and diverse the cultures and civilisations evolving around us have been. These are truly wonderful collections, from the unbearably and insanely miniature pieces of metal jewellery and religious sculpture to the towering silo-sized plaster cast works, 50 feet columns, immense doorways and arches - and not to mention Michaelangelo’s David. Yep, no queues folks - here he is in all his splendour. Egad, a fake! Nevertheless still stunning. But hell the V.A. is good. Take the swag, the picnic hamper and Deep Heat, drop the Driz-a-Bone at the cloak room (I love these places), and set off on an exhilarating journey from year dot to the present day. It will take days.

I’m coming back to this one, but for now I've grabbed a copy of the Irish Post, the voice of the Irish in Britain, checked the travel section and it's off to Guinness country for us.

George
