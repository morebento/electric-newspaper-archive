Monday, April 27, 1998

A Hawkers view of London
by George Aldridge in the Mother Country

Story so far: The Electric Newspaper's George Aldridge has swapped the scenic delights of Hawker, in the Flinders Ranges, for the sights and sounds of London, in the Mother Country. Now read on... on...

Cor'Blimey! In this tiny street off Kings Road, Parsons Green SW6 (SW stands for sort'f wherever), lives the entire population of the Flinders Rangers Region. A parking space in this street as valuable as a corporate box at Wimbledon and just as hard to obtain.

Now parking aside, it's the actual movement of traffic that fascinates me.

A spin through the London peak hour with a local Londoner late for an appointment is far more exciting than rubber band ankle-jumping from the top paddock windmill, the city being potentially the largest outdoor driving school for grand prix F1 drivers.

And they're good.

As chaotic as it appears, it does run relatively smoothly.

With large rubber buffers around the entire vehicle a sensible accessory.

London is immense, awesome, a throbbing, pulsating, clunking, clattering city. On the surface, overhead and underground, it resembles a constantly whirring and heaving Heath Robinson invention. Those seeking to solve the puzzle of perpetual motion would be well advised to visit this city.

Now for any travelling Jack and Jillaroos or keen Oz bag-handler eager to bounce the Blundstones along the tarmac at Heathrow I'm going to offer some sound travel advice over the next few weeks and point out some lovely little scenic sights from the window of your P.C.

A great piece of advice for any terror stricken arrival in a kangaroo rib corset is to get into the swing of using the underground.

The underground - it's brilliant, it works, it doesn't stink down there and for a cartoonist suffering culture shock it's easy to follow!

Spin around under London's floorboards for a few days and you'll see a rail system that runs with consummate ease. There's no hardening of the arteries down here.

For those going topside (you've got to come up eventually), intent on dodging the Double Decker's and securing a place in one of London's famous queue's outside an even more famous point of interest, please give Madame Tussauds a whirl.

Take it from a devoted queue hater, this was truly worth it! This is some bizarre show, extraordinary, weird and wonderful in wax.

It appears crowded once you first enter, but don't worry! You soon realise that half the crowd is made out of wax and they're lovely people. You're right in amongst them, from Mel Gibson to Michael Jackson (Jacko looks like he's made of wax, funny that!)

We even stumbled across John Howard, I immediately recommended to an attendant that they provide some sort of box for the M.P. and tidy up his hair-do where small children have been patting on the head.

And stand perfectly still for a minute or so whilst someone scans intensely for your title - great fun.

Another very classy establishment boasting moderately small queues but magnificent exhibitions is the National History Museum.

On a similar scale to Britain's Houses of Parliament (they have more Dinosaurs!) the building itself is a splendid piece of sculpture and being the Mecca to museologists holds some absolute treasures, wall to wall Plesiosaurs, sensational animal sculptures and is an absolute buzz factory for children - take two days and two pairs of shoes.

Museums proliferate this country and one I will mention in the next log is possibly the one covered by the most rigorous security system, is the most difficult to get admission to and is very expensive - but what do you expect from Harrods!

Till then.
George.
