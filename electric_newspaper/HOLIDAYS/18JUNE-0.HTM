Thursday, June 18, 1998

London to a brick
by Brenda Kuhr *

Before we went to London recently a friend sent us seven pages of advertisements about what's on in London theatre. Many of these ads were the size of the "Wanted to Buy" notices in our newspapers. Seven pages filled with delight.

Ah, the challenge of choice!

We felt as though they have the Festival of Arts happening every day.

We booked a couple of things before we left and left the others until our arrival.

The first thing we saw was 'Oh What A Lovely War', performed by the National Theatre in a big top.

This play has not been performed professionally for years, since the copyright owner, Joan Littlewood, has refused any but amateur requests.

I understand her proviso this time with the National Theatre was that the play could not be performed in a theatre - hence the circus tent. This was not however an alien environment to the Pierrot, music hall, black comedy style of the piece.

I've come away saying that this was the best production out of the five seen during our time in London.

It is a lively, colourful and moving performance, which is presented by a troupe of actors/singer/dancers working as an ensemble.

The horrors of the First World War were graphically described in the performance, leaving me chilled and convinced that these messages must be repeated.

I will never forget the Sergeant Major who had us all in stitches as he yelled at his troops in gibberish for an entire scene. Or the meeting of British and German soldiers in No-man's Land at the front on Christmas Eve where the nightmare of 'man's inhumanity to man' is transcended in a moment of simple cheer.

This production is currently touring England and is one not to miss.

Take a walk on the Bankside of the Thames and visit the new Globe Theatre.

We went to see Shakespeare's 'As You Like It' there, which is just the sort of thing you ought to do in London. Not only will you see a bit of the Bard, but do so in a theatre, which has been lovingly created in the style of theatres in Shakespeare's time. There are no nails, and no lighting. We went to the 4pm showing which was performed in natural light under the open roof of this circular theatre. Patrons can stand for 5 pounds or be seated in boxes.

The great test of performing Shakespeare, I believe, is to ensure that everyone effortlessly understands what is said. The current cast of 'As You Like It' certainly managed this. I have been surprised to learn that Anastasia Hille who played Rosalind has recently been chosen to perform in a wide range of other leading roles. For me, she was at times egotistically aware of playing to a large audience and her own personality rather than Rosalind's coloured her performance. She missed the depth of the role. Others in the cast were marvellous, particularly Paul Hilton as Orlando and John McEnery as Jaques who were at all times captivating to be with.

The 'I'll never forget' in this performance was the theatre itself. Sam Wanamaker's vision to build a truly Shakespearian theatre is an inspiring achievement and I'd certainly go back for more.

We saw the play 'Art' with Richard Giffiths (of 'Pie in he Sky' fame) at the Wyndham Theatre.

The production magically created three separate living environments by changing only the lighting on he stage. Oh, and of course one painting.

Three men make up the cast of friends with the script focussed on the dilemmas caused when one of them buys a painting for two thousand pounds. The canvas is totally white. Is this art? The script is tight and funny and very popular among current actors. The cast is completely changed every few months and I'm told they're lining up to have a role in this one.

The author, Yasmina Reza, won Best Comedy of the Year for the script and when she stood to receive the award shocked everyone by saying that she was very surprised to be receiving it because she had not written a comedy.

'Art' nevertheless is very funny and well worth seeing. Lots of others think so too, so you will need to book early for this one.

Richard Griffiths has great delivery skills and certainly carried off the role believably. At times I found it uncomfortable to watch such a large man move around the stage. Will he really fit in that chair? Of course he can, but as an audience member I found that his size limited his ease of movement for the part and was a distraction which drew me away from my suspended disbelief.

Nevertheless I'd love to see another cast work with this script.

Go out and practice your reverse parking, take up a study of London's yellow pages rather than go to "Cleansed". This is definitely a no-go area.

The play is on at the Royal Court which has a policy of supporting new writers. This can sometimes bring brilliance to light. I understand that Peter Gill's 'Small Change' was discovered at the Royal Court.

'Cleansed' is best left alone. Whatever happened in the minds of the board members when this passed as worthy of performance?

Does anyone really want to watch someone having their tongue cut out, then their hands and feet removed? Maybe there are times for these ideas to be shown in the theatre. Not when they are so badly done though and not when they are performed without any apparent meaning.

This play is incomprehensible. A doctor drugs his patients and gets off on removing their body parts. In the final moments a penis is transplanted onto a woman who's bloody T-shirt is meant to indicate that the doctor has removed her breasts. Why these things happened and what meaning it has for anyone escaped us all.

I left this performance wondering why so much money was spent on the play. The stage was alive with moving parts throughout the piece and the technical costs must have set the Royal Court back more than a bit.

Well, save your money and don't go.

Three Pinter plays were on at the Donmar Theatre. The critics were raving about one: 'A Kind of Alaska' which turned out to be my least favoured of the three. Pinter himself is performing in 'The Collection' and there's no doubt that this man knows how to 'do' Pinter. Both Douglas Hodge and Lia Williams performed with ease. The plays themselves were somewhat dated. These are not the great Pinter scripts like 'The Homecoming', 'The Birthday Party' or 'The Dumb Waiter' whose style can stand the test of time. Nevertheless the performance by those named made this a worthwhile evening.

So the smorgasbord is over for now. If you're heading off, do check out The Globe and track down 'Oh What A Lovely War' which the T-shirt says is being performed at locations all over England.

* Brenda Kuhr is a theatre goer and critic, and collector of buttons, as well as a semi-professional op. shop advisor.
