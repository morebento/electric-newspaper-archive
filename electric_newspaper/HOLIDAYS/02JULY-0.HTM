Thursday, July 2, 1998

The lost fauna of Adelaide

an exhibition at the South Australian Museum
by Alice Carey

What a surprise it would be to discover the scratching noise in the roof was a sleek little spotted quoll curiously exploring your house. Or to find the family bilby proudly emerging from the pantry with a mouthful of mouse. This wildlife which could have been part of our lives is now extinct in South Australia.

The SA museum's 'Lost Fauna of Adelaide' exhibition is a shocking reminder of how disruptive European settlement really has been.

Extensive clearing of land for agriculture and housing, water pollution , hunting and trapping for fur trade and sport, all contributed to the extinguishment of a diverse range of animals and insects from the Adelaide plains. The 'Lost Fauna of Adelaide' exhibition highlights the lifestyle and actions of the European settlers who in forging the city of Adelaide, unknowingly denied future South Australians unique and beautiful Australian wildlife.

Photographs of wool-washing on the banks of the Torrens suggest the platypus may have found their home no longer palatable. A lush platypus fur rug suggests even less palatable reasons to abide in the Torrens and Onkaparinga rivers.

The beautiful spotted quoll was all but stuffed when its fur was desired by traders and its chicken-killing antics irritated the new Adelaideans.

As the grasslands disappeared the large bustard, or 'wild turkey', was soon on the run to more southern regions and the pygmy bluetongue lizard hot-tailed it for Burra and less cosmopolitan areas.

In the mid-nineteenth century the bilby, or 'pinkie', was numerous in the north-west parklands and at Thebarton, but the activities in the parklands were not simply netball, soccer and jogging. Cattle and sheep markets, snake farming, limestone quarrying, firewood collection, circus performances and dumping of rubbish, offal and human excrement, contributed to the bilbies hasty retreat to a cleaner, green state.

The Adelaide plains, once known to the Kaurna Aborigines as 'Tandanya, the place of the red kangaroo' has changed beyond recognition in the brief span of 162 years. In only 30 years species such as the tammar wallabies became extinct on mainland South Australia.

The fragility of nature is encapsulated in the 'Lost Fauna of Adelaide' exhibition. Taxidermied animals of the past era resound a warning of the possibilities. The skins of feral animals point to their contributing role as exterminators. Photographs and old newspaper articles reflect the ignorance of humans past actions which too easily could be translated to today's prima facie environmentalism.

"This exhibition will help us appreciate the animals and plants that remain," said Dr Mark Hutchinson, exhibition curator.

The SA museum hopes the exhibition will encourage people to actively preserve the native habitat and wildlife still present and to support conservation programs striving to protect endangered species.

Exhibition: Showing until December 1998, Level 2, S.A. Museum, North Terrace, Adelaide.
