Monday April 20, 1998

Adelaide flicks: David Bradley's guide to films now showing

THE BORROWERS (G) - An enormous John Goodman suffers more bizarre and outrageous punishment in The Borrowers than Wile E. Coyote ever did, and it's the presence of Goodman that really gives this intriguingly fanciful kids' movie a kick. As odiously swank real estate swindler Ocious Potter, Goodman tricks his clients, the Lenders, out of their Great-Aunt's house, and incurs the wrath of "The Borrowers", a morally-upright family of four-inch high eccentrics recently befriended by the young Pete Lender (Bradley Pierce), leading to a catalogue of cruelties directed at the big, bad "human bean".

With striking special effects (a melding of stop-motion and computer-generation), an unexpectedly surreal look straight out of Terry Gilliam's Brazil, likeable performances by "The Borrowers" (including Jim Broadbent as Dad "Pod Clock"), and juicy guest spots for Hugh Laurie and Ruby Wax, The Borrowers is that rarity: a film directed at the rugrats that adults will enjoy hugely, jam-packed with off-colour humour, flavoursome weirdness and Goodman violently thwarted at every turn. Seriously - I'm not having a lend of you. ***1/2

DECONSTRUCTING HARRY (M) - Woody Allen's harshest movie since 1992's Husbands And Wives is this phantasmagoria upon his life with his acclaimed author "Harry Block" suffering a writer's-block-fuelled semi-breakdown, as past, present and fiction blur. His ex-wife's sister (Judy Davis) wants to kill him for fictionalising their secret affair in his new novel; another ex (Elisabeth Shue as a smitten one-time fan of Harry's) is leaving him for the very inoffensive Billy Crystal; his old college wants to honour him and he rolls up (after a minor odyssey upstate) with a brassy black hooker (Hazelle Goodman in her debut) and, eventually, a corpse in tow; and his imaginary characters keep popping up to discuss finer details of the plot.

A veritable Who's Who of Woody concerns (the Grim Reaper makes a grumpy appearance, Harry attends therapy, grabs his estranged son at school and pens a book about - get this - an unforgettable Oriental prostitute - you can see the royal thumbing of his nose at us every step of the way), with several of his favourite players (Davis, Julie Kavner and Mariel Hemingway) rubbing shoulders with a slew of new faces (Stanley Tucci, Bob Balaban, Kirstie Alley, Julia Louis-Dreyfus, Richard Benjamin, Eric Bogosian and even Robin Williams and Demi Moore), Deconstructing Harry is as personal as Allen's most difficult movie, Stardust Memories (1980) - perhaps even more so. Whether his dedicated contingent of admirers want to know quite this much about him (or be subject to this level of vitriolic criticism) is another matter entirely, but there's no denying Deconstructing Harry is Woody's first movie in ages which really has guts. ****

FALLEN (M) - Denzel Washington is irritatingly bland as a serial-killer-specialising detective onto the epidemic spread of an ancient Evil after the passing on of said Evil from a incantation-mumbling mass-murderer (Elias Koteas) recently gassed (after striking Hellfire-and-tilted-camera opening credits). John Goodman is pretty good in the flat role of the "buddy", while Donald Sutherland and Embeth Davidtz walk through it as, respectively, Washington's superior and a supernatural expert, and every passer-by "infected" (in cliche sequences borrowed from The Hidden, as the Evil moves through crowds like incredibly virulent measles) tunelessly sings, in a tedious running joke, the Rolling Stones' Time Is On My Side, while the film noir-inspired rain pours down outside and everyone does their best to look edgy.

However, Fallen badly over-reaches itself trying to convince you that it's actually a bona-fide Apocalypse fable, and throws any suspense out the window with a finale so obviously filmed in an imitation-snowy studio, and with nervy characters pointing guns at each other willy-nilly, that the effect is sorely ridiculous. Armageddon out of here! **1/2

GREAT EXPECTATIONS (M) - Dickens' novel gets the grand updating treatment in this flashily empty, ego-driven nonsense that's always watchable if pretty brainless. Finnegan Bell blossoms as an artist in Florida, passing his childhood days taking dancing lessons with dotty Mrs. Dinsmoor (Anne Bancroft hamming it up) and her niece Estella, and having a terrifying experience at the mercy of fugitive murderer Lustig (Robert DeNiro, fearsome and foul-mouthed enough to give any Dickens groupie heart palpitations). Growing up to be the callow Ethan Hawke, he's tempted to New York and an exhibition of his fussy paintings, where he runs into Estella, now grown up to be emaciated Gwyneth Paltrow and engaged to Hank Azaria, but still happy to show up at Finn's apartment and get her gear off for some posey portraits.

American-debutting director Alfonso Cuaron trowels on the zappy editing and, especially in the Florida sequences, a troppo-from-the-Sun look that's rather annoying, while also allowing Bancroft and the under-used DeNiro to hog the limelight away from the empty performances of the star-crossed lovers (it's Hawke's worst performance ever, while Paltrow looks ready to pass out from starvation any second). It's perhaps not as bad as it could have been, but don't expect too much. **1/2

LOST IN SPACE (M) - This long-awaited movie version of the 60s cult TV classic series is a huge disappointment, an ugly, crummy, hard-to-follow mess with much-trumpeted special effects that look unfinished and vary from the competent to the idiotic. The set-up is much the same as the series as, in 2058, John Robinson (William Hurt) plans to take his family on a ten-year cybersleep trip to Alpha Centauri to hopefully colonise distant space, but his plans are scuppered when Dr. Zachary Smith (Gary Oldman) sabotages the ship and, when abandoned by his superiors, winds up going with them, kicking, screaming and conspiring all the way. Hurt and wife Mimi Rogers spout hateful American family values bereft of any irony, Heather Graham's Judy Robinson bookishly engages in reams of cod philosophy with none of the knowing humour of the old show, Matt Le Blanc (from TV's Friends) struts about drearily knocking off awful one-liners as the dorky Major on hand for would-be love interest, and the main gist of the story (a time-continuum warping of the future with crab-like aliens thrown in) is confusing, irritating and, incredibly enough, boring.

The monsters pale in comparison to Starship Troopers' marauding beasts, and a friendly, product-placement alien (soon to be on every toy-shop shelf across the globe) looks fuzzily super-imposed and ready to suddenly advertise Coco Pops, while the actors are awfully wasted, with Oldman, once the greatest British actor of his generation, poncing through a role that should have been simply wondrous (the fey, cowardly, queeny Smith having been so memorably played by Jonathan Harris in the original) and obviously having a huge time as he takes the money and runs, but hampered by dialogue so badly post-synched it seems Smith's strayed out of a spaghetti Western.

Indeed, the whole movie is obviously hurried - even unfinished - as if to meet a release deadline, as Hurt and Rogers fall in love all over again in the most risible circumstances and their kids have various rites of passage as lamentably five-minutes-ago effects setpieces lurch into shot and director Stephen Hopkins (The Ghost And The Darkness) treats the viewer with all the contempt he feels they deserve. And while Sphere might offer a terrible conclusion, Lost In Space doesn't even have one, and just fizzes out all of a sudden and you're left aghast and thinking Hopkins and Co. just ran out of film, money or (more probably) patience. A sad and sorry filming of an esteemed camp legend, Lost In Space is hopelessly misplaced. **

LOVE ETC... (M) - This twee French comedy with millennial underpinnings but a heart of pure cliche has a love triangle between two chums, Benoit (Yvan Attal) and Pierre (Charles Berling), and the woman (Charlotte Gainsbourg) dreary Benoit marries (yep, that old chestnut). Gainsbourg is almost appealing but totally wet as the fragile girl, while Attal is a drip and Berling intolerably smarmy as Pierre, and director Marion Vernoux gussies up every line of dialogue, every endlessly meaningful glance between the three leads, and seemingly every shot in a desperate attempt to trend-ify a hoary old story. It isn't anywhere near the rarefied cinema many would have you believe, as Love Etc... just brims with pretension, emptiness, arrogance and so on and so on... and so on. **1/2

MONEY TALKS (MA) - Chris Tucker (who you may remember as "Ruby Rod" in The Fifth Element - no? hardly surprising really) is nearly fried when his prison bus is blown up by a jail-breaking arch-bad-guy, and must seek the help of journo Charlie Sheen to save him both from the cops who think he's in with the goons and the scumbag guys themselves. Almost totally unfunny (with real actors Paul Sorvino, Veronica Cartwright and David Warner all wasted), this "comedy" isn't worth a red cent. *1/2

SPHERE (M) - Michael Crichton's connect-the-dots novel is translated to the screen by director Barry Levinson, who effectively captures the sweaty claustrophobia of being stuck on the bottom of the Pacific Ocean but just can't get past the cliches and thorough-going predictability. Dustin Hoffman, Sharon Stone, Samuel L. Jackson, Liev Schreiber and Peter Coyote are better actors than usual for this sort of thing, as they assemble in Davey Jones' Locker to study a strange craft that's apparently 300 years old and come face-to-face with a shimmering special-effects Sphere that makes their deepest fears reality, and Levinson seems to be about to cut through with some promise of big thrills indeed (such as what seems to be an approaching giant squid). But then "Jerry" shows up, reveals that all this is in their minds, which paves the way for plenty of moralising and intellectualising, and the chance for Levinson to let himself off the hook financially by frustratingly keeping most of the monstrous creatures expected (an impressive shoal of killer jellyfish aside) implied or imaginary.

The cast turn in creditable enough performances, considering what they've got to work with, and there are some grisly highlights (gooey tendrils fished out of a corpse's nose and another body knocking down a fleeing character in a rush of water), but they can't stop the fact that the whole thing sinks without a trace with a nebulous punchline that'll leave a curse on your lips. Someone should drown Levinson for that one!!! **1/2

UNDER THE SKIN (R) - Samantha Morton's Iris goes off the rails after the death of her Mum (Rita Tushingham) and descends into a squalid, self-destructive world of masochistic near-rape, psychological battery, frequent auto-eroticism and frenetic over-acting in this garbled, poverty-row mess from writer/ director Carine Adler, the sort of horrible, depressing, mentally pulverising experience many will misconstrue as feminist profundity as they don't have a clue as to anything else it could possibly be. Morton tries hard in a role that could have been daring and taboo-breaking but instead winds up absolutely infuriating. It didn't get Under My Skin, but it definitely upset my stomach and insulted my mind. **

THE WEDDING SINGER (M) - Adam Sandler's retro-80s comedy doesn't plumb the depths of his Billy Madison or Happy Gilmore, but that's not for want of trying. His wedding singer, popular all over town way back in '85 but suffering a near-breakdown when his own fiance doesn't show for his wedding, starts to feel longings for a nice waitress (Drew Barrymore) he keeps bumping into. Unfortunately she's engaged as well (to a nasty guy characterised mainly by hideously-patterned underpants and a fondness for perving at other girls' backsides), so Sandler has to take a stand. There are several anachronisms amidst all the desperate 80s mood-setting (the Rubik's Cube, for example, was out of fashion in 1981), and the comedy is low-brow as all get out, but a few cameos (Steve Buscemi and Jon Lovitz deglamourised mightily) and a jerky gag or two make it less painless and IQ-decreasing than you might think. And, as for Sandler's career - HA! - it'll never last!!! **

THE WOODLANDERS (M) - One of Thomas Hardy's less glum novels gets modest handling here, with unlikeable leads Rufus Sewell (as Giles) and Emily Woof (!!!) (as Grace) playing out a story of social standing and unrequited love that's not quite as depressing as Jude, but which will still bring any audience way, way down. Grace returns from a "proper" education alienated from her timber-merchant family, the British working classes and her childhood sweetheart Giles (Sewell, looking sour), which in turn upsets the community and another lass (Jodhi May) longing for Giles herself. It all leads to misery, not too surprisingly, with a nicely atmospheric evocation of the period lost in the grim and uneventful plotting. Sewell and Woof try hard, but The Woodlanders is a hard slog that'll leave most audiences stiff as a board. **1/2
