Thursday May 21, 1998

Nil by Mouth
(Nova Cinema, Rated R)
reviewed by David Bradbury

Thespian Gary Oldman's writing/ directing debut strives for Ken Loach (Carla's Song) or Mike Leigh (Secrets And Lies) territory, but a tendency to rave robs it of some power. Still, it's not for want of trying: this gruellingly low-budget character study unflinchingly chronicles a working class London family's turmoil when Raymond (Ray Winstone) becomes violent to wife Valerie (Kathy Burke) while under the influence of alcohol and drugs, and her brother Billy (Charlie Creed-Miles) starts to freak as substance abuse takes hold.

Oldman, who apparently lived under similarly hideous domestic circumstances as a child, doesn't skip on the depressingly dead-ended mood (by way of only the greyest locations and grimly ordinary interiors, as well as a savagely pattery ear for shockingly foul dialogue), and places the audience right in there as Ray beats the pregnant Valerie or bites Billy's nose (a painful moment that, nonetheless, segues into queasy comedy as Billy spends the rest of the movie looning about with a huge bandage and being referred to a Coco The Clown): you feel the blows and, none too surprisingly, by the end feel the crap's been kicked out of you too.

Perhaps some will question why we have to see this particular despairing story told yet again, but there's no denying that Oldman's a force to be reckoned with behind the camera (if not quite as much as he is before it, as sections of the narrative seem rather forced and unmotivated). The performances, by a cast of unfamiliar character players, keep you watching, adding the human dimension to Nil By Mouth that's so punishing: Creed-Miles is agreeably cocky as hopeless Billy, and Burke devastating as the downtrodden Valerie (when she admits to it being only her thirtieth birthday late on in the film, you'll be horrified), while Winstone is awesomely terrifying as the irrational Ray, never quite sympathetic but certainly so realistic you can't help but respond. It's impossible not to be drawn into Oldman's Nil By Mouth (an odd term explained during the story), whether you want to or not, but it's certainly not a movie for everyone (rated R not only for drugs and violence, but more profanity than even Good Fellas, Harlem Nights and Scarface - indeed this must qualify as the most bad-mouthed movie in Cinema History).

You'll leave it feeling you've been smacked in the gob (Oldman also dedicates the movie to his own late Father, something that should ostracise him from the Oldman clan for generation to come).

****
