Wednesday May 6, 1998

THE SOUND OF ONE HAND CLAPPING (Palace East End and selected cinemas, Rated M)
Reviewed by David Bradley

Richard Flanagan makes his writer/ director debut with The Sound Of One Hand Clapping (based on his own novel, unusually enough), but many will be wishing he hadn't after this puzzlingly portentous exercise in narrative inscrutability. Set in a grim Tasmania that keeps intercutting between various pasts and now (1989), we follow Kerry Fox as she returns home and flashes back over a troubled migrant past in Hobart. Having left home in 1963, she's now pregnant (naturally - another theme wouldn't hurt, would it?) and looking to reconcile with her embittered, alcoholic Father (dour Kristof Kaczmarek), while also meditating upon various levels of frustratingly abstract meaning about her childhood. Everything's thrown into the mix willy-nilly: fanciful devices such as Fox's discovery of overly symbolic broken China she smashed as an infant; endless overly-ominous dream sequences; a memory of a nasty neighbour (who disconcertingly looks a little like Bruno Lucia) intent on molesting her; her Dad turning to drink, violence and ruin; and too many parallels frustratingly running roughshod.

In short, TSOOHC is that odd movie (or maybe not that odd - see also Lawn Dogs) where there's just too much to swallow (Slovenian dispossession, various true-blue racist and archaic attitudes, parental relationships, maternity fears, alternating planes of recall and lies, lies, lies), a curious affliction that seems to strike movies such as this with increasing frequency (and one that attacks as virulently as a film with nary two ideas to its credit). Flanagan's eye for striking countryside comes through, but Fox is irritatingly mannered and the script too damn unfathomable. Many "enlightened" viewers will wrongly palm this off as "sophistication", and embarrass anyone alienated by this one into reading the acclaimed book but, as always, this is a movie, not a novel, and you shouldn't have to follow a logarithmic chart to "get it". Perhaps the main thing The Sound Of One Hand Clapping proves conclusively is that some novels are unfilmable.
