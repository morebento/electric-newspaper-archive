I approached Copland, the new film written and directed by James Mangold (whose one previous screen credit is Heavy), with considerable expectations. The film's posters and publicity have taken two basic tacks. The first is that the movie has a cast of Scorcese alumni (Robert de Niro, Harvey Keitel, Ray Liotta). The second is that, after years of Rocky and Rambo movies and other assorted action fare, Sylvester Stallone is actually acting. As with many things in movies, there is an actuarial proof of this: Stallone not only gained weight (evidence of capital-A acting ever since de Niro did it for Raging Bull), but also took a pay cut.

These things constitute some kind of empirical proof of quality or worthiness. I wasn't there to see Stallone act, particularly; I was there because the cast signalled this as an unambiguous guy film, but one with enough of a cachet of quality that I wouldn't have to spend time defending it to my tertiary educated mates (thus allowing me to save energy to mount a spirited defence of Starship Troopers).

The audience, at an 11.30 am screening on the first day of release, seemed to be there for largely similar reasons. A lot of the men might have seen Taxi Driver when it first came out. I felt comfortable with the audience in this context: they shared a longstanding appreciation of Robert de Niro, and of Harvey Keitel from way before Quentin Tarantino made him an icon for the new generation of indie filmmakers.

The opening shot of Manhattan managed to be a little novel (it was done at night), and conveyed an immediate sense of a director happy to be transparently symbolic but not dumb. This was borne out an the scene in which Stallone is introduced, playing pinball: (1) the machine insists on "more coins", prefacing the theme of corruption, and (2) Ray Liotta states that Stallone is a pinball game kind of guy rather than a video game guy, implying old fashioned values out of step with the times. Stallone is no saint, though; he unlocks a parking meter to get quarters to feed the pinball machine.

Super Boy, nephew of Ray (Keitel), drives home drunk. Two black youths sideswipe him. He imagines they have a gun, and shoots them dead. Rather than face an enquiry, he fakes his suicide at Ray's urgins. Stallone is a time-serving sheriff, whose lifelong ambition to join the NYPD has been thwarted because of partial deafness, sustained when rescuing a woman from a submerged car in his youth. We see the rescue in flashback. (Cars falling from bridges have associations with political corruption in American history - Ted Kennedy at Chappaquidick - and in movies, e.g. Blowout.) He can't enforce the law, because the cops are a law unto themselves.

Meanwhile, Liz's husband is having an affair with Ray's wife. Liotta burns down his own house for insurance - how does Stallone recognise the bomb squad cop? Super Boy is still alive, but Ray has to rub him out on the orders of - the Mob? the police union? This fails, and Super Boy escapes.

Stallone has the hots for cuckolded Liz (Annabella Sciorra, playing low-key for once), whom he rescued. This is played out to Springsteen's Drive All Night. The romance never goes further than this, which surely can't be just because it would be adulterous; it's nice to see a movie not derailed by a gratuitous romantic/sexual subplot.

Liz's husband (Peter Berg, from The Last Seduction) threatens to rat on his mates, so they let him die when confronting a criminal. Stallone tries to right a wrong, at the urging of I.A. guy de Niro (lots of familiar smiles and tics at first - the hand held palm down as a sign of his authority, but the performance settles down).

Much has been made of the claim that Stallone does some "real acting" in this film. The weight gain helps, actually, in that Stallone looks the part, like a regular guy whose vestiges of unexpected physical beauty hint at how much better his life might have been. He evokes a sense of sadness and lost opportunities quite effectively, his romantic scenes are wisely restrained, and his transition to gun-toting hero doesn't have quite the sense of inevitability and artifice that accompanies Clint Eastwood's transformation at the end of Unforgiven. Best of all, he doesn't shout a lot. Pretty much everyone else shouts sooner or later, 'cause that's what people pay to see Keitel, Liotta, etc do.

My favourite scene is that in which Stallone and Annabella Sciorra fumble around the edges of romance while Bruce Springsteen's Stolen Car plays on a turntable. Aspiring slacker film makers take note: in the featured songs department, James Mangold has shown the way.
