Wednesday June 17, 1998

Godzilla
(Hoyts and saturation cinemas as from June 11th, Rated PG)
reviewed by David Bradley

While nowhere near the unmitigated crud that Independence Day (or Underpants Day to its detractors) was, Godzilla still suffers from many scaly problems, some expected (overlength, over-familiarity, over-hype) and some unexpected.

Perhaps the most insurmountable drawback to the mutant dinosaurian in this long-awaited American remake is exactly the one thing that attracted director Roland Emmerich and scripter/ co-producer Dean Devlin to the project in the first place (and resulted in the tiresome catchcry SIZE DOES MATTER): Godzilla is so damn huge that, unlike the slavering giant reptiles of Spielberg's Jurassic Park and The Lost World or, indeed, Godzilla's main contender for Most Famous City-Flattener In Cinematic History, King Kong, all he can really do is crash down Big Apple streets and smash buildings asunder accidentally.

Gone are the fears of being eaten alive from the T-Rex, or the anthropomorphic tragedy of Kong - instead the big green guy (here looking something like an inbred Iguanodon and not some underpaid Japanese guy in a rubber suit) is so enormously impersonal the main dangers are now him either treading on you by mistake or generally lowering land values.

When Godzilla arrives in New York (hey, isn't that where all good creatures wind up, after all?), he's accompanied by swarming factions of the army brainstorming for all their worth about how to best destroy this extraordinary new species, and Chernobyl earthworm expert Matthew Broderick (trying for a genuine performance but hamstrung, naturally, by hackneyed material) thinks he knows what to do (but, wouldn't you know it, the military brass won't listen - how original's that?). Maria Pitillo is quite awful as Broderick's ex, a squeaky journo, Hank Azaria strains to make gonzo cameraman "Animal" interesting, and Jean Reno (The Professional) steals his scenes easily as a French "insurance salesman" with an abiding love for Elvis, but they're all frustrating second-stringers to - and massively upstaged by - the CGI FX that make Godzilla seemingly so life-like.

Or are they? Towards the end of this 138 minute epic, the trickery start to wear thin and, after the undeniable initial elation at the stupendous sight of Godzilla (or "Gojira!", as one Japanese survivor rants in a nice homage to the original Toho series) you can't help but look more closely at the computer-generation that is this creature, and start to see not the biggest bloody beastie of 'em all but just some ingenious but obviously unreal opticals. While good fun for undemanding audiences (and far better than ID4, once again), Godzilla just doesn't properly work: it lacks the knowing corn/ mythic qualities of the Toho Godzillas, and is so blatantly intended as a license-to-print-money US franchise (which, as the press hasn't hesitated in pointing out, it has somewhat fallen short of) that the overall effect, for Godzilla/ Gojira purists, is monstrous.

**1/2
