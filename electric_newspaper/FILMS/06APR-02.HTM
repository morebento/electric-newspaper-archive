Monday April 6, 1998

Rusty visage
"The Man in the Iron Mask"
Reviewed by Peter Wels

Still riding high on the 'Titanic' tidal wave, Leonardo Di Caprio's latest period piece doesn't match up, and may leave you with a sinking feeling.

The three musketeers are back on the screen in the directorial debut from screenwriter Randall Wallace ('Braveheart'). However, unlike the last Three Musketeers film - which featured the questionable acting talents of Charlie Sheen, Chris O'Donnell, Oliver Platt and Kiefer Sutherland - we have real actors filling the shoes of the swashbucklers in 'The Man In The Iron Mask'.

Taking in the role of Porthos is Gerard Depardieu (one of the few bona fide French actors on display here), while Irishman Gabriel Byrne ('The Usual Suspects') portrays D'Artagnan. Filling out the rest of the lead roles are Jeremy Irons (as the saintly Aramis) and John Malkovich (as Athos).

The film - based on the novel by 'Musketeers' scribe Alexandre Dumas - takes place after the events of the original 'Three Musketeers', with Athos, Porthos and Aramis now well retired. D'Artagnan, however, is still a musketeer. Now head of the Musketeers, D'Artagnan has the job of guarding the tyrannical King Louis (Leonardo DiCaprio). And while he may not like the king, D'Artagnan is bound by honour to serve him.

Meanwhile, the now-retired three musketeers have come up with a plan to restore honour to the throne of France. Louis has a twin brother, Philippe (the Man In The Iron Mask of the title, also played by DiCaprio), and Aramis wants to swap him for the real king.

Considering this film comes from the writer of Mel Gibson's Academy Award winning flick 'Braveheart', you could be forgiven for thinking 'The Man In The Iron Mask' may have some substance. But the film is uneven, never knowing whether it wants to play as a serious drama or as a camp swashbuckler. The confusion begins with the casting: We have the very French Anne Parillaud (from 'La Femme Nikita' and more recently 'A French Vampire In America') playing the mother of the obviously American Leonardo DiCaprio. Added to that, the musketeers are played by an Englishman (Irons), an Irishman (Byrne), an American (Malkovich) and only one Frenchman (Depardieu).

Out of all the actors on display, the only ones to escape unscathed are Brit Hugh Laurie (who has an amusing cameo) and American John Malkovich, whose moments of seething anger bring the film to life for a few, very brief moments.

As a director, Randall Wallace shows a lack of skill in handling the action sequences. While a couple of scenes are amusing, most of the fight scenes are handled with a lack of suspense and any form of flair.

However, 'The Man With The Iron Mask' does have a its moments - the jailbreak scene is fun and a nude scene from the gargantuan Gerard Depardieu is one of the most comically grotesque sights to grace the screen in a long time.
