Tuesday May 12, 1998

The Boys
(Palace East End Cinemas as from May 14th, Rated MA)

The Boys, based on Gordon Graham's acclaimed play, features, without a doubt, the most frightening character in Australian cinema. David Wenham (who played the same role on stage and worked as the movie's Associate Producer) is Brett Sprague, released from prison one morning after a year inside for a botched assault, gladly rejoining his Mum (Lynette Curran), girlfriend (Toni Collette) and, especially, his brothers (John Polson and Anthony Hayes) in the family home. Since he's been inside, things have changed, with brother Hayes having "knocked up" a teenager (Anna Lise), who's now moved in, while Polson has moved out with brittle Jeanette Cronin and Mum has taken up with Maori drifter Pete Smith - and Brett is none too happy about any of these developments. As the day drags on, Brett revisits the site of his arrest, provokes the local police, argues incessantly with his brothers and Collette, becomes increasingly angry at the instability of this depressingly dead-end life and, when evening comes, heads out with the boys for some fun.

Wenham's Brett isn't just an unnervingly real character because of his outbursts of irrational violence (as in one of the most shocking scenes in the movie, his and Collette's tooth-and-nail clash in the back shed, as he bashes her head against the wall for taunting his impotence) or even his obvious resentment at his downward-spiral existence: it's the fact that, at crucial moments (in a brilliant touch by director Rowan Woods), Brett is revealed to be one of the less horrible people in the film (his calm reassurance of weepy Lise, understandably desperate to get away from the claustrophobic house, is truly unsettling). Almost unremittingly naturalistic, The Boys is the Australian equivalent of America's Henry - Portrait Of A Serial Killer or Belgium's Man Bites Dog, yet manages to be even harder than those notorious urban horrors by drawing you into the lives of this hopeless family, and acquainting you with someone charismatic, intelligent and suddenly, shockingly violent (and, perhaps worst of all, someone the like of which you may very well have met before). Certainly not a film for everyone, but hands down the best Australian movie of the year thus far. You have been warned.

*****
