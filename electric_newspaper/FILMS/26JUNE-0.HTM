Friday June 26, 1998

Kundun
Palace East End Cinemas
reviewed by Victoria White

Kundun is the story of the 14th Dalai Lama. It was based on the current Dalai Lama's own story, and is directed by Martin Scorsese.

The story begins with the Dalai Lama's birth, and follows his life as he is discovered as the latest incarnation of the Buddha, his rule during the Chinese occupation of Tibet and his subsequent flight into India for the sake of his people and his religion. It is a story that is both beautiful and harsh.

This film not only shows with great compassion the plight of the Tibetan people and their chosen religion, it is also a brilliant piece of cinema by Scorsese. It is beautifully shot in Morocco with flawless effects of the Himalayas and the Dalai Lama's residence in Tibet. The principal actors are all Tibetan, and the costumes and set design indicate impeccable attention to detail.

Only two criticisms which come to mind. Given that the Chinese occupation of Tibet has also meant that the Tibetan language has been replaced by Mandarin/Cantonese in Tibetan schools, it would have been nice to have heard the actors speaking in their native tongue with the use of English subtitles.

Secondly, although the film is compassionate, it does tend to gloss over the violence and destruction of the Tibetan people and their culture at the hands of the Chinese. Since not only Chinese occupation, but continuing violence such as genocide, rape, and torture threatens to end the Tibetan race, more attention chould have been placed on this.

Nevertheless, this is a fantastic film with a wonderful and somehow quite peaceful story about one of, if not the greatest, heros of our time.

Go see it now.
