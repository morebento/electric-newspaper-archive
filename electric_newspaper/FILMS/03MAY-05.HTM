Wednesday May 6, 1998

BLUES BROTHERS 2000 (Greater Union and selected cinemas, Rated M)
Reviewed by David Bradley

Supposedly setting a record for the longest time ever between a legendary original and its sequel (1980 and 1998, although commentators appear to have forgotten Psycho and Psycho 2's twenty-two year gap), the long-awaited, much-anticipated, oft-announced Blues Brothers 2000 isn't an embarrassment for amassed cultists, but does suffer from a little too much sanitisation. Maybe it was a bad idea to get this one off the ground after the downward spiral of its director's (John Landis) career and the deaths of three major cast members (Jake Blues himself, John Belushi, as well as much-lamented Cab Calloway and John Candy), but a fair stab is made at bringing the Blues into the modern day, as an older (but still surreal) Elwood is released from prison, learns of the death of his brother and mentor, and wonders where to go from here.

Assigned an orphan (ten-year old J. Evan Bonifant, who threatens to out-blues one and all) to care for, he's soon off and trying to recruit Calloway's "son" (Joe Morton), a high-ranking Detroit police official, and teaming up with Mighty Mack McTeer (John Goodman in swaggeringly deadpan form), as lots of jaded 90s gags spring up, production numbers kick off at the drop of a porkpie hat (no less than Wilson Pickett sings his heart out, all of a sudden and for no apparent reason), and cameos galore proliferate (faves Aretha Franklin, James Brown, the esteemed Blues Brother band - naturally - and even Frank Oz, as well as a joke super-group that includes Eric Clapton, Travis Tritt, B.B King, Steve Winwood, Isaac Hayes and many more). It's pretty ragged at times (much in the spirit of the first movie and, indeed, director Landis' entire filmography), and seems reluctant to get down and dirty in quite the same care-to-the-wind 1980 fashion, but there's still much to enjoy in Blues Brothers 2000 - and, believe you me, it could have been so much worse. I feel good.
