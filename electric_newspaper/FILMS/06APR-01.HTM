Monday April 6, 1998

Stalking Bill
"Good Will Hunting"
Reviewed by Victoria White

Will Hunting is a genius. The same may be said for Matt Damon and Ben Affleck who wrote and star in Good Will Hunting, their first ever feature film. In case you missed the Oscars, Damon and Affleck walked away with the prize for Best Original Screenplay, transforming them from virtual no-names to Hollywood's latest golden boys almost overnight.

In the fickle world of films is this one worthy of all the accolades it has received, or is it a result of Hollywoods mega-money train putting two young, good looking fellas in the driver's seat, full steam ahead?

The story is one that has screams blockbuster. Small-town larrikin, parentless, and abused in the foster homes in which he was forced to grow up, Will Hunting (Matt Damon) just happens to also possess a healthy knack for solving the world's greatest math problems. While pushing his janitors trolley down the hallways of the prestigious Massachusetts Institute of Technology, he is discovered solving problems real math students can only dream of, much to the excitement of Professor Lambau (Stellan Skasgard), head of physics at MIT and apparently himself "super-smart".

Lambau can see that Will is like no other person he has ever met, solving mathematical mysteries with the ease of a ten year-old riding a bike, despite his lack of formal education. The only problem is, Will is a wisecracking smart-arse, still carrying with him the scars of his violent upbringing. If he is ever to achieve in the world of white-collar genii, he must face his greatest fear, himself.

Enter Robin Williams as Sean Maguire, a psychiatrist from Will's own neighbourhood, still mourning the death of his wife years before. Williams is somewhat of a father figure assigned to help Will overcome his ghosts. The two men enter a journey of discovery about themselves, each other, and the world in which they live.

Good Will Hunting is pleasant enough, and might even make you cry. It is beautifully shot by Gus Van Sant, with a charming soundtrack by Danny Elfman. The cast look great, with Ben Affleck as Will's beer-drinking buddy Chuckie, and Minnie Driver as Will's gorgeous and witty girlfriend Skylar, not to mention Damon and Williams.

The end result is a feel-good flick, even if you can't help thinking that you may have seen it all before.
