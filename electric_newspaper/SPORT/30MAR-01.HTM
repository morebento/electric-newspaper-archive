Monday March 30, 1998

Rams catch Doggies
by Phil Owens

Adelaide came of age on Friday night with a gutsy, cool and polished display that had the home crowd on their feet several times in the second half. The Rams trailed the Canterbury Bulldogs (Sydney) all night, and were 20-10 down midway through the second half. Tries scored by centre Peter Clarke (64th minute) and back row forward Marty McKenzie (75th minute), both converted by half back Craig Kimmorley, put the Rams in front on the scoreboard when it counted, and got them their first win of 1998, defeating the Bulldogs 22-20 in a tense, high quality and very entertaining match.

This was the best game of rugby league played at Adelaide Oval so far. Both teams were tigerish in defence, and it was this aspect of Adelaide's game late in the first half when Canterbury enjoyed a glut of possession that ultimately saved them. The Rams were also able to obtain good field position and capitalise. Early on they conceded relatively soft tries to Greg Fleming (fullback) and Mat Ryan (centre) but were able to respond with tries of their own to stay in touch with the Dogs and trail 14-10 at half time. At that stage the difference between the teams on the scoreboard was the goal kicking of Daryl Halligan.

Kimmorley, standing in at half back for suspended Noel Goldthorpe, had an excellent game for Adelaide. His quick reading of the defence, and well timed pass put big Rams winger Joe Tamani over for their first try. Tony Iro started on the Adelaide bench as usual, and played in the second row in defence and in the centres in attack. His timing, running and ball handling skills caused panic in the Canterbury defence. He threw the final pass to Wayne Simonds who scored Adelaide's second try late in the first half. Simonds' try was a classic piece of football and came after two inspiring bursts down the centre of the ruck by hooker/dummy-half, Kerrod Walters. After the second of these incisive runs, the Rams moved the Bulldogs defence to their right by attacking the northeast corner. The tiring berries defence could not cope with the well executed backline movement that put Simonds over in the north western corner on the next play.

As usual, the Rams forwards were able to dominate their opponents in the latter periods of both halves. Their fitness, speed and enthusiasm, plus coach Rod Reddy's judgement on the interchange, are their strengths. Unlike previous home matches however, the Rams were able to switch the point of attack once the opposition defence was rolling back. In the final analysis, the Rams attacking ability and ball control won them the game. Their kicking and kick chase in the second half put the Bulldogs under enormous pressure. Despite trailing by ten points midway through the second half, Adelaide methodically strangled the life out of Canterbury. The Dogs kicking game fell apart, and this allowed the Rams to put Canterbury's defence under sustained pressure. The Bulldog's did not deserve to lose after such determined tackling, but Adelaide did not allow them to escape from their own quarter for almost the last 15 minutes of the game.

Canterbury coach Steve Folkes might consider the result unfortunate since ace goal kicker Daryl Halligan hit the uprights twice.

It would be unfair to single out any individual in the winning side because there were so many excellent contributions to a great team effort. Adelaide's luck was bound to improve sooner or later, after gallant losses in the first two rounds of the season. This win, over much more fancied opponents (Adelaide had ten-and-a-half points start on footytab), will lift the Rams confidence for their next clash against the Penrith Panthers in Sydney, who are coming off a loss against the Brisbane Broncos.
