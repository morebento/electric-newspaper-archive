Monday May 25, 1998

Superbikes: Australia's Troy Corser leads world championship

Troy Corser, who hasn't won a Superbike race this season, nevertheless leads the world championship following Sunday's Spanish race

The Australian has always finished strongly in the points.

But yesterday's two rounds belonged Race One winner PierFrancesco Chilli and Race Two winner Carl Fogarty.

The first three bikes in Race One were all Ducatis - PierFrancesco Chili, Troy Corser, and Spanish privateer Gregorio Lavilla in third.

Corser's second place gave him the championship lead, when Noriyuki Haga and Carl Fogarty finished 10th and 9th respectively.

"I was closed down by two riders at the start, but when I saw that Colin (Edwards) didn't pull away, I thought I could stay with them," Chili said after the race.

"I had a few carburation problems so I had to do all my passing on the brakes. Congratulations to Lavilla - he rode a great race."

The early morning saw driving rain. Morning warm-up was delayed while track staff tried to clear standing water in some of the corners. In a departure from the norm, the riders were allowed three sighting laps prior to the race, and race was shortened from 26 to 20 laps.

Championship leader Haga led the pack away ahead of Lavilla and Honda's Colin Edwards. However the lead was short-lived, and Haga dropped to 8th, leaving Kawasaki's Neil Hodgson leading Edwards and ex-125 GP champion Gramigni, on a Ducati.

By lap 4, Lavilla had used his local knowledge (and greater courage) to good effect - he took the lead and started to make the break, leading Edwards, Troy Corser, Aaron Slight and Chili to battle as the rain still continued. However Chili was on a charge, and five laps later had passed everyone except the Spaniard and was closing at a rate of 0.8 seconds per lap.

At just over half distance, Chili had closed down Lavilla and in fact appeared to be waved by. Behind them, Troy Corser had managed to get into third and set about chasing the leading pair, while the two Hondas, Hodgson's Kawasaki and fast Yamaha-mounted Scott Russell battled for the remaining places.

"I think we made the right tyre choice here," said a confident Russell. "Colin went for a softer compound while we chose the hard version and it lasted better as the race went on. As I passed Hodgson and Colin the bike must have sucked in some water and started to run on three cylinders, so I waved them past. Then two laps later it cleared again so I came back. I'll be on the podium for sure in race 2."

Chili was clear and away by the end of the race, and Corser forced his way into second on the last lap to make it a factory 1-2 for Ducati and a full podium for the Italian manufacturer.

Carl Fogarty dominated the race two with a comfortable win, after taking to
the front by the first bend, which also saw the biggest incident of the
race as four riders crashed on the first lap.


Race two was held in the dry, with all riders on slick tyres - however the
race was still held over the shortened distance of 20 laps. The Castrol
Hondas failed to make it out for the sighting lap, but made it to the grid
for the start. 


As the lights went green, Fogarty was first away and led into the first bend.


Behind him there was mayhem as Yanagawa went in hot under Lavilla and then
     lifted the bike up, forcing Lavilla into the rear of Hodgson and
causing Whitham and Edwards to run off the track. As all four bikes went
out, Whitham was able to get back on the track and fought his way back into
10th by the end. Hodgson was less lucky, but still managed to nurse a
damaged bike to 14th, while unluckiest of all both Lavilla and Edwards
failed to restart as both machines were too damaged to continue.


Up front, it was Yamaha's Noriyuki Haga who yet again showed a dazzling
display of lurid slides and exciting style to try and keep up with a smooth
and confident Fogarty. Eventually his tyres gave up the struggle, and as
the race progressed he fell backwards into the grasp of first Aaron Slight
and then Troy Corser.


In a spread-out race, it was left to Fogarty to come home a comfortable 6
seconds ahead of the rest.


After the race, Fogarty was in a bouyant but philisophical mood. "The way I
rode in the first race I needed sacking, so I needed to do something! I
felt confident all weekend but I needed the weather to be good to prove
myself. It's important to get a good start here and then I just got my head
down and felt in control really - it was good. I just need to get it right
for both races; I seem to be doing one good and and bad all season at the
moment."


Fogarty has now moved into second position in the championship behind new
       leader Corser.
