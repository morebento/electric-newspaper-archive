Friday June 19, 1998

Blues will try to maul Maroons in decider
by Phil Owens

Queensland seemingly face mission impossible tonight at the Sydney Football Stadium. They managed to win after the siren in game 1 when their forwards looked gone for all money. In game 2, the New South Wales "Blues" pummelled the Queensland "Maroons" into the dust at Lang Park (Suncorp Stadium, Brisbane) and ran out comfortable winners. Tonight, expect more of the same from the Blues forwards.

The match is probably going to be played at the fastest pace ever seen in a game of rugby league. This was the case with game one, only to be outdone in game two. We're in for a real cracker.

The Blues squad looks very different without Rod Howe and Paul Harragon bulldozing their way forward with the ball and smashing the Maroon's ball carriers. Their replacements, Tony Butterfield and Glenn Lazarus, are certainly big, strong, tough and experienced, but don't quite have the same work rate as Howe and Harragon. NSW have stacked their interchange bench with forwards so this problem can be minimised.

The injury to Paul McGregor in the centres is also a major loss for NSW. Queensland did not handle him at all well in game two.

To cover the loss, Blues coach Tommy Raudonikis has shuffled star five-eighth Laury Daly into the centres and moved lock Brad Fittler to five-eighth. Daly hasn't played regularly at centre for some years, and might lack the pace of his Queensland opposition.

Queensland, on the other hand, look stronger than in game two. The inclusion of Gorden Tallis to the forward pack is a big, big plus. So is the addition of Robbie O'Davis to the three-quarters.

The Maroon's still have to deal with the recently exposed weaknesses of Darren Lockyer to the rolling and bouncing ball and Wendell Sailor with anything airbone.

The series and therefore state pride is at stake tonight. But there's more. The Australian team for the test series against Great Britain to be played at the end of the season will be heavily influenced by performances in the origin decider.

Some might question the patriotism of professional football players. They might wonder whether laying it on the line for your state and your country is something NRL players really do. Even if this cynical (realtist?) view is correct, there is of course the over riding attraction of money. State of Origin representative players get paid more by their clubs. The players with the biggest contracts, that is to say the contracts with the greatest amounts of dollars in them, are the Test players.

So, no matter how you look at it, there's no shortage of incentive tonight!

The most crucial of tonight's match ups will be the one between incumbent test half back, Alan ("Alfie") Langer, and his NSW opponent, Andrew Johns, the heir apparent.
