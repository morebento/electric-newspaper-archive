Tuesday June 9, 1998

Canadian Formula 1: Schumacher's controversial win


Alexander Wurz of Austria takes to the air after colliding at the first corner during the Canadian Grand Prix in Montreal yesterday

Michael Schumacher's dramatic victory in the Canadian Grand Prix was confirmed nearly two hours after the finish on Sunday night when a protest from the Williams team regarding the German's driving was thrown out.

Williams was left angry that its driver, German Heinz-Harald Frentzen, had been forced off the track on lap 21 when Schumacher, emerging from a pit stop, pulled across on him.

The crash wrecked Frentzen's hopes of a podium finish and, although Schumacher was punished with a 10-second stop-go penalty for his part in the incident, Williams felt it was not enough.

They protested to the stewards, in a bid to have Schumacher stripped of his victory, but their claims were rejected by the race stewards.

Schumacher, who later apologized to Frentzen, claimed that he was not aware of the incident until after the race.

"I just do not know what happened," he said. "I looked in my mirrors and saw nothing, so when I was called in for the penalty, I did not understand as I knew I had not speeded in the pit-lane."

Schumacher saw the pacesetting McLarens of Mika Hakkinen and David Coulthard both retire with mechanical problems early on and then overcame a strong challenge from Italian Giancarlo Fisichella in a Benetton to record a victory that would be the 29th of his career and second of the season.

But the German did not have an easy afternoon. He moved to the front when leader Coulthard retired on lap 18 with a throttle problem, but then lost the lead a lap later when he entered the pits to refuel.

That left Fisichella at the front in his Benetton and when Schumacher was handed the penalty for pushing Frentzen off the track as he emerged from the pits -- adding to his lengthy list of driving controversies -- it seemed to be the Italian's day.

But Schumacher recovered, taking the lead on lap 42 when Fisichella stopped for fuel, and he built up enough of an advantage to stay in front after his second stop on lap 50. He finished 16.662 seconds ahead of Fisichella.

Briton Eddie Irvine was third for Ferrari with Austrian Alexander Wurz fourth in a Benetton despite causing a first corner race stoppage when he barrel-rolled his car. Three other cars were involved but all the drivers made the restart. The safety car came out to slow the field moments after a second multiple accident on the re-start and was used again later because of further accidents and debris on the track.

Schumacher has a long history of being involved in controversial collisions with rivals. In 1994 he won the world championship after colliding with rival Hill, while last year he was stripped of the runner-up spot in the world championship following a collision with Jacques Villeneuve in the final race.

And the German was left fuming himself with former world champion Hill after accusing him of dangerous driving.

The incident occurred on lap 38 when the pair were battling for second place. Hill weaved across the track a number of times to defend his position and, as they headed toward the final chicane, the pair nearly banged wheels.

"That was purely dangerous," Schumacher said. "If you kill somebody, you have to do it in a different way -- not at 320 kph.

"It is all right to defend your position once, but to move across the track three times is unacceptable. There is no excuse for nearly banging wheels.

"I wonder why he doesn't get punished and I find it impossible that such an experienced man does that. I will be having words."

Damon Hill later accused Formula One track judges of giving Michael Schumacher preferential treatment as he launched into a verbal assault on his old rival on Monday.

Hill called the German a "bully" as their war of words which erupted after their clash in Sunday's Canadian Grand Prix in Montreal rumbled on.

"Michael just came straight out of the pits, swooped across the track and Frentzen had nowhere to go," said Hill. "He pushed him off the track.

"It's not the sort of thing that a professional driver really should be doing. There is a way of racing without being a bully about the whole thing.

"Michael is an extremely talented driver, but he is occasionally given the benefit of the doubt. The guy is treated leniently for whatever reason, and I think that gives him the feeling he can do pretty well what he likes in the racing car.

"He has got a set of rules all for himself. We all have to stick to the rules and respect them. If he does not like that he should keep his mouth shut."

Schumacher famously escaped punishment when he collided with Hill in the controversial climax to the 1994 season in Adelaide to pip his British rival by just one point and clinch his first drivers' crown.

The 29-year-old also upset McLaren's David Coulthard in Argentina earlier this year when an overtaking manoeuvre went without censure despite the Scottish driver's claims he was too aggressive.

Schumacher, who claimed he simply did not see Frentzen as he emerged from a pit-stop, had launched into an attack on Hill. He said Hill, driving a Jordan, could have got one of them killed in a battle for second place.

Hill answered Schumacher's claims after the race and returned to the attack on Monday, saying: "Michael was not very happy with my approach, but I am sure he will live. We had some fun - I just wish he had a little bit more humour about him sometimes. We were racing for second position.

"He cannot complain about someone else doing something dangerous when he is doing the same thing."

Schumacher did apologise to Frentzen after the race but did not go and see Hill, despite saying he would have a 'big word' with him.

"He is always threatening to do something, but he is never brave enough," blasted Hill, who eventually retired with an electrical problem.

"If he had come to see me I would not have been sympathetic."
