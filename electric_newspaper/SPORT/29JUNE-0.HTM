Monday June 29, 1998

And the winner is... The post race media conference: Doohan, Biaggi, and Crafar
Mick Doohan speaks unexclusively to The Electric Newspaper:

"It's good fun to have a close race, and it's always close at Assen - first with Schwantz, then Barros, Puig, Criville and now these guys," Doohan said after the race.

"Crafar came past a couple of corners after I caught my knee slider in the grass - that nearly tore me off the bike."

Max Biaggi was happy to lead the championship again. "It wasn't an easy race," grinned Biaggi. "I got a great start but Abe got in my way and then I had to catch Mick. From then on we were so close every lap, and sometimes Crafar came past at the chicane. But I was faster through other sections of the track. The last few laps were a lot of fun - they were the key to the race and I had some really long and controlled rear-wheel slides. On the final lap I tried to go inside Doohan a few times, but he closed the door on me.

"I'm happy because I'm back on the podium and - thanks to our tests at Barcelona last week we are back on track with the bike, so I'm looking forward to Donington. The points lead means nothing though, not at this stage of the season."

Simon Crafar showed he's another new force to be reckoned with in the 500 class, taking the lead in the late stages, finally finishing third for his first 500 podium. "I was quite happy at that pace," said the Red Bull Yamaha man. "I was having to use more corner speed to make up what I was losing in other places, but I had a great time out there!

"I feel really good. Everything just worked really well. I really enjoyed the race. In the beginning I didn't get as good a start as I would have wanted and I was worried that Mick might get away. When I got up there I was happy to just sit behind him. I had really good corner speed but I just didn't have such good drive out of the slow turns. Mick seemed to be dropping the pace at one stage and I saw Barros catching us a bit so I went past him to try and pick up the pace again. I knew that Mick could get past with the tow so I didn't really think I was going to win.

"It was a little scary at times because I was having to ride so hard. This bike has always been good enough to be right up there and the real change has come from Dunlop. I think the front tyre was probably the best out there. I really hope we can carry on now. I like Donington and my results have always been good there and Dunlops work pretty well there so I'll be going for it again."

Alex Criville could only manage sixth at Assen, losing the World Championship in the process. "I've never had such a bad race at Assen," said the Repsol Honda rider. "I had some bad chatter problems which were costing me more than a second a lap. But the battle for the title is still wide open."

Nobuatsu Aoki fought through the pain barrier and shrugged aside physical weakness to claim seventh place in today's Dutch TT - less than 24 hours after a crash in practice threatened to rule him out of the race. Although escaping without fractures, Aoki suffered a painful blow to the base of his spine after a dramatic high-speed high-sider crash in the final practice session. Doctors only passed him fit to race this morning, and he was unable to use his legs and weight to control the bike. Even so, he recovered from a poor start to move gradually through to seventh place, overtaking other works riders in the process, to gain a top-ten finish and valuable championship points.

"I'm exhausted. Because of my crash I couldn't put power through my legs to move around on the bike and help to steer it. It means I had to do everything with my arms and shoulders. After about 15 out of the 20 laps had passed, I could hardly control the bike anymore. But anyway, I finished. I got some points, and there are many races left. I rode the bike in standard condition, with no special settings to make it easier. It was the best setting we had all weekend, and the tyres were working very well. It was just missing a bit of braking performance. That was my only problem."

Ralf Waldmann was forced to withdraw from the Dutch GP following a heavy fall in yesterday's final qualifying session. The German Modenas rider has fractured the radius bone in his right arm, where the bone articulates with the wrist. He is also likely to miss next weekend's British GP, returning for his home race at the new Sachsenring on July 19.

"The fracture is in line, so it's not a huge problem, but there's no way Ralf can twist the throttle and use the brake at the moment," said team trainer Dean Miller. "I'd say he's doubtful for Donington, but we'll see how things look later next week."
