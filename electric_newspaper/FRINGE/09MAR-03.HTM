Monday March 9, 1998

Snakesong/Le Pouvoir
Venue: Playhouse March 4, 8
Reviewed my Matthew Schulz

Snakesong/Le Pouvoir is sexually deviant theatre that shows pain and ecstasy are close cousins.

A dancer gestures the opening and instantly the audience is flung into pitch black, giggles drowned out by a chamber piece that builds to an oppressive volume.

As our eyes adjust, light that could be from a candle reveals an indistinct couple in sexual torment.

By then many in the audience are distressed by the music's hellish invocations, squinting and plugging their ears. We stay on edge for the rest of the show.

Writer director Jan Lauwers of Needcompany has drawn on the work of George Bataille, a writer obsessed by the ecstasy of agony.

This is his starting point for retelling the Greek myth in which the god Zeus, in the guise of a swan, forces himself on the Princess Leda who conceives two children.

The first act reveals a mythic past on a stage bare but for three small columns. `Dead' as a result of their ferocious sexual zeal, Zeus and Leda face trial by the Queen, Leda’s mother, through a haze of drugs.

In a shimmering dress, split to the thigh, Leda denies then recounts taking pleasure from a bird writhing in blood. This is the encounter we half witnessed in the dark.

In the second act, a family in modern Antwerp hold a dinner party for a mother just out of detox, each revealing more violent sexual urges, then flicking the absurd switch, tearing off their clothes and arguing over the use of a sock to filter coffee.

Mother sums it up when she says, `I wanted to prolong the pleasure until they're all screaming'.
