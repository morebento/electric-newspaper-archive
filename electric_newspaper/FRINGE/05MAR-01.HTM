Thursday March 5, 1998

Archer on target
By Elaine Moore

Adelaide Festival director Robyn Archer has called for greater recognition of the benefits the arts can have, if art is properly nurtured and allowed expression in our society.

Ms Archer was speaking at the opening of the National Health Conference at the Convention Centre.

The conference discussed creative approaches to promoting health.

The following is an edited text of her speech:

"Arts is used as a generic term fo drama, dance, music, film (to some extent). It' s something different to sport yet it often involves the same kind of hand-eye co-ordination, the same kind of energy, the same kind of skills training, talent, the same kind of commitment.

"But somehow there is something different between sport and art. Within the framework of Arts Iare two very distinct categorise: that is, art and entertainment.

"Entertainment tries to provide something that's akin to cerebral massage. It's something that you are encouraged to do when people are stressed, when they have had a hard week. It's the kind of work that my Dad says me, 'Rob, I don't want to have to think too hard, I just want to be entertained'.

"I don't discourage entertainers because that's where I began. I didn't discover art till I was about 25, when I was thrown in at the deep end of New Opera South Australia to open the Space Theatre in the Festival Centre. I had no idea that people trained, I came from a tradition of vaudeville music halls, in which one simply got up and did it. You learnt from records, you watched your Dad singing, you started to sing at his gigs. I was an entertainer and I was an entertainer for a very long time and from time to time I am still an entertainer.

"The object of entertainment is to keep people happy. It is not to challenge them; it is to find those things you believe your audience wants and to give it to them.

"I believe that art does exactly the opposite. I think that quite often 90 per cent of the time, art ends up entertaining you. But the intent is different. The intent is to use art in the way a scientist would use it, or an engineer would use it, or a challenging architect would use it - and that is to stir up the mind, to get at the soul , to get at the heart of life and make people think.

"Invariably, when it is truly the intent, art is challenging and deeply disturbing quite a lot of the time. And if you have experienced art I think that this would be the touch test - you would walk out of the experience in some way changed.

It's an interesting way to view the arts, in terms of art and entertainment, because it means that a great deal of opera and a great deal of symphonic music is entertainment.

"A great deal of those modes that we have learnt to love and seen as the pinnacle of arts set out are, in many cases, only entertainment. Take opera sung in in a language you do not understand, which if sung in a language you did understand would prove to be a llot of codswallop. Different themes of social mores long out of date, beautiful costumes, sweet music - which is harmonically familiar for the past hundred years so it doesn't challenge your ear. It sets out to give a nice night out.

"Now, there is nothing wrong with a nice night out - there is nothing wrong with entertainment. It's a fantastic thing to do. In fact, I encourage you to enjoy the Adelaide Festival where you can experience a great deal of entertainment - in particular the construction on the Plaza between Parliament and the Adelaide Festival Centre called the Squeezebox. Every night at 10.30 there will be the greatest accordion-based bands from all around the world entertaining you for free. It is an opportunity to sit and drink and dance and talk the night away under fantastic hot Adelaide Summer nights. That's entertainment and that's absolutely fantastic.

"You're also going to see another terrific show that has some particular relevance to health issues and that's a show called 'Circus Ethiopia'. It has many, many performances at all kinds of different times to suit families. It's entertainment, it's got a great African band, it has wonderful costumes, you see a beautiful company of kids from Ethiopia from nine years old to twenty years old who have made circus theirs because the kids in Ethiopia needed hope.

"It really is a health project in the sense that kids needed to galvanise around something; they needed to develop skills, they needed something to look forward to and something that would bind them together in a society that was becoming increasingly fractured by everything from drought to war, to civil destruction. It's a beautiful show to watch. You will come out revived and entertained and full of wonder that such terrific kids in such stretched circumstances are able to give so much joy and pleasure. Of course some of the 'intent' will be about art, it will be precisely that you will go away not only with a nice afternoon or night out from this company but you will think about the origins of the company and that too may change your world view and that's where that entertainment becomes in my opinion 'art' - because it's the desire of that company to communicate some of their great self-worth that's been earned through this work.

"It's a fantastic thing to see.

"So for many audiences, entertainment is absolutely fantastic. When you experience art as an audience you may come away deeply disturbed. Take for example The Architect's Walk, by Australian playwright Daniel Keene, directed by Adelaide's Tim Maddock for the Red Shed Theatre. It stars Ralph Cotteril - fantastic actor - as Albert Speer, the Nazi architect who was confined in Spandau prison for 20 years. Speer built a garden and in those 20 years he walked 23,000 kilometres around circularly in his garden and imagined travelling around the world. It is a deeply disturbing show, frightening and horrifying. It has phenomenal skills by the actors and the musicians. However I believe that our society at the moment values entertainment over art. I think that if we are trying to say that the arts are good for our health, we have to be very careful that we're not just saying entertainment is good for our health.

"Entertainers, essentially, simply capitalise on given values. Artists make people think, disturb them, question, be oppositional, say 'this is the way I see the ills of life or this is the depth or the extreme of passion that I want'.

"And quite often, people don't want to experience those extremes - daily life is getting harder and more stressfull. I think that if people are getting more unhealthy and feeling less well because they are working 16-hour jobs and feel immense pressure in all directions - I believe that we need artists to remind us what the true values of life are.

"Entertainers won't usually do that. They'll give you a good laugh and that's fantastic - and in the Adelaide Festival this year are lots of laughing and lots of good times and you will just do what I do when I sing and that is 'blow it out' and be tired and go home and wake up refreshed from that big blow out.

"If I don't have a good sing, a really big sing once a month, I start to get physically ill. So if I haven't actually got a gig before a 2000-strong audience, which is the best blow, I put on the Abba CD and I drive out into the desert and I scream my head off. So that's important and I hope you do a lot of that in the Adelaide Festival because that's what the big buzz is about.

"But I also believe that we need to cherish the difficult, hard, challenging artists, because they are the ones who are saying don't just sit back and let it flow over you, get moving that gift that we are given, to really chew out what life is really about.

"I believe that if we don't do that we will become a sick society.

"I believe that artists first and foremost, the hard ones, the tough ones, the difficult ones, the ones who remain poor and are quite often called wankers - I think they are the ones that are the true heros - they are, to me, the living national treasures that must be cherished above all because if they get a society to think, to question, to challenge.

"Unless we keep challenging in that way, and arts leads by entertaining people, by getting them in and entertaining them but at the same time twisting that knife every time to say think about this, feel this, feel it deeply, don't let things skim along the surface, feel it so deeply that you want to shout or scream or cry or walk out into the night after this experience and say I don't know what the world is anymore, this is a huge challenge.

"When artists in challenge in that way and get the creative muscle flowing, it flows on to science, to engineering, to health, to medicine, to all those otherthings in society.

"Our society is in danger of just trying to aim for comfort because life is too hard. I've never accepted that. I think you come out more rejuvenated by being challenged than you do by simply being satisfied. I think the best sign of a healthy society will be when people debate, when they talk to each other, when they value artists who challenge us all the time instead of only valuing the entertainers.

"Real heros will never be recognised publicly. They'll have a difficult life, they may die unsatisfied. But their works actually keep us sane, keeps us healthy and keeps us challenging all those bits of society that are in danger of going wrong. "
