Monday March 16, 1998

Gone into new territories
by Elaine Moore

The Festival was over. Robyn Archer, the Artistic Director of the Festival, and General Manager Nicholas Heyward, were congratulating themselves - and everyone else - for producing what amounted to a classic Adelaide Festival.

And as she praised the audience's discerning tastes, Ms Archer warned that Adelaide's 2000 Festival will have a very different personality.

The 1998 Adelaide Festival reinforced the popularity of the strong model, trialed by past festivals, of an assorted selection of classics and new pieces, Ms Archer said. However, in the year 2000 we can expect something different from a festival that is brewing under the working title "The New Territory".

"I would like to go for things that are fresh out of the oven. Not necessarily pushing the boundaries of art anymore than this festival has, but in some cases - yes, so that for the first time we have the situation that instead of a range, everything is fresh out the oven", she said.

How different the pieces will be, partially depends on what Ms Archer finds out on her overseas trip later this year. The year 2000 Festival is expected to be launched in May 1998.

At the moment, the Artistic Director and General Manager are basking in a feeling of satisfaction and expectation with Adelaide's festival abilities. With box office figures expected to pass $2 million and an arts-appetised audience, Ms Archer is hoping that Adelaide can really live up to its Festival State potential. And lunch with the Premier on today may secure support for propagating Archer's great expectations of an arts-led recovery.

Many of 1998 Festival performances were sold out or otherwise close to capacity. Writers Week had approximately 45,000 people visit , 20% up on last year. On average 1,000 people attended its daily sessions.

Over 2,000 people went to the lunch-time Forums and around 2,000 people packed into The Squeezebox each night.

Ms Archer was happy with the popularity and participation in the free community- orientated events, praising the public's involvement in creating an atmosphere of festivity.

"I set out to offer a festival which would remind audiences of what an Adelaide festival means - the whole huge multi-arts experience and I think that has happened", Ms Archer said.

As this year's festival draws to a close the twice crowned Festival Artistic Director seems to be summing up her energy to let go off the affectionately approved, classic model of a festival to stride into 'new territories'. Ms Archer firmly believes that "...in 2000 it's worth stepping out".
