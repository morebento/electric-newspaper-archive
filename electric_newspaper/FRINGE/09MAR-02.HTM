Monday March 9, 1998

The right note: Songs of the Wanderers
Venue: Festival Theatre
Reviewed by Bridget Bannear

Anyone who has practised meditation or yoga may identify with this spectacular event. It opens to the unamplified sound of rice falling from the ceiling onto a monk standing rock-still in prayer on the left front of the stage for the entire performance - a mesmerising scene.

One is barely distracted as players appear at the rear of the stage moving very steadily in slow motion.

The pace picks up imperceptibly - during a version of "Prayer to the Sun" the performers dive into piles of rice snapping the viewer out of meditative repose. The audience doesn't need to understand everything to feel the power of certain scenes, including those dealing with procreation.

Very beautiful, extraordinarily difficult movements displayed huge strength, control, suppleness and practice. At one point six women execute a neck stand, with their heads to one side. All I could think of was the chiropractor.

Haunting Tibetan chants, the tinkling of bells, the sound of body parts hitting the ground and the shhhhh of rice falling are the only accompaniment to the performance. Which brings me to the four tonnes of rice. We had the steady stream on the highly polished head of the monk, spectacular walls of rice across the entire stage, first at the front then further back, and then an almighty thunk as an entire tonne hit the ground at once. Lighting effects added to the variations.

The rice seemed to "dance" as well - making arcs and fountains from the hands of the performers who were contorting in the piles all over the stage.

I was concerned for the well being of the monk on account of the accumulative effect of a tonne of rice falling on his head, but he looked OK when he came out to acknowledge the applause at the end.

Likewise the way rice was flung about reminded me of parents yelling at children to stop throwing sand, but no-one (on the stage) seemed to sustain eye damage.

Despite the tremendous round of applause at the end, the audience was not neccessarily prepared for the slowness of the opening.

An exceptional performance.
