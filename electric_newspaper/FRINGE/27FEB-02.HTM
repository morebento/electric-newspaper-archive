Friday February 27, 1998

BANJO - A Dramatic Performance
Reviewed by Cathy Adamek

The Red Ochre Bar and Grill - renowned for its innovative bush tucker - provided an appropriately mod oz venue for Craig Gillespie's 'Banjo'.

A one man show, Gillespie has woven a tighty- paced narrative out of Andrew Barton (Banjo) Patterson's poetry, journalistic narratives and war correspondence.

The small upstairs loft was effectively adapted by good economic lighting, an unobtrusive and well incorporated sound track and a selection of leathery, woolly, brown Aussie type props. (The 30 degree heat and real fly helped spice up the authenticity too.)

These provided the actor with his basic instruments to create different spaces and to transform into a rather overwhelming array of characters and 'voices' from our mythic past.

During the one hour show our man works very hard, and full credit to him. One-man shows place full demands on the talents of an actor and are an ambitious undertaking.

There is a lot of good writing, and Craig Gillespie handles the poetry intelligently and with good vocal energy and variety.

Whether it was the inhibiting size of the space, or first night nerves, I felt Gillespie didn't relax sufficiently until the riveting and fully-focused 'The Man from Ironbank' in which he allowed his vocal and physical characteristics to fully develop with detail and texture, drawing the audience into his world rather than us merely observing it.

This show will appeal to people who have a strong affinity with all those enduring Australian archetypes: wild colonial thigh-slapping larks and honest days droving, the Diggers were simple lads done over by the British - all the tales are there.

I find Patterson's work most effective in the journalistic vignettes and local colour poetry. The obvious sentiment in a few sections failed to stir much deep emotion on my part : too many connections with grade 7 rote learnt poems - mulgas, brolgas and brown dry creek beds for my taste.

But the mostly older audience greeted it with deserved warmth and appreciation.

It is a well wrought, pleasant piece of heartfelt theatrical patriotism.
