Li'l Abner No. 17
by Al Capp
pub Kitchen Sink Press copyright 1951
rrp $US18.95
reviewed by Denise Wels

When I was a very small child, I was enamoured of an American comic strip, 'Li'l Abner' by Al Capp.

This strip had a profound effect on the American people. Even here in Australia surely we have all heard of Sadie Hawkins Day, when eligible bachelors are chased by man-hungry spinsters in search of a mate. A village of Dogpatch, named after the hillbilly Yokums' home, was set up in Arkansas (even though the comic strip town was in Kentucky). Two movies about Li'l Abner have been made, one a musical, and even today there are revivals of the musical throughout America. There was even a stamp featuring Li'l Abner and Daisy Mae issued.

There are many sites on the Net devoted to Al Capp, Li'l Abner and Dogpatch but my favourite is at http://www.cei.net/~grtescps/ . If you are a Li'l Abner enthusiast, it is worth looking at.

This particular volume is devoted to strips appearing in the dailies in 1951 and contains rather longer adventures than those in other volumes. It includes an article by Capp which escalated the feud between him and Ham Fisher, the creator of the strip 'Joe Palooka' (another of my childhood favourites). Capp worked for Fisher on 'Joe Palooka' for a time, and his depiction of a starving artist's encounter with The Monster was apparently a thinly veiled rendition of the horrors Capp endured - albeit heavily exaggerated (one trusts) - of working for Fisher.

Capp had a sharp eye for human foibles and no one was safe from his barbed brush. He poked fun at the rich and famous, the army, universities, wrestlers, politicians and virtually everyone else around. This volume also contains a spoof on the endurance and inscrutability of Indians, now known as Native Americans. In those days there was no such thing as political correctness. I have an idea Capp would have had great fun with that particular notion.

Abner was the perennial, goodhearted innocent, prey to all the money making villains wishing to exploit his talents. Daisy Mae, his voluptuous would-be girl friend (and eventual wife, although not in this volume) was the unbelievably virtuous and loyal American maiden. Mammy Yokum was shrewd, clever and able to evaluate any wickedness in a very short time, and then to defeat it by sheer brute strength, while Pappy Yokum was another naif.

In this volume, Li'l Abner has a very close brush with matrimony when Nancy O sets out to capture him, and in the process a competition to discover the sweetest face in America was set in motion.

Alas, there is no mention of Kickapoo Joy Juice in this book (although I noticed a voodoo doll of Hairless Joe who, along with Lonesome Polecat was responsible for its manufacture, in the possession of hag Nightmare Alice, the evil witch). The Dogpatch Ham rates a mention as it is intended to succour and nourish Abner during one of his forays to University ... in this volume it is Scalp U, the university of barbering (what fun Capp would have with our universities today with some of the then unlikely subjects now taught, some even including the work of Capp himself). The Dogpatch Ham seems to be closely related to the Shmoo, of other times.

We meet Marrying Sam as he prepares to officiate on Sadie Hawkins Day, together with gorgeous Moonbeam McSwine, Daisy Mae's relatives the wicked Scraggs, the pig Salomey (an ancestress of 'Babe'?), Old Man Mose who predicts that Abner will escape all predators aided by Orson Wells, and many others of Capp's regulars.

Capp's characters were necessarily overdrawn : his heroes were larger than life and very handsome, his villains incredibly evil and ugly - but all, somehow, comprised a far simpler day than our own.
