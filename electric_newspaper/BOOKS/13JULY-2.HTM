Kat Scratch Fever
by Karen Kijewski
pub Berkley Books
rrp $US6.99 copyright 1997
reviewed by Denise Wels

I was surprised, when inspecting the printing history of this book, to note this was the second printing: presumably the first was in hard cover. My surprise was due to the fact that I did not think a book of this calibre would arouse sufficient interest to prompt more than one printing.

The bio notes that Karen Kijewski is a triple award winner of seven previous Kat Colorado thrillers. It also states that she lives in Sacramento, California (where the action of the novel takes place) but beyond that, nothing but a photograph. Industrious research on my part dug up further facts: she is in her fifties, married in the past couple of years and is a former English teacher and bartender. And apparently is very protective of her privacy.

'Kat Scratch Fever' deals with the nasty topic of blackmail. Kat Colorado, Kijewski's PI, has been hired by a law firm to investigate a client, James Randolph, as he has appeared to be depressed in the recent past. Kat follows him... and discovers a dead body. Randolph has killed himself.

Investigations from that point turn up the fact that Randolph was being blackmailed. Further digging discloses that quite a few prominent people are being blackmailed with the unlikely slant that the blackmail payments were usually one off, and the money was to be paid to a charity for children.

It is unfortunate that books about widows and children give all too great a scope for authors to dally with sentimentality and, alas, Kijewski has not been able to resist the impulse to dabble her toes in the puddle of that topic. On the whole, she manages to maintain a hardboiled surface, but every now and then a crack appears.

Perhaps it is the fact that, as an Australian, I have been exposed to politicians' pasts being dug up and flaunted before the public, yet those people surviving the most scurrilous stories about them and going to on to further their careers, but the reasons Kijewski provides her blackmail victims for being blackmailed simply do not seem to me to be sufficient for them to fork out large sums of money, or, in Randolph's case, to commit suicide.

There is a modicum of violence present in the book, with a bit too much descriptive emphasis on the results thereof, something which I always tend to scan through quickly, accompanying the reading with a ladylike shudder. I did not find Kijewski's characters terribly interesting nor very attractive. Nor did I find the references to Kat's romantic involvement more than heigh-ho. Her apparent attraction to a particularly slimy journalist was, for me, puzzling and I certainly was not urging her on to consummate the inclination, although I think that was the author's intention.

Kijewski's writing is competent. Since she has received three awards I feel it must be a lack in my own appreciation that provoked such a luke warm response. At least she did not commit the (for me) unforgiveable linguistic crime of using 'off of', always a sure fire way of alienating me from an author.
