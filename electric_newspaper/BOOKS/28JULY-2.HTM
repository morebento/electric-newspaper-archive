Puberty Blues
by Gabrielle Carey and Kathy Lette
pub McPhee Gribble
rrp $12.95 copyright 1979
reviewed by Denise Wels

After reading "Puberty Blues,' first published almost 20 years ago, I sat stunned and depressed. Yes, there are parts of the book that are extremely funny, and if one can discount the fact that it is autobiographical, written by a pair of 18 year-olds, then one can disregard the darker implications. Initially.

The book is written in what, I suppose, could be called Australian dialect. The question arises as to how successful the book was overseas since it must have been incomprehensible for an American audience at least. The first part of the story is quite innocent as it describes the scrambling for position on Cronulla Beach, south of Sydney by the growing children. The beach is divided up into class areas and the adolescents ascend the social scale according to the sections of the beach on which they are permitted to disport themselves.

The boys, as seen through the eyes of the two girls writing the book, are totally self absorbed, preferring to surf than spend time other than that in which they are unable to surf, with their girl friends. The couples appear to pair themselves off according to what they look like as a unit rather than any emotional tie. Then it is made clear that thirteen year old females, and underdeveloped ones at that, are expected to have sex with over developed seventeen year old males.

Some girls are brutalised as they are regularly gang raped by the boys. Then the authors describe the insidious introduction of drugs into their culture. The last two pages are a damning and horrifying list of what happened to the group of adolescents when they grew up, and the ghastly fates most of them suffered.

To one who lived in Sydney during the sixties the narrative would represent an interesting study, reflecting as it does what would have been observed of life in general at the time. A realisation that this is what really went on below surface observation could prove chilling, lessening the humour for an unwary reader.

One can hope that coming of age for the adolescents of today is not as harrowing, but perhaps things have not improved. The book is as relevant today as it was in 1979.
