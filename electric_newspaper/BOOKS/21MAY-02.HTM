'Silent Conspiracy. A Lincoln Keller Mystery'
by Lee E. Meadows
published by Proctor
rrp $US24.95.
reviewed by Denise Wels

Lee E. Meadows is a black American and I believe this is his first novel, although he apparently has almost finished a second, also featuring Lincoln Keller, and begun a third featuring a character making brief appearances in the first two.

The book is an exploration of the conditions black Americans in Detroit suffered through in the fifties. I was told that several incidents in the book were thinly-veiled real life occurrences, including the moving funeral scene toward the end of the book. An examination of the social ills besetting black American of the time is almost as fascinating as the mystery itself.

P.I.Lincoln Keller is drinking in a Bar and Grill when he is approached by a beautiful black woman with the unlikely name of Erotica. This woman persuades Keller to investigate the mystery of the disappearance of a group of five musicians known as The Sentiments who disappeared after a performance in Johnny's Lounge in Detroit in the fifties. For some reason her dying husband wants to know the fate of these men.

Keller agrees to investigate the mystery but of course he thereby puts himself in considerable danger. At one stage his life is saved by an engaging cat (of a feline type) named Free Ride.

Meadows has undertaken considerable research for this book in the area known as 'the Blackbottom' of Detroit. The entire book bears the aura of authenticity. He has plotted the novel with considerable ingenuity and one wonders if anything is as it seems. Gradually the investigation of the disappearance of the musicians is subsumed in the further mystery of the disapeparnce of a white woman, Cheryl Crown, known as Swan.

Keller travels over the state of Michigan following one false trail after another, on the way encountering truly delightful people such as the redoubtable Mrs. Dibell, an eighty-six year old who was responsible for the early musical education of the Sentiments and who holds a vital clue to the resolution of the mystery (also a keen gardener).

I thoroughly enjoyed the descriptions of Detroit, past and present and Michigan as explored by Keller. I doubt a more thorough exploration of Erotica is likely to enthrall a woman reader but I find myself really looking forward to further examples of Meadows' writing.

I don't know if this book is available in Australian bookstores, but if you are reading this review you would know about Barnes and Noble, and Amazon Books which are Internet bookshops.
