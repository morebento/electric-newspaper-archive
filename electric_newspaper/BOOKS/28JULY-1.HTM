Cybersaur
by Claire Carmichael
a Mark Macleod book from Random House Australia
rrp $9.95
reviewed by Denise Wels

'Cybersaur' is the second book in the Virtual Realities trilogy. This fact is not mentioned anywhere at the front of the book which is a nuisance for anyone wishing to begin at Book One of a series. Perhaps I am unusually slow, but it was only after reading a couple of chapters that I twigged that it was a continuation of an earlier beginning. Nonetheless, it stands alone.

Claire Carmichael is an Australian author living in Sydney. The bio states that she travelled and taught but has now settled down to writing full time. Perhaps her claim that she bought and read Hawking's 'Brief History of Time' indicates that she could have an interest in science fiction. She writes a good story!

I am not sure whether 'Cybersaur' should be categorised as science fiction or fantasy ... probably the former. It deals with the ability of a young boy, Andy Tremain, to conjure up dinosaurs from his subconscious when under the influence of Virtual Reality. These creations are not under his conscious control. On reading the description of the demise of one of his monsters, I found myself wondering if a child's subconscious would have a sufficient grasp of anatomy to provide the animal with all the bits and pieces that spilled out when it was ripped apart.

The three Tremain siblings together with their irritating older cousin Steven (who sees himself as the 'adult' of the group, despite only being in his early twenties, and therefore in loco parentis to the others) are taken by their Aunt Laura, an expert developer of Virtual Reality software, from Australia to the U.S. so that Andy's talents can be studied and controlled. Raine and Max are Andy's older twin siblings and are very protective of their younger brother.

Laura Tremain leaves the children at the H. William Kaplan Institute, a clinic to which difficult adolescents are brought by despairing parents for 'tough love'. This alerts the Tremain youngsters to the fact that they have become prisoners and their energies are then directed to rescuing Andy from those who would 'help' him ... including Laura's wicked ex-husband who wants to make millions out of Andy after having illegally copied Laura's work.I thought the idea of using lasers to create images on the retina rather than relying on miniature TV screens was a very nice touch.

The children, together with unregenerate Kelly who has fooled the authorities at the Institute into granting her privileges, manage to escape to Las Vegas where some of the most colourful scenes take place, including a battle between two tyrannosaurus rex (reges?). Carmichael depicts the Las Vegas casinos in all their tinsel tawdriness very entertainingly.

I have to wonder, and I imagine children would too, since they seem to be more aware of inconsistencies than I, just how the baddies would be able to convince government authorities, including the C.I.A., that a small boy would be able to create monsters, before actually seeing the evidence. Perhaps children have been indoctrinated sufficiently to know instantly that the C.I.A. are all powerful. all knowing and probably also pretty evil and therefore are able to accept such a premise.

The book moves along well, the plot is involving and the character of Andy is very realistic ... probably as a result of the author's experience in teaching! Certainly as a childrens' suspense novel 'Cybersaur' is a success.
