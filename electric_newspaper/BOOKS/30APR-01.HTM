A different fantasy: Fiddler Fair by Mercedes Lackey pub Baen
Reviewed by Denise Wels

I am not a great fan of what passes these days for fantasy. In the good old days (says she creakily) fantasy masqueraded under the science fiction (not sci-fi) banner. Nowadays you still find a few fantasies listed under that category, but not many.

I read Lackey's 'Fire Rose' and felt it was really only wish fulfilment on paper. The first quarrel I have with the anthology, and one assumes Ms Lackey cannot be held responsible for this particular failing, is the glaring error of the artist who created the cover. The story it represents, 'Fiddler Fair', concerns a girl who dresses as a boy in order to compete in trials to permit her to be taken on as an apprentice within the Bardic Guild. Part of the story makes it quite clear what would happen to a girl who attempted to enter if allowing herself to be seen as a girl. If the cover is supposed to be true to the story, then that particular world has strange looking boys who wear plunging necklines and suffer from severe gynecomastia.

Most of the stories have appeared in other anthologies first, and Lackey details these, although at least one is original to this collection. Some were written in collaboration, one with Larry Dixon with whom she apparently consults before writing anything at all. The stories on the whole, are quite polished, but they lack tension. When two maidens wander independently through a forest ('The Cup and the Caldron') in an attempt to find a talisman that will Save Their Land (shades of Thomas Covenant), and are set upon by bandits who threaten to rape them, I didn't believe they would not be rescued somehow nor that each would desert the new found stranger of opposing beliefs in order to save her own skin.

I took issue with the author's depiction of a modern day Arthur ('Once and Future') because I feel her protagonist displayed no characteristics in common with the mythical King. If you're going to set about reincarnating someone, lady, at least make him a credible rebirth. This bloke could more believably be a reincarnation of Mordred!

The one story that I felt contained a modicum of plot tension was 'The Enemy of My Enemy' which appeared in a Friends of the Horseclans anthology. Even so I felt it obvious that the heroine and hero would escape all dangers unscathed.

Probably my jaded palate simply needs more spicy fare (I am a devotee of Charles de Lint's urban fantasies as well as Orson Scott Card's writing): fantasy addicts will likely swoon on reading this collection.
