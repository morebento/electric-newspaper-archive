The Flight of the Magical paper Cranes
by Lea Stevens
This is an edited text of Ms Steven's adjournment debate on March 25.
Ms Stevens is the Labor Member for Elizabeth.

Last Wednesday I had the honour of attending the launch by Elizabeth Mansutti of a book 'The Flight of the Magical Paper Cranes' in the courtyard of the Migration Muesum on North Terrace.

The book is a compilation of stories by English-as-a-second-language students at Adelaide's Fremont-Elizabeth City High School.

'The Flight of the Magical Paper Cranes' is the fourth book produced by those students during the past 10 years, and is the first to carry students out of Australia and across the world; the other books in the series tell of journeys into Australia.

The book centres around students' imaginings of a paper crane flying out from the school's English-as-a-second-language classroom to various countries from which the students themselves came, or which they've experienced in some way.

There are 1000 paper cranes hanging from classroom's ceiling and they were made at the Blair Athol language centre for new migrants. Teacher Janice Shephard writes the book's first story, describing why the cranes were made and that the class had boys and girls from Serbia and Bosnia straight from the war.

"As we made the cranes, conversation began to flow, friendships were made... we were united by that single belief, a hope for the human spirit which can lift itself up and beyond the agony of dispair," Ms Shephard writes.

At the school last year, ESL students made another 1000 paper cranes, including a big golden one for wishing under. Ms Shephard says she was in the classroom one night watching the cranes move in the breeze, when she imagined the cranes flying off across the world to various places. She and her colleague, teacher Peter Causby, talked to students about the concept and asked them to write many stories about becoming paper cranes and flying away.

They could afford to print only 13 of the stories by the students in the small book, but they are beautiful stories. An El Salvadorean student designed the front cover and the many illustrations that accompany the short stories.

Many of the writers were able to express feelings of loss, and migration experiences through these stories. For example, one student writes, "I see not only death; I see babies being born. It gives happiness. I see not only people lose each other; I see people find each other. I see not only bad things; I see good things."

The book is on sale for $5 a copy, and it is a global experience from second language learners.
