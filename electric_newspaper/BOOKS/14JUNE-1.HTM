'Stopping for a Spell'
by Diana Wynne Jones
pub. Puffin (this edition of the three stories published 1996)
rrp $US3.99
reviewed by Denise Wels

Diana Wynne Jones writes ostensibly for children, from the very young (to whom this book is directed) to the young adult spectrum. The author always writes about magic and frequently about time travel. This anthology features only magic.

The three stories in this volume are 'Chair Person', 'The Four Grannies' and 'Who Got Rid of Angus Flint?'.

After looking for the last-mentioned story for years I was bitterly disappointed to discover, when I conveyed the gladsome tidings, that my son, who was the original object of my intended largesse, was no longer interested in having me sit by his bedside to read it to him! 'Sharper than a serpent's tooth 'tis to have an ungrateful child' Just because he is going on 21... Oh well, I read (and enjoyed) it myself.

Wynne Jones has an accurate eye for adult foibles and recognises and employs the honesty peculiar to children in writing about them.

In 'Chair Person', Auntie Christa is the typically organising woman who always manages to get everyone else to do all the hard work while being too busy with overseeing projects to do any of the donkey labour herself. She is perpetrating a children's party on behalf of the Caring Society and has turned up at the home of Marcia and Simon's family bearing a box of broken toys she has obtained from Mr. Pennyfeather's secondhand shop, and demanding the cooperation of the family in helping with the party the next Saturday afternoon.

Auntie Christa dumps the box of toys, one of which is a Conjuror's Set containing a leaky Crystal Ball, on an ugly old armchair which the family had decided to throw out so they could buy a new one. The family manages to manoeuvre the old armchair into their shed so they will have room in the house for the new chair but....The next morning they hear thumps coming from the shed and discover the armchair is now a person... an ugly and unpleasant person, but a person nonetheless. The rest of the story is devoted to the family's attempts both to satisfy Auntie Christa and rid themselves of garrulous and bossy Chair Person.

The next story is 'The Four Grannies'. Erg's Dad and Emily's mum have divorced previous partners and married each other, thus giving the children a set of four grannies.

The parents intend going away, but someone must be found to care for the two children during the parents' time away. Of course, as every parent knows, there is always a granny who would be very anxious to look after children. In this instance, each granny is strangely reluctant so to do.

Just as it looks as though the children might be forced to look after themselves, first one, then all, of the grannies arrives, possibly as the result of Erg's magic wishing machine.

Then Emily disappears, again, possibly as a result of the wishing machine.

Erg is horrified... he must take care of all the grannies, keeping them out of his way and out of trouble, while attempting to retrieve Emily from whatever limbo to which she has been banished by his ill-advised wishing.

At one point Erg displays the devastating honesty common to children by saying innocently to the four squabbling grannies that he never realised before how much they hated one another ... which provokes false smarminess and a temporary truce amongst his elders.

The final story in the collection is 'Who Got Rid of Angus Flint'.

Angus Flint at the sort-of invitation of Candida's father, comes to visit that family after his wife leaves him (and no wonder).

How many of us have had to entertain unwanted guests who decide to come to visit and take over the domination of the hosuehold, never wanting to leave? (I have!) Angus Flint is such a visitor. Candida's older sister is away on a vacation of her own, thus Angus is able to take over her bed while making himself thoroughly unpopular with the remaining three children and their parents. How to be rid of him?

In this story, too, Wynne Jones has made use of animated furniture to bring about her happy ending.

I would thoroughly recommend this book as well as Diana Wynne Jones' tales for older children, to all wanting to read enjoyable books to their children.
