The Secret
by Cynthia Victor
pub Onyx Books
rrp $US6.99 copyright 1997
reviewed by Denise Wels

Cynthia Victor is the pen name of Cynthia Katz and Victoria Skurnick. The bio on the back jacket announces that 'she' lives in Connecticut and New York City.

The novel is not badly written. Perhaps the character of Stephen could be described as caricature and certainly his metamorphosis from nice bloke, when he and future wife Miranda are students, to ruthless, self seeking allround rogue is not frantically convincing. A few more hints as to the possibility of such a change should, in my opinion at least, have been included at the start of the tale. Miranda's character, on the other hand, is pretty consistent from the first chapter although she does not remain listlessly static from the beginning: she develops quite well.

The authors make a reference to the traumas suffered by children after a major tragedy occurs in their lives, but I think they could have dwelt on this a little longer and expanded on it. This is something that happens to many people and I don't think a bit more of an exposition would have been detrimental to the action.

Briefly, Miranda is one of two siblings born of a broken marriage. When still at college she falls in love with Stephen who is the child of two academics who cannot understand how they produced a less than super intelligent son. He and Miranda decide to get married and Stephen becomes an advertising executive, thereby disappointing his parents.

Whilst working for an advertising firm, Stephen plays around with the idea of writing a 'Ninja' novel ... working out the formula for the first and succeeding novels. His success amazes both him and Miranda. The secret of his identity must be preserved, though not from Miranda, as his parents would disapprove.

Miranda is a copywriter for an advertising firm but as Stephen accumulates more and more money, he convinces Miranda she ought to stop work. Then their children arrive and she does not have much alternative to not working.

Three children and some millions of dollars later, Stephen decides Miranda is not the wife for him and begins to siphon money from their accounts and hide it so that only he can gain access to it. Then a chance bombing has people thinking he has been killed so he is able disappear and resume life elsewhere under a different name, deserting his wife and children and, incidentally, his pen name since he is certain he can continue writing, but this time a 'serious' novel, with another nom de plume.

What can Miranda do? That is pretty predictable stuff. How she manages to save herself and her children from poverty does not really contain much tension. I am used to the action of mysteries and suspense novels so perhaps am too demanding to read Romance. There is a probably obligatory tepid sex scene, but I am left wondering if that is the standard fare that is presented to the romance hungry reading population out there. Certainly it served neither to titillate my imagination nor to inflame the tattered shreds of my libido.

This being said, I must admit I enjoyed the book to the extent that if another by Cynthia Victor comes my way ... I shall read it.
