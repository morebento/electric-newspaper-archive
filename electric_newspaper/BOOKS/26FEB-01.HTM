MUTUAL CONTEMPT : LYNDON JOHNSON, ROBERT KENNEDY AND THE FEUD THAT DEFINED A DECADE.
JEFF SHESOL. W.W. NORTON. $49.95 (HC)
Reviewed by Simon Royal

"Lyndon Johnson and Bobby Kennedy loathed one another". Hardly an opening line that stands on ceremony, but then with with JFK's constitutional and familial heirs describing one another respectively as "...an animal in many ways" and a "“grandstanding little runt", this was hardly a relationship of great finesse. It is also the point of departure and conclusion of Jeff Shesol's book, Mutual Contempt: Lyndon Johnson, Robert Kennedy and the feud that defined a decade.

Defining a decade is a towering claim for a book, but so were the figures and the times. There's no argument about how Robert Kennedy and Lyndon Johnson bestrode the American political stage post JFK. But was theirs the defining relationship...hmm? And would you want to write a 500 page book about it? And would other people want to read it? Well I would. But if we are declaring interests, I have long loved the Kennedys, especially that Jack. Each ‘new' Herschesque revelation about JFK's inability to remain in his own pants for long makes me more fond of him and his times. After all, a leader who spends more time doing it to their staff, starlets and other vaguely sentient beings, has less time to do it to his or her country. Think of it as a healthy tonic for democracy. But this isn't about JFK, its about the two men who were, at once, the custodians and casualties of Camelot.

Shesol sketches beautifully the magnitude of the events that contained and shaped the relationship between Robert Kennedy and Lyndon Johnson. These were very different men. So many things separated the two: geography, background and temperament to name a few. Then there was the jockeying for the Democratic nomination for President in the 1960 election. All of these things divided John Kennedy and Johnson too, but were not divisions as such. Whereas the older Kennedy and Johnson had, after the bitterness of their contest in 1960, a cordial relationship, neither RFK or LBJ forgave one another the slings and arrows of their first major political encounter. LBJ, the consummate backroom, backslapping politician was everything the cool and somewhat rigid Robert Kennedy detested. As a result, RFK had no appreciation of the ‘qualities' that made Johnson such a formidable legislator. Johnson with vast insecurities to match his vast abilities, could not fathom anyone who wasn't pre-eminently a political creature.

And then there was Dallas. We forget now, if we are old enough to rememder, just how cataclclysmic an event John Kennedy's murder was. In the heightened anxieties of the Cold War, the assassination was arguably the most traumatic transtion of power were in American history. Neither RFK or LBJ had the style or disposition to make it any different. Here Shesol's powers of description falter a little.

He talks of Johnson working the phones in the days immediately after Dallas, but doesn't quite convey what a man possessed he was. Johnson's dedication to the ‘dog and bone' in late November 1963 makes a '90's phone company's efforts to poach a competitor's business look lethargic.

We know from dictaphone ta!pes, made on LBJ's orders, that the man beseeched everyone from the janitor to the joint chiefs of staff with the line, "I need you more than he[JFK} did". And we know, from those tapes, how haunted Johnson was from the very outset of his Presidency by the irreconcilable aims of accepting the mantle, placating the family, and outshining his predecessor.

So what kept Johnson and Kennedy in one another's orbits for so long? The usual suspects: ambition and expendiency. Revealing the heights, or depths, of those ambitions is one of the great strenghts of Shesol's book. In public Johnson needed the support of the Kennedys, such was the raw tug of affection for John F. Kennedy. For RFK, paradoxically, he could not challenge Johnson openly until just before his own death. There was always the fear of resurrecting the old charges of "ruthless Bobby". And believe it or not, RFK needed Johnson's presidential prestige for his political purposes. I had no idear just how far Robert Kennedy rode on Johnson's coat tails into the Senate in 1964.

It is, however, the abysmal depths of Johnson's venality and paranoia that is most arresting in Mutual Contempt. Johnson had won one of the greatest mandates in presidential history in the election of 1964. He passed all of John Kennedy's stymied legislation - a testament of course to the martyred President, and also to LBJ's greater skills as a legislator. Then he hit Congress with a welter of more legislation, again unmatched in American history. But still Johnson could not escape his shadows of JFK, or the living legacy RFK. Not even RFK's assassination changed LBJ's tendency to smallness.

"On the morning of June 6, within hours of Kennedy's death, within hours of declaring Kennedy a noble and compassionate leader, a good and faithful servant of the people, the president phoned Clark Clifford and demanded to know whether Bobby had the right to be buried at Arlington. I was stunned, Clifford recalled. I was dumbfounded. The president's call marked one of the saddest experiences in my long friendship with Lyndon Johnson".

There was an element of Texas hoedown gone haywire to Johnson in the closing days of his Presidency. He though nothing of tying up the bureaucracy with byzantine requests to make sure this minor appointment, or that minor piece of legislation wasn't a "Kinnidy Kinspiricy". Lyndon Johnson just couldn't rise above himself to apply to RFK one of his own political maxims: (to be said with a Texan darwl): "It's better to have a man inside your tent pissin' out, than have him on the outside pissin' in". The Liberal Party of South Australia please take note.

If Lyndon Johnson is one of the great tragic conundrums of morden U.S. history, then RFK emerges as one of the great unlikely ditherers. Another Shesol strength is his revelation of the extent of Rob!ert Kennedy's indecision over running for President in 1968. Not so much ruthless Bobby, as rudderless Bobby.

Shesol's book is a portrait of looming figures, set against a titanic backdrop of Dallas, Vietnam and the civil rights explosion. Both Bobby and LBJ changed policy and positions, often for the worse, simply out of desire to gazump the other. America no doubt suffered because of their mutual contempt: but the question is how much? Did the relationship between RFK and LBJ define the decade? I am not so sure it did. Certainly the rivalry divided the Democrats, but the things that were to give rise to Reagan's democrats of the 80s, ie. the defection of traditional southern Democrats, had a life beyond Kennedy and Johnson. Changing demographics, incomes and Ronald Reagan himself tell us more about how that happened. It was certainly a relationship that defined Lyndon Johnson's approach to government. Then again, Johnson was what he was with no help from Bobby: hardly a relationship in the proper sense. Bobby's distaste for Johnson may well have driven him to the Senate and on the road to the Presidency, but let's not forget it was a journey he didn't complete.

Surely the great defining political relationship of the time was that of John Kennedy and Richard Nixon. Christopher Matthews in his excellent book, Kennedy and Nixon: The rivalry that shaped post war America, persuasively argues just this point. This was the relationship that in a way helped ease the paranoid Nixon on to the road to Watergate.

Oh dear, those naughty Kennedy brothers. Into everyone... and everything: a Kennedy for every paranoid occasion. Mutual Contempt is tempting stuff for those interested in contemporary U.S. and world history. Read it as a study of two great characters of the 60s, but not as the Rosetta stone of America between Dallas 1963 and Los Angeles 1968.
