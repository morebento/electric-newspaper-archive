Doppelgangers
by Nick Bacon
a Z-Fave book from Kensington Publishing Corp
rrp $5.95 copyright 1995

Doppelgangers, a novel targetted at young adults, is by the relatively unknown author Nick Baron.

The prologue narrates a most unpleasant murder which dwells alarmingly on gory detail. There is no mention in the main body of the book about any police hunt for the murderer of the young tennis star and the only thing that ties it to the remainder of the story is the autographed photograph of India Isaacson, a movie star whom the murderer, Devoted, promises will be his next victim. To me it seemed insufficient as a prologue, making me wonder if there was something within a previous draft of the book which may have been cut, thereby rendering pointless the prologue which remained. I think the suspense and horror could have been increased more efficiently with subtle additions to the narrative yet still establish the necessary premise.

In the beginning of the story, young Candayce Townsend is walking through a mall, having finished her shift in a hamburger joint, fantasising that someone will accost her and promise to make her famous. She becomes aware that she is being followed by two people, a man and a woman, and takes refuge with a friend working in a music store. While he watches she approaches the two and discovers, miracle of miracles, that they want her as a replacement for a movie star, India Isaacson, whom she closely resembles. After due checking, and permission from her single mother who is desperately working in an attempt earn enough to pay their bills and establish a good life for Candayce, she agrees. The money will certainly help her mother and, of course, herself.

Candayce soon finds she is being stalked by a fan who sends her flowers and signs himself 'Devoted'. How Candayce is successfully able to carry out the deception, and attempt to evade the murderer comprises the rest of the tale.

The initial treatment implies that the story will be a young girl's dreams come true with a bit of menace thrown in. The writing is involving and clear but at times I think it gets lost a bit. Nonetheless, I think the targeted audience should enjoy it.

I wish blurb writers would sometimes make the effort to read the final draft of a book. The back jacket blurb proclaims that Candayce Townsend becomes the prey of "a serial madman known as 'The Autograph Killer'". Oh really? Not in this draft he's not. The proofreading of the work also leaves something to be desired since words are left out in places and inverted commas go astray and are inserted needlessly. That is probably just a quibble, since I do know how hard it is to eliminate every error when proofing.

My principal complaint concerns the ending of the novel. After a competently written and worked out main body of the book, the ending is contrived and gives the appearance of simply being stuck on with perhaps the intention of providing a further frisson for the suspense. Doesn't work, for me, at least and I doubt if sophisticated young adults will find it convincing. Young readers deserve as much respect for their intelligence as do adults.
