'Witch'
by Barbara Michaels
pub Berkley, copyright 1973
rrp $US6.99

'The Hippopotamus Pool'
by Elizabeth Peters
pub. Warner, copyright 1996
rrp $11.95

reviewed by Denise Wels

Barbara Mertz = Barbara Michaels = Elizabeth Peters

Thanks to the amazing industry of the person who puts together all the information found on MysteryLadies site at http://www.geocities.com/Wellesley/3400/ I was able to discover that the above three authors were one and the same.

I have read nothing by Barbara Mertz (the lady's real name) and only two books by Barbara Michaels ('The Dancing Floor', pub Harper, rrp. $13.95, copyright 1997. and 'Witch' pub Berkley, rrp $US6.99 copyright 1973) I have read some short stories in the name of Elizabeth Peters as well as a novel in paperback, 'The Hippopotamus Pool', pub. Warner, rrp $11.95. copyright 1996.

Barbara Mertz has a Ph.D. in Egyptology and as Elizabeth Peters draws heavily on her knowledge, without becoming didactic. Take heart all you academics attempting to enthuse your Ph.D. students with a passion for getting on with their work: this woman was writing much fiction while at the same time working at obtaining her Ph.D. in '86. It can be done!

In her Michaels' persona 'The Dancing Floor' is novel no.28: as Elizabeth Peters, 'The Hippopotamus Pool' is no.25. I gather she writes learned tomes as Barbara Mertz. I think 'prolific' is a fair description of the lady.

Over the intervening 24 years between 'Witch' and 'Dancing Floor' Michaels has refined her style. The first mentioned is heavily laced with the supernatural and a large dash of romance and horror while the second is more subtle in its approach.

Despite 'Witch' being an obviously early and far less mature work than 'Dancing Floor' it is nonetheless quite enjoyable if one is not looking for anything too heavy and thoughtful and does not object to dollops of romance.

Ellen, a thirty-eight year old divorcee with a teenage daughter has been keeping house for her widowed brother-in-law and his three sons for some years. Now Ellen's daughter is going to Europe for a vacation then returning to attend college away from home. Jack, Ellen's brother-in-law, has accepted an overseas diplomatic posting and his three sons will all be in college so, faced with the breakup of the family, Ellen buys a house in a small village in the country.

Naturally the house is supposed to be haunted, by the spirit of a witch from the early eighteenth century and Ellen quickly acquires an unenviable reputation with the local populace who we note have no sense of humour, after making some fun predictions for the local youth, which come true. Coincidence, of course, or is it? COULD Ellen be a witch?

Ellen, we learn at the beginning of the book, is in love with Jack but in those days it was apparently politically incorrect to give one's brother-in-law any hint of non-sisterly affection. When Ellen meets her neighbour, eligible Norman McKay, the reader can easily sense romance is, somehow or other, afoot. There are several flies in the ointment one of which is Norman's mentally disturbed nephew Tim. Another is that Norman is an ailurophobe, something that apparently acts as a great attraction to Ishtar (the only reference to Ms. Michaels' Egyptological background), Ellen's cat; but Norman is possessed of two fierce dogs which terrify both his nephew and Ellen.

The village has the almost obligatory lecherous minister in charge of the wonderfully named Earthly Church of the Wrath of God. The minister, naturally, makes oblique advances to Ellen, who rebuffs him and also alienates Mrs. Grapow, the local shopkeeper and leader of the village's women.

What witch would be complete without a familiar? A white cat, reputedly belonging to the witch, Maria,who had set up an animal sanctuary around the house, is seen near Ellen's house (perhaps Maria should have taken out a patent on something more effective than Geritol, to work that small miracle). Supernatural manifestations aplenty bedevil Ellen but she begins to feel a strange rapport with the long dead witch. Whose is the female shadow that appears in Ellen's bedroom? Can it be (gasp) that of the dead enchantress? Come to think of it, aren't ghosts referred to as 'shades'?

Because I had read the (much) later book by Michaels, I enjoyed this one despite its comparative lack of depth. I do intend reading some more of her intermediate work and look forward to reading her inevitable later work.

As Elizabeth Peters, Mertz presents us with totally different face.

'The Hippopotamus Pool' exhibits almost Wodehousean humour crossed with the Saturday matinee perils of Indiana Jones type adventurers.

The hippopotamus of the title is the Egyptian goddess, Taueret , the patroness of childbirth.The Hippopotamus Pool is the title given by Amelia Peabody Emerson to a papyrus fairy tale which she is translating.

The story is told in the first person by Amelia, wife of Radcliffe Emerson (known to his Egyptian workers as 'Rather of Curses' because of his easy employment of swearing) and mother of enigmatic Walter Peabody Emerson, commonly known as Ramses and feared as a demon by the local population. How a fifteen year old boy could acquire sufficient maturity to appear enigmatic defies my imagination, but then I doubt Peters sees her characters as other than caricatures.

Amelia (addressed by her husband as 'Peabody', her maiden name) and Radcliffe (addressed in return by Amelia as Emerson ... he loathes his given name) are Egyptologists seeking a new tomb, reported to be undisturbed by grave robbers and with the mummy of Queen Tetisheri intact. A statue of Tetisheri bears an uncanny resemblance to Nefret, the teenage ward of the Emersons. Amelia is told that Nefret is the reincarnation of Tetisheri.

Peters blithely mixes historical figures with her her fictional characters, allowing them a part in the action.

Peters carefully overdraws her character... Emerson is frequently portrayed as 'snapping' his teeth, this, presumably as an attractive aspect of his masculinity. Amelia is heroically drawn, she as well as her son being portrayed as intimidating the locals with her magical powers, not the least of which are held in a special parasol which she uses to defend herself when threatened physically. She and Emerson are deeply in love despite his attraction for (not to) lovely ladies throughout the story.

The book begins with a strange masked man, Saleh, claiming to be the reincarnation of Heriamon the High Priest of the ka of Tetisheri, seeking an interview with Emerson. He displays an ancient ring which he insists has been handed down for thousands of years and that each of his incarnations has borne the responsibility of ensuring Tetisheri's safe rebirth. He exhorts the Emersons to excavate the tomb of Tetisheri and is about to give them a map of its location when he falls into a fit, the lights go out, Amelia flees to find a doctor, and the body of Mr Saleh disappears. Saleh's corpse is later discovered, but no word of a further incarnation is spoken.

There are mysterious and threatening figures appearing almost on every page. Is Miss Marmaduke, the lady who offers her services both as assistant to the Egyptologists and instructor of the children a spy or a vapid foolish woman? Is the child David, rescued from one of the antiquities forgers and grandson to Abdullah, the Emerson's foreman, the abused although talented artistic child he appears or a spy for the aforesaid dealer?

Two cats, Anubis, who claims Emerson as his own, and a female, always referred to as'the cat Bastet' (at one stage Ramses exhorts her 'Come along, the cat Bastet' ... and she does!) play important parts in the action.

Peters introduces journalists, both good and bad, into the plot: the story abounds with inimical as well as benevolent people ... but how can one distinguish between the two? Implications of the supernatural abound but are never overt as they are with her Michaels pseudonym.

Ms. Mertz gives the reader the benefit of her vast expertise in Egyptology but never lets the pace flag nor bog down in didacticism. She is both funny and witty as she has (non-archaeological) digs at standard heroes and villains alike.

Had I not read in the MysteryLadies site, that font of information on mystery fiction, that Michaels and Peters were the one person, I would never have been tempted to read books by both pseudonyms concurrently and thus would have missed an exjoyable experience.
